# Chapter 11: The Dismissal

## Scene 1

The letter to Hamilton had reached its third paragraph when the knock came.

Pickering did not look up immediately. He finished the sentence—*the General's assessment of the commission's authority remains, in my judgment, the only sound basis upon which to resist the further encroachments of*—and then set down the quill with the unhurried deliberateness of one who has decided the caller will wait as long as the work requires. The anteroom beyond the door was Fenton's province; Fenton would manage it.

The knock came again.

Fenton's voice, then a second voice—younger, uncertain, carrying the register of someone whose errand has been described as important and who is now somewhat oppressed by the description.

Pickering rose. The State Department office on a May morning had its own smell, distinct from the evening hours: cold tallow not yet overcome by fresh candles, the dusty warmth of documents long undisturbed, and beneath it a faint brine from the window left cracked against the unseasonable heat. He crossed to the door and opened it himself—not his custom—because twenty years in positions where subordinates formed opinions had taught him to take the measure of whoever stood there before the meeting could establish its own terms.

The young man—perhaps twenty-three, dressed in a coat that was good without being remarkable, his hat held at his side with the correctness of rehearsal—extended a sealed letter.

'From the President's House, Mr. Secretary. I am instructed to await acknowledgment.'

The seal was the executive seal. The hand on the direction was not Adams's own.

Pickering took the letter. He did not break it immediately. He moved to the window so that the street light fell across the paper, and stood there with the letter unopened while the young man remained in the doorway and the smell of old ink thickened around them both.

The draft to Hamilton lay on the desk, third paragraph visible, the phrase *the General's assessment* plain enough to any literate eye. Pickering moved to the desk as if to set down the sealed letter beside it, and in the motion—continuous, unremarkable—his left palm came down upon the half-drafted page and drew it beneath the folio of Lisbon dispatches. He aligned the folio's edges with excessive precision, pressing the paper flat, his palms moving once, twice, until the corners met the desk's edge and nothing showed but the consular seal of the port of Lisbon facing upward in the morning light.

Then he broke Adams's seal.

He read it standing, with the deliberateness of one who refuses to be hurried by another's urgency. The letter was brief. It requested—the word was *requested*, Pickering noted that, filed it—his resignation as Secretary of State, citing the public interest and the exigencies of the present season, and expressed the President's confidence that Mr. Pickering would, upon reflection, discern the wisdom of a voluntary departure over any alternative the Constitution might provide.

He read it again from the salutation.

*Requested.* Not *demanded*. Not *directed*. A man who had already decided would not have written *requested*.

A cramp tightened across the heel of his right hand—the hand that had been writing—and he flexed his fingers once, twice, and let his arm fall to his side.

'The Secretary will respond in writing,' he said, without turning from the window. 'By close of business on the twelfth.'

'I am to convey your acknowledgment now, sir, if—'

'You have conveyed it.' He turned then. His voice was level, stripped of inflection, the way a ruled line is stripped of ornament. 'The Secretary acknowledges receipt and will reply in writing. That is the acknowledgment. You may report it so.'

The young man's hat turned once in his hands—a single rotation, arrested—and he said, 'Very good, sir,' and withdrew.

Pickering listened to the footsteps cross the anteroom, the outer door settle closed, the silence that followed—the silence of an inventory completed, a known quantity removed, the shelves still standing.

Then he returned to the desk, lifted the Lisbon folio, and drew out the draft.

*Requested*, he thought again.

He uncapped the inkwell and reached for the quill.

## Scene 2

The lodgings on Walnut Street were cold in the way that rented rooms are always cold—not the cold of a house shut against the season, but the cold of a house that has never quite been warmed, that holds its chill in the plaster and the floorboards and gives it back at night regardless of what fire is laid. The candle on the writing desk stood at his left, burning low, its light pulling the room inward so that the walls and their shadows seemed to press closer than they were. The Madeira sat untouched at his elbow. Somewhere below in the street, a watchman's step passed and faded, and the silence that followed was fuller, more absolute, as though the night had closed behind the sound like water.

Pickering sat with the draft before him—four pages in his own hand—and read it as he would read any document of consequence: first for argument, then for exposure. The argument was sound. He was declining the President's request. The grounds were principled, the language measured. He had not accused Adams of unconstitutional conduct, though the case was there to be made. He had not named Hamilton, though every informed reader would recognise the hand behind the policy he defended.

That was the passage. Third page, second paragraph.

*Having acted throughout in conformity with the counsel of those best positioned to judge the republic's interests in its present exigency, and having received no directive from the Executive that superseded the established course of policy as communicated to me—*

His thumbnail pressed into the margin, leaving a small white crescent in the paper.

*As communicated to me.*

By whom. Through what channel. Adams had been reading the department's correspondence with an attention Pickering had not credited to him—he was certain of that now, certain in the way one becomes certain of a thing only after the evidence accumulates past the point of comfortable doubt. The counsel in question had not come from the President. It was there in the subordinate clause, precise and damning, and the precision was his own doing: a man whose policy derived from the President would not write *as communicated to me.* He would write *as directed by the President.* The distinction was small and absolute.

He had written it without thinking. That was the thing that sat with him now, in the cold room, with the candle burning down—not the exposure itself, which could be managed, but the fact that the phrase had come naturally, as the truth does, before the more careful self had time to arrive and correct it.

The candle guttered. The flame bent sideways, contracted to a blue thread, and very nearly went out. He pushed back his chair, found the scissors on the shelf by touch in the sudden near-dark, and trimmed the wick with hands that required two attempts before they were steady enough. The flame steadied and rose, and the room expanded again, and he sat for a moment without moving, his eyes on the wall above the desk where the shadows had retreated.

He had been head of this department for three years. He had managed a correspondence of such delicacy that the wrong word in the wrong dispatch might have brought a French fleet to the Delaware. He had done this competently, in the service of principles he held to be correct—not merely expedient, not merely Hamiltonian, but correct, in the way that a mathematical proof is correct, admitting no revision.

And he had written *as communicated to me* without thinking.

He dipped the pen and drew a line through the paragraph—not the light crossing-out of revision but a black score pressed deep enough that the nib dragged, leaving the original legible to anyone who cared to hold the page at an angle to the light. He drew a second line through the first, and a third through the second, until the paragraph was obliterated.

Then he rewrote it.

*Having acted throughout in faithful execution of Federalist principles as I understand them, and in conformity with the obligations of my office as they have been consistently exercised since this department's establishment—*

He read the new sentence. It said nothing that could be used against him. Nothing that was precisely untrue. Nothing at all, in the way that certain very careful documents say nothing while appearing to say a great deal, and this was, he recognised, the document's final form.

He wrote the closing paragraph standing, the pen moving quickly. He signed it. He sanded it, folded it, and reached for the sealing wax.

The flame took the wax and he pressed his seal into it—the impression of the department, which he remained entitled to use until formally relieved, whatever Adams imagined his letter had accomplished. The wax hardened. He held the letter at arm's length, turned it once, and set it on the desk.

It sat there in the candlelight, addressed, sealed, ready.

He did not pour the Madeira. He stood for a moment with the wax cool and slightly rough under his left thumb, the paper faintly warm from the sealing, the cold pressing in through the window glass at his back. The letter would be delivered in the morning. Adams would read it. Whatever Adams did in consequence, he had given him nothing he could use.

This was correct. This was the only correct course.

He went to bed.

## Scene 3

The second messenger arrived at half past nine.

Pickering heard him before he saw him—a deliberate tread on the stair, unhurried, the footfall of one who had been told there was no occasion for haste. Nothing like the quick, apologetic step of young Hartwell two days prior. Something heavier. More considered.

He had sent Fenton away an hour earlier. The dispatches sat on the sorting table in their red cord, uncut. He had not touched them. There had seemed little point.

The knock, when it came, was two strokes. Measured.

'Enter.'

The man who came through the door was perhaps fifty, grey at the temples, dressed in a plain coat of good dark wool—one of the senior household staff from the President's House, not a secretary. Chosen for reliability rather than rank. For the capacity to deliver a thing and say nothing further about it.

He carried a single letter.

'Mr. Secretary.' The address was correct. The tone was not precisely anything.

'You have something for me.'

'I do, sir.' He crossed the room and presented it.

The seal was the presidential seal, pressed firm and clean into red wax. The direction was in a hand Pickering did not recognise—a junior clerk's, impersonal, as though even the address had been assigned to someone whose name would not be remembered. He turned it in his hands.

The spring sunlight through the window lay warm and insistent on the back of his neck. He reached up to loosen his stock, caught himself, let his hand drop.

'Be so good as to wait.'

The messenger withdrew three steps and stood with his hands at his sides, looking at nothing in particular. He had been chosen well.

Pickering broke the seal.

One paragraph. He read it.

He read it again.

The President of the United States no longer required his services as Secretary of State. John Marshall had been nominated to the Senate that morning as his successor. He was to consider himself relieved of his duties with immediate effect, and the department's seals and keys were to be surrendered to such person as Mr. Marshall should designate.

No enumeration of grievances. No citation of acts. No acknowledgment that he had served, written, refused, replied. Three years of his life reduced to a subordinate clause—*no longer required*—delivered in the hand of a clerk whose name he would never learn, sealed with a stamp bearing the face of the republic he had given those years to preserve.

He set the letter on the desk. He stood quite still.

The messenger waited.

Pickering reached for his quill. His right hand would not cooperate at first—the fingers stiff, the grip uncertain—and he had to close and open his fist twice before the barrel sat properly between them.

He drew a clean sheet from the upper drawer.

He wrote seven words.

*Received this day, 12 May 1800. T.P.*

He sanded it, shook it, folded it once. He rose and crossed to where the messenger stood and held it out. The man took it with a small inclination of the head that was not quite a bow and not quite anything else, and Pickering watched his own hand in the transaction—the slight, involuntary tremor as the paper passed between them, visible if one were watching for it, which the messenger, to his credit, was not.

'Good morning,' Pickering said.

'Good morning, Mr. Secretary.'

The man turned and walked out with the same measured tread by which he had arrived. The outer door closed. His footsteps on the stair descended and were gone, absorbed by the building's ordinary noise—a cart in the street below, someone calling to someone else across the cobbles, the indifferent percussion of a city that had not been informed it was witnessing anything of consequence.

Pickering stood in the centre of the room.

The dispatches sat on the table in their red cord, still uncut. The letter lay on the desk where he had left it, its single paragraph complete, its meaning entire, admitting no reply.

## Scene 4

He did not summon Fenton. Fenton came anyway.

The clerk entered through the anteroom with his customary efficiency, a sheaf of correspondence tucked beneath his arm, and crossed toward the table as though the twelfth of May were no different from the eleventh. His footsteps were unhurried. He set the papers on the table's edge and began to work the cord loose with two fingers, his eyes moving across the labels with the practiced attention of one who has performed the same action three hundred mornings running and expects to perform it three hundred more.

Pickering watched him from across the room.

Sounds from the street filtered in through the open window: a cartwright's apprentice somewhere below calling out measurements, a horse objecting to something with a short, percussive complaint. The sounds of a city that had not paused, had not marked the morning, had not troubled itself to register that the head of this department had, as of nine-forty-three, ceased to hold that office. Philadelphia went about its commerce.

'Leave those.'

Fenton's hands stilled. He turned, the cord still looped around his fingers.

'Sir.'

'I said leave them. Set them down and go.'

A beat. The clerk's face offered nothing—not alarm, not comprehension, not the faintest tremor of the satisfaction that Pickering suspected was in there somewhere, banked carefully behind the professional blankness of one who had learned to wait. Fenton set the papers on the table, the red cord still intact, and withdrew without asking the question he had plainly formed.

The door closed.

Pickering moved to the desk.

He lifted the Lisbon folio—the same folio, the same desk, the same morning light falling across it at the same angle as it had two days before when he had slid the draft to Hamilton beneath it—and retrieved the half-finished letter from where it lay folded beneath. He placed it inside his coat, against the left breast, and felt the paper settle against his waistcoat.

Then he straightened, and looked at the room.

Washington's portrait occupied the far wall, as it always had—that wide, composed face, the eyes that gave nothing and asked nothing and registered no particular opinion about what was transpiring beneath them. Pickering had noted once, early in his tenure, that it was a poor likeness. He still believed it.

He opened the upper-left drawer and removed what was his: a small penknife with a horn handle, a memorandum book in green calfskin, three letters from his wife that he had read and reread in the months of their separation until the folds had gone soft as cloth. He held one of them for a moment without opening it—the paper thin and worn, the ink of her direction faded to brown at the crease—and then placed it with the others in the leather dispatch case he had carried from Salem thirty years ago, which had outlasted two wars and one administration and would, it appeared, outlast this one.

From the right-hand tray, he lifted his quills. Nine in number—he counted them, then stood with them in his palm and did not immediately wrap them, only stood there with the nine quills in his hand, the same hand that had written the letter Adams had read, the letter Adams had refused, the seven words of acknowledgment that were all he had permitted himself in the end. The room was very quiet. Through the open window came the smell of the street—horse dung and river mud and the faint sweetness of something baking somewhere on Chestnut—and beneath it, faint and persistent, the smell of old ink that had been the room's constant companion and was now, he understood, no longer his to breathe.

He wrapped the quills in a square of brown cloth and placed them in the case beside the memorandum book.

He buckled the case.

At the door he paused, turned back as though he had forgotten something, stood in the doorway for a long moment looking at the desk, at the uncut dispatches on the table, at the chair behind the desk that would within a week hold a different man. The room had the quality of a room already vacated—not empty, but no longer inhabited, its objects already beginning their slow reclassification from his to the department's to the next occupant's. The portrait looked on with its customary absence of opinion.

He walked out.

The stairwell was cool and dim. His left knee buckled on the first step down to the street—stiff from the hours at the desk—and he caught the doorframe with his free hand and steadied himself before anyone below could observe it, then continued down.

The sun on Chestnut Street was hard and clear, the kind of May light that showed everything without mercy.

He turned north.

The letter in his breast pocket was warm from his body. He had not finished it. He would finish it tonight, in cipher, and it would travel by private hand to New York, where Hamilton's pamphlet sat at the printer already composed, already typeset, awaiting only the intelligence that would make its timing perfect—the intelligence that the Secretary of State had been dismissed, that Adams had broken his own cabinet, that the Federalist fracture was now irreparable and public and could be laid at the President's feet before the November balloting. Pickering carried that intelligence against his heart, and he walked north through the indifferent city, and he did not look back.

The pamphlet would be in every newspaper office in Philadelphia within the fortnight. Hamilton had written it to destroy Adams, and Pickering's dismissal was the final proof it required, the last weight on a scale that had been tipping since February. Whatever the republic thought of him now—whatever verdict the party rendered, whatever history chose to record—he had not been idle. He had not been silent. The work was not finished, and the men who would finish it were already in motion, and the morning's humiliation was not an ending but a charge, and he carried it north through the hard May light with the city's noise pressing in on all sides and the letter warm against his ribs, and somewhere between the State Department and the corner of Fifth Street the understanding arrived that had been waiting since half past nine: that there was no appeal, that Hamilton's pamphlet would not restore him, that the republic had accepted his removal with seven words and the quiet closing of a door.

He walked on. The sun was merciless. The street was loud.

And the pamphlet was at the printer, and Adams did not yet know it.
