# Chapter 3: The Oath Before Breakfast

## Scene 1

The room at Brown's Indian Queen was not built for historical reflection. Its ceiling was low, its single window giving onto Pennsylvania Avenue, where the gas lamps still burned against a sky the colour of old pewter. The washstand held a basin of water gone cold since the servant had brought it up, and the tallow candle on the mantelpiece had guttered to a nub, leaving behind a smell of scorched wick and spent animal fat. Tyler stood in the middle of the floor in his riding clothes—coat stiff with dried mud along the hem, boots leaving dark prints on the boards—and took account.

He had not slept. He had ridden through the night from the Williamsburg landing and his body registered every mile of it: the stiffness across his shoulders, the raw place on his left palm where he had gripped the rein too long without a glove. The valise on the floor sat unopened. He had not thought to open it.

The knock, when it came, was a young man's knock—the knock of obligation discharged, not admittance sought.

Fletcher Webster stood in the doorway with the careful composure of his father's son—the same heavy brow, the same quality of attention deployed as a form of judgment—though the father's authority had not yet settled onto the son's frame and perhaps never would. He was travel-worn and trying not to show it. He held the letter in both hands, which was, Tyler thought, a small unnecessary ceremony.

'Mr. Tyler,' Fletcher said. 'I trust your journey was—'

'You have something for me.'

Fletcher extended the letter. Tyler crossed the room and took it.

The seal was already broken—he had read this letter, or its twin, on the steamer, the original copy folded in his breast pocket where it had been since Williamsburg. He read the second copy now because the act of reading it here, in this room, with this young man watching, was itself a form of reply. His eyes moved along the salutation: *To John Tyler, Vice President of the United States.* He did not permit his expression to change.

A sharp cramp seized his right calf and he gripped the bedpost with his free hand, standing briefly on one leg while the muscle unknotted itself. Fletcher said nothing. Tyler read on.

The letter was not long. It informed him of President Harrison's death. It expressed the cabinet's condolence. It stated that the executive powers and duties had devolved upon him and requested his attendance at a cabinet meeting at the earliest practicable hour. It was signed by the members of the cabinet in their official capacities and addressed, throughout, to the Vice President.

*Powers and duties.* Not the office. Not the presidency. The cabinet had weighed each word and chosen these, and the choosing was the message.

Tyler turned and set the letter face-down on the washstand without folding it.

Fletcher had begun to speak. 'The secretary—my father—believes that the cabinet meeting should be your first act in the city, sir. There is much to be arranged, and the members are already gathered, and it would set a tone of—'

'What is the condition of the city?'

Fletcher paused. 'Sir?'

'Is the city awake? Are the courts open? Are men abroad at this hour?'

'It is not yet six o'clock. There are some people in the streets. The mourning has been proclaimed. The bells have been ringing since last evening.'

Tyler looked at him for a moment. Fletcher's eyes moved to the letter on the washstand and back.

'Mr. Webster,' Tyler said, 'where may I find Chief Justice Cranch at this hour—his residence, if you know it.'

The silence that followed was brief and entirely revealing.

'Judge Cranch resides on Capitol Hill, I believe.' Fletcher's voice had acquired a careful quality. 'Though I cannot imagine the purpose—the cabinet meeting would surely—'

'Thank you.' Tyler crossed to the window and looked down at the avenue below, where a drayman was guiding his cart through the early grey and a single lamp swayed in the wind above a shuttered door. 'You have delivered your letter faithfully. My compliments to your father.'

Fletcher did not move at once.

Then he moved.

Tyler waited until he heard the footsteps reach the bottom of the stair. Then he put on his hat.

If he went to the cabinet first, the question of what he was would be decided for him before he opened his mouth. Webster would have arranged the room, the seating, the very grammar of the occasion—*acting* president, *temporary* authority, *pending* whatever Congress chose to determine. The cabinet would be gracious about it. That was the danger. Graciousness was the mechanism by which a man was reduced without his noticing until the reduction was complete.

He had perhaps two hours before Fletcher Webster reached his father.

## Scene 2

The bells of Christ Church had been tolling at intervals since before dawn, each stroke arriving not merely as sound but as a deep concussive pressure that climbed through the soles of his boots and into the bones of his legs. He counted them as he walked: seven, eight. Between strokes, the city held a silence Washington was never given to—the vendors not yet crying their wares, the carriages stilled, the ordinary clamour of the capital suppressed as though by civic agreement. Black crepe hung from every doorway he passed, from the lintels of counting houses and ordinaries alike, dark fabric stirring faintly in the April air, carrying with it the faint smell of damp wool and cedar.

He walked alone.

No aide, no secretary accompanied him; no one knew he had gone. Pennsylvania Avenue lay behind him now, and he had turned east toward the Hill, moving through streets he knew from his years in Congress but which felt, this morning, strangely foreign—a city whose gate no one had yet agreed to open.

His coat was the same he had ridden through the night in—the mud on the left hem had dried to a pale crust, and he brushed at it with his fingers as he walked, breaking the dried clay into powder that drifted and was gone. The gesture accomplished nothing of consequence. He continued it.

He had not eaten. Somewhere south of Fredericksburg, a cold supper—some bread, a piece of salt meat—and nothing since. His stomach made its protest audibly in the silence of a cross-street, a sound belonging to no occasion of dignity whatsoever, and he walked on without breaking his stride.

Cranch's house was on the north side of B Street, a federal-style brick residence of moderate distinction, its shutters closed against the morning. A mourning wreath had been affixed to the door, the black ribbon tied with the careful knot of a household that understood ceremony. He mounted the two stone steps and knocked.

The wait was long enough that he knocked again. The door opened on a manservant of middle age, in shirt-sleeves and a waistcoat hastily buttoned, who examined him with the measured scepticism of a man who had opened this door to uninvited callers before and had developed opinions about them.

'The Judge,' the servant said, before Tyler could speak, 'is at his breakfast and not receiving callers at this hour. If you wish to state your business, I will inquire whether he is disposed to see you.'

'I wish to see him directly.'

'Sir, he is at breakfast—'

'I have heard you.' Tyler reached into his coat and then stopped, because there was no card to present, no letter of introduction, no document that established what he was about to claim. He stood on the step of a judge's house in mud-spattered clothes, without escort, without credential, without anything in the world except the conviction that the law was on his side and the constitutional text to support it. If Cranch refused him—if the door closed again and the household returned to its breakfast—the cabinet would have its morning undisturbed, and the question of the presidency would be decided by men who had already decided it.

He kept his hands still at his sides.

'You will tell the Judge,' he said, 'that the Vice President of the United States requires a word with him on a matter of urgency.' He paused. The servant's expression did not hold. 'That is—'

He said it.

'The President of the United States.'

The words came out level, unhurried, in the cadence of a man stating a boundary established by deed and survey—one that does not require the listener's concurrence to be true. He had not said them before this moment—not to Fletcher Webster, not to his son Robert, not to Letitia in her chamber at Sherwood Forest. He had thought them, had constructed the argument for them, had read the succession clause until the paper was soft at the fold. But saying them aloud was a different act entirely: the difference between a deed held in a drawer and one read into the record.

The servant's face changed. Not dramatically—a slight widening of the eyes, a stillness replacing the habitual wariness of a man at a door. He stepped back.

'I will inform the Judge at once, sir.'

The door opened wider. Tyler crossed the threshold.

## Scene 3

The study smelled of coal smoke and old paper, and held the particular cold of a room where the fire has not been tended since midnight. Cranch received him in his dressing gown—a man of sixty-eight or thereabouts, with the careful, unhurried manner of a jurist who had outlasted three chief justices and expected to outlast more. He had set aside his breakfast without apparent resentment and was reading the succession clause by the time Tyler had removed his hat.

He read it twice. His lips moved on the first pass, slightly, the way an old man's do when he encounters a text he wishes to be certain he has not misread. Then he read it aloud, in the careful, uninflected voice of a judge who has learned that intonation is itself an argument.

"'In case of the removal of the President from office, or of his death, resignation, or inability to discharge the powers and duties of the said office, the same shall devolve on the Vice President.'" He set the page down on his desk, beside the half-eaten breakfast, and looked at Tyler. "The same."

"The office," Tyler said.

"Or the powers and duties of the office." Cranch pressed two fingers against his lips. "The pronoun admits both readings, sir. You will acknowledge that."

The coal fire had burned low in the night and not yet been built up again; the room held the accumulated cold of the small hours, the kind that settles into plaster and book-spines and does not yield to a dying grate. Tyler stood close enough to feel the faint, irregular pulse of warmth against his shins. His coat was damp at the shoulders still. He had not removed it.

"I acknowledge," Tyler said, "that men of imperfect attention have read it so. The framers did not intend two offices to exist simultaneously—a presidency vacant in name and occupied in function. The Constitution does not contemplate a government by committee of the sort that would follow from that reading."

"I have no quarrel with your constitutional reasoning." Cranch stood, moved to the window, stood with his back to the light. "My quarrel, if I may call it that, is with the precedent. This has not been done before. No man has stood where you stand. No court has ruled. No Congress has acted." He turned. "If I administer this oath in the unqualified form and your claim is subsequently challenged—by the Senate, by any tribunal of competent jurisdiction—I shall have been a party to a constitutional usurpation."

"Or to a constitutional necessity."

Cranch was silent. A log shifted in the grate with a sharp crack, and both men flinched.

"There is another consideration." Cranch moved back to his desk but did not sit. He set one hand flat on the surface, as though steadying something. "The cabinet is assembled at this hour. If they learn you have come here first—before attending them, before any consultation—they will say you acted in haste. That the oath was obtained by circumvention."

"They will say it regardless."

"Yes." A pause—the pause of a man who has just been told something he already knew and is deciding whether to resent the telling. "But they will say it with less force if the record shows appropriate caution on my part." He took up his pen. "I will administer the oath. But I must ask your indulgence in one respect. I will insert a preamble—brief, and for the record only. That you take this oath as doubts may arise, and for greater caution." He looked up. "The language does not diminish your claim. It protects the record."

It diminished his claim. Tyler knew it as certainly as he knew the room was cold and that his breakfast at Brown's sat untouched. Cranch's preamble was a judicial hedge dressed as a courtesy, and it would be cited—he could see the pamphlets already, Clay's voice rolling through the Senate chamber—as evidence that even the administering judge had harboured reservations. A precautionary oath. An oath taken in doubt.

He held his right hand steady.

"Proceed," he said.

Cranch retrieved a Bible from the shelf behind his desk—a broad, cloth-bound volume, its spine cracked with use, carrying the faint smell of old leather and dust. Cool against Tyler's left palm when he placed it there. His right hand he raised. His left hand, where no one could observe it, gathered a fistful of his coat's fabric and did not release it.

Cranch read the preamble first, in the same judicial monotone. *As doubts may arise, and for greater caution.* Then the oath itself, clause by clause, Tyler repeating each after him.

Here the prose must stop and let the words stand alone.

*I do solemnly swear—*

*—that I will faithfully execute the Office of President of the United States—*

*—and will to the best of my Ability, preserve, protect and defend the Constitution of the United States.*

The words of Article II fell into the cold air of a private study in a city draped in mourning crepe, witnessed by no cabinet officer, no senator, no representative of any institution save the Circuit Court of the District of Columbia. They were not loud. They did not need to be.

Cranch signed the certificate he had prepared. His script moved across the paper in the loose, slightly trembling characters of a man past sixty who has not yet breakfasted.

Tyler took the pen. His signature was even, the letters formed without haste, each character complete. He saw Cranch's writing beside his own—the older man's ascenders wandering, uncertain at the close—and said nothing about what he saw, though the seeing was not without its satisfaction.

"I shall require a copy of this certificate," Tyler said. "Written in your own hand, signed, and delivered to me before I quit this house."

Cranch regarded him for a moment with the expression of a man who has just administered a sacrament and is uncertain whether the recipient understood its weight.

Then he drew a fresh sheet toward him and began to write.

## Scene 4

The pen scratched once and stopped.

Outside, a vendor was crying his wares along Pennsylvania Avenue—something unintelligible at this distance, the syllables worn down by repetition to pure sound. Wheels on cobblestone. A bell, somewhere north of the hotel, tolling for Harrison. Washington was waking to its grief, and Tyler sat at a writing desk no larger than a church pew's end, in yesterday's clothes, staring at a word he had just crossed out.

*Duties.*

He had written it and known immediately it would not serve. The cabinet's letter had given him duties. Cranch's preamble had given him duties. The entire edifice of what they wished to make of him rested on that word—its suggestion of the delegated, the temporary, the commission held in trust for a principal who was merely absent rather than gone. He drew the pen through it once and set the pen down.

The breakfast tray breathed cold beside his elbow—coffee gone to something approaching room temperature, a plate of bread and cold meat untouched, the fat on the meat beginning to congeal at its edges. He reached for the cup without looking at it, drank, and set it back.

The governing principle had come to him on the walk back from B Street, surfacing from boyhood reading the way such things do when a man is cold and hungry and has just done something he cannot undo. His father had kept a copy of Blackstone on the study shelf at Greenway Court, the binding cracked from use, the margins annotated in a hand that grew progressively less certain as the years advanced. *The king never dies.* The sovereign is continuous; the office does not fall vacant at the moment of a monarch's death but passes instantaneously, without interruption, without the fiction of a gap in which lesser men might insert their authority. The republic had no king, and Tyler wanted none—but the principle held. The presidency was continuous. It must be occupied fully, not administered by a committee of department heads answering to a Senate leader in Kentucky.

He took up the pen again.

*For the first time in our history, the person elected to the Vice Presidency of the United States has had devolved upon him the Presidential—*

He stopped. Crossed out the last word. The pen hovered.

*Office.*

He

He wrote it. Not *powers.* Not *duties.* The *office*—the thing itself, indivisible and entire, the freehold rather than the lease, the title rather than the tenancy. A man either held the land by deed or he did not. There was no constitutional estate held at the sufferance of Congress, no title that read *subject to doubts as may arise.*

His writing hand cramped. The pen slipped; a blot spread across the page. He set it aside, flexed his fingers against the edge of the desk, and took it up again.

He read the sentence back. Then read it again.

The knock at the parlour door was brisk and entirely without apology.

He did not look up. He read the sentence a third time, fixing it in his mind as a man fixes a boundary stone before witnesses arrive to dispute it.

'Sir.' The servant's voice, tight with some suppressed urgency he had not troubled to conceal. 'Secretary Webster and Secretary Ewing are arrived downstairs, sir. And—' The hesitation was not that of a man reading from a card. It was something else. 'And Mr. Clay's man, sir. He came separately. He will not give his name, but he says he carries a communication that will not hold until morning.'

The pen was still in his hand. The ink on the page had not yet dried.

Three of them, then. Webster to persuade, Ewing to observe, and now a courier from Louisville whose errand could not wait for daylight—which meant the communication had not originated in Louisville at all, but somewhere far closer, somewhere a man might dispatch a messenger at this hour without attracting the notice of the press galleries.

He set the pen down with care, so as not to smear what he had written.

'You will inform Secretary Webster and Secretary Ewing,' he said, to the closed door, in a voice that carried no particular emphasis, 'that the President will receive them directly.' He paused. 'Clay's man will wait.'

He blotted the page. He folded it once, precisely, and placed it in his breast pocket beside the certificate Cranch had written out in his trembling hand. Then he stood, smoothed his coat, and went to the door.

Below, in the entrance hall, he could hear it now—the low, urgent register of men conferring in whispers they believed did not carry, the particular quality of silence that falls when three people stop speaking at the sound of a footstep on the stair.
