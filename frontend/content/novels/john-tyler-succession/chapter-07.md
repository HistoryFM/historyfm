# Chapter 7: The Lion Arrives

## Scene 1

The Everett letter was not going well.

Webster had the opening paragraph under control—the formula of diplomatic courtesy that preceded any communication touching on British territorial sensitivities required a certain ceremonial patience, and he had spent thirty years acquiring it—but the second paragraph, the one that would establish the American position on the St. Croix boundary without either conceding ground or provoking a counter-claim, resisted every construction he attempted. He crossed out a sentence. The ink spread where the nib had pressed too hard, and a persistent fly, drawn by the warmth of the inkwell, buzzed close and retreated to the window glass.

The room smelled of old leather and the particular dustiness of official paper—documents that had passed through many hands and absorbed the residue of every transaction. Three weeks into his tenure, and the State Department's correspondence had already buried the desk in a sediment of accumulation: dispatches from Everett in London, consular reports from Havana and Liverpool, a memorandum from the Treasury that had arrived in the wrong building and been forwarded with what appeared to be genuine bafflement.

He picked up his pen.

The fly returned. He swatted at it, and the half-formed phrase he had been carrying—*the line as established by the Treaty of 1783 admits of no construction that would*—dissolved. He set the pen down.

Caleb Cushing entered without ceremony, which was Cushing's way.

'Mr. Secretary.' Quick-eyed, thirty-eight, with the energy of one whose usefulness to larger men was itself a form of power. 'I trust I do not interrupt anything that cannot be resumed.'

'You may trust what you like, Mr. Cushing.' Webster indicated the chair across the desk without rising. 'Sit, if your business requires it.'

Cushing sat. He carried his hat in his hands—a new one, the felt unmarked by weather—and turned it once, then held it still. 'Senator Clay arrived at Gadsby's yesterday evening. I thought you would wish to know, if the news had not already reached you.'

'I had heard something to that effect.'

He had heard nothing. His secretary had mentioned Clay's departure from Lexington, and he had made the calculation himself—but that was not the same as having heard it, and the distinction seemed worth preserving.

'The Senator is most eager to see you before the session convenes,' Cushing said. 'I believe he intends to call on several members of the Administration. Informally, of course.'

'Of course.'

'He met with Mr. Ewing last evening. And with Mr. Crittenden.' Cushing let this sit for a moment, the way a man sets down a card and waits to see whether his opponent will reach for it. 'Quite a full evening, by all accounts.'

Webster looked at the crossed-out sentence. The ink still shone faintly at the correction's edge.

Ewing. Crittenden. Last evening, while Webster had been at his lodgings with a glass of Madeira and the uneasy sense that the ground was shifting in ways he could not yet map, Clay had been in conference with two members of the cabinet. Two men who held their seats at Clay's sufferance and knew it.

'I am gratified,' Webster said, 'to learn that Senator Clay occupies his time so productively.'

Cushing smiled, recognising the non-answer for what it was. 'He will be at Gadsby's through the week, naturally. The Senator mentioned that he hoped very much to see you today—before the evening, if your schedule permitted.'

'My schedule is rather fully engaged this morning.' Webster took up his pen. 'I have correspondence that requires my attention, and two appointments this afternoon that were made some time ago and cannot be deferred without discourtesy to the parties concerned. I shall call upon Senator Clay at half past four.'

The time was his own invention. He had no afternoon appointments. But the hour was late enough to suggest a man arriving on his own terms—and if Clay had already seen Ewing and Crittenden, then Webster's delay carried a message of its own. *I am not among those who come when called.*

Cushing rose. 'I shall convey your regards.'

'You may convey that I look forward to our conversation.' Webster did not look up. 'Senator Clay will find me, I trust, in excellent health and entirely at my ease.'

The door opened, closed.

Webster resumed the letter. The fly had gone. The room was very quiet, and the second paragraph still would not yield.

He was not thinking about the Everett letter.

## Scene 2

Pennsylvania Avenue in late May was a punishment. The unpaved stretch between the State Department and Gadsby's had absorbed the afternoon's heat and was releasing it now in slow, gritty waves, and the smell of horse dung and fresh-turned earth rose from the roadbed in a way that no amount of civic optimism could dignify. Webster walked it anyway, his coat darkening at the collar, his cravat wilting by degrees. He had dismissed his carriage at the State Department's steps.

A Secretary of State who arrives in a hired conveyance announces a man who has been sent for. Webster had not been sent for. He had elected to come.

The distinction would be lost on no one who mattered.

Gadsby's came into view through the haze of dust thrown up by a passing dray—substantial, self-satisfied, the kind of establishment that had absorbed so many political transactions that it had begun to believe itself a participant in them. Even from fifty yards he could read the hotel's current allegiances by the density of men on its steps: a dozen at least, coats darkening at the back with sweat, conversing in the clustered, purposive manner of those who have just left one meeting and are already contriving the next. He recognised three of them. Not one paid court to the White House.

He paused at the corner of Sixth Street, drew out his handkerchief, and mopped his brow—three strokes, unhurried, the gesture of one who finds the weather inconvenient rather than oppressive. Then he crossed the avenue.

The side entrance—a narrow door off the alley, identified for him years ago by a State Department clerk who understood Washington's social geography—admitted him into a passage that smelled of coal oil and old mop water, cooler than the street by perhaps five degrees. The boy stationed there pocketed two silver dollars without comment. Webster removed his hat and held it.

Through the wall came the low percussion of a great many men talking at once—not a sound but a pressure, the collective weight of appetite and calculation pressing through the plaster. Clay's court was in session. Webster had attended courts of law for thirty years and knew the register precisely: the hum of men waiting to be seen by someone who held something they wanted.

He was composing himself for the turn toward Clay's rooms when a door opened at the far end of the corridor and Willie Person Mangum emerged, his face carrying the satisfaction of one who has just received a confirmation he had not needed.

Mangum came forward with both hands extended, his broad North Carolina face arranged in an expression of warmth so complete and so immediately legible that Webster found himself cataloguing its component parts with the detached interest of a naturalist examining a familiar specimen.

'My dear Webster.' Mangum gripped his hand. 'I had hoped we might encounter one another before you went in. Senator Clay is in excellent spirits—most eager, I am told, to receive you.'

'You are well, I trust.' Webster held the grip a moment past its natural conclusion. 'And Mrs. Mangum—she is in Washington?'

'She remains in Person County until the session's heat has passed.' Mangum's eyes moved briefly to the hat in Webster's hand—noting, no doubt, that it had not been checked with the boy, that Webster was holding it as one holds something he intends to carry back out with him shortly—and then returned to his face. 'You will find the Senator in very fine form. He has been productive, these past days.'

'I have no doubt of it.' Webster released his hand. 'I had meant to raise a matter with you, in any case, before the session convenes. There is some question as to whether the rules of the last Congress carry over without formal readoption—a procedural point, but one with bearing on several departmental reports I shall need to transmit. I wonder whether the Senate leadership has given the question any attention.'

Mangum blinked. 'I confess the matter has not—'

'A trifling point, no doubt. But trifles have a way of becoming consequential at the worst possible moment.' Webster allowed himself a brief, collegial smile that communicated nothing. 'I should be grateful for your attention to it when occasion permits. My warmest regards to Mrs. Mangum.'

He turned toward Clay's rooms before Mangum could recover his footing.

Behind him, he heard the man begin to say something, then think better of it. The corridor smelled of coal oil and, faintly, of the tobacco seeping through Clay's door for the better part of three days.

Webster walked toward it.

## Scene 3

The bourbon sat at his elbow untouched.

Clay had poured it himself—a gesture of easy proprietorship, as though the decanter and the suite and the late-afternoon light filtering through the half-drawn curtains were all extensions of his person, to be dispensed as he saw fit. The cigar smoke had colonised the room, sweet and faintly acrid, woven into the drapes and the upholstery; it was the atmosphere of days of court-holding, of senators and editors and committee chairmen received and dispatched with their instructions.

Clay had not sat. He moved as he spoke—not restlessness but the occupation of space, the way a commanding officer walks a perimeter he already owns.

'The Finance Committee will take it up within the week,' Clay said, 'once the session is formally convened. Ewing has the draft. He knows what is wanted.' He paused at the window, glancing down at Pennsylvania Avenue with the mild interest of a proprietor surveying his grounds from the upper floor. 'Berrien has some reservations about the branch provisions. Berrien's reservations are a matter of temperament, not principle. He will come round.'

Webster turned his bourbon glass slowly in his hand, watching the amber liquid catch the light.

'You have spoken with Berrien.'

'I have spoken,' Clay said, 'with everyone worth speaking with.' He turned from the window, and his expression was serene—the serenity of one who has already conducted the battle in his mind and found it won. He crossed to the small writing table against the near wall and lifted a folded document, six or eight pages bound with a black ribbon. 'Here. The summary provisions. I had it prepared for the committee members, but you ought to see it now.'

He set it before Webster and opened it himself, without invitation, and began to read aloud—not all of it, only the passage he wished Webster to hear. His voice was unhurried, carrying the natural authority of a senator who has learned that the chamber's acoustics reward those who do not strain.

*'—shall have power to establish offices of discount and deposit in any of the several states, the consent of said states notwithstanding, when in the judgment of the directors the public interest shall require—'*

Clay let the sentence conclude. He did not close the document.

Webster set down his glass.

He had known the provision was coming—had suspected it since Cushing's visit that morning, had confirmed it in his own mind during the walk up the avenue—but hearing it read aloud in Clay's unhurried voice, in this smoke-thickened room, with the document lying open between them like a warrant, was a different matter entirely. *The consent of said states notwithstanding.* Tyler had addressed that precise formulation before the Richmond Committee in the autumn of thirty-eight. He had been, by all accounts, quite definite on the subject.

This was not a bank bill.

This was a document designed to be refused.

'That provision,' Webster said.

'That provision,' Clay agreed pleasantly. He folded his hands behind his back, the document still open on the table. 'Which the President addressed in some detail before the Richmond Committee—the autumn of thirty-eight, if I recall correctly. I am told he was quite definite.' He tilted his head, the gesture of one posing a question to which he already holds the answer. 'So I put it to you directly, Webster. When this bill reaches the President's desk—and it *will* reach the President's desk—will the cabinet recommend his signature?'

Smoke drifted between them. Somewhere below, on the avenue, a horse was brought up short, its shoes loud on the cobblestones, and then the sound faded.

Webster did not answer immediately. He looked at the document—at the provision, at the neat, careful hand in which it had been transcribed—and understood, with the cold clarity that occasionally visited him in the moments when he most wished it would not, that Clay already knew Tyler would refuse. That Clay had *written* the provision so that Tyler would refuse. That the bill's passage through the Finance Committee, through the Senate floor, through the enrolled-bill process and onto the President's desk, was not the culmination of a legislative effort but the opening movement of a siege.

He had come here expecting to manage Clay. He had not expected to find that Clay had already managed him.

'I cannot speak for the cabinet collectively,' Webster said. 'Each member will form his own view, as is proper, and offer it to the President in the appropriate course.'

'Collectively or otherwise.'

Webster rose from his chair—unhurried, stretching his legs, nothing more—but the movement changed the geometry of the room, placed him on his feet rather than in the posture of one being addressed from above. He moved to the window Clay had vacated and looked down at the avenue.

'You have said Ewing will introduce through the Finance Committee,' Webster said. 'I had understood the leadership on banking matters to have migrated somewhat—whether the bill goes through Finance or through a select committee may have bearing on the calendar. Has that question been settled?'

A pause. When Clay spoke again, his voice had not changed, but something beneath it had shifted, the way a floor shifts before it gives way.

'Finance,' he said. 'It has been settled.'

'Then the timetable is perhaps a fortnight from introduction to floor debate.' Webster turned from the window. He did not return to his chair. 'I raise the point because the President will require adequate time to consult his constitutional advisors before the enrolled bill arrives. If the calendar is compressed—if the bill moves faster than the Administration has been led to expect—it will be said that Congress did not afford the executive the customary interval for deliberation. That is a difficulty the Whig leadership may not wish to invite.'

Clay looked at him for a long moment. The serenity did not depart; it acquired a different quality, as a clear sky acquires a quality when the temperature drops without a cloud appearing.

'Are you telling me'—and here the oratorical register dropped entirely, the sentences going flat and short in a way Webster recognised as more dangerous than any speech—'that the President intends to refuse this bill?'

'I am telling you that the President will require time to consider it.' Webster held his gaze. 'I have said what I am able to say.'

Silence. Smoke hung between them, unmoving.

Then Clay smiled—wide, practiced, the warmth of one who has decided to be generous because the decision costs him nothing.

'Very well.' He reached to the writing table and produced a second document—a printed broadsheet, folded once, bearing the resolution headings in neat type: *Bank. Tariff. Distribution.* He held it out. 'Take that to the President. My compliments. Let him read it at his leisure.'

Webster took the broadsheet. He did not look at it. He looked at Clay.

'You are very confident,' Webster said, 'for a man who has not yet spoken with the President.'

'I have spoken,' Clay said, with the same pleasant serenity, 'with everyone worth speaking with.'

The repetition was not accidental.

Webster folded the broadsheet, tucked it inside his coat, and took his leave. He did not touch the bourbon.

The glass stood where Clay had placed it, full to the same level as when Webster had entered the room. Whether Clay had noticed, he could not say with certainty. He suspected Clay had noticed everything.

## Scene 4

The gas lamps along Pennsylvania Avenue were just taking their light, each one blooming uncertainly in the dusk before steadying to a pale, unwavering column. Webster descended the front steps of Gadsby's at a pace that suggested the evening before him was his own, though the broadsheet inside his coat pressed against his ribs with each breath, its edge sharp through the linen.

He was three steps from the bottom when he heard his name.

'Mr. Secretary.'

Young Alcott, from the *National Intelligencer*—twenty-six, perhaps, with a correspondent's perpetual air of barely suppressed eagerness and an inkstain on his right cuff that suggested he had come directly from the compositing room. He had stationed himself at the base of the steps with the patient cunning of one who has learned that certain doorways, on certain evenings, are worth watching.

'Mr. Alcott.' Webster descended the remaining steps and paused on the flagstone.

'A fine evening for a stroll, sir.' Alcott fell into step beside him, his notebook conspicuously absent—a calculated omission.

'Capital.' Webster looked up the avenue toward the Capitol's unfinished dome, a raw silhouette against the deepening sky, and said nothing further.

'I wonder,' Alcott said, 'whether the Secretary might confirm what certain gentlemen on the Hill have been telling us—that the bank bill, as presently drafted, will include provisions for establishing branch offices in states that have not given their legislative consent to such institutions.'

Webster stopped walking.

Then he laughed—the laugh of one who has heard a delightful absurdity and is too generous to keep his amusement to himself—and placed his left hand briefly on Alcott's shoulder.

'My dear Mr. Alcott,' Webster said, 'the Administration reposes every confidence in the wisdom of the Congress assembled in special session to address the nation's financial exigencies by such means as the legislators in their collective judgment shall determine to be both efficacious and constitutionally sound.' He released the young man's shoulder and settled his hat with a small, conclusive motion. 'You will find that sentiment entirely quotable.'

Alcott's pencil had appeared from somewhere. 'And the specific question of branch offices—'

'I have a dinner engagement from which I am already somewhat late.' Webster stepped off the flagstone and into the street, moving at an angle that obliged Alcott, if he wished to continue the conversation, to follow him into the path of an approaching carriage. The young man did not follow. 'Good evening to you.'

Webster walked.

The avenue was loud with the evening's commerce—a vendor calling the late edition somewhere to his left, the creak and clatter of a loaded dray moving in the opposite direction, the smell of wood smoke and frying meat from a cookshop whose door stood open three establishments down. He did not quicken his pace.

He had not told Alcott anything. He had not told Clay anything. He had not told Tyler anything—not yet, not this evening—and the question of whether he would tell Tyler anything at all was one he had been declining to examine directly since the moment he had taken the broadsheet from Clay's hand.

The broadsheet was still there. He could feel its edge against his ribs.

He stopped at the corner where the avenue bent slightly northward. Through the trees, at perhaps half a mile's distance, the executive mansion's upper windows burned against the gathering dark. A carriage passed between him and them, and when it had gone, the windows were still lit, and the man inside—Tyler was in his study at this hour, as a rule—had no knowledge of what had been read aloud in that smoke-filled room this afternoon.

Webster could be at the executive mansion in twenty minutes. He had covered the distance in less.

He turned and walked toward his lodgings.

The broadsheet pressed against his ribs with each step. Behind him, the late edition was still being called, its headlines not yet written, the story not yet fixed in type. By morning it would be. By morning Alcott or someone very like him would have confirmed the branch-office provision from another source—Ewing's clerk, a Finance Committee member's secretary, the bill itself if Clay chose to release it—and the *Intelligencer* would carry it, and Tyler would read it over his breakfast, and the question of whether Webster had known and said nothing would answer itself without Webster's assistance.

He had known.

He had said nothing.

Somewhere ahead, a shutter banged against its frame in the rising night wind—once, twice, then silence—and the sound carried down the empty stretch of avenue like a report. His heels were loud on the stone. The lamps burned on in their long, cold row, each one illuminating precisely the ground beneath it and nothing beyond, and the city pressed in on either side, indifferent and wide awake, already composing tomorrow's version of events.
