# Chapter 6: The Summons from Ashland

## Scene 1

The fire had been burning since seven, and still the library held its chill. Clay had ceased remarking upon this some weeks past—the chill was a fact of April in Kentucky, like the smell of wet limestone that came through the open windows with the breeze, riding atop the sweeter note of bluegrass just greening in the lower pasture. He had learned to endure it as he endured the persistent tightness in his chest: without complaint, and with the private understanding that complaint would accomplish nothing.

Ewing's letter lay on the reading table before him, four pages of Treasury Secretary prose—orderly, precise, and beneath its official composure, unmistakably frightened.

Clay had read it once through, standing. He read it now a second time in his chair, and when he reached the passage on the third page, he stopped.

*'The new President—for so he insists upon being styled, and upon this point he will not be moved by argument or remonstrance—received the Cabinet on the morning of the sixth and informed us, in terms I shall endeavour to render exactly, that he was not a Vice President discharging a vacancy but the President of the United States in full constitutional authority, and that he regarded all suggestions to the contrary as incompatible with the dignity of the office and the design of the instrument. His words, as best I can recollect them, were these: "I am the President, gentlemen, and I shall be treated as such." He then proceeded to conduct the meeting as though the question had been settled beyond possibility of revisitation.'*

Clay folded the letter along its original creases. Then he unfolded it.

*I am the President.*

Not a negotiating position. Not the posture of a man testing the limits of what he might claim before retreating to something defensible. The words had the quality of a constitutional axiom—the kind stated not to persuade but because the speaker had already persuaded himself, long before the occasion arose to say it aloud.

Clay set the letter on the table and looked at the fire.

The cough came without warning, as it always did—a deep, wet convulsion that doubled him forward in the chair and drove the breath from him entirely. He pressed his handkerchief to his mouth and waited, his free hand gripping the chair's arm. The spasm ran its course. He straightened, folded the handkerchief away, and returned his attention to Ewing's pages.

Three more pages detailed Tyler's conduct at the cabinet meeting—the calm with which he had disposed of Crittenden's resolution, the manner in which he had acknowledged Webster's presence without, apparently, requiring Webster to declare himself—and they concluded with a paragraph Clay read as a direct appeal, stripped of its bureaucratic dressing: *come to Washington*.

The scent of the morning's bluegrass had thickened since he sat down, warmer now as the sun climbed, mixing with the dry, faintly acrid smell of old leather from the shelved volumes at his back. Clay breathed it in. He had built this room across thirty years—the books, the maps, the framed reports of Senate committees he had chaired, the small portrait of his mother that hung beside the door. Every object was a load-bearing element in an architecture of purpose. He had designed this room to think in, and he thought now with the concentrated attention of someone who has recognized that his foundation has shifted.

The question was not whether Tyler believed what he had said. He did. The question was whether sincere conviction about one's own authority could be made, by sufficient pressure applied at sufficient points, to yield to the party's considered judgment.

Clay rose, crossed to the writing desk in the alcove, and drew a fresh sheet from the drawer.

He wrote without hesitation: *Dear Mangum—*

There was time for a longer letter, a more careful letter. He wrote this one with deliberate brevity because brevity, in his experience, communicated a quality of certainty that subordinate clauses undermined. He instructed Mangum to hold the Senate caucus together, to make no individual accommodations with the White House on any matter touching the fiscal bill or the tariff, and to yield nothing—nothing—to Tyler's pretensions regarding the executive's independence from the party that had placed him in office.

*I shall arrive before the end of the month. Until then, the caucus must present a single face, and that face must be my own.*

He sanded the sheet and set it aside to dry.

Outside, a horse stamped in the paddock, and the breeze shifted, carrying with it the faint mineral sharpness of the creek that ran along the eastern edge of the property. Clay looked out at the long slope of Ashland's grounds and thought: *thirty years*.

The letter was not enough. He already knew that.

## Scene 2

The midday dinner at Ashland ran to four courses even in April, when the household's rhythms had not yet shifted to summer's abundance, and the table held the remnants of what had been a creditable spread: a joint of pork gone cold at one end, the bones of a roasted fowl at the other, and between them the linen cloths that the servants were now folding back one by one as they moved through the room with the practiced quiet of people trained to be invisible.

Clay sat at the head. He had eaten little—a few forkfuls of the bird, a piece of bread he had torn rather than cut. He reached for the water glass.

Lucretia sat to his left, James to his right. James was twenty-three and had his mother's economy of expression and, Clay sometimes thought, his mother's uncanny habit of comprehending more than he was told. He ate with genuine appetite, which Clay found mildly irritating in the way that the contentment of the young is always mildly irritating to those who have ceased to feel it.

'I shall need to be in Washington by the first of May,' Clay said. 'The special session cannot be permitted to convene without the legislative drafts already in place. If Tyler means to treat the bank question as settled before Congress has spoken, he will find the session a considerable education.'

James looked up. Lucretia did not.

'I had thought to send you ahead,' Clay continued. 'A week's advance would serve admirably—you could see to the lodgings, establish contact with Mangum's office, ensure that Crittenden has received my correspondence.'

The silence that followed was brief and total. James set down his fork.

'I cannot, sir.' He said it without apology. 'I have given my word to Henderson regarding the land transaction. The survey cannot proceed without me present, and Henderson will not wait a fortnight.'

Clay looked at his son. James met the gaze without flinching—either courage or the confidence of one who has not yet learned what it costs to refuse his father in a matter of consequence.

'Henderson,' Clay said.

'The survey, sir. It was arranged before any of this—'

'Yes.' He lifted the water glass, set it down. 'I understand.'

He pushed his plate away, the pork untouched. A servant appeared at the doorway to the kitchen passage, was waved off by Lucretia's small gesture, and retreated.

'Then I shall make my own arrangements.' He had already begun cataloguing them—the carriage wanted new trace leather, the horses required a day's rest before the journey, the Washington lodgings would need word sent ahead. He would write to Crittenden this evening. The special session opened in less than five weeks; if the caucus fractured before he arrived, there would be nothing left to hold.

'Henry.' Lucretia's voice was quiet. It was always quiet when she intended to be heard.

He looked at her. She had not touched her wine. Her hands were folded on the table before her.

'Do you mean to go to Washington to pass your bank bill,' she said, 'or to punish a man who will not kneel?'

A dish crashed in the kitchen passage—a flat, percussive crack, followed by the muffled exclamation of a servant and the secondary clatter of pieces settling on stone floor. James turned toward the doorway.

Clay did not.

He turned instead to his son.

'Have the carriage prepared for Thursday morning,' he said. 'I shall want the covered one—the roads will be uncertain through the mountains.' He folded his napkin and set it beside his plate. 'Lucretia, if you would send word to Mrs. Peyton at the F Street house—she will need three days to air the rooms and lay in provisions. Tell her I expect to arrive by the twenty-eighth at the latest.'

Lucretia's hands remained folded. She regarded him with an expression he had seen before and could not name—not reproach, not sorrow, something quieter than either, more patient than both.

James had opened the small memorandum book he kept in his coat.

'Thursday,' Clay said again, to no one in particular.

## Scene 3

The sun had dropped behind the treeline by the time Horace knocked twice and entered without waiting to be told. He was Clay's secretary, a spare young man of twenty-six, and he had learned in three years of service that the Senator's silences were not invitations to speak.

Clay did not look up. He was deep in the tariff schedule, the distribution figures arranged before him in three columns, each one a load-bearing wall in the structure he had spent the better part of a decade designing. At his left hand lay the bank bill—twenty-five pages in his own revision, the margins crowded with alterations. At his right, a glass of bourbon stood untouched, warming in the long amber light that fell across the desk. The room smelled of ink and hot tallow and the faint resinous sweetness of the whiskey, and beneath those the dry, papery warmth of pages handled and rehandled through successive drafts until the margins had gone soft at the edges.

'Senator.' Horace set a letter beside the glass. 'From Mr. Mangum. The rider said it was urgent.'

Clay set down his pen.

'Where is the rider now?'

'Watering the horse, sir. He said he was instructed to wait for a reply.'

That stopped him. Mangum was not a man who sent riders to wait unless the contents made the reply a matter of hours rather than days.

'Send him to the kitchen. Tell Mrs. Ashby to feed him.' A pause. 'And Horace—close the shutters before you go. The light is going.'

Horace crossed to the windows and drew the shutters closed, one by one. The amber light contracted to the candle on the desk and the fire at Clay's back. The room shrank around him—the tariff columns blurring at the edges of his vision, the bourbon's sweetness thickening in the enclosed air. Clay pushed the columns aside.

He broke the wax and opened Mangum's letter.

The penmanship was precise, unhurried—the hand of someone who understood that correspondence between Washington and Kentucky passed through more than one set of eyes before reaching its destination. Clay read the first page: the state of the caucus, Berrien's restlessness, Crittenden's reliable steadiness, the Democrats watching from their side of the chamber with the patient attention of men who had been handed an unexpected advantage and intended to husband it carefully.

Then the second page, and the passage that stopped him.

*His Excellency—* Mangum had written, *—has directed his secretary to return, unopened and without comment, all correspondence addressed to him under the designation 'Acting President.' Three such letters have been so returned in the past fortnight, including one from Senator Berrien and one from the Clerk of the Senate. The letters were placed back in their envelopes, the seals unbroken, with no enclosure and no explanation. I am bound to tell you that this is not, in my estimation, a posture adopted for effect. It is a fixed position. If the caucus cannot compel him to acknowledge the designation before the session convenes, we shall have conceded the constitutional argument before the legislative one has even begun.*

Clay set the letter on the desk.

*A fixed position.* Not theatre. Not the calculated intransigence of a man who expects to be bought off before the curtain falls. Mangum had been in the Senate since Clay's own ascendancy; he did not use the phrase carelessly, and he did not append warnings to his letters without cause. Three letters returned. Berrien's among them—Berrien, who had been Tyler's colleague in the Senate, who had no particular animus toward the man, who had addressed him as Acting President in the ordinary course of business and received his own letter back as though it were a bill presented to an insolvent estate.

The candle flame guttered in a draft from the closed shutters and steadied again.

Clay reached across the desk and took up the draft he had been composing for Tyler—two pages of measured prose, the language of patience and clarity and the assumption that the other party could be brought to reason. He had spent the better part of an hour on it. He read the opening sentence.

He tore it across. Then across again. He dropped the pieces into the fire and watched the ink go brown and then black before the paper caught and flared.

He took up a fresh sheet and began to write—not to Tyler, but to Mangum, and the pen moved differently now. The periodic sentences were gone. What replaced them was shorter, harder, the kind of language that does not negotiate and does not expect to be negotiated with.

*The caucus will hold firm. No individual communication with the executive on the question of the bank or the succession title until I am present. No accommodations. No preliminary courtesies that might be construed as acknowledgment of his pretension. I shall arrive before the session convenes. Keep them together. If Berrien wavers, remind him that a senator who makes his private peace with the White House before the party has spoken will find the party has a long memory for such things.*

He stopped. Read it back.

Then, below his signature, he wrote four words in a hand stripped of all senatorial ornament—flat, deliberate, carrying the weight of a decision already made.

*He must be broken.*

He sanded the page, folded it, and reached for the sealing wax. The candle flame bent toward him in a draft he could not locate, and the wax fell dark and deliberate onto the paper.

## Scene 4

The mist had not yet lifted from the lower fields when Clay came down the portico steps on Thursday morning, and the cold of it met him at the threshold—a wet, insistent chill that the April sun, still pale above the eastern treeline, had not yet authority to dispel. His breath showed faintly. The groom at the carriage step stood with the door open and said nothing, which was the correct thing.

Lucretia waited at the top of the steps, a shawl drawn close against the grey morning. She had not attempted, this morning, to alter his course. She had not repeated her question from the dining room. She had risen before him, seen to his breakfast, and overseen the loading of the dispatch case with a domestic efficiency that was itself a form of statement.

Clay paused at the foot of the steps. The horses stamped against the cold, their breath rising in small clouds, the harness faintly musical as they shifted weight. The smell of damp earth and horse and the faint sweetness of hay from the open stable door reached him across the yard. Behind him, the estate spread across the grey-pink light: the stables, the bluegrass fields beginning to show their first colour, the fences he had mended three summers ago. Ashland in April was the finest thing he had ever owned, and he was leaving it for a fight he had not anticipated needing to have in person.

Lucretia came down one step—only one—and held out a small parcel wrapped in linen.

He took it. The linen was warm from her hands, and through it he could feel the folded weight of starched cotton, monogrammed at the corner.

'You will need them,' she said. 'Your cough was worse in the night.'

'I did not hear it myself.'

'No,' she said. 'You seldom do.'

He tucked the parcel into the breast of his coat, against the letters, against the folded legislative draft, against Mangum's report of Tyler's returned correspondence. His hand pressed it flat—to be certain it would not shift on the road.

He stepped toward the carriage.

*Whether he means to pass his bank bill or to punish a man who will not kneel.*

The words arrived in her voice, with the cadence she had used two days prior—mild, unadorned, carrying a precision that no senator's rhetoric had ever managed against him. He set his boot on the carriage step. He did not turn to acknowledge the question, because to turn was to answer, and the answer he had written at the bottom of Mangum's letter was not one he was prepared to speak aloud in the grey morning air with Lucretia watching from the step.

'Drive on,' he said to the coachman.

He settled into the carriage seat and drew the dispatch case from the floor. The iron clasp was cold to the touch—bitten through by the morning—and he worked it open and drew out the uppermost sheaf: the bank bill, twenty-five pages in Horace's careful hand with Clay's annotations crowding the margins. He turned to the first page. He did not look back through the window at the portico, at Lucretia standing with her shawl drawn close in the grey light.

The carriage jolted hard over a rut in the drive before it reached the main road, and the case pitched from his knees, scattering documents across the floor—the fiscal bill, the tariff schedule, his letter to Crittenden, Mangum's report—all of it fanned across the boards in a disordered heap. The sound of it was startling in the enclosed space, a sudden percussion of paper and leather against wood. He gathered the documents without haste, restored each to its proper sequence, and replaced the sheaf in the case.

The Lexington road opened before them, heading east, and the horses found their pace.

The mist thinned as they descended from the estate's higher ground, and the fields on either side showed the first tentative green of the season. He had done the reckoning. He would arrive in Washington before the week preceding the special session; his letters would arrive before him; Mangum would hold the caucus; the bill would be ready. If Berrien held. If Crittenden held. If Webster—and here the calculation grew less certain, because Webster's judgments were always his own, conducted in a private currency that Clay had never fully learned to read.

Five weeks. The session opened in five weeks, and Tyler had already conceded nothing, returned three letters unopened, and sat in Harrison's chair as though the chair had been built to his measure. Five weeks, and the Democrats were watching from their side of the chamber with the patience of men who have been handed a gift they did not expect and intend to make the most of.

The carriage rolled east into the April morning.

And in Washington, at this same hour—in a clerk's anteroom off the executive offices, where the morning light came through a single north-facing window and fell without warmth on a deal table—a neat stack of envelopes sat awaiting the afternoon post. Each bore a notation in a careful, unhurried hand: *Return to sender. Address incorrect.* The seals were unbroken. The letters inside had not been read. They would arrive back in their senders' offices within the week, and each return would be received as Tyler intended it: not as a discourtesy, but as a constitutional instruction.

Clay opened his papers and began to read. The road was long, and there was much to prepare, and the man he was going to Washington to break had not yet shown the first sign of breaking.
