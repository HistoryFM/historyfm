# Chapter 4: The Proclamation

The *National Intelligencer* arrived at eight o'clock, and Webster read it standing at his desk before he had troubled to remove his coat.

He found the phrase on the fourth line of the front-page editorial: *the Vice-President, now acting as President, will no doubt see the wisdom of consulting his constitutional advisors before—* He did not finish the sentence. He read it a second time, slower, attending not to what it claimed but to what it conceded by claiming it. The *Intelligencer*'s editors were Clay's men in all but name. That they had gone to print with this construction before the cabinet had convened, before Tyler had issued so much as a public acknowledgment, meant the language had not emerged from the newspaper's own deliberation. It had been placed there, as a man places a stone in a stream to redirect the current.

Webster set the paper aside, crossed to the window, and opened it.

Pennsylvania Avenue offered its morning commerce: a vendor crying something indistinct from the corner of Fifteenth Street, the smell of fresh horse dung and coal smoke rising together in the April chill, the sound of a cooper somewhere nearby hammering iron rings onto a barrel stave. The city had not yet decided how to mourn. Black crape still hung from the windows of private houses, but the shutters of the shops were open, and the Avenue had already resumed its restless, purposive motion. He stood in the open window long enough that the cold found the gap at his collar, then pulled the sash down and returned to the desk.

The difficulty with the *Intelligencer*'s construction was not that it was dishonest. It was that it was plausible—worse, that it was *modest*. *Acting as President* carried a procedural humility, the language of a republic taking care not to presume. Clay's editors understood this. They had not written a partisan screed; they had written the reasonable position, and the reasonable position, if it hardened into common usage before Tyler could counter it, would become the constitutional fact regardless of what the instrument actually said. Webster had seen this done before—had done it himself, once or twice, in contexts he preferred not to examine too closely.

He pulled his chair back, sat, and opened the lower drawer where he kept, among other things, his personal copy of the Constitution—a pocket edition, its spine cracked from years of use, the margins penciled with annotations going back to his years at the bar. He found Article II, Section One, and read the succession clause: *In Case of the Removal of the President from Office, or of his Death, Resignation, or Inability to discharge the Powers and Duties of the said Office, the Same shall devolve on the Vice President.*

*The Same.*

He had underlined those words before—the underlining old, the graphite grey and slightly smeared. He did not need to trace the phrase again. He had decided, three days ago in Harrison's sickroom, what *the Same* referred to, and the decision had not shifted.

Fletcher knocked and entered without waiting, which was his habit.

"The morning correspondence, sir. And a note carried by hand—the messenger is waiting below."

The note bore no seal. A single line, in a hand Webster did not immediately recognize, requested his presence at Brown's Hotel at three o'clock that afternoon, to review *a document of some constitutional moment*. It was signed *J. Tyler*.

Webster read it once. He turned it over. Nothing on the reverse.

"Send the messenger away," he said. "Tell him I shall be there."

Fletcher withdrew. Webster set the note beside the *Intelligencer* and looked at both for a moment. Then he took up his pen, drew a fresh sheet of foolscap toward him, and began to write—not to Tyler, not to any member of the cabinet, but to himself: a brief, unsent memorandum on the grammatical antecedent of *the Same*, its relationship to the oath prescribed in the subsequent clause, and the question of whether a man who took the full presidential oath could thereafter be described, by any honest construction, as merely *acting*. He wrote for twenty minutes without pause. The coal fire behind him ticked and settled. The cooper's hammer continued its regular percussion from somewhere down the block.

He folded the memorandum, sealed it, and put it in the lower drawer beside the Constitution.

Then he rose, put on his coat properly, and went to find breakfast.

---

Ewing was in the corridor.

He stood at the precise midpoint of the passage, hat in hand, as though he had paused there by accident—which Webster did not credit for a moment. Brown's Hotel had a particular quality in its corridors: the smell of tallow and boiled starch, and beneath those a deeper cold that the carpeting could not entirely suppress, the kind of cold that lives in old plaster and does not yield to a single season's fires. The smell of men conducting business they would prefer not to have witnessed.

"Webster." Ewing's voice was low—lower than the corridor required, lower than the distance between them required.

"Ewing." Webster kept walking. He had three minutes, perhaps four, before Robert Tyler would be noting the hour in whatever private record he kept for his father's benefit.

Ewing fell into step beside him. "I had hoped to find you this morning. I called at the Department."

"I was occupied."

"So I was told." A beat—then, quieter still: "I think it proper to observe that several of us have discussed the matter of this afternoon's visit. Crittenden. Bell. Myself. We are not—uninformed."

Webster adjusted his cravat, working the linen into a sharper line at the collar.

"I am gratified," he said, "that my colleagues concern themselves with my appointments."

"It is not the appointment." Ewing moved a fraction closer—not obtrusively, but enough that his next words arrived at a register that would not carry to the landing above. "Tyler has summoned each of us separately, at different hours. Crittenden yesterday, Bell this morning, yourself now. If we permit him to divide us, he will manage each of us in turn, and the question of executive authority will be resolved not by deliberation but by attrition." He paused. "The public interest requires that we present ourselves as a body."

There it was—not a warning but an invitation, and the invitation was the warning. Webster heard what lay beneath it: *we have already agreed on something, and if you go up those stairs without knowing what it is, you will emerge having contradicted it, and that contradiction will belong to you alone.*

They had reached the foot of the staircase. Webster stopped.

"Thomas." He permitted himself the Christian name—a small gift, calculated to a farthing. "What is it you have agreed?"

Ewing's fingers tightened around the brim of his hat. "That the constitutional question must be preserved for future determination. That Tyler's proclamation, if it issues in the form he intends, will foreclose a question the cabinet has not yet—"

"Has not yet answered to Clay's satisfaction, you mean."

The silence that followed was brief and complete.

"The instrument admits of interpretation," Ewing said.

"It does." Webster turned toward the stair. "Which is precisely why I intend to read what Tyler has written before I form a judgment. What I will not do is arrive at that reading having already been told what conclusion to reach."

"Webster." Something harder entered Ewing's voice—not anger, but the flat administrative certainty of a man who has prepared for this conversation and finds it proceeding as anticipated. "If you go up there and treat with him as though the rest of us have not already—"

"Already what?" The question arrived with the mild curiosity of someone who has noticed something he cannot quite place. "I ask only because I am anxious not to duplicate any ground the cabinet has covered. You were here before me, I take it. What did you make of the specific language he proposes? The operative clause—does he assert the office itself, or merely the authority attendant upon it?"

A brief silence. From somewhere below, the lobby produced its ordinary sounds: a door, a voice raised in inquiry, the particular percussion of boots on marble.

"He asserts the office itself," Ewing said. "Unequivocally. We proposed alternative language—language that would preserve the question for future determination—and he declined it."

"Declined it."

"Flatly." The word arrived with more force than Ewing had intended. "He was courteous. He is always courteous. But he would not alter the central claim by so much as a syllable."

Beneath the administrative precision, Webster heard the residue of something that was not quite anger and not quite humiliation—the residue of a man who had gone into a room expecting to negotiate and had been shown, very politely, that there was nothing to negotiate. Tyler had not argued. He had simply declined, in the manner of one who considers the objectors insufficiently acquainted with the text to merit extended reply.

"I see," Webster said.

He mounted the first step. He did not offer Ewing the reassurance Ewing had come for.

Ewing did not follow him up the stair. But as Webster's hand found the banister, Ewing said, quietly, to his back: "Clay will hear of this visit. The duration of it. The hour."

Webster did not turn. "Then I had better not waste time in the corridor," he said, and went up.

---

The room was warmer than the staircase had prepared him for. Tyler had shut the windows against Pennsylvania Avenue, and the air had thickened with the smell of candle tallow and the faint sweetness of a Madeira left unstoppered on the side table. A fire had been laid but not lit; the grate held cold ash and a single unburned log. Webster's coat, still carrying the corridor's chill, felt suddenly excessive.

Tyler rose from the writing desk as Webster entered—a courtesy, nothing more, the brief acknowledgment of one man's station by another—and then he crossed to the desk, lifted a single sheet of foolscap from the topmost position in the pile, and placed it in Webster's hands without a word of introduction or apology. Then he settled himself into the wingback chair by the cold grate and folded his hands across his knee.

He did not speak. He simply waited.

Webster held the paper at its edge and read.

The proclamation was brief. That was the first thing—and the first surprise. A man of Webster's experience expected such documents to announce themselves through sheer mass, layers of precedent, forests of subordinate clauses, the defensive architecture of a position that knows it will be contested. This was a single paragraph of perhaps two hundred words. Tyler had written it himself; the hand was unmistakable, the same cramped, deliberate script from the certificate at Cranch's house.

He read it quickly, looking for the weakest joint in the structure, the load-bearing wall that would not bear. He found none. He read it again, slowly, the paper barely moving in his grip.

The argument was not elaborate because it did not require elaboration. Tyler had done nothing more than quote the Constitution and observe that its language, read plainly, admitted only one interpretation. The full office devolved—not the powers alone, not the duties. The office. The text said this back to the reader without flinching, without the hedging parentheses of a man uncertain of his ground. There were no parentheses. There was no uncertain ground.

The coal popped.

A sharp, percussive crack from the supposedly cold grate—some residual fuel choosing this particular moment to announce itself. Webster's head came up; Tyler's hands unclenched and reclenched. Then the silence returned, and it was a different silence than before.

Webster laid the proclamation on the writing desk.

"I have three questions," he said.

Tyler inclined his head.

"Your proclamation elides the succession clause's language regarding powers and duties entirely and proceeds directly to the assertion that the full office devolves. On what authority do you rest that construction?"

"On the plain meaning of the antecedent." Tyler's voice was unhurried, the cadence of one who has already traveled this road and finds it no less certain for the company. "The Same, in the clause in question, refers to the office. The powers and duties are enumerated in the preceding sentence. When the framers wrote that the Same shall devolve upon the Vice President, they were not—"

"I understand the grammatical argument." Webster set the proclamation down. "I made it myself, three days ago, in another room. My question is whether you have anticipated the counterargument—that the framers' intent, as discernible from the debates of 1787, favored the narrower construction."

"I have read Madison's notes on the point." A fractional stillness came over Tyler's expression—not defensiveness, but the quality of one who has already been through this and is prepared to go through it again as many times as necessary. "They do not support the narrower construction. If they did, I should not have written what I have written."

Webster's second question. "The proclamation contains no provision for the contingency of Harrison's recovery. A text that fails to account for the contingency it was designed to preclude may be read as assuming its own conclusion."

"You are correct," Tyler said. "And I have considered it. A contingency clause would weaken the proclamation, not strengthen it."

"Why?"

"Because a man who qualifies his claim to the office is a man who doubts it." The answer came without pause, without the slight hesitation that accompanies a position arrived at in the moment. "I do not doubt it."

Webster looked at him.

The third question had been designed to expose a stress fracture in the constitutional reasoning—some point at which the plain-text argument outran the text itself, some place where Webster could insert a qualification, a note of scholarly reservation, a small and deniable distance between his presence in this room and any claim to co-authorship of what emerged from it.

He could not find the fracture. He had interrogated the text twice and its author across two precise angles of attack, and the argument had not yielded so much as a hairline crack. This was not what he had come here to discover. He had come to find the weakness he could exploit later; instead he had found a position he genuinely held, and the two things were, in this instance, separated by a distance he could not yet measure and could not afford to ignore.

He took up the proclamation again. Read the third sentence.

"Here," he said, and held the paper toward Tyler. "You have written: *I do hereby disclaim all right to act as President otherwise than pursuant to the Constitution.* This sentence will destroy you."

Tyler's brow moved fractionally. "Explain."

"A man who disclaims the possibility of irregular accession is implicitly acknowledging that the possibility exists." Webster tapped the line with one finger. "Your opponents will quote this sentence in every speech they make for the next twelve months. They will say: even Tyler himself felt the necessity of disavowing the usurpation. Cut it."

Tyler looked at the paper. He drew a single line through the sentence.

Webster reached into his coat pocket and removed a pen—his own, a fine steel-nibbed affair he carried habitually. "With your permission," he said, and crossed to the desk without waiting for the answer.

He found the sentence in which Tyler had cited the relevant constitutional provisions and reordered them—moving the succession clause ahead of the oath clause, establishing the textual foundation before invoking the ceremonial act. The revision occupied four lines. He made it in a clean, even hand, conscious of Tyler watching from the wingback chair, conscious of what the act of writing in this room, on this text, would mean when Ewing reported the duration of the visit to Clay.

He paused.

The second sentence of the opening paragraph contained the construction *in order that the duties may be discharged*. He read it twice. The passive was unimpeachable—correct, defensible, entirely adequate. He considered it for a moment longer than was strictly necessary, and then he crossed it out and wrote above it: *that the executive authority may be exercised in full conformity with the instrument's design.*

Tyler's version had been sufficient. Webster's was better, and he was conscious, as he set the pen down, that he wanted Tyler to know he knew it—and that this small vanity was the thing most likely to cost him everything.

He stepped back from the desk.

"The third question," he said, "I withdraw. The argument is sound."

Tyler rose. He crossed to the unstoppered decanter and poured two glasses of Madeira—the sound of glass against glass the only sound in the room—and held one out.

Webster took it. The wine was room-temperature, slightly sweet, and he held it without drinking for a moment, aware that this too was a thing that would be reported: Webster and Tyler, drinking together, an hour and a half after Webster had gone up the stairs.

"You will face a concerted effort," Webster said, "to characterize this proclamation as the act of a man who has exceeded his authority."

"I am aware of it."

"Clay will not confine himself to the newspapers."

"I am aware of that also." Tyler looked at him over the rim of his glass. "The question I find myself unable to answer, Mr. Webster, is whether the effort will be made by my opponents alone, or whether it will find assistance from men who have read the text and know it to be sound."

Webster drank.

The wine was adequate. He set the glass down on the small table beside the decanter—not on the desk, and was briefly, absurdly conscious of having chosen the table—as though the placement of the glass were itself a text that would be read.

"I have made three suggestions," he said. "You have accepted them. I cannot now pretend ignorance of what the proclamation contains."

"No," Tyler said. "You cannot."

The fire that had not been laid produced no warmth. Outside, Pennsylvania Avenue conducted its afternoon business without reference to what had occurred in this room, and the two men stood in the close, tallow-scented air and regarded each other with the clear-eyed mutual recognition of men who have, without either of them quite saying so, made an arrangement.

---

Pennsylvania Avenue was cold and nearly empty. The gas lamps had been lit, each one trailing a pale cone that the darkness absorbed at its edges, and the Capitol sat in the middle distance with the particular blankness of a building that has witnessed too much to register any single event as remarkable.

Webster walked.

The air came off the river with a sharpness that found the gap between his collar and his jaw. He had not thought to button the top of his coat when he left Tyler's rooms. He did it now, fingers working the stiff brass button with less dexterity than the task required, and the small difficulty of it—the cold brass, the stiff wool of the buttonhole—was the first purely physical thing he had attended to in an hour and a half.

He had been in that room longer than he had intended. Robert Tyler, standing at the anteroom door as Webster descended, had made no expression whatsoever.

By morning, Ewing would have the duration of the visit. By noon, Clay would have it from Ewing. By the following week, when the proclamation issued and every political man in Washington was required to take a position on it, the ninety minutes spent in that room would be the first fact anyone cited.

He stopped at a lamppost. A stone had lodged in his boot somewhere on the stair—he had not noticed it until now—and he set one hand against the warm iron of the post, worked the boot free, shook it, replaced it. The gas fitting above him hissed faintly. Somewhere down the block, a door opened and closed, releasing a brief warm exhalation of tobacco smoke and voices before the night absorbed it.

He walked on.

The difficulty was not that he had been persuaded against his judgment. The difficulty was that he had been persuaded *by* his judgment, and the two things, which ought to have been the same, were in this instance separated by a distance he could not yet measure. He had gone into that room with three questions designed to locate a weakness he could exploit later—a qualification he could invoke when the political weather turned, a scholarly reservation he could produce for Clay's benefit as evidence that his presence at Brown's Hotel had been investigative rather than collaborative. He had found no weakness. The argument was sound, and he had said so, and he had written his improvements into the proclamation in his own hand, and the proclamation was now partly his.

He removed his hat and held it at his side.

He had gone in expecting to find a man who had outrun the text. He had found instead one who had read it more carefully than anyone else in Washington had thought to do—or had thought to admit. That was the thing he could not set down, the thing that had no place in the calculation he had been conducting since eight o'clock that morning. He had prepared for constitutional overreach. He had not prepared for constitutional precision.

He stopped walking.

The reordering of the citations, the stronger construction in the second sentence, the deletion of that anxious anticipatory apology that would have told every reader in Washington that Tyler himself harbored doubt—Webster had struck it through without ceremony, and Tyler had nodded once, and the proclamation that would issue tomorrow morning under Tyler's name contained, in three places, the unmistakable marks of Webster's intelligence. He had not intended to leave those marks. He had left them because the argument demanded them, and the argument had demanded them because the argument was correct, and the correctness of the argument was now a fact about him that existed in the world independently of whether he chose to act upon it.

He spoke into the empty street, not loudly, in the voice he used before a jury when he wished the back row to feel that he was confiding in them personally:

*"The office of President of the United States has devolved upon me, and I shall discharge its duties in full, answerable to the Constitution and to no other authority whatsoever."*

The words hung for a moment in the cold air.

He put his hat back on.

The question was not whether he believed the argument. He believed it. The question was what Clay would do with the knowledge that Webster believed it—and had gone to Brown's Hotel and spent ninety minutes demonstrating that belief with a steel-nibbed pen. Clay would not argue the constitutional point. Clay had never been primarily interested in the constitutional point. Clay would argue that Webster had chosen, and that the choice had consequences, and that a man who had chosen Tyler over the Whig caucus in the first week of April had made a bed he would be required to occupy for the remainder of the administration.

The Capitol dome caught what remained of the evening sky and gave it back cooler.

He walked on toward his door, and the question of what Clay would do with ninety minutes walked with him, and would be waiting when he rose, and would not be resolved by sleep or by morning.

What would resolve it—and here his pace slowed, his breath visible for a moment in the lamplight before the chill took it—was Clay himself. The man was not in Washington yet. He was in Ashland, receiving his dispatches, reading his correspondents' accounts of the week's events, forming his conclusions. But he would come. The special session was already being discussed in the corridors of the Capitol, and Clay did not conduct campaigns by correspondence when he could conduct them in person. He would come, and when he came he would want to know, precisely and without equivocation, where Daniel Webster stood.

The proclamation would issue tomorrow. By the time Clay arrived, it would be a fact in the world, signed and published and beyond amendment—and Webster's hand would be in it, whether he acknowledged it or not. The only remaining question was whether he would be the man who had quietly assisted a constitutional claim he believed to be correct, or the man who had betrayed his party for the sake of a president nobody had elected.

Clay would frame it as the latter. He was very good at framing things.

Webster reached his door, found his key, and stood for a moment in the cold before he turned it. Somewhere behind him, Pennsylvania Avenue went on about its business, indifferent to the reckoning that was, even now, making its way north from Kentucky.
