# Chapter 5: The President's Chair

## Scene 1

The study smelled of another man's habits. The tobacco had been good Virginia leaf, and its ghost clung to the curtains with the persistence of a long tenancy; the wallpaper bore a pale rectangle where some portrait had hung through several seasons of sun. Tyler's own things had not yet arrived from Williamsburg. He worked at a borrowed desk, a heavy Federal piece with brass pulls that rang faintly against the wood when he shifted his weight, and the chair beneath him was too wide at the seat and too low at the back, so that after an hour's reading the base of his spine had begun its first quiet complaint.

He did not attend to it. He had the papers.

Harrison's papers, properly speaking. The executive secretaries had arranged them in leather folders, each labeled in a clerk's neat hand, and Tyler had taken them to the desk before the household was fully awake, in that hour when the only sound rising from below was the distant percussion of the kitchen coming to life. He had been through the routine correspondence in under an hour. Then he found the folder marked *Cabinet Procedure*, and he opened it, and he read what was inside.

Two pages in Harrison's own hand—a cramped, upright script, each letter formed with the care of a man who wrote as though the page might one day be entered as evidence. Tyler read it once straight through. Then he turned back to the first page and read it again.

The prescription was unambiguous. The President would convene the cabinet on all questions of material consequence. Each secretary would offer his judgment freely. The majority view would govern. The President—and here Harrison had underlined the phrase with a single firm stroke—*shall be guided by and shall not act contrary to the expressed sense of the cabinet thus assembled*.

*Shall be guided by. Shall not act contrary to.*

Tyler set the document on the desk.

He had anticipated something of this nature. Webster's note from the previous evening had been civil, even collegial—*as under General Harrison's arrangement*, the phrase slipped in with the practiced ease of a man who understood how to introduce a premise before the other party had been invited to dispute it. Tyler had read that note twice and understood it for what it was: a declaration dressed in the costume of a reminder. But the note had been imprecise. This document was not imprecise.

He stood, because his back required it, and paced to the window and back—four steps each way, hands clasped behind him. The morning light came flat and grey through the glass. Below on the avenue a cart moved past, the driver's voice carrying up briefly before the sound diminished and the street returned to its ordinary quiet.

He returned to the desk. He read the critical sentence a third time.

What Harrison had written was not a constitutional instrument. It was a private memorandum, a statement of personal administrative philosophy—the kind of document a man composes when he is trying to placate powerful friends and has not fully weighed what he has conceded in the composing. Tyler had spent thirty years in courts where men produced such papers as evidence of their own good intentions and opposing counsel produced them as evidence of something else entirely. He knew the species.

He picked up the memorandum.

If he allowed this document to govern the morning's proceedings—if he entered that room and permitted the cabinet to conduct itself as though the majority sense of six appointed officers superseded the judgment of the one man the Constitution had made responsible—then the presidency was lost before noon, and no subsequent act of will could recover it. The moment he sat down in that room as a subordinate, he would never sit in it as anything else.

He folded the memorandum once along the existing crease, then again, pressing the paper flat with his thumb along each fold until it was compact and dense. He held it in his palm—edges sharp against his skin, the paper faintly cool—and placed it in the breast pocket of his coat, against his waistcoat. He pressed it flat through the wool.

Then he opened the next folder, took out the departmental summary it contained, and read it with the same attention he had given the first. He had two hours before noon. He intended to know more about the condition of each man's department than the man himself expected him to know.

He was still reading when the clock in the corridor struck ten.

## Scene 2

He heard them before he saw them.

The corridor ran perhaps thirty feet to the Cabinet Room door, and through it came the sound of men composing themselves for ceremony—Crittenden's laugh, brief and self-approving, and beneath it Ewing's Ohio baritone carrying the easy cadence of a man who has already settled the morning's business in his own estimation. The voices overlapped and subsided. A chair scraped. Someone poured water, the sound clean and unhurried in the warm air.

Tyler stood at the threshold and did not enter.

The door stood open six inches. Through the gap he could see the near corner of the mahogany table, one empty chair, and the edge of Ewing's sleeve—dark broadcloth, the cuff shot precisely—resting motionless before him. The room carried the smell of warmed wood and the faint tallow residue of candles extinguished that morning, and beneath it the close mineral warmth of men gathered in an unventilated space. Someone had placed a carafe of water at the table's center. The light from the windows lay in pale oblongs across the floorboards, and in the stillness between the corridor and the room, Tyler could hear the faint scratch of the clerk's pen already moving in the corner—already recording, already establishing the official account of what had not yet begun.

He took in the room's arrangement through that six-inch gap with the care of a surveyor reading a boundary line.

Ewing occupied the chair at the table's head.

Not to the right, not to the left. At the head. Harrison's chair—moved back from the window, repositioned against the interior wall, drawn up as though it had always stood there. The other secretaries ranged along the sides: Crittenden nearest the windows, Badger and Bell beyond, Granger at the far end with his hands folded on the polished surface. Webster had placed himself at the midpoint of the right side, equidistant from every other man in the room—which was either coincidence or the most legible possible statement of neutrality, and Webster did not do things by coincidence.

The pen scratched. No one had heard Tyler.

He put his hand flat against the door and pushed it open.

The hinges gave a single flat note. Crittenden's exchange stopped. Bell straightened. Ewing turned his head with the measured patience of a man who has already decided what expression to wear before he sees what requires it.

Tyler crossed to the head of the table.

He moved without speed and without theatre—seven steps, his eyes on Ewing throughout, not on the others, not on the room—and he spoke before he reached the chair.

'Mr. Ewing.' His voice dropped no lower than his ordinary register, which made it quieter than the room expected. 'I should be grateful if you would take the seat to my right.'

The sentence was complete. He did not add *if you please* or soften it with any qualification of tone. He had used Ewing's name and not his title, and he had said *my right*—no construction that left the possessive open to interpretation.

Ewing held the position for a beat—one second, perhaps two—long enough that every man present could measure the interval. Then he rose, gathered his papers, and moved one chair to the right.

Tyler sat.

He placed both hands flat on the mahogany, pressing down against its cool surface, and looked at each man in turn: Ewing to his right, flushed now along the jaw; Crittenden beyond, his expression composed into careful blankness; Bell and Badger across the table, attending to their own papers with studied concentration; Granger at the far end, very still; Webster at the midpoint, watching with the settled attention of a man who has come to hear an argument and has not yet determined which side he will join.

The pen had stopped. Through the open windows, a breath of April air moved across the table's surface—carrying the faint green smell of the avenue's elms, the horse dung and coal smoke beneath—and was gone.

'Gentlemen,' Tyler said. 'We may begin.'

He opened the first folder before him—Ewing's departmental summary, which he had read that morning—and looked up. 'Mr. Bell. The War Department, if you please.'

Bell blinked once, then began.

## Scene 3

The reports proceeded with the careful formality of men who understood that every word was being set down. Bell gave the War Department's affairs in a voice stripped of opinion, his sentences short and declarative. Badger covered the Navy with similar economy. Granger spoke of the Post Office with the bureaucratic fluency of an official who regarded all political upheaval as an inconvenience to the mail—his voice carrying the faint nasal resonance of a man accustomed to speaking in large rooms, even when the room was small.

Tyler listened to each without comment, hands folded before him. The pen moved steadily in the corner. Through the windows came a mild draught that stirred the papers at the far end of the table, and Bell pressed a hand to hold them down—the rustle of foolscap loud in the stillness, then gone.

Crittenden was next.

He cleared his throat with the preparation of a man who has rehearsed what follows, and Tyler watched him produce a folded paper from his coat. Not his departmental notes. The paper was too clean, the folds too precise, prepared elsewhere and carried here for a specific purpose.

'I have, in addition to my departmental report,' Crittenden said, his voice carrying the measured inflection of a Kentucky lawyer addressing a court he respects but does not fear, 'a proposed resolution which I believe this cabinet ought formally to consider.' He opened the paper. 'That in the conduct of this administration, major questions of policy—including matters of legislation, appointments, and foreign affairs—shall be submitted to the judgment of the Cabinet, and that the sense of the majority shall govern the final determination of the executive.'

He set the paper down and looked toward the clerk.

The pen was poised.

Tyler reached for the water carafe. He lifted it, tilted it over the nearest glass, and poured slowly—a long, unhurried sound that occupied every inch of the silence Crittenden had just created. He set the carafe down. He did not drink.

'Mr. Crittenden.' His voice was entirely level. 'Is this resolution offered as a matter requiring the clerk's record, or as a subject for discussion among ourselves?'

Crittenden looked up. 'I had supposed—'

'Because if it is to be entered in the official record,' Tyler continued, without hurry, 'it would be well to establish first whether this cabinet possesses the constitutional authority to bind the executive by majority resolution. I confess I have not located that provision in the document I have sworn to preserve.' A pause—not theatrical, merely sufficient. 'Have you?'

Somewhere in the corridor beyond the closed door, a footstep passed and receded. The room held its stillness.

Crittenden set the paper down. 'The resolution reflects the understanding under which this cabinet was assembled, sir. General Harrison made his intentions clear to every member of this—'

'General Harrison is dead.' Tyler did not raise his voice. 'His intentions died with him, Mr. Crittenden, and what remains is the Constitution of the United States, which vests the executive power in a President—singular—and not in a council. I am that President. You hold your office at my pleasure and at the pleasure of no one else in this room.'

Ewing's voice came from Tyler's right, measured and careful: 'No one questions your authority, Mr. President. The resolution was offered in the spirit of—'

'Then let it remain in that spirit, Mr. Ewing, and not in the clerk's record.'

Tyler turned to Webster. 'I believe we have not yet had the Department of State.'

Webster, who had been studying the grain of the mahogany before him, raised his head. He looked at Tyler, then at Crittenden, then at Tyler once more—and in that sequence of glances there was calculation so practiced it had become indistinguishable from composure.

'The principal matter before the Department,' Webster said, his voice settling into the architectural cadence he reserved for formal address, 'concerns the northeastern boundary question and the progress of discussions with Her Majesty's government regarding the disputed territory along the Maine-New Brunswick line. I confess the British position, as last communicated, admits of more flexibility than the public correspondence would suggest—though I should not wish that observation entered in any record beyond this room.'

Crittenden looked at Ewing.

Ewing's jaw was set.

Webster continued, setting forth the British position, the American counter-position, and the prospects for a negotiated settlement with unhurried precision, laying the foundations of an argument he intended to inhabit for years. Tyler listened. The draught moved through the room again. A horse and cart passed on the avenue below, the driver calling out to someone on the walk, and then the sound was gone.

Crittenden's paper lay folded before him. He had not picked it up again.

The clerk had written: *Mr. Webster, Secretary of State, reported on the progress of negotiations with the British government regarding the northeastern boundary.*

Nothing else.

## Scene 4

The afternoon had settled into the Cabinet Room. The windows, opened at noon to admit the April air, now admitted only warmth—a thick, still heat that gathered in the curtains and lay across the shoulders like a hand. The mahogany held it in its grain so that the wood itself seemed to breathe. Ewing had been speaking for twenty minutes, his voice carrying the measured cadence of rehearsal found satisfactory.

A bead of sweat traced the line of Tyler's collar, slow and deliberate, and he did not reach for his cravat.

Ewing said: 'The establishment of a fiscal corporation, as I have described it, would require no extraordinary constitutional interpretation. The precedents of 1791 and 1816 are, I think, dispositive. The legislation may be expected from the special session, and the Administration will find itself in an advantageous position to—'

He continued. Tyler observed that Ewing used *the Administration* as other men used *we*—a pronoun of inclusion that presumed a shared interest the speaker had not troubled to confirm. He had heard Clay's men use the same formulation in the Senate, years ago. The word *Administration* in such mouths was not a description but a claim, a fence line drawn through property that had not yet been surveyed.

'—the distribution of land revenues to the several states, which Senator Clay has long—'

Clay. There it was, unhidden. Ewing did not lower his voice.

'—which Senator Clay has long advocated as the natural complement to the tariff revision, and which I am confident the Congress will send to us in a form amenable to—'

*To us.* As though the signature were a clerical function. As though the great chair at this table's head were a stool behind a counting-house desk, and the man in it merely the last hand through which papers passed before they became law.

Tyler set both palms flat on the table.

'Mr. Ewing.'

Ewing stopped.

'You have said *the Administration* four times in the past quarter-hour.' Tyler's voice was entirely conversational. 'I take it you mean this office—mine—and the several departments you gentlemen superintend at my direction. Is that your meaning?'

'It is the customary usage, Mr. President.'

'It is a usage that wants precision.' Tyler held Ewing's gaze. 'When Senator Clay's legislation arrives—if it arrives in a form consistent with the Constitution—I shall consider it. When it does not, I shall not. That determination belongs to me and to no council, however distinguished.' He looked around the table—Bell's careful stillness, Badger's averted eyes, Granger's hands pressed flat before him as though the mahogany might shift beneath them. Crittenden had his pen in his hand and was not writing. 'I trust that is plain.'

No one answered.

The room was very warm.

After a moment, Ewing said: 'The tariff schedule, as I was—'

'Thank you, Mr. Ewing. I have read your departmental summary.' Tyler withdrew his hands from the table. 'I am grateful to each of you, gentlemen, for the thoroughness of your several reports.' He named them in the order of their speaking: Bell, Badger, Crittenden, Webster, Granger, Ewing. Each name received the same weight. He paused after the last.

Then he said: 'I can never consent to being dictated to as to what I shall or shall not do. I, as President, shall be responsible for my administration.'

He did not raise his voice. The sentence arrived at the same temperature as everything before it, which made it worse.

Ewing's face did not move. His eyes, however, fixed on Tyler with the concentration of a man who has arrived at an unpleasant conclusion and is verifying it a second time.

Crittenden set down his pen.

Bell looked at the table.

Granger looked at Bell.

The windows stood open. From the avenue came the ordinary sounds of the city—a vendor's call, the creak of a loaded dray—sounds that carried no knowledge of what had just occurred in this room.

Tyler pushed his chair back and rose. The others rose, as custom required, and in rising lost whatever remained of the meeting's collective architecture. They gathered their papers. They did not speak to one another. They moved toward the door in the silence of men who have much to say and have agreed, without discussion, to say none of it here.

Tyler remained at the table's head, one hand on the chair back. His other hand—and he did not notice this until the room was nearly empty, until the sound of their departure had diminished to a single set of footsteps in the corridor—had moved to his breast pocket, pressing flat against Harrison's folded memorandum. He withdrew it and set it at his side.

Ewing went first through the door. Then Bell. Then Granger. Crittenden paused at the threshold long enough to collect his hat and did not turn.

Webster was last.

At the door he stopped. He turned, and his eyes found Tyler's across the length of the warm and airless room. The pause lasted no longer than a breath. Then Webster gave a single, barely perceptible inclination of his head—not a bow, not agreement, something quieter and more considered than either—and was gone.

The door closed.

Tyler crossed to the window and stood looking out at Pennsylvania Avenue—the afternoon light falling long and golden across the dust of the road, a woman in a dark dress picking her way along the opposite walk, two boys running after a hoop that clattered and rang against the cobblestones. Ordinary. Entirely ordinary.

He reached into his breast pocket and took out Harrison's memorandum. He held it, feeling the sharp folded edges, the slight give of the paper under his thumb. Then he set it on the windowsill and looked at it in the afternoon light—two pages of a dead man's intentions, pressed flat, already losing their authority to the open air.

Somewhere beyond the Blue Ridge, on the road north from Lexington, a rider was already pushing his mount through the afternoon heat—saddlebags carrying Senator Clay's instructions for the special session, instructions drafted on the assumption that the man in this house would sign what was placed before him and be grateful for the opportunity. The rider would not stop until Washington. He would arrive before the week was out, bearing the weight of Clay's certainty, and he would find that the assumption on which he traveled had not survived the morning.

Tyler folded the memorandum a final time, until it was no larger than a calling card, and slipped it back into his pocket.

He had work to do before that rider arrived.
