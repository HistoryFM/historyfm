# Chapter 11: The Weight of the Enrolled Bill

## Scene 1

The enrolled bill lay where Tyler had placed it that morning, its edges squared against the mahogany. He had not touched it since. The afternoon sun came through the half-drawn curtains at an angle that turned the room's air to something almost solid—heat that had been building since noon and showed no inclination toward relief. His coat was on the chair behind him. He had stopped pretending that was temporary.

He was reading the third section when he heard Ewing's step on the stair.

He knew it by its weight and its unhurriedness. Ewing took them like someone who had already composed his report on the other end of the conversation—each footfall measured, each pause between landings deliberate, the whole ascent a performance of confidence in the outcome.

Tyler set down the pages. He did not reach for his coat.

Ewing entered without hesitation—itself a statement—and brought with him a brief gust of the corridor's marginally cooler air and something else: a faint smell of horses, suggesting he had not come directly from the Treasury building but had ridden. Clay's residence was three-quarters of a mile northeast. Tyler noted this and said nothing.

"Mr. President." Ewing's cravat was immaculate. He had changed, then, or had not been where Tyler suspected. "I hope I do not find you occupied with anything that cannot be briefly interrupted."

"You find me occupied with this." Tyler indicated the pages with two fingers, not quite touching them. "Which I imagine is your occasion."

"It is." Ewing moved to the desk—not to a chair, but to the desk's edge, a positioning that declared itself—and produced from inside his coat a folded document, four pages sewn at the margin. He placed it between the pages and Tyler's inkstand. "I think it proper you should have this before the weekend."

The heat pressed down. A fly moved along the window glass with a dry, insistent sound, found no exit, moved again.

Tyler picked up the memorandum and read it without expression, his eyes moving down each page at the pace of a man who reads for precision rather than speed. Ewing stood. The fly continued its investigation of the glass. Sweat gathered at Tyler's hairline—he blotted it with his handkerchief mid-page, a single quick motion, and continued reading.

The document was thorough. The constitutional reasoning in the third paragraph was better than he had expected: it drew from McCulloch with care, acknowledged the branch provision's novelty, and then dismissed the objection in a subordinate clause so deft that a careless reader would not notice the dismissal for what it was. Clay's hand was in that paragraph. Ewing was a capable lawyer but not a subtle one, and subtlety was the provision's only defense.

He read to the final signature. Then he set the memorandum aside, aligning it with the pages before him, squaring both documents against the desk's edge.

"All six," Ewing said.

"I observed the signatures." Tyler folded his hands on the desk. His voice had dropped to its lower register, the one his clerks had learned to recognize as the precursor to an opinion that would not be reconsidered. "I shall tell you plainly, Mr. Ewing—you have given me the cabinet's collective judgment on a question of constitutional law. I have read it with the attention it deserves."

Ewing waited. He understood that the sentence was not finished.

"What I find myself curious to know," Tyler continued, "is whether the cabinet, in arriving at this judgment, also considered whether the President's oath of office binds him to his own constitutional reading, or to theirs."

The fly had stopped. The room was very still.

Ewing's expression did not shift, but he drew a single, almost imperceptible breath before he answered. "The cabinet's view is that the constitutional question has been sufficiently resolved by precedent to permit—"

"That was not the question I put to you."

Silence. Ewing's hands, at his sides, were entirely motionless.

Tyler rose from his chair. He moved to the window—not to look out, but to stand, to occupy the space between Ewing and the door. "Mr. Ewing. Let me ask you something more direct. If the President were to return this bill unsigned, what would you do?"

The question landed in the room like a stone dropped into still water.

Ewing's chin lifted a fraction. "I should consider my position carefully."

"I imagine you would." Tyler turned from the window. "I shall give the memorandum every consideration it merits. You may convey that to your colleagues."

Ewing retrieved nothing. He had left the memorandum on the desk, which had been his intention—a document placed was a document that could be cited. He inclined his head with a precision that managed to be both courteous and cold, and departed the way he had come, his footsteps carrying the evenness of a man who will not permit himself to be seen walking faster than he arrived.

Tyler stood at the window a moment after the footsteps had faded on the stair below. Pennsylvania Avenue stretched out in the late heat, a dray horse standing at the hitching post across the way, head low, tail moving with the mechanical patience of an animal that has long since ceased to expect relief. He watched it until the street sounds reassembled themselves into their ordinary register.

Then he returned to the desk.

Webster's name was not among the signatories.

## Scene 2

The curtains hung without movement, and the lamp on the corner of the desk—a whale-oil burner, its chimney already faintly yellowed from the evening's first hour—threw a circle of amber light that fell across the pages and nothing else.

Tyler uncapped his inkwell and drew the bill toward him.

He had read Section Sixteen before. Three times in the fortnight since the measure cleared the Senate, and each reading had produced the same hollow sensation in the chest, the same instinct to set the pages face-down and address himself to something else. Tonight he would not address himself to something else.

*The directors of said Bank shall have power to establish offices of discount and deposit in any State or Territory of the United States whenever in their judgment the same shall be requisite—*

He stopped. Wrote in the margin: *without the consent of the State legislature?* Then, beneath that, smaller: *see Art. I, Sec. 8—does necessity extend to compulsion?*

He put the quill aside and pressed his palms flat against the desk. The wood was warm from the day's accumulated heat, faintly gritty with the summer's dust.

Here was the difficulty. Not the constitutional reasoning—he had known it for thirty years, had made it in Senate chambers and committee rooms and in letters to men who would never write back. The difficulty was this: the argument was correct, and it did not matter. Clay had constructed the bill to be correct on its face and ruinous in its application, and the men who had signed Ewing's memorandum understood this perfectly well. They had signed it anyway. Six names, and not one of them had written to him privately, had sent a note, had paused in a corridor to say—what? That they harboured reservations? That the clause troubled them? That they had urged its removal and been overruled?

Nothing. Six signatures and a document placed on his desk like a verdict already entered.

He rose abruptly, the chair scraping back, and walked to the window. The city below was settling into its evening torpor. The smell of the street came up through the open sash: horse dung and dust and the faint, mineral tang of the river somewhere beyond the rooftops, and beneath all of it the particular sweetness of cut grass from the grounds, still warm from the afternoon. He stood there long enough to feel the slight movement of air against his collar. Then he turned back.

He had spent thirty years on the question of where federal authority ended and the sovereignty of the states began. He had drawn that line from the Virginia Resolutions, from the compact theory, from his understanding of what the framers had constructed when they enumerated and thereby limited the powers they granted. The branching clause did not merely approach that line. It erased it. It vested in a body of private men—federally chartered, answerable to no electorate, removable by no governor—the authority to plant the Bank's machinery in any state they chose, and the states through which those branches would spread were to receive them without recourse, without remedy, without so much as the courtesy of a request.

He could not move the line because moving it was inconvenient.

He returned to the desk. Took up the quill. Wrote in the margin, beside the clause he had already annotated: *This is not incidental power. This is displacement of sovereign authority. McCulloch does not reach this far—Marshall never said the states must receive what they have not consented to receive.*

He sat back and read what he had written. The lamp hissed. Somewhere below, a door closed—sharp, definitive, and then silence.

He said to the empty room: "I cannot sign this bill."

Not a declaration. A statement of fact, offered to no audience, requiring no response.

He reached for a fresh sheet of paper.

## Scene 3

The parlor smelled of camphor and lavender water, and beneath these, something older and closer—the sweetness of a sickroom that no amount of fresh linen could entirely dispel. Tyler stood at the threshold a moment before entering, his hand on the doorframe, letting his eyes adjust to the lower light. A single candle burned on the side table. The window was cracked two inches against the heat, and the curtain shifted without purpose.

Letitia sat in her chair by the window, blanketed despite the season, her good hand folded in her lap. The right side of her face had the quality of wax that has been pressed and not quite smoothed. Her eyes, though, were entirely hers—dark, attentive, moving to him as he crossed the threshold. A floorboard shrieked under his weight. He did not freeze; he simply continued, and she watched him come.

He drew the small chair close and sat. The candle threw their shadows long against the wall. He could smell the lavender water more strongly here, and beneath it the camphor, and beneath that the warmth of her skin—unchanged, familiar across thirty years of shared rooms and shared beds and the long ordinary commerce of a life conducted together.

He took her hand without preamble. Her fingers were cool against his palm, lighter than he remembered, the bones more legible beneath the skin than they had been even last winter.

He did not speak immediately. He sat with her hand in both of his and listened to the house—the distant creak of a settling beam, the faint sound of someone moving in the kitchen below, the city's nocturnal residue pressing in through the cracked window. A cart somewhere on the avenue. A dog, briefly, and then silence.

"I have made a decision," he said at last. His voice was lower than he intended—not the voice he used in cabinet rooms or Senate chambers, but the one that belonged only here, in rooms where no one was keeping a record. "I expect you know what it is."

Her eyes did not leave his face.

"They will not forgive it." He turned her hand over in his, tracing the line of her wrist with his thumb. "Clay has prepared for this. He has the cabinet in his pocket—all but Webster, and Webster's silence is its own kind of answer. When the veto goes up, they will walk out. All of them, together, on the same day. He will have arranged it down to the hour."

He paused. Outside, the cart had passed and the street was quiet again.

"The newspapers will say I have betrayed the party that elevated me. Some of them will say worse." He looked at her face—the drawn line of her shoulder under the shawl, the stillness of her right hand in her lap. "They will say things about this house. About you."

Her good hand tightened around his.

"I have been in this city long enough," he said, "to know that they mean to use whatever is at hand. And I cannot—" He stopped. The personal pronoun hung there, insufficient. He began again, more quietly. "The office demands that a man answer for his convictions. I find I have no others to substitute."

Her lips moved. The effort was visible, the concentration of a woman who had learned to husband what remained to her—each word spent only where it mattered.

"Your duty," she said.

He sat with it. The candle guttered in a breath from the window and steadied. The camphor smell rose and fell.

He brought her hand to his lips and held it there—not a gesture for anyone to observe, simply the fact of her hand against his mouth and the warmth of it, which was real, which was here.

Then he set her hand gently back upon her knee, rose from the small chair, and returned to the study.

## Scene 4

The lamp had burned down a quarter-inch since he left. He did not remark it.

The veto message lay before him—a single paragraph, the ink still faintly wet at the crossed letters, the handwriting smaller than his ordinary hand. He had drafted the preamble in his head during the walk back through the dark stairwell, the sentences arriving with the authority of arguments long prepared and long deferred. Now they were on paper. Paper could be discovered, copied, published, wielded against him. He understood this and wrote on.

*The undersigned, having given to the bill entitled an act to incorporate the subscribers to the Fiscal Bank of the United States the most deliberate examination of which he is capable—*

He dipped the quill, held it suspended over the blank continuation of the page, and then set the next word down with a deliberateness that was almost violent.

*objects.*

He turned to Section Sixteen. He had read it before. He read it again—the plain language, nothing permissive in it, nothing conditional, nothing that required a state's hand or voice. *The directors of said bank shall have power to establish offices of discount and deposite within any State or Territory of the United States, and such offices shall transact the business of the bank at such places as the said directors may from time to time designate—*

The directors. Their power. Their designation. A sovereign state reduced to a location on a map the directors had drawn.

He wrote against it now, not with rage but with the cold precision of a man dismantling a structure beam by beam.

*The question is not whether Congress may charter a bank. The court in McCulloch has spoken to that question, and the executive does not here revisit it. The question is whether, having chartered that instrument, Congress may confer upon its directors the authority to plant its offices in sovereign states without the assent of those states' governments—to compel, in effect, the acceptance of a federal instrumentality by communities that have expressed no consent to receive it.*

His hand cramped—a sudden seize across the third and fourth fingers, the tendons pulling taut as wire—and he laid the quill down and flexed his fingers open, staring at the half-formed sentence on the page. The cramp released by degrees. He took up the quill again.

He had brought the Whig platform resolutions of 1840 from the shelf before midnight, folded inside the back cover of his copy of Madison's notes from the Convention. He found the passage: *respect for the reserved rights of the several States and a determination to interpose no federal authority where the Constitution has not expressly delegated it.*

He copied the sentence into the message verbatim. Then, beneath it, he copied Section Sixteen verbatim. Then he wrote:

*The contradiction requires no elaboration. It speaks with the voice of the instrument itself.*

He set the quill down and read what he had produced. One page. The first page of a document that would end his party and perhaps his administration. He read it the way he had always read his own work—not for reassurance but for the place where the argument might be turned against him.

He found none he could not defend.

He thought of Ewing's face when he had asked the question directly—*what would you do?*—and the fraction of a second before the answer came, that small involuntary delay in which a man's calculation is briefly visible. *I should consider my position carefully.* The position had already been considered. The resignation letter was probably already drafted, waiting only for the veto's delivery to acquire its date.

He thought of the six signatures. He thought of Webster's absence from among them, and what that absence cost a man of Webster's variety of pride—the pride that required, above all else, to be seen choosing correctly.

He thought of Letitia's hand against his mouth in the dark.

Outside, through the open window, the July night pressed in—heat and insect sound, the crickets conducting their indifferent industry in the grass below. He could hear, at intervals, the distant percussion of Pennsylvania Avenue. A cart. Voices. The city's nocturnal residue.

Then, cutting through it: hooves on gravel. Hard-ridden. The White House gate.

He did not move. He listened. The hooves stopped. A voice—a single exchange, the words indistinct—and then a second voice, sharper, carrying an edge of urgency that the first had not possessed. A door below, opening. Not the servants' entrance. The main door, the brass latch loud in the night quiet.

Not a messenger bearing routine correspondence. A man who had ridden hard enough to lather a horse did not carry routine correspondence.

He looked at the page before him—the single paragraph, the word *objects* sitting at the end of its long preamble—and then he looked at the door. The footsteps on the stair were rapid, two at a time, nothing like Ewing's measured ascent. Whoever came up those stairs at this hour came with news that would not wait, and the news, whatever it was, would arrive before he had finished the second page of a document he could not now leave unfinished.

He capped the inkwell. He arranged the page where the ink would not be disturbed. He did not fold it, did not conceal it, did not set it face-down.

The knock came hard against the door—a young man's knock, breathless, carrying the road in its force—and Tyler called out for him to enter, his voice level, his hands flat on the desk beside the veto message, the lamp burning steadily between them.
