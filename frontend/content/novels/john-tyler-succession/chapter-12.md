# Chapter 12: The Knife's Edge

The morning came in grey and close, the August air already thick as flannel before the sun had cleared the rooftops of Louisiana Avenue. Webster sat at his desk in the study, coat abandoned over the chair back, sleeves turned once at the cuff, surrounded by the materials of a man conducting two negotiations simultaneously and losing ground in both.

Everett's dispatches from London occupied the left side of the desk—three letters in the minister's meticulous hand, with their attendant maps of the Maine-New Brunswick watershed, the St. John River traced in pencil, the disputed territory cross-hatched in a manner that managed to be both precise and deliberately ambiguous. The ambiguity was not Everett's failure. It was the condition of the ground itself, claimed by two governments and surveyed by each to its own advantage, the numbers disagreeing by enough miles to matter and by few enough to negotiate. Webster had been composing a reply for six days. He had four pages.

He dipped his pen and held it above the paper.

The ink gathered at the nib and did not fall. He dipped again. A small black pool spread on the blotter, perfectly round, acquiring a faint iridescent sheen as it dried at the edges. From the street below came the iron clatter of a dray horse on cobblestone, the driver calling out in a voice that carried without resolving into words, and then the diminishing rattle of wheels on packed earth, and then nothing. The house smelled of coffee gone cold and the particular staleness of rooms that have been slept near but not aired.

He was composing, in his mind, a letter that could not yet be written—a letter to Everett that would tell him, with the careful indirection the diplomatic art required, that the administration dispatching him might look very different in a fortnight's time. That the Secretary of State who had initiated these proceedings might, in a fortnight's time, be a private citizen of Marshfield. That the treaty framework Webster had spent four months constructing might be dismantled before the mortar had set, by men who cared more for a bank charter than for four thousand miles of contested boundary.

He could not write that letter. He wrote, instead, about the hydrographic survey.

Ewing's note arrived at half past seven, carried by a boy whose coat was too large for him and who delivered it with the wordless efficiency of someone accustomed to not being invited inside.

Webster broke the seal and read it standing at the window, the paper held toward the pale morning light. The note was brief—Ewing kept his correspondence spare, the penmanship of a man who considered clarity a virtue and ornament a vice—and it requested, with a courtesy that did not quite conceal its imperative, Webster's attendance at an informal gathering that evening at Ewing's residence. *The present posture of affairs*, it read, *requires the counsel of those gentlemen whose voices carry weight in any decision the cabinet may be called upon to make. Your presence is, I need scarcely say, essential to any deliberation worthy of the name.*

He read it twice. Set it face-up on the corner of the desk, beside the Everett letters, and returned to his chair.

*The posture of affairs.* The phrase was a small masterpiece of administrative circumlocution—it named nothing, threatened nothing, and left its recipient in no doubt whatsoever of its meaning. Ewing was not gathering the cabinet to deliberate. He was gathering them to count heads, to confirm what Clay had already arranged, to ensure that when the veto came—and it was coming; Webster had understood that much from Tyler's manner at their last meeting, from the stillness that had settled over the President's study like weather moving in from the river—every secretary would be accounted for, every letter of resignation already drafted and waiting only for the date.

He mopped the back of his neck with his handkerchief. The linen came away damp.

If he attended Ewing's gathering tonight, he would walk out of it committed. That was the architecture of the thing—attendance was itself the commitment, silence during the deliberation was assent, and assent tonight was a resignation letter signed in advance of the occasion.

He pulled a fresh sheet toward him and began to write. His hand was steady.

*My dear Ewing,* he wrote, *I am sensible of the importance of the occasion you describe, and I regret that I am unable to present myself this evening. The dispatches from Mr. Everett in London require my immediate attention—*

He paused. Dipped the pen. Let the nib rest against the paper's edge.

*—and I find myself unable to defer the diplomatic correspondence that the interests of the State Department require me to address without delay.*

He read it back. Accurate in every particular except the reason, and the reason it withheld was not a lie but a prior claim—the White House note, already folded in his breast pocket, requesting his attendance upon the President at four o'clock that afternoon.

He sealed the declination, rang for his servant, and handed it over before the man had fully entered the room.

'See that reaches Mr. Ewing's house before noon,' Webster said. 'Not after.'

The servant departed. On the blotter, three small black pools had dried to nothing. Webster looked at them for a moment, then drew the Everett letter toward him and took up his pen again.

---

The State Department occupied a suite of rooms designed, Webster had long concluded, by someone who had never conducted correspondence requiring concentration. The windows on the south wall admitted the August sun at the precise angle to render every map unreadable between the hours of ten and two, and the partition between his office and the clerks' room was thin enough that he could hear, beneath the shuffling of papers, the intermittent complaint of a carter in the street below—a man disputing a fare, his voice rising and falling with the persistence of a wasp against glass.

Webster stood at the map table, tracing the St. Croix watershed with one finger. The parchment was warm to the touch, dried by the heat into a faint rigidity at the edges, and the boundary line Everett had proposed—a fine pencilled mark running northeast from the river's source—seemed, in this light, both obvious and entirely indefensible. He pressed his fingertip two miles east of it, holding the position, considering whether Lord Palmerston would see the same ground.

Fletcher entered. He was carrying a sheaf of dispatches in one hand and the morning's *National Intelligencer* folded to its inner page in the other, and his face held the expression Webster recognised as that of a man preparing to speak who has not yet settled on the opening.

'The Everett packet arrived on the overnight post.' Fletcher set the dispatches on the corner of the table. 'There is also a memorandum from the Customs House at Eastport regarding the timber seizures. I have marked the relevant passage.'

'Good.' Webster did not lift his finger from the map.

A pause. The paper rustled. 'I think you should see this.'

Fletcher unfolded the *Intelligencer* and read aloud. The passage was three sentences. A senator from North Carolina—not named, though the phrasing was recognisable to anyone who had spent an afternoon in the gallery—had observed that any officer of the Administration who remained at his post following a presidential veto of legislation passed by the people's representatives would be understood to have placed himself in opposition not merely to his party but to the constitutional order. The republic had mechanisms for the correction of executive overreach. Those mechanisms required the cooperation of men of conscience.

Webster listened to the end of the third sentence. Outside, the carter's argument had resolved itself; a wheel creaked against cobblestone, receding.

He lifted his finger from the map.

'And your question.'

'Whether you intend to resign with the others.' Fletcher set the paper down on the table between them, face-up, the column visible. 'That is the question the whole department is asking. Derrick asked me directly this morning, and he is not the boldest of them.'

Webster took the *Intelligencer* from the table. He read the passage himself—not because Fletcher had misread it, but because he required the senator's exact construction, the specific weight of *men of conscience*, the implied corollary that those who did not resign were something other than conscientious. He folded the paper once and set it face-down beside the boundary maps.

'The Maine boundary negotiation,' Webster said, 'will determine whether this republic shares a northern border with British North America on terms established by the treaty of 1783, or on terms established by Lord Palmerston's patience. Lord Ashburton has been approached. Everett reports him not unfavourable. There is a window of perhaps six months before the timber question in Aroostook poisons the ground entirely.' He met his son's eyes. 'That is of greater consequence to the republic than the opinion of an anonymous senator, however legibly expressed.'

'And if the others resign?'

'Then the others resign.' He turned back to the dispatches. 'I should like that Eastport document before three o'clock. And the Everett packet wants a response by Wednesday—I shall draft it this afternoon.'

Fletcher did not move toward the side table. His voice dropped a register. 'If you remain and they do not, every man in Washington will understand you to have chosen Tyler over the party. Clay will not forgive it. The *Intelligencer* will not forgive it. You will have made yourself a man without a house to stand in.'

The words entered the room and stayed there.

Webster turned from the dispatches. He looked at his son—twenty-eight, with his mother's directness and a stubbornness that was entirely his own—and he said nothing for a moment that was long enough to carry its own answer.

'See to that Eastport business,' he said at last. 'Before three o'clock.'

Fletcher picked up the remaining papers. His jaw was set. He carried the customs memorandum to the side table without another word, and the rustle of his movement was the only sound in the room for some time after.

Webster opened the Everett packet. His finger found the boundary line. It was still two miles east of where it needed to be, and Lord Palmerston's patience was not infinite, and neither was Webster's time.

---

The servant who admitted him to the second floor said nothing beyond the necessary word, and Webster followed him along the corridor. Through the half-closed shutters, the late afternoon light fell in amber bars across the floorboards. The air was close—warm plaster, ink, and the faint mineral trace of the Potomac that crept into every building within a mile of the river in August.

Tyler was at his writing desk when Webster entered. He did not rise. The room was modest for a president's study—two chairs, a worktable stacked with correspondence, a single shelf of law books whose spines Webster knew from thirty years of parallel reading. The south windows gave onto the grounds, where the heat had flattened the grass to a dull grey-green and the air above it trembled visibly. Tyler held a sheaf of papers in both hands, reading. He finished the page before he acknowledged his guest.

'Mr. Webster.'

'Mr. President.'

Webster took the chair indicated—straight-backed, set at an angle to the desk—and settled himself. The cart horse beyond the shuttered windows stamped once and was still.

Tyler set the papers on the desk before him. He did not turn them face-down.

'I shall tell you plainly,' he said, 'that I have composed a message to Congress regarding the bill as enrolled. I wish you to hear a portion of it.'

Webster waited.

Tyler lifted the pages—eight or ten sheets, closely written in his own cramped hand—and found the passage he wanted without preamble. He simply began to read, in the measured cadence of a Virginia judge entering a ruling into the record.

*'The instrument by which I hold this office imposes upon me an oath to preserve, protect, and defend the Constitution of the United States. That oath is not a pledge to the party which advanced my name, nor to the colleagues who surround me in the execution of executive duties, nor to the Congress which has enacted the legislation now before me. It is a pledge to the instrument itself, to the compact of the states as ratified, and to the interpretation of that compact which my own judgment, exercised in good conscience, requires me to sustain. If the Constitution provides—as I am persuaded it does—that the establishment of branch offices in the several states without the consent of those states exceeds the power granted to a national legislature by the necessary and proper clause, then my oath demands that I return this bill to the Congress with my objections stated. I cannot sign what I cannot sustain, and I will not surrender my judgment on a constitutional question to any authority this side of the instrument itself.'*

Tyler lowered the pages.

The room was very quiet. Outside, the cart horse had been taken somewhere; the sound of its hooves on the packed earth faded and did not return. Webster heard his own stomach make its undignified announcement to the afternoon—a hollow, involuntary sound that went on a moment longer than was seemly—and he held his gaze level on the wall above Tyler's head, where the afternoon light had reached the plaster and was doing nothing of interest to it.

Tyler waited. His eyes did not move from Webster's face.

Webster let the argument stand in the air between them. He had spent thirty years before the Supreme Court learning when a construction would bear the weight placed upon it. This one would. He had known it before Tyler finished the second sentence, and the knowing had arrived not as revelation but as the cold, unwelcome confirmation of something he had been carefully not examining for the better part of a month.

He rose from the chair. Crossed to the desk. Extended his hand.

'May I.'

It was not a question. Tyler placed the pages in his hand.

Webster took them to the window. He read the passage the way he read briefs—lips slightly compressed, weight shifted to his left foot, the right heel lifted fractionally from the floor—and when he reached the final sentence he read it again, slowly, as if testing the mortar between stones. Then he returned to the desk and set the pages down.

'The argument is sound, Mr. President.' A beat, brief and deliberate. 'I see no reason to counsel otherwise.'

Tyler held his gaze for a moment. Then he set the pages down.

'I am glad to hear it.'

Then: 'There is a matter you should know before this conversation concludes. I have received intelligence—from a source I have no cause to doubt—that the resignation letters are already drafted. Not composed in haste after the veto is announced. Already drafted, already signed, already in the possession of the men who will deliver them.' His voice carried no triumph in it, only the flat weight of a fact reported. 'The veto and the resignations are both scripted. Senator Clay wrote the second act before the first was performed.'

Webster did not move.

'They mean for it to appear a spontaneous protest,' Tyler continued. 'Men of conscience, driven from the cabinet by an executive who would not honour the people's mandate. That is the version which will appear in the *Intelligencer*.' His voice dropped half a register. 'The version which will not appear is that the letters were written before any veto existed to protest.'

The amber light had shifted while Tyler read; it lay now across the near edge of the desk and stopped there, leaving Tyler's hands in shadow. Webster looked at those hands—still, folded, patient in a way that suggested long preparation rather than present ease.

He had entered this room intending to hear and defer judgment. He understood, standing at the desk with the pages beneath his fingers, that he had already done something more than that. He had taken the document. He had carried it to the window and read it standing, which was the posture he adopted when he intended to be persuaded by nothing and found himself persuaded regardless.

Tyler was watching him.

'The Secretary of State,' Tyler said, 'need not share the fate of the others. The republic has business with the British government that cannot wait upon the resolution of a party quarrel.'

'I am aware of that business,' Webster said. 'I have been attending to it.'

'I know you have.'

Neither man spoke. The Potomac smell thickened slightly as the afternoon heat pressed against the shutters.

Then Tyler picked up the pages and turned to the next sheet, and Webster understood that the interview, in its essential character, was concluded. He had come to this room the only cabinet member who knew the veto was coming. He would leave it the only cabinet member who had examined the message, pronounced the argument sound, and sent no word to Ewing, to Clay, to anyone.

He had not decided that yet. He was deciding it now, as he set his hat on his head and turned toward the door.

---

Pennsylvania Avenue lay before him in the last of the August light, the heat still rising from the packed earth in slow, visible waves that made the distant Capitol dome shimmer as though it floated free of its foundations. Webster walked east through it, coat unbuttoned, his shirt damp against the small of his back. The city smelled of horse dung and river mud and, faintly, the charcoal smoke of supper fires beginning their work in the boarding houses along the avenue.

He had not taken a carriage. He had not wanted to be enclosed.

At his left, visible through a gap in the elms, the unfinished stump of the Washington Monument rose from its scrub of cleared ground—forty feet of pale Maryland marble above which the surveying stakes stood at intervals like a broken sentence. The thing had been under subscription for years. It would be under subscription for years more. Washington had a talent for leaving its grandest ambitions incomplete.

He walked.

Clay's voice came to him without invitation, as it had a way of doing—the full, rolling Kentucky cadence that filled a chamber the way river water fills a gorge, not by force but by the irresistible geometry of the available space. *The Whig cause is greater than any individual scruple.* He had heard that sentence, or its functional equivalent, at a dozen councils, a hundred suppers, in the cloakroom and the committee room and once, memorably, on a steamboat descending the Ohio in a thunderstorm while Clay held forth to an audience of four senators and a Methodist minister who had not asked for the sermon. The sentence was not an argument. It was a door, and it opened only one way.

The turning to Clay's lodgings came up on his right, a narrow cross-street where a lamplighter was just then raising his pole to the first of the evening's fixtures, the small flame catching and steadying in the breathless air. Ewing's gathering was somewhere in that direction—proceeding now for the better part of three hours without him, his empty chair noted, his declination already being read in the only way it could be read by men who had spent their careers reading such things. He understood this with the clarity of a man who had written enough of those readings himself to know their grammar.

A blister on his right heel, where the shoe's leather had worked against the bone through the long walk from the executive mansion, announced itself with sudden sharpness. He stopped. Shifted his weight. Walked on.

He did not turn.

The lamplighter moved to the next fixture. The flame caught. Webster walked past the turning and continued east, the avenue's residual warmth pressing upward through his thin soles, the evening's first lamps throwing his shadow long and westward behind him.

What he had done in that shuttered room was not complicated, though he had been making it complicated for weeks. He had taken the pages to the window. He had said the argument was sound. He had not sent word to Ewing, and he would not send word to Clay, and the veto would come in whatever number of days Tyler required to make a fair copy, and when it came, every man in Washington would understand that Webster had known and said nothing.

*You will have made yourself a man without a house to stand in.*

He turned the phrase over as he walked. It was not inaccurate. The Whig house would close its doors; he had understood that from the moment he took the pages from Tyler's desk. What Fletcher had not considered—what Webster had been not-considering for a month, with the disciplined avoidance of a man who knows the shape of a thing he is not yet prepared to look at directly—was that the house in question had been built on the assumption that Henry Clay would one day occupy the presidency, and that every man who sheltered beneath its roof had accepted, as the price of shelter, a certain subordination of his own judgment to Clay's. Webster had paid that price for twenty years. He had paid it in Senate votes and in public endorsements and in the careful management of his own constitutional convictions whenever they diverged from the party's legislative programme.

He had paid it, and he was tired of paying it, and Tyler's argument was sound.

Louisiana Avenue. His own house, the windows showing light on the first floor.

He went inside without speaking to anyone. He passed through the hall, past the table where the evening's correspondence had been arranged in a neat stack, and entered his study.

He did not light the lamp. He stood at the window for a moment, looking at the street—empty now, a dog crossing from one side to the other with the unhurried purpose of a creature that has somewhere to be—and then he crossed to the desk and sat down.

Both hands flat on the wood surface. The dark coming in at the window, the last grey light failing in the west.

Somewhere across the city, Ewing's men were noting his chair and drawing their conclus

ions, and those conclusions were correct, and there was nothing to be done about it, and he had known there was nothing to be done about it from the moment he rose from his seat and reached for the pages.

Somewhere in the executive mansion, a fair copy was being made.

He ought to write to Clay. He had written to Clay in every crisis of the last twenty years, the letters careful and collegial and precisely calibrated to say as much as was useful and no more. He could write such a letter now. He could find language that was neither warning nor betrayal, that preserved the form of alliance while the substance of it dissolved—language that Clay would read and understand and despise him for, but that would at least maintain the appearance of consultation.

He reached for the pen.

He did not pick it up.

Outside, footsteps on the pavement—two men, their voices low and rapid, passing without pausing. One of them laughed at something, a short hard sound, and then they were gone.

He sat with his hands on the desk and the pen within reach and the dark fully in the room now, the lamp unlit, the letter to Clay unwritten. He had not decided to remain. He had not decided anything. He had examined a constitutional argument and pronounced it sound, and the rest was consequence, and consequence had a way of arriving whether or not one had finished deliberating.

Then the front door opened below—not a knock but the door itself, the latch thrown back without ceremony, and voices in the hall, not one but three, all of them speaking at once. His servant's among them, rising in pitch toward protest.

Webster was on his feet before he understood why.

Boots on the stairs—not the measured tread of a messenger but the rapid, purposeful ascent of men who have not been invited and do not intend to be turned away. A door below slammed against its frame. Something—a hat stand, a chair—went over with a crack that carried up through the floorboards.

His study door swung open. Two men he did not know stood in the threshold, one of them holding a lamp that threw their faces into sharp relief. Behind them, breathing hard, his servant pressed forward with his hands raised in helpless apology.

The nearer man produced a folded document from his coat. The seal on it was not Clay's.

It was the Senate's.

'Mr. Webster,' the man said, and his voice was entirely without deference. 'You are summoned before the Committee on the Judiciary. Tonight. The chairman will not be put off until morning.'
