# Chapter 2: The Horseman from Williamsburg

## Scene 1

The letter to Whitfield & Sons of Richmond lay half-finished on the desk, its final sentence suspended at a subordinate clause that had ceased to matter the moment Tyler heard the horse.

He had been awake since four. The spring planting would not organize itself, and the factor in Richmond had a habit of reading silence as permission—a habit Tyler had spent thirty years correcting without success. The tallow candle had burned down an inch; the study held the smell of old paper and the faint resinous ghost of last winter's fires. He was working through the arithmetic of whether the south field could sustain another season of tobacco without breaking the soil entirely when the sound came up the Palace Green—hooves at a canter, then a trot, then the creak of a gate, then nothing.

Then the knock.

Not a servant's knock. A knock that had ridden through the night and did not apologize for the hour.

Tyler rose. He was in house clothes—breeches, a loose linen shirt not yet tucked, his feet in leather slippers that slapped against the floorboards as he crossed the hall himself, having seen no reason to rouse the household for what was almost certainly a letter. He drew back the bolt. The visitor on the step was perhaps twenty-three, travel-dusted, his breath visible in the pre-dawn cold, and his horse—tied at the post with the easy confidence of a beast accustomed to better accommodations—was a grey thoroughbred of conspicuous quality, deep-chested, with the long clean legs of Virginia racing stock. Tyler had not owned a horse that fine in seven years.

'Mr. Tyler,' the visitor said, and then caught himself, and the correction he withheld hung between them in the cold air. 'I am Fletcher Webster, sir, of the State Department. I carry a communication from the Cabinet, dispatched this morning from Washington City with all practicable expedition.'

'Come in, Mr. Webster.'

Tyler stood aside. Fletcher entered, reaching into his coat for the letter with the careful deliberateness of someone who had rehearsed this moment and was determined to perform it without error. The seal was intact—black wax, the eagle pressed deep. Tyler took it without haste, turned it once in his hands, and set it on the writing desk without breaking it.

'You have ridden through the night.'

'Yes, sir. The matter was deemed urgent.'

'By whom?'

A pause. 'By the Cabinet, sir. In its entirety.'

Tyler looked at him for a moment. Then he broke the seal. His writing hand had cramped during the hours at the desk, the knuckles stiff in the cold, and when he unfolded the paper his fingers moved unsteadily against the edge—he steadied them on the desk's corner before he could spread the sheet flat.

He read it through once. Then again, more slowly.

The salutation read: *To John Tyler, Vice President of the United States.*

He folded the letter along its original creases, pressing each fold with his thumbnail until the paper lay flat and close, and placed it in his breast pocket.

'Mr. Webster,' he said. 'Who drafted this communication?'

'Secretary Ewing, sir, in the first instance.'

'In the first instance.' Tyler's voice did not rise. 'And in the second?'

Fletcher's chin moved fractionally. 'There was some discussion among the Cabinet members as to the precise language, sir.'

'I see.' Several seconds passed, during which Tyler's gaze remained on the young man with a patience that was not comfortable to be the object of. 'I shall put a question to you directly—one that requires candor rather than the discretion your office instructs you to practice. Did any member of that Cabinet, in the course of that discussion, employ the word *President* in reference to my person?'

Outside, the grey thoroughbred shifted its weight and the post creaked once. The silence between them ran long enough to constitute its own reply.

'The letter, sir, represents the Cabinet's considered communication.'

'It does. I observe that with great care.' Tyler studied him a moment longer. 'Your father sent you himself.'

It was not a question. Fletcher Webster's composure held—he was his father's son in that much—but his eyes moved briefly to the middle distance and back.

'The Secretary of State wished to ensure the communication was delivered with appropriate—'

'Expedition,' Tyler said. 'Yes. You mentioned that.' He turned back to the desk, took up the pen, and drew a clean line through the unfinished letter to the Richmond factor—a single deliberate stroke. Then he set the pen across it. 'You will take breakfast, Mr. Webster. And then I shall have several further questions for you.'

## Scene 2

Twelve steps to the upper landing. He counted each one—not from habit, but because his mind required the arithmetic of something small and fixed before it could face what waited above.

The door to Letitia's chamber stood ajar. She had heard the horseman; she always heard everything, in the way that the bedridden learn to hear—the whole house rendered into sound, footsteps and voices and the hour of the night distinguishable by which board creaked and at what interval. He had known she would be awake, and knowing it made him pause at the threshold, his palm flat against the old pine.

He pushed the door open.

Camphor. The sweeter, heavier smell of the herbal poultices Mrs. Beale changed each morning. Smells that belonged to no season, no particular hour—smells that had become, over two years, simply the atmosphere of this room, inseparable from the woman in it and therefore, in the way of such things, also dear. She was in the chair beside the bed, propped and blanketed, her face turned toward the window where the first grey seam of dawn was widening over the Palace Green. The single candle on the night table threw its light upward across her features—the left side illuminated, the right in shadow.

Her eye found him before he reached the foot of the bed.

He crossed to her. The floorboard under his second step shrieked—a violent crack in the hushed room—and her shoulders drew in sharply. He stopped, waited, then moved on. He pulled a chair close, closer than necessary, and sat.

'Harrison is dead,' he said.

He watched her breathe. The blanket shifted with each rise and fall of her chest. He had learned to read her this way—by the rhythm of her breathing, the angle of her chin, the small muscular work around her left eye that constituted, now, the full range of her expression.

She was afraid for him. He knew it before she spoke, and the knowing settled in his chest like a coal banked against morning.

'Letitia.' He leaned forward, elbows on his knees, closing the distance between them. 'I must go to Washington. Today.'

Her left hand moved on the blanket—a slow, deliberate motion, the fingers spreading and then closing against the wool. She was working toward speech. He waited, resisting the urge to fill the silence, which was its own form of discourtesy.

'Go, then.'

Two syllables, dragged up from somewhere effortful. He could not read the inflection—the paralysis had stripped that instrument from her, left only the bare words, which he could assign any meaning he chose and which she could not correct. He knew she knew it. The frustration of it crossed her eye sometimes like a weather change: the knowledge that she was legible only to the degree he permitted.

He took her left hand in both of his. Her skin was cool, the knuckles pronounced, the fingers thinner than he remembered, and he held them with care.

He brought her hand to his lips and held it there a moment.

'I will send for you when the house is ready,' he said against her fingers. 'You will come to Washington.'

He set her hand down. Rose. Walked to the door without looking back—not because he could not bear to see her face, but because he had already decided, and looking back would make the decision answerable to whatever her expression held, and he could not afford that. Not this morning. Not with the letter in his pocket and Washington two days north and the cabinet already laying the architecture of his diminishment.

He went.

## Scene 3

The household had come half-awake in the wrong direction—servants moving too fast, carrying the wrong things, a trunk dragged across the kitchen passage with a sound like splintering wood. Tyler descended the stairs already buttoning his coat and found the front hall in the condition of a camp struck before orders had been issued: Moses with a hat-box, young Priscilla clutching a pair of boots that belonged to no one present, the smell of burned cornmeal drifting from the kitchen where someone had abandoned the fire to come and stare.

'Set that down,' Tyler said. 'Moses—saddle the bay. The others may stand.'

Moses went. The others dispersed. Robert did not.

He had appeared in the study doorway, and Tyler read his son's face before the young man opened his mouth: the set jaw, the too-careful composure of a lawyer preparing to argue against his client's instructions.

'Father—'

'The bay, Moses.'

Robert moved to the hallway's narrowest point, not quite barring the way but occupying it with the fullness of someone who had decided the doorway was his to hold. He was twenty-five, with his mother's eyes and his father's stubbornness, and he had read the letter. Tyler had known he would.

'You cannot leave this morning.'

'I find that I can.'

'Without preparation. Without allies. Without so much as a word sent ahead to—'

'I have received notification of the death of the President.' Tyler came down the last step and moved toward the study. Robert followed. 'That is not a communication from strangers. That is a communication from the government of this republic.'

'A government that addressed you as Vice President.'

Tyler stopped.

The word hung in the cold hallway between them. Outside, the bay stamped once in the yard, impatient, and the sound carried clearly through the window glass.

Robert pressed forward, his voice dropping to the register of a man who has rehearsed the argument and believes it. 'I read the salutation while you were upstairs. They have not called you President. They have called you Vice President and informed you that certain powers and duties have devolved upon your office—and that language is not an accident. Ewing is too careful for accidents. If you ride into Washington like a candidate arriving for his own election, with no one at your side, you hand them the argument they want. You appear to be claiming something you know is contested.'

He pressed on. 'One day. Send to Governor Gilmer. Write to Rives. Arrive with Virginia behind you.'

Tyler went to the desk. He opened the second drawer and took out the small pocket edition of the Constitution—worn at the spine, the leather darkened along the edges where his thumb had worked the same path for years. He found the page without consulting the index. He held it toward the window where the early light, still grey, fell across the text, and he read aloud in the voice he had used on circuit when he wanted a jury to hear the law rather than his opinion of it:

*'In Case of the Removal, Death, Resignation, or Inability of the President, the Powers and Duties of the said Office shall devolve on the Vice President.'*

He closed the book and set it in his coat pocket.

'The document,' he said, 'does not require Governor Gilmer. It does not require Rives. It does not require a delegation, a letter of introduction, or any man's permission save the Almighty's. It requires my presence in Washington. That is all it requires.'

Robert's mouth opened.

'Furthermore.' Tyler moved past him toward the hall. 'If I arrive with Virginia at my back, I have conceded that my title requires endorsement. I will not make that concession. Not to Gilmer. Not to Rives. Not to Clay.'

His boot heel caught the loose board in the hallway and he stumbled, catching himself against the doorframe with his left hand. He straightened and kept walking.

'Father—' Robert's voice came from behind him, the argument gone out of it, something rawer in its place. 'They will say you seized it.'

Tyler paused with his hand on the door. He did not turn.

'They will say that regardless,' he said. 'The question is whether they say it of a man who came to Washington with his hat in his hand, or of a man who came as the Constitution directed him to come.' He lifted the hat from the peg. 'You will see to your mother. Arrange for Dr. Peachy to attend on Tuesday. Write to me in Washington and I will answer promptly.'

He set the hat on his head and opened the door onto the cold morning air.

'Do not send ahead. I require no announcement.'

He went out.

## Scene 4

The fog had not lifted. It lay on the Potomac in long, unbroken rolls, so that the Virginia shore dissolved twenty yards out and the far bank might as well not have existed. The steamer moved through it by memory and inference, navigating by the sound of water against the hull.

Tyler stood at the rail and did not open the Constitution.

He had read the clause enough times. He knew what it said and he knew what it refused to say, and standing here with the river invisible on all sides, he found he had no appetite for the clean geometry of legal argument. What pressed against him now was not a question of construction but a question of nerve—whether he possessed, in sufficient quantity, the particular species of courage required to walk into a room full of men who had already decided what he was, and tell them they were wrong.

His coat was wholly insufficient for the river at this hour. The damp worked through the wool and settled against his shirt, and when the steamer altered course to avoid a sandbar—the pilot shouting once, flatly, from the wheelhouse—the wind off the turn was cold enough to draw water from his eyes. He did not move from the rail. He gripped it with both hands and let the cold work on him, because the cold was honest in a way that the warm cabin below was not.

He thought of Ewing's letter. Not the words—he had those by heart—but the act of composition: the careful men gathered around a table in Washington, the drafts passed back and forth, the discussion Fletcher had declined to describe. *The powers and duties of the Presidential office have devolved upon you.* He could hear Ewing's voice in it, precise and lawyerly, the construction chosen not for its clarity but for its studied ambiguity. Not *the office*. Not *you are President*. The functions, the instruments—while the office itself floated in the language like an unmoored vessel, available for the cabinet to claim as theirs to define.

They had sent a young man on a fine horse. They had addressed him as Vice President. They had been, in their way, perfectly courteous—and courtesy, in this instance, was the most efficient instrument of diminishment available to them. A man who accepts a courtesy accepts the terms on which it is offered.

He released the rail. Turned his back to the fog and faced the length of the deck, empty at this hour, the planking wet and dark, the engine's vibration working up through the soles of his boots.

He had been a lawyer before he had been anything else. He knew the argument—had assembled it, tested it, found it sound. The Convention's original draft had spoken of the Vice President *acting as President*, a construction the Committee on Style had subsequently revised. That revision was not decorative. The framers had looked at *acting as President* and found it insufficient—had replaced it with *devolve*, a word from the law of inheritance, describing the passage of property from one holder to the next. Not custody. Not stewardship. Passage.

The argument was sound. He would not make it.

The moment he argued for the presidency, he conceded that the presidency required argument—that it was a question to be resolved by others rather than a fact already established by Harrison's death and the plain operation of the Constitution. A man who submits the title to his own land for another's adjudication has already half-surrendered it. He would not go to Washington as a petitioner. He would not present his brief to Ewing's cabinet and wait for their verdict as though they were the court and he the applicant.

He would take the oath. The full presidential oath, administered before the cabinet could convene and present him with their interpretation as though it were his own. The argument was his armor. The oath was his deed. There was a difference between the two that every lawyer understood and that every politician was trained to obscure, and he intended to make that difference irreversible before the sun was fully up on the morning after his arrival.

The steamer's bell struck—two short notes and then a third—and the pilot's voice carried back from the wheelhouse: the landing was close.

Tyler looked toward the Virginia bank where it emerged from the fog. There—on the road running north along the river, already pulling away from the landing—a rider. Moving fast. Moving north, at a pace that admitted no leisure.

The fog took him before Tyler could fix his coat, his color, the direction of his gaze.

He watched the empty road. The rider could be Robert's man, sent ahead despite instructions. He could be a courier from the State Department carrying word of the departure. He could be Clay's—dispatched with instructions for the cabinet regarding the precise form of welcome, or the precise form of diminishment, with which they intended to receive the man who was coming to claim what they had decided he could not have.

Tyler did not know. He would not know until he arrived, and perhaps not then. What he knew was this: whoever rode that road ahead of him, he would not arrive as a suppliant, hat in hand, waiting to be told what he was. He would arrive as the Constitution had made him—or he would not arrive at all.

The steamer's hull ground against the landing dock. The deckhand threw the line and it snaked out over the dark water, catching the post on the first cast.

Tyler picked up his bag and walked toward the gangway.
