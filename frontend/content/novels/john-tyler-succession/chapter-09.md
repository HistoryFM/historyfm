# Chapter 9: The Secretary's Dilemma

The memorandum had been twelve days in the failing.

Webster could see its inadequacy in the very arrangement of the pages: three false starts folded back upon themselves, a fourth attempt abandoned mid-sentence where the nib had hesitated and left a small black star of ink that was not punctuation but paralysis. *The constitutional provision for a national bank, as examined in the precedents established by McCulloch v. Maryland and the debates of the First Congress, would appear to sustain the proposition that—* And there the sentence stopped, because what it sustained depended entirely on which provision one examined and which debates one credited, and Webster had not yet determined which answer he required.

He set the pen down. Pressed the heels of his hands against his eyes.

The State Department offices on Fifteenth Street had not been designed with June in mind. The windows on the south wall admitted light but not air—their sashes warped in the summer moisture so that they could be raised perhaps three inches before sticking fast—and the heat that had been building since eight o'clock now occupied the room with the settled authority of a long-term tenant. His coat lay over the back of the chair. The Madeira decanter on the sideboard had gone from half to considerably less, and he had not remarked the transition.

He had intended the memorandum to settle the matter. He would present Tyler with a considered constitutional opinion—the bill defensible on its merits, the discount provisions susceptible to modification, the whole affair resolvable if both parties exercised the quality Washington so conspicuously lacked and which Webster, in his better hours, believed himself to possess: judgment. He would be the indispensable man. He would hold the bridge.

Twelve days had not produced a bridge. They had produced a sentence ending in a star of ink, and a second problem he had not anticipated when he began: he now knew his own answer to the question Tyler would put to him, and it was not the answer he could give.

He rose from the desk. Crossed to the window. Pressed one palm flat against the glass—the pane was warm, almost feverish—and looked down at the avenue below, where a vendor's cart moved with a grinding of iron on stone, the driver's head bowed against the heat. *If the bill came to me,* he thought, *I would not sign it. Not because it is unconstitutional—it is not, quite—but because Clay designed it to be signed, and I have not yet determined what I am worth to a man who has already decided the price.*

He withdrew his hand from the glass. The palm print faded slowly, the fog of it shrinking from the edges inward until nothing remained.

He was still at the window when Derrick knocked.

The clerk was twenty-two and had the useful quality of appearing always to be looking at something slightly to the left of whatever was actually happening. He stood in the doorway with a small folded note held between two fingers, his eyes directed at a point near Webster's left shoulder.

'Messenger, sir. Didn't leave a name, but'—Derrick's gaze drifted, fractionally—'the livery was Kentucky.'

Webster took the note. The seal was a confident impression in blue wax, pressed with a signet ring: the kind of mark a man makes when he has no need to conceal the making of it. He broke it without ceremony.

Four lines. No salutation. The hand was a secretary's—Clay did not waste his own penmanship on notes of this nature—but the voice was unmistakably the Senator's: the blunt, forward-leaning syntax of one who had never required circumlocution.

*Dinner this evening at seven o'clock. I shall expect you. There are matters regarding the President's disposition toward the legislation before the Finance Committee that require the counsel of a man in your position.*

Webster read it a second time, not because the meaning was obscure but because the second reading confirmed the first.

*A man in your position.* The phrase had been chosen, not merely written.

Derrick had not moved. His gaze had settled on the window, as though the stuck sash were a matter of professional concern. Webster was aware—had been aware since the clerk's first week—that Derrick's brother-in-law held a compositing position at the *National Intelligencer*, a fact which Derrick had disclosed voluntarily and which Webster had filed accordingly.

'That will be all.'

When the door closed, Webster drew a sheet of paper and wrote.

*My dear Senator—I am sensible of the honour of your invitation, and I regret that the press of departmental correspondence will prevent my attendance this—*

He stopped. Read it. The sentence had the sound of a declaration.

He tore the sheet in half. Then in quarters. Then he aligned the pieces with two precise movements along the desk's edge before sweeping them into the waste-basket. Drew a fresh sheet.

*My dear Senator—I shall attend with pleasure. Seven o'clock.*

He sanded it, folded it, and called for the messenger before he could reconsider.

The memorandum on the bank bill remained on his desk, its final sentence still reaching toward a conclusion it had not yet found. He did not look at it again. He already knew what it said.

---

The President's study smelled of heated beeswax and something older beneath it—Harrison's pipe tobacco still faint in the curtains, the ghost of a man who had not lived long enough to air the room out. A military map of the Northwest Territory, pinned too hastily to the plaster, had begun to peel at one corner, and Webster found his eyes drawn to it: the dead man's geography, still governing the wall.

Tyler sat in a cane-backed chair with his coat across the arm, his shirtsleeves rolled to the wrist. He held the bill—the actual printed text, not a summary—with the practiced grip of one who had been reading law since before Webster argued his first case before the Circuit Court. He did not look up when Webster entered. He turned a page. The afternoon heat lay across the room like a compress; the half-drawn curtains moved barely at all. Somewhere beneath the window, a cart horse shifted its weight on the cobblestones, the iron shoes ringing once and then going still.

'I am grateful you have come,' Tyler said, still reading. 'I wished to ask you about Section the Fourth.'

Webster had prepared for this. He had prepared a structure: the constitutionality was defensible under the necessary-and-proper clause, the provisions for establishing offices in the several states presented questions of policy rather than fundamental law, a request for modification would be received by the Senate as deliberate engagement rather than hostility—

'The provision for establishing offices in the states without reference to state legislative consent,' Tyler continued, setting the bill face-down on the desk. 'You will have read it.'

'I have.'

'And your opinion of its constitutionality?'

Webster drew a breath and reached for the pen from Tyler's desk—a long quill, the nib recently trimmed—and turned it once in his fingers. 'The constitutional provision is susceptible of more than one construction, as the President will know from his own examination. The necessary-and-proper clause has been broadly interpreted by the Court, and there are arguments of considerable weight in support of the bill's general framework. The provisions in question present a more refined matter—one on which reasonable men have differed, and on which I should wish to review the committee's reported language before—'

'Mr. Webster.'

The name fell between them without inflection. Webster set the pen down.

'I have reviewed the committee's language,' Tyler said. 'I have also reviewed the language of my own speech of 1838, which Senator Clay was kind enough to incorporate.' A pause. 'I shall tell you plainly—I am not asking you to rehearse the arguments.' He paused again. A fly entered the silence, buzzing near Webster's left ear with the mindless persistence of a thing that did not know it had chosen an inconvenient moment. Webster's hand jerked upward; the fly departed. Tyler waited, his hands folded on the desk. When he spoke again his voice had dropped to something just above conversational. 'If you sat in this chair—if the bill lay before you, and the pen were in your hand—would you sign it?'

The question stood in the room.

Webster opened his mouth. 'The question, sir, is somewhat hypothetical in its framing—'

'I do not find it hypothetical.'

Silence.

He had built the structure carefully: the bill was defensible, the modifications were preferable, the path between Clay and Tyler ran through the Secretary of State's office and required him to remain standing precisely where he stood. All of it was true. All of it was also, in this moment, inaccessible to him, because the honest answer—*I would not sign it, but I would not say so, because my reasons are not constitutional*—was the one sentence he could not speak aloud in this room or any other.

Tyler did not press him. He simply waited, with the attentive stillness of one reading testimony he had already decided how to weight—and that patience, Webster understood, was not courtesy. It was the instrument itself.

Then Tyler rose from the chair—not quickly, not with any show of displeasure, but with the deliberate ease of long practice—and crossed to the window. He stood with his back to the room, looking down at the avenue below. When he spoke, his voice was conversational, almost pleasant.

'The Northeastern boundary,' Tyler said. 'I understand Lord Palmerston's government has indicated a willingness to negotiate. That is a matter of some consequence for your department.'

Webster understood, with sudden and complete clarity, what had just occurred. Tyler had not been asking for constitutional counsel. He had been taking a measurement. And the measurement was complete.

'It is,' Webster said.

'You will keep me informed.'

'Of course, Mr. President.'

Tyler remained at the window, his back still turned. He did not reach for the bill. The dismissal was not the end of a conversation but the opening of a calculation that would proceed without Webster's participation.

---

The servant had laid out his evening clothes on the chair beside the parlor door—coat brushed, cravat pressed into a white rectangle of expectation—and Webster stood before the mirror above the mantelpiece and did not move to change.

Well. There it was.

He had sat in a dead president's study, turned a borrowed pen in his fingers, and when a direct question was put to him—plainly, without ceremony, by a man who deserved a plain answer—he had produced a subordinate clause. Tyler had not required the silence to run its full length. He had simply stood, turned to the window, and changed the subject to a treaty negotiation, which was his way of saying: *I know what you are, and I shall govern myself accordingly.*

The brandy was good—he kept better stock at home than at the office—and it finished clean, leaving only a faint astringency at the back of the throat. He had poured it not from thirst but from the need to have something in his hand while he thought, the way a surgeon picks up an instrument before he has decided whether to cut.

Upstairs, Caroline moved from one room to another. A door. A pause. The particular creak of the board outside her dressing room that he had intended to have fixed since February.

The man in the mirror was large, dishevelled, coat still over the chair. He had argued *Dartmouth College* before the Supreme Court—had felt Marshall's attention fix upon him like a beam of cold light—and delivered the Reply to Hayne on the floor of the Senate with the whole chamber holding its breath, every man in the gallery leaning forward as though the republic itself had asked a question and was waiting to hear whether anyone could answer it. Thirty years of arguments that held weight. Thirty years of knowing, when he entered a room, that the room would be altered by his presence.

This afternoon the room had altered him.

He raised the glass toward his mouth. Set it on the mantelpiece instead, aligning it with the edge.

*Deliberating with care.* That was what he would tell Clay this evening. Four words, shaped like information, containing none—a vessel designed to appear full while remaining empty. Clay would press, and Webster would elaborate, and the elaboration would be more of the same: the careful man's version of silence, dressed in the vocabulary of statesmanship.

He said it aloud, to the empty parlor: *Deliberating with care.* His voice sounded practiced. Reasonable. He tried the emphasis differently—*deliberating* with care—and found the second version conveyed slightly more of Tyler's seriousness without committing to its direction.

He was, he reflected, very good at this.

The thought did not comfort him.

He crossed to the chair and took up the coat. The evening was already half-gone; Clay's dinner began within the half-hour, and the walk was twelve minutes in the heat. He checked the time. He put on the coat.

From upstairs, Caroline's footstep crossed the board outside her dressing room. Creak. Pause. Then nothing.

He took his hat from the table by the door and went out into the Washington evening, carrying his phrase before him—a lantern whose fuel he had already decided not to examine.

---

The other guests had not arrived.

Webster understood this within thirty seconds of being shown into Clay's parlor—the table set for six, the candles lit along its length, the chairs arranged with the precision of an occasion planned down to its seating. But the chairs were empty, and the servant who had admitted him retreated at once, and Clay rose from the single armchair beside the window with a warmth so complete and so immediate that the temperature of the room seemed to shift without any fire having been added to the grate.

'Webster.' Clay extended his hand. 'I had begun to wonder whether you had found a prior engagement more worthy of your company.'

'The evening was fine,' Webster said. 'I walked.'

Clay's smile held. 'A philosopher or a penitent. Come. Sit.'

The roasted fowl arrived before Webster had fully settled—borne in by a servant who set it between them with the practiced efficiency of one accustomed to serving two where six had been expected, and retreated without a word. The smell of it was rich and slightly scorched at the skin, mixed with the beeswax of the candles and something underneath both, a faint brackish note from the open windows where the Potomac's exhalations drifted in with whatever the evening's breeze saw fit to carry. Clay poured the wine himself. He did not explain the absent guests.

'Tyler,' Clay said, without preface. 'What is his mind?'

'He is weighing the matter with considerable care.'

Clay set down the decanter. He regarded Webster for a long moment—the look of one who has received a document and found it unsigned. 'You sat with him this afternoon,' Clay said, 'and that is what you bring me.'

'He has read the bill. He has objections to the provisions regarding state offices.'

'Rives has objections. Wright has objections—though Wright's objections are weapons, not scruples.' Clay lifted his glass, drank, set it down. 'I am asking you what Tyler intends to do.'

'I cannot tell you what I do not know.'

A silence. Clay turned his glass by the stem, once, twice. The candle nearest Webster guttered—a quick collapse of the flame—and spat a small bead of hot wax onto the tablecloth near Webster's hand. Webster drew his hand back; Clay watched it.

'You sat in that room,' Clay said, 'and watched a man who has already made his decision perform the theatre of deliberation for your benefit, and you have come here to perform the same theatre for mine.' He picked up his knife and fork, cut a piece of the fowl, set them down again without eating. 'I am not Tyler. I will not be managed.'

Webster cut a piece of the fowl on his own plate. He raised it toward his mouth. Set the fork down. 'You are asking me to read a man's mind.'

'I am asking you to read his conduct. You are a lawyer. It is what lawyers do.'

The candle steadied. The smoke from the wax drifted past the edge of the tablecloth and dispersed. Clay leaned back in his chair and folded his hands across his waistcoat, and when he spoke again the conversational warmth had gone entirely—not anger, something quieter and more deliberate, the register he used when the speech was done and the sentence had been delivered.

'If Tyler vetoes this bill,' Clay said, 'the cabinet resigns. Every man. It is already arranged.'

Webster looked up from his plate.

'Ewing is prepared. Crittenden is prepared.' Clay did not look away. 'The resignation must be unanimous to carry its proper weight. Any officer who remains in that cabinet after the veto has chosen Tyler over his party and his country, and he will be remembered accordingly.'

'You mean to include the Secretary of State in that number.'

It was not a question. Clay heard it as one anyway. 'I mean to include every man who values his standing in this party.'

Webster lifted his fork a second time. The fowl had cooled. He set the fork down.

Outside, a carriage passed—iron rims loud on the paving stones, then fading—and the curtain moved once in the Potomac air and settled.

Webster reached for his wine. He drank.

'You understand what I am telling you,' Clay said.

'I understand you perfectly.'

Clay watched him and said nothing further, and the silence between them was the silence of men who had said the only things that mattered and were waiting to see who would be first to pretend otherwise.

Webster set down his glass. 'The evening has grown late.'

He was still pulling on his coat in the doorway when he heard Clay, behind him, take up his knife and fork at last.

---

The street was dark and close, the June air sitting on the city like a weight that had accumulated all day and found no means of escape. The paving stones gave back the heat they had absorbed since morning; the smell of horse dung and river mud and something sweetly rotten from the market stalls on Pennsylvania Avenue rose together in the still air. Webster walked.

Tyler's question moved through him—*if the bill lay before you, and the pen were in your hand, would you sign it?*—and against it now, Clay's declaration, flat and final as a door closing: *any officer who remains has chosen Tyler over his party and his country.* Between the two of them, the bill moved through its committees toward the President's desk with the indifference of a river that has not been asked its opinion of the towns through which it flows.

He had not answered Tyler. He would not answer Clay. He was, he understood, in the process of answering both of them—not with words but with the accumulation of his silences, each one a small forfeit, each one narrowing the ground on which he stood until the ground itself would make the choice for him.

He did not look back at Clay's windows. He did not need to. Behind him, in the lit parlor, the Senator was eating his dinner with the appetite of one who has issued an ultimatum and is content to wait for the reply. The letter was already forming—Webster could feel it taking shape somewhere behind him in the candlelit room, the careful syntax of a man who understood that a threat committed to paper carried more weight than one merely spoken over wine.

Somewhere ahead, on a desk in the Executive Mansion, the enrolled bill waited—patient, indifferent, its blank signature line open as a question and final as a verdict. The man who sat behind that desk had taken his measure of the Secretary of State and found the result instructive. He would not consult Webster again on the constitutional question. He would not need to.

The phrase *deliberating with care* had carried Webster through the evening. It would not carry him into the week that followed. The bill would reach the President's desk within the fortnight—Ewing had confirmed as much before the session rose—and when it did, every man in the cabinet would be required to declare himself by act rather than by evasion.

Webster walked faster. The city pressed around him, dark and warm and close, and behind him—he was certain of it now, certain in the way one is certain of a sound heard at the edge of sleep—Clay had already begun to write.
