# Chapter Ten: The Dinner at Gadsby's

## Scene 1

The tally sheet lay on the writing desk like a document that had decided to be unhelpful. Clay had arranged it three times—once by chamber, once by state, once by the brutal arithmetic of yes and no—and the numbers declined to improve through rearrangement. Twenty-eight firm. Four wavering. Two likely no, of whom Rives had, until this afternoon, occupied the more negotiable column.

Until this afternoon.

The letter had arrived at four o'clock, carried by one of Rives's own servants rather than a hotel boy, which was itself a communication. Clay had read it once standing at the window, the late June sun pressing against the glass and through it onto his shirt, and then sat down and read it again. The second reading confirmed every suspicion the first had planted.

*My constitutional scruples regarding the branch office provisions*, Rives had written, *are not of recent manufacture, as the Senator from Kentucky will know from my public record. I have examined the bill as reported from committee and find myself unable, in good conscience, to vote for a measure that would establish federal instrumentalities within the sovereign territory of the several states without the express sanction of those states' legislatures. I do not advance these objections as novelties; they are the objections I have held since—*

Clay set the letter aside and refilled his glass of Madeira without looking at it, his eyes fixed on Rives's handwriting as though the penmanship itself were the betrayal.

The objections were not Rives's. That was the thing. They were precise, they were lawyerly, they were arranged in exactly the sequence a man would employ who had spent years on the circuit bench constructing arguments from the inside out—establishing the principle before the application, citing the instrument before the precedent. Rives was capable of such argument, but Rives was also a man who had never, in thirty years of public life, produced a constitutional objection without first discovering that it was politically convenient to hold one. These objections had been handed to him. Their architecture was Williamsburg.

The smell of the Madeira reached him before the taste—warm, faintly raisined, the sweetness of a bottle opened too long ago. He drank without pleasure and set the glass aside.

Clay pressed the heel of his hand against the tally sheet and drew it toward him. Twenty-eight. The four wavering he could manage through the dinner if he handled the evening correctly—Archer, Morehead, Preston, and Henderson, all of whom responded to social pressure the way iron filings answer a lodestone. But Rives moving to likely no meant the margin had shrunk to something that would not survive further defection, and it meant something worse: if Tyler was writing to Rives, he was writing to others. The letter was not a private exchange between two Virginia gentlemen of shared constitutional conviction. It was the first visible corner of a correspondence that ran, Clay was now certain, through a half-dozen Southern Whig offices.

If two more men received letters in the same hand before the floor vote, the measure was gone. Not wounded—gone.

The June heat in the closed room was suffocating; he tugged irritably at his cravat before remembering he had not yet put on his coat.

He pulled a fresh sheet from the desk drawer and dipped his pen.

*Senator Rives—I have received your letter of this afternoon and find myself in some perplexity as to its occasion, given that the Finance Committee has not yet concluded its deliberations and that the floor vote remains some days distant. I should be obliged if you would call upon me at Gadsby's at your earliest convenience, and in any case before the bill reaches the Senate floor, as I believe a private conversation would resolve certain difficulties that correspondence cannot. I remain, with great respect—*

He sanded it, folded it once, pressed the crease with his thumbnail. Then he took Rives's letter from the desk—that careful, borrowed, Williamsburg-scented document—and placed it in the breast pocket of his coat alongside the reply. Both of them. The reply he would send by messenger within the hour. The original he would carry to dinner.

Not to read aloud. He was not so clumsy as that. But to hold, as a man holds a deed to disputed property—the knowledge of its existence sufficient, the paper itself unnecessary until the moment it became necessary.

The clock on the mantelpiece showed half past four. He had two hours before the dinner, and the dinner was the last instrument available to him before the floor.

Clay rose and went to the window, looking down at Pennsylvania Avenue in the failing afternoon light. The street moved below him as it always moved—hacks and carts and the occasional barouche of someone who wished to be seen arriving—and he watched it without seeing it, his mind already in the dining room, already arranging chairs.

## Scene 2

The staircase at Gadsby's was narrow enough that two men descending together could not pretend they were not speaking in confidence, and Crittenden had timed his interception with a precision Clay recognised and did not admire. He had materialised from the second-floor corridor at the precise moment Clay's boot found the top step—not an accident, not a coincidence, but the practiced emergence of someone who has been waiting behind a door.

"A word before we go in." Crittenden's voice was barely above a murmur.

The smell of roasting meat rose from below, rich and faintly scorched, threaded with tobacco smoke from the public rooms. Someone's cigar had preceded them down the stairwell—a sweeter Virginia leaf, the kind favoured by the Tidewater men. Clay descended one step and stopped, his hand on the banister, his eyes on the lower landing rather than on Crittenden.

"Mangum has been talking." Crittenden pressed close, keeping pace, his words absorbed by the noise rising from below. "Nothing formal. But he has been at Archer's elbow since this morning, and Berrien's as well. Suggesting the President might look more favourably upon the measure if certain modifications were introduced—the state-veto provision."

"What modifications, precisely."

"His words, as I had them, were that a man of genuine constitutional scruple might be brought to sign a measure that preserved the principle of state consent."

Clay's knee caught on the narrow turn of the stair—a sudden seize in the joint that had plagued him since the Lexington road—and he gripped the banister hard before the pain released.

"How many has he spoken to?"

"Archer, certainly. Berrien, I believe. Perhaps Preston."

Three men from the wavering column, and the Senator from North Carolina had reached them before the soup. Clay's grip on the banister did not slacken. "Has he gone to Henderson?"

"I don't know."

"Find out. Before we sit down." He resumed his descent, voice dropping further. "And John—I want you beside Archer tonight. His left hand, from the soup through the fish. Mangum is not to have a private word with him before I have addressed the table."

Crittenden was quiet a beat too long.

"Can you manage it," Clay said. Not a question.

"I can manage it." Something in Crittenden's jaw tightened and released. "But I put it to you plainly, Henry. There are members of our delegation who believe some accommodation is still—"

"An accommodation." Clay stopped on the landing and turned. The noise from below was louder here, the smell of the roast thickening around them. He kept his voice low and level. "John. We were elected on a platform. The people voted in November for a national bank. Harrison was the justification and the mandate both. Tyler sat on the same ticket. He cannot now discover scruples that were apparently invisible to him when the ballots were being counted."

"He has held those views for thirty years. That is what Mangum—"

"Then he should have disclosed them before we put his name on our handbills." Clay placed a hand on Crittenden's shoulder—not warmly. "If Mangum has reached Henderson, I need to know it before I sit down. Go."

Crittenden went.

Below, voices rose and fell in the private room—the sound of men settling, glasses finding tables, the small social percussion of an evening beginning. Clay descended the last steps alone, the smell of roasting meat closing around him, the sound of his guests' voices rising to receive him.

He had perhaps forty minutes before Mangum made his move. The dinner was the field, and he had just lost the advantage of surprise.

## Scene 3

The candles had burned down two inches since the soup, and the room held the heat of twelve men and their opinions like a closed oven. The air was close with tallow smoke and the residue of the roast—a joint that had come in on a great silver dish and been carved with ceremony and consumed with the appetite of men who had been arguing since morning. Clay sat at the table's head with his fourth glass of Bordeaux, a wine that had arrived from the cellar tasting of cork and ambition—thin at the front, flat at the finish, the kind of bottle a man serves when he is more interested in the occasion than the vintage. He had mentioned this to their host, Wickliffe, at the third pour, with a smile of such refined commiseration that Wickliffe had thanked him for the observation.

The talk had been good. That was the art of such evenings: you did not lead the conversation, you merely owned the room through which it moved. Clay had guided it through the tariff, through the Cumberland Road appropriations, through a digression on Jackson's old land grants that had produced the evening's first real laughter. He had watched the wavering men settle and warm. Archer was expansive. Henderson had drunk two glasses of the inferior Bordeaux without complaint, which was promising.

Mangum waited. That was what Clay had observed about the man across a decade of Senate sessions: he waited for a room to exhaust itself, and then he spoke into the quiet left behind. The Senator from North Carolina sat midway down the table, his expression attentive and mild, one hand resting beside his glass. He had said almost nothing since the fish course. He had touched his lapel twice.

Senator Archer was concluding a story about the Finance Committee—something involving a disputed quorum and a clerk who had miscounted—when Mangum set down his fork with a small, deliberate sound.

'There is a question,' he said, into the silence that followed Archer's last word, 'that I think the gentlemen present have been courteous enough not to raise directly.'

The table shifted. Not visibly—these were legislators who had spent careers not visibly shifting—but Clay felt it, the way a card player feels the weight redistribute when someone reaches for a card they have been pretending not to want.

'The President's disposition,' Mangum continued, 'toward the bill as drafted. I am bound to say—and I advance this not as opposition but as counsel—that certain modifications regarding the states' power of assent over branch offices might secure his signature and spare us all the considerable inconvenience of—'

'The considerable inconvenience,' Clay said, 'of governing.'

Laughter ran the table. Mangum waited for it.

'I mean only to observe that a man of genuine constitutional conviction might find in a revised instrument sufficient grounds to—'

'My friend.' Clay set down his glass and regarded Mangum with the expression he reserved for arguments that did not yet know they had lost. 'I have known a great many men of Virginia. I served under one for eight years, and I may say without undue immodesty that I understand the constitutional convictions of Virginia gentlemen rather better than those gentlemen sometimes understand themselves.' He picked up the glass again, turned it once between his fingers, set it down. 'The difficulty with our present—occupant—is not that he is a man of principle. The difficulty is that he arrived at the presidency by an accident of mortality and has since convinced himself that the accident was Providence.'

Someone down the table laughed. Clay did not acknowledge it.

'We are asked to modify the bill,' he said, 'not because the bill is defective—it is not—but because His Accidency requires the flattery of consultation.'

The phrase arrived in the room and remained there.

*His Accidency.* He had not planned it. It had come to him as the good phrases did, from that region of the mind where contempt and precision occupied the same territory. He watched it move from face to face around the table—the recognition, the suppressed delight, the small recalibrations of men deciding whether to laugh or to look solemn. Most of them laughed.

Mangum did not. His eyes moved, briefly and deliberately, toward the door, which stood three inches open onto the corridor beyond.

Clay did not follow the glance. He already knew what was in the corridor. He had known since the staircase—since Crittenden's whispered report, since the moment he had noted the young man from the Madisonian seated at the table's outer edge, careful and silent, with his habit of appearing to study the ceiling at moments when the conversation grew most useful. The door had been three inches open since the soup. He had not closed it.

He reached for his glass and drank.

The laughter continued a moment longer than was strictly comfortable. Then it stopped, and no one at the table raised the subject of modification again.

## Scene 4

The corridor was poorly lit—two gas lamps burning at intervals too great for the space between them, leaving a long reach of shadow near the main entrance where the smell of the public rooms had gathered and settled: tobacco, horse-damp wool, the faint sweetness of spilled claret from some earlier evening now dried into the floorboards. From the avenue outside came the steady percussion of a carriage horse on cobblestone, patient and indifferent.

Clay stood in the dimness. He had come out without his hat, which he now recognised as an error, and the night air from the open entrance was cooler than the room he had left—a relief that felt, in the present moment, like a small reproach. Behind him, the dinner was breaking apart: the shuffle of men collecting themselves, the low exchange of goodnights, Archer's voice raised in some final pleasantry. Mangum had gone without a word. Crittenden had departed twenty minutes before that, with a nod that said nothing and therefore said a great deal.

He became aware of someone approaching from the direction of the private room.

Young. Slight. A dark coat that wanted pressing. He carried himself with the careful purposefulness of a man committed to an approach he had rehearsed and could not now abandon.

Fletcher. The Madisonian's correspondent.

"Senator Clay." Fletcher stopped at a respectful distance, the shadows softening his youth. "I had hoped to pay my respects before the evening concluded."

"You are kind." Clay turned to face him fully—not retreating toward the wall, not angling toward the entrance. He planted himself in the corridor's centre and waited.

A carriage wheel struck a rut in the avenue outside, and the sharp report carried through the open door. Neither man acknowledged it.

Fletcher had produced a small notebook from his coat—not conspicuously, but with the practiced ease of someone who wishes the gesture to appear incidental. "I confess I found your remarks this evening most illuminating, Senator. Particularly your characterisation of the current occupant of the Executive Mansion." A pause. He was watching Clay's face with the attentiveness of someone paid to watch faces. "His Accidency, I believe you said."

The phrase hung between them in the corridor's dim air.

Clay heard it—heard it as a man hears his own voice returned by a wall, carrying a resonance the original utterance had not possessed. Over the laughter of men who had wanted to laugh, it had been a witticism. Here, in a journalist's careful diction, set down in a notebook already returned to a coat pocket, it was something that would not be unwritten.

He held Fletcher's gaze.

"I wonder," Clay said, "whether the Madisonian intends to print a full account of the evening's conversation, or only those portions its editors find most serviceable to the Administration."

"We print what is of public interest, Senator."

"Of course you do." Clay took one step toward the entrance, then stopped and looked back at the young man. "Tell me—does Mr. Tyler read the Madisonian himself, or does he have it read to him?"

A beat of silence. Fletcher's pen was not moving. "I believe the President reads it himself, sir."

"Then he will find the account most instructive." Clay settled his coat across his shoulders—a deliberate adjustment, unhurried. "Good evening to you."

He walked to the entrance and pushed through into the avenue's noise without waiting for Fletcher's reply.

owly after dark, the smell of horse and dust and the distant sweetness of something burning at the edge of the city. He stood on the step a moment, hat still absent, the avenue moving before him with its ordinary commerce.

The word was out. He knew it with the certainty of thirty years spent watching words travel in this city: by morning to the Madisonian's compositing room, by day's end to the wire services, within the week to every paper from Boston to Charleston that took pleasure in the Administration's difficulties. Tyler would read it in that particular stillness his intimates described—hands folded, voice dropping—and whatever constitutional reasoning had previously governed his deliberation over the bank measure would acquire, now, a second and less tractable foundation.

Wounded pride was a far more durable material than principle. Clay knew this from long acquaintance with both.

He descended to the pavement and had taken perhaps a dozen steps north when he heard his name—not called, precisely, but pronounced, in the flat declarative manner of a man who has been waiting and means to be heard.

"Senator Clay."

The figure stood at the edge of the lamp's reach, where the light gave out against the dark: a young man, not Fletcher, not anyone Clay could immediately place, holding an envelope with both hands in the manner of someone who has been instructed to surrender it and nothing else. His coat was dusty with hard riding, his boots carrying the pale clay of the river road.

"From the Executive Mansion, sir." He extended the envelope. "I was told to wait upon your reply."

Clay took it. The seal was intact—not the formal departmental wax of official correspondence but plain red, pressed with a thumb rather than a device, the mark of a communication dispatched in haste and without ceremony. The paper itself was warm from the messenger's grip, or from the ride, or from whatever room it had been written in.

He did not open it.

"Who sent you?" he said.

"I was not given a name to give, Senator."

Clay turned the envelope once in his hands. The address on the face was in a clerk's hand—careful, anonymous, the penmanship of a man paid to write without being remembered. Beneath it, in a different hand entirely, three words had been added, pressed hard enough to score the paper: *before the vote.*

Somewhere behind him, through the Madisonian's thin walls, the fiddle had stopped.
