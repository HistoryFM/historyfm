# Chapter 8: The Gavel Falls

## Scene 1

The cloakroom behind the Senate chamber held, at twenty minutes past ten, the compressed atmosphere of men who had been talking since before the galleries opened: wool coats steaming from the walk up Capitol Hill, the raw bite of Virginia tobacco from three separate pipes, and beneath it all the sourness of coffee gone cold in its cups. Clay stood at the far end of the room with his back to the coat-pegs, his draft speech folded in his breast pocket, going over Mangum's vote count for the fourth time. Not because the numbers had changed. Because enumeration was itself a form of control.

Rives arrived at twenty-two minutes past. Clay knew the time because he had looked.

William Campbell Rives was a careful man—which was what made him useful in ordinary sessions and dangerous in this one. He carried his hat in both hands, turning it by the brim, a habit Clay had observed across fifteen years in the Senate and had never seen him conquer. They exchanged the customary greeting. They were alone except for Mangum's secretary, who had taken himself to the far corner with the studied invisibility of a functionary who understood his purpose.

"I came early," Rives said, "because I thought it better to speak before the galleries filled."

"That was considerate of you, William." Clay crossed the room in four strides, pulled the nearest chair from the wall, and set it squarely between Rives and the door. He did not sit in it. He stood behind it, hands on the back. "Speak, then."

The directness visibly unsettled Rives—a small tightening around the eyes, a fractional halt in the hat's rotation. "I have a difficulty with the bill as drafted."

"The branch provisions."

Rives blinked. "Yes. The language establishing offices in the states without legislative consent. I cannot in conscience pledge my vote to it."

The tobacco smoke drifted between them. Somewhere beneath the floor, a door opened and shut, and they heard the distant percussion of the chamber filling—the collective scrape of chairs, the low hum of men settling into their places.

"The Finance Committee will entertain amendments in the ordinary course. No man's concerns go unheard in committee."

"It is not the committee I am thinking of." Rives reached into his coat and produced a letter. The paper was folded once, the crease sharp and recent. He laid it on the chair seat between Clay's hands.

Clay picked it up. He read the salutation: *My dear Rives—* The hand was small and deliberate, each word pressed into the page as though its author expected it to be read by a court. He read the body of the letter standing, without expression, and when he had finished he refolded it along its original creases—pressing each one down with his thumbnail, slowly—and held it out.

Rives did not take it immediately.

"He articulates," Rives said, "the very objection I had formed independently. That the bill as drafted would constitute an exercise of power nowhere granted by the Constitution—that the states' authority over banking within their own borders cannot be abridged by congressional fiat, whatever the commercial convenience—"

"William." Clay's voice dropped to the register he reserved for private ultimatums. Not cold—colder than cold, the temperature of a room from which all warmth has been deliberately removed. "If you walk through that door and onto the floor this morning carrying the President's constitutional scruples as your own, you will have handed the Democracy a weapon before the session is an hour old. Every correspondent in the gallery will file that story. Every Whig senator who reads it tomorrow will know which of his colleagues chose the opening day of a special session to fracture his own majority." He pressed the letter into Rives's hand. "That is not a threat. It is arithmetic."

Rives took the letter. His hat had stopped turning.

"Bring your objections to the Finance Committee," Clay continued, his voice returning to its ordinary register as though the other had never existed. "I am pledging you that hearing. Formally. Today. Your concerns will be recorded, addressed, and resolved—within the committee, where they belong."

"And if they are not resolved to my satisfaction?"

Clay looked at him steadily. "Then you will have exhausted the proper remedies, and whatever you choose to do afterward will be your own affair, and the history of this session will record it as such." He released the chair back and straightened his coat. "But you will not have given the Democrats their opening before I have delivered my first speech."

From the chamber beyond the wall, the sound of the galleries settling into expectant silence reached them—a hush that pressed against the door like a held breath, the suspended attention of several hundred people waiting for something to begin.

Rives turned his hat by the brim. Once. Twice. Then he set it on the table.

"I will bring my objections to the committee," he said.

"Good." Clay moved toward the chamber door. "Come, then. The session will not wait."

## Scene 2

The gavel came down at half past ten, and the sound rang through the Senate Chamber like a rifle shot fired into a cathedral—sharp, declarative, and immediately swallowed by the room's indifferent stone.

Mangum had been speaking for eleven minutes.

Clay tracked it by the watch in his waistcoat pocket, withdrawing it with the unhurried deliberation of a man consulting a document rather than marking time, the gold case catching the light from the western galleries as he tilted its face toward him. Eleven minutes. The rules of the Senate provided for a brief address upon the occasion of organizing the special session; they did not provide for a meditation upon the history of the body, the sacrifices of its former members, or the particular obligations incumbent upon senators in this hour of national trial. Mangum, settled behind the presiding officer's desk with the gravity of an officer who had at last arrived somewhere he had long imagined, appeared to have found no reason to distinguish between what the rules required and what his own sense of occasion demanded.

Clay restored the watch to his pocket and arranged his face.

The chamber pressed in around him—the tiered galleries above packed with correspondents and citizens who had arrived before dawn to secure their places, the air already close with the smell of damp wool and the human warmth of several hundred bodies confined beneath a low ceiling. Below the gallery rail, the Senate's mahogany desks curved outward in their concentric arcs, the Whig majority on one side, the Democratic minority ranged opposite with the disciplined quiet of men who had come to observe everything and record it faithfully. The Democrats had come to listen. Clay had noticed that before Mangum's gavel fell, and it had told him something he did not yet know how to use.

Mangum concluded.

The chamber offered the mild percussion of approval—desktops rapped, a sound like a brief, courteous rain—and then fell into the expectancy that precedes a performance everyone has come to witness.

Clay rose.

He rose, and the chamber changed. Not loudly—there was no eruption, no dramatic intake of breath—but the quality of attention in the room sharpened and concentrated the way light concentrates when a lens is brought to bear, and every man in the gallery leaned forward by some fraction of an inch, and the scratch of correspondents' pens against paper ceased all at once, as a mill-wheel ceases when the sluice is closed. He had felt this for thirty years. He gathered his notes, set them aside, and addressed himself to the chamber with the ease of a man standing in his own doorway.

He began with Harrison.

He could not have begun anywhere else. Harrison was the mandate, Harrison was the justification, Harrison was the forty-nine thousand votes by which the republic had declared its preference for the American System over the ruin of Democratic finance—the old general, dead now seven weeks, whose sacrifice had purchased this majority, this session, this hour. Clay let the name carry its weight, let the periods build and resolve with the measured cadence of a funeral oration that was also a declaration of war, and he watched the Whig senators' faces as he spoke: gravity, assent, the small nods of men who are being told what they already believe and are grateful for the reminder.

He moved to the bank. His voice rang in the upper registers of the chamber, filling the stone vault, reaching the gallery correspondents who had bent again to their pages—he could hear them now, the industrious scratch of a dozen pens against paper, the sound of the session being committed to the record that would survive all of them.

He had moved on to the branch provisions—the very language Rives had named an hour ago in the cloakroom—when he saw it.

Wright sat in the third row of the Democratic benches, hands folded on his desk, posture that of a scholar attending a lecture on a text he has already annotated. His eyes moved—a single, unhurried transit across the aisle—and found Rives, and in the moment of contact something passed between them that no correspondent in the gallery could have recorded in his notebook. Rives's chin dropped a quarter of an inch and rose again.

Clay's sentence did not break. His voice did not alter its cadence by a syllable.

He turned the speech. Where he had intended to conclude with the tariff, he did not conclude. He built instead—clause upon clause, the periods lengthening, the rhythm gathering pressure—toward the men on the southern end of the Whig benches, the Virginians and Georgians and Carolinians who had come to this session with reservations they had not yet named aloud. He addressed them without naming them. He invoked the obligation they had assumed when they accepted the Whig nomination, the trust that Harrison's voters had placed in this majority, the judgment that history would render upon any man who, having been given the instruments of national restoration, chose to lay them down over a scruple that no court had sustained and no scholar had certified.

He did not say *Rives*. He did not say *Virginia*. He did not need to.

He let the final sentence arrive at its destination, let the last word fall where it would, and sat.

He gripped the edge of his desk, knuckles whitening against the mahogany, though his face settled into a senator's composed satisfaction—the expression of a man who has said exactly what he intended.

The gallery applauded—an unusual thing, quickly suppressed by a rap of Mangum's gavel.

Wright had not moved. His hands remained folded. He was already thinking about what came next.

## Scene 3

The committee room smelled of pine resin and something older beneath it—decades of argument absorbed into the grain of the oak table, the close, faintly sour air of a space where men had said things they could not unsay. Clay stood behind a chair he had pulled from the table, both hands on the back, the wood smooth against his palms except where a previous occupant had gouged the rail with something sharp, leaving a ridge that caught under his thumbnail each time his grip shifted. The afternoon had turned cold; a draft moved through the gap beneath the window sash, and the single lamp on the table threw a restless light that made the papers before Ewing appear to shift.

Ewing sat across from him, coat buttoned to the throat, his documents arranged in orderly columns.

"The threat," Clay said, "must be delivered as I have framed it. Not softened, not qualified, not wrapped in the language of collegial concern. The President signs the bill as presented, or he signs his separation from every Whig in this Congress who has a future to protect."

"I think it proper to observe—"

"I am not interested in what you think proper to observe, Ewing. I am interested in whether you will carry the message."

A pause. The window gave onto the Capitol grounds, where the afternoon light lay flat and indifferent across the grass, and somewhere below a door swung on unoiled hinges with a rhythmic complaint that no one had thought to address.

"I will carry it," Ewing said. "Though I believe it will harden rather than persuade."

"He hardens regardless. The only question is whether he hardens with the knowledge that a veto destroys him, or hardens in ignorance of the cost." Clay's thumbnail found the gouge in the rail; he pressed it flat. "What else?"

Ewing's hands moved to his documents and stopped there, not lifting them. "I think you should know—and I tell you this as a man who serves the same interest—that Webster visited the President two days ago."

The door below swung and swung.

"On what matter?"

"The bank bill." Ewing's voice had acquired the careful flatness of a man delivering information he had held longer than was entirely honest. "He proposed to Tyler that a modified measure—one permitting states to refuse the establishment of branch offices within their borders—might resolve the constitutional difficulty. A state opt-out provision, essentially."

Clay's hands did not move from the chair back. His face did not change.

"Webster," he said.

"Yes."

"Webster, who sits in this Administration at my sufferance, who was shown the bill in my rooms, who took my broadsheet to the President as my emissary—"

"I cannot speak to his reasoning."

"I am not asking you to speak to his reasoning." The words came level and deliberate, each one placed where it would do the most work. "I am telling you what has occurred. Webster has gone to the President with a compromise that renders the pure bill unnecessary, that gives Tyler an escape, that makes my threat meaningless before it has been delivered—and he has done this without my knowledge and against my express design."

A splinter from the rough underside of the rail caught under Clay's thumbnail as he shifted his grip, driving in cleanly. He pressed his thumb against his trouser leg and held it there.

"You will carry my message to Tyler as I have written it," he said. "Unaltered. You will not tell him that Webster has counseled otherwise, because that information gives him comfort I do not intend him to have."

"Understood."

"And you will have no further communication with Webster regarding this legislation—none, Ewing, not a word, not a note, not a conversation over dinner—until I authorize it. Whatever Webster has told you, whatever he proposes to do next, you will neither encourage nor obstruct it. You will report it to me."

Ewing straightened the papers before him, a small adjustment that accomplished nothing. "The Finance Committee meets tomorrow morning."

"I know when it meets." Clay released the rail and moved toward the window, where the grounds lay quiet and the door below had finally gone still. "Accelerate the schedule. I want the bill out of committee before Webster can broker another arrangement. Whatever timeline you had, compress it."

"By how much?"

"By enough that Webster runs out of days."

Ewing gathered his documents and rose. He paused at the threshold with the particular courtesy of a subordinate who has been given an order and wishes to be seen receiving it without resentment.

"Wright speaks this afternoon," he said.

"I know."

"It may not be a comfortable afternoon."

Clay did not turn from the window. "It will be a necessary one."

## Scene 4

The afternoon light came through the western windows at a low angle, falling in long bars across the mahogany desks and the dusty air above them, and Silas Wright rose without ceremony—no clearing of the throat, no gathering of papers—simply stood, and the chamber went quiet the way it does when a man who rarely wastes words prepares to use them.

Clay sat at his desk and watched.

Wright's first sentences were procedural in their courtesy, acknowledging the chair, the distinguished gentleman from Kentucky, the gravity of the session. Then the courtesy ended.

"The question before this body," Wright said—his voice was not loud; it never was, and the chamber strained toward it the way a room strains toward a candle rather than a fire—"is not whether a national bank is desirable. Men of good faith have disagreed upon that point for fifty years, and I shall not rehearse arguments my colleagues have heard before. The question is whether the Congress of the United States possesses the constitutional authority to charter such an institution in the manner proposed—and whether, in exercising that authority, it may compel the states to receive its agents without their consent."

In the upper gallery, pens moved against paper with a dry, continuous sound.

Wright had notes, but he did not consult them. "I grant the Senator from Kentucky this much: the question has been argued. It has been argued by Hamilton and by Jefferson, by Marshall and by the Congress itself in its several deliberations upon the charters of 1791 and 1816. I do not propose to resolve what those giants left contested. I propose only to observe that the present bill contains a provision of peculiar constitutional difficulty—the establishment of branch offices in states without the consent of their legislatures—and that this provision has been opposed, on precisely these grounds, by men whose Whig credentials are beyond challenge."

Clay's pen hovered above the sheet before him. The nib did not touch the paper.

"Indeed," Wright continued, "I find myself in the unusual position of quoting to this body the recorded words of the gentleman who now occupies the executive mansion. In the session of 1833, upon the question of rechartering the Second Bank, the then-Senator from Virginia spoke as follows." Wright lifted a single sheet. He read without inflection, which made it worse. *"I have ever been of the opinion that the establishment of a national bank, with power to plant its branches in the several states without their consent, is not warranted by a fair construction of the Constitution, and is an encroachment upon the reserved rights of the states as plain as though it had been written in the instrument itself."*

The words fell into the chamber and did not echo. They simply remained.

Clay looked to the Whig benches. Rives had not moved, but the angle of his shoulders had shifted—a small thing, a degree or two—and his hands, which had been flat upon his desk, were now folded. Two rows behind him, Morehead of North Carolina had stopped writing entirely and was staring at the middle distance with an expression that had no name but was not comfortable.

Wright set the sheet down. "I do not cite this passage as a partisan instrument. I cite it because it represents a constitutional position long held, clearly articulated, and not susceptible to revision by the pressures of a party calendar. The President has not arrived at his objections to this legislation in the past fortnight. He has held them for eight years, and he has stated them in this very chamber. This body would do well to consider whether a measure so constituted can command the executive assent that its passage requires—and whether the states whose reserved rights are implicated will acquiesce in their abridgement."

He sat.

Clay was on his feet before Mangum found his voice.

"Mr. President—" The western light struck him full in the face; he raised one hand against it, a sharp, involuntary gesture, and lowered it as the angle shifted. "I rise on a point of order. The Senator from New York has occupied the floor with an extended excursion into historical citation, the relevance of which to the pending calendar motion I submit is not established."

Mangum hesitated—one beat, two—and Wright said, quietly, "I yield to the chair's ruling on relevance."

The hesitation was itself a ruling. Mangum said: "The chair finds the Senator's remarks germane to the general question before the body. The gentleman from Kentucky may proceed."

Clay released the edge of his desk. He spoke.

"My friend from New York is a man of considerable constitutional learning, and I should be the last to diminish what he has offered. But I would remind this chamber—and through this chamber, the nation—that the people of this republic, in the election of 1840, rendered a judgment upon precisely these questions. They did not consult the debates of 1833. They consulted their circumstances, their distress, and their hope, and they returned a Whig majority to this Congress and a Whig executive to the mansion at the end of Pennsylvania Avenue." He paused. His throat had gone dry—the long afternoon, the close air, the smell of tallow from the lamps being lit now in the upper gallery. He did not reach for the glass on his desk. He swallowed and continued, his voice rougher for it, which was perhaps better. "The Senator quotes a speech of eight years past as though it were Holy Writ. But constitutions are not administered by speeches. They are administered by governments. And this government was elected upon a platform that includes, as its central pillar, the restoration of sound national finance. To treat the President's private scruples—however sincerely held, however eloquently expressed in other seasons—as a constitutional bar to legislation that the people have mandated is to substitute one man's opinion for the sovereign will of the republic."

He sat. The gallery murmured, and Mangum's gavel came down once.

The correspondents were writing.

Clay looked at the sheet before him—the one on which the nib had never touched the page. He looked at Rives, who was studying the middle distance with the elaborate attention of a man deciding something he had perhaps already decided. He looked at Wright, who was not looking at him at all but was speaking quietly to the senator beside him—Buchanan, of Pennsylvania—and Buchanan was nodding.

He had the votes—or he had believed he had them, this morning, before Rives and his letter, before Wright and his eight-year-old quotation, before the afternoon had arranged itself into this configuration that was not what he had built.

He could accelerate: force the bill to the floor before the opposition could consolidate, before Rives could be further worked upon, before Webster could broker another private arrangement in some anteroom Clay had not thought to watch. The risk was a vote on contested ground with the southern Whigs uncertain—not merely Rives but Morehead, and perhaps one or two others whose names he could not now set down with confidence.

He could seek compromise: allow the opt-out provision Webster had proposed, give Tyler his escape, preserve the majority. The cost was the principle he had refused to surrender all day—the principle that Tyler would accept this bill entire, or stand condemned before his party and the nation as a man who had accepted the presidency under false colors.

Neither path was clean. Both had been clean this morning.

The session would adjourn within the hour. The correspondents would file before dark. By morning, every paper in Washington would carry Wright's quotation alongside Clay's reply, and editors from Boston to Charleston would weigh them against each other and render their verdicts, and in the Executive Mansion at the far end of Pennsylvania Avenue, a man who had spoken those words in this very chamber would read them again in print. He would read them and understand that the Democrats had given him his constitutional armor, had fitted it to his measure, had buckled the straps—and that it fit him as though it had been made for him, because it had, because he had made it himself, eight years ago, in this room, in this chair, speaking to men who were now dead or gone or sitting opposite with folded hands.

The armor fit. That was the fact Clay would carry out of this chamber.

He gathered his papers. The session would reconvene at ten o'clock tomorrow—and before ten o'clock, Webster would have to be found, and the Finance Committee schedule would have to be redrawn, and Rives would need to hear from someone he trusted more than Clay that his objections had been heard and would cost him nothing. Three conversations, each of which required a different instrument, none of which could be delegated to Ewing, who had already shown today that his discretion had a ceiling. The night was not long enough, and the morning would arrive regardless, and Wright's quotation would be in every correspondent's dispatch before Clay had reached his lodgings.

He rose. His coat smelled of the chamber—tallow and wool and the particular staleness of argument breathed and rebreathed beneath a low stone vault. He did not remove it. He walked toward the door through which, in eleven hours, he would have to return and begin again.
