# Chapter Seven: Sixty Million Francs

## I.

The chairs scraped against the floor and the sound carried too clearly in that high-ceilinged room, lasting a beat longer than it should. Barbé-Marbois remained standing at the window, his back to the Americans, and let them settle.

Behind him, in the grey April light, the map on the east wall showed its deficiencies plainly: the coastlines drawn with some confidence, the interior resolved into a cartographer's hesitation, broad unmarked reaches where the continent had simply declined to be known. He had studied that map for three weeks. It had told him nothing he could sell.

He had not slept well. The taste of the morning's coffee—bitter, reheated, taken standing in his anteroom at six o'clock—still coated his tongue, and the April cold had not left his feet despite two hours indoors.

Monroe set his leather portfolio on the table and clasped his hands over it. He was leaner than Livingston had described, with the deliberate composure of rehearsal about him. Livingston sat to his left, already watching—the angle of the body, the set of the jaw, the small adjustments that precede a stroke.

'Citizen Minister,' Monroe began, in his plainspoken French, 'we are grateful for the occasion to place before you, in formal terms, the position of the United States.'

Barbé-Marbois turned from the window. He inclined his head but said nothing, and crossed to the table with the unhurried stride of someone who intends to control the tempo of what follows. He did not sit.

Monroe opened the portfolio. The document he withdrew was four pages in close hand, sealed and now broken, with the heading of the American special mission in Monroe's own careful script. He read the first paragraph aloud—his authority as special envoy, superseding the resident minister's ordinary discretion—and slid the memorandum across the table with the flat certainty of one who believes the document speaks for itself.

'Fifty million francs,' Monroe said, 'for Louisiana in its entirety, inclusive of all American claims against France. This is the position of the United States government, as authorised by the President and the Secretary of State.'

Livingston's hands were flat on the table. He did not look at Monroe.

Barbé-Marbois took up the memorandum. He read each page without expression, without haste, his eyes moving down the columns of argument at the measured pace of one who has read ten thousand documents and intends to read ten thousand more. A draught found the window frame—the casements in this building had never fitted correctly, and the April wind knew every gap—and one of the candles on the sideboard guttered and nearly failed. The flame recovered.

When he had finished the last page, he set Monroe's memorandum down with the deliberate flatness of a card he did not intend to pick up again.

'Fifty million,' he said. He let the number sit in the room. Then he reached inside his coat.

The document he produced was a single sheet, the handwriting dense and fast, the seal at its foot broken and reaffixed in red wax. He unfolded it on the table and read aloud—his voice even, without inflection, each word placed as though it were a figure in a ledger that would not balance if he hurried.

The First Consul's instruction: that the Minister of the Treasury was empowered to conclude the sale, and that no figure below sixty million francs for the territory itself would be acceptable to the French Republic, and that this figure was not a preliminary position but a ministerial floor beneath which no representative of France was authorised to treat.

He folded the sheet and set it before him.

Monroe's jaw tightened. 'Sixty million is beyond our instructions, Citizen Minister. You must know this.'

'I know what the First Consul requires.' Barbé-Marbois placed both hands flat on the table and leaned forward, closing the distance between them by six inches. 'And I know that the British squadron at Martinique will not wait for your Congress to deliberate. If this territory passes to England—and it will pass, Mr. Monroe, by September if not by June—your western settlements will find themselves tenants of the Crown for the second time in thirty years. That is not a diplomatic observation. It is a naval fact.'

The room held the sound of the street outside—a wagon, a shout, the city proceeding without interest in what had just occurred at this table.

Monroe's memorandum lay where Barbé-Marbois had placed it. No one moved to retrieve it.

'We will need to confer,' Monroe said.

'You will need to calculate.' Barbé-Marbois gathered the written instruction and returned it to his coat. 'I shall expect your answer by Thursday. After Thursday, I cannot guarantee the First Consul's patience, and I will not attempt to.'

## II.

He had sent a junior functionary to detain Monroe with some question about credentials and protocol—the sort of procedural courtesy that could consume an hour without appearing to consume anything at all. He had perhaps forty minutes.

The office was close, the coal smoke worked into the plaster and the leather spines of forty years of ledgers. Barbé-Marbois stood at the writing table, not behind it but beside it, arranging the two ledgers he had prepared so that their spines faced the door. A staging. He was aware of it as such.

Livingston entered without knocking and crossed to the annotated map, his back to the room. He had not asked permission.

'Your interior distances,' he said, without turning, 'are estimates.'

'All distances in that country are estimates.' Barbé-Marbois opened the first ledger—the one he had ruled in his own hand three days ago—and set it at the table's edge. 'Sit, Mr. Livingston. I have figures that will interest you more than the cartographer's guesses.'

The American settled into the chair across the writing table with the ease of someone who intended to stay. The surface between them was buried—bond schedules, correspondence from the banking houses of Hope and Baring, a running calculation in the margin of a page that had been crossed out twice and restarted. Livingston reached across without ceremony and opened the uppermost ledger to the debt-service column.

He read the annual interest figure aloud. His voice was flat, each syllable given equal weight, as though reciting a sentence already handed down.

'No treaty committing the republic to service of this magnitude can be ratified without prior Congressional authorisation. That is not my objection, Citizen Minister. That is the Constitution.'

Barbé-Marbois did not answer immediately. He was thinking of the winter of 1795, when he had sat in a room not unlike this one in Philadelphia, reviewing the accounts of a republic that could not feed its own army. He had learned something then about the distance between constitutional principle and fiscal necessity—a distance that could be measured in bushels of grain, in the hollow sound of an empty commissary wagon on a frozen road.

He drew the second ledger from the adjacent shelf—thinner, its cover worn at the corner from handling—and placed it open to a single page, turning it so the figures faced across the table.

'The annual revenue lost through the suspension of the right of deposit at New Orleans. Calculated at the rates your own Treasury reported to Congress in December last.'

Livingston looked at the second column. His right hand moved to the side of his head—the deaf man's habit, the unconscious adjustment—and then he caught himself and set the hand flat on the table.

'You are asking me to perform a subtraction.'

'I am providing the columns. The arithmetic is yours.'

Barbé-Marbois picked up his quill to note a correction in the margin and his right hand cramped around the shaft with a sudden, seizing tightness—the knuckle of the index finger white, then slowly pink again. He set the quill down and flexed his fingers, four slow extensions. The cost of five days at this table, written in the joints. He found his place from the line above and made the notation.

If the Americans refused sixty million, the written authorisation from the Tuileries would not be revised downward. It would be withdrawn entirely—Barbé-Marbois had seen the look in those eyes at the palace, the cold calculation of a commander who would rather burn a house than sell it cheaply. And if the offer was withdrawn, he himself would have failed in the only commission that mattered. The Treasury was empty. Saint-Domingue had consumed forty thousand men and two hundred million livres. There would be no second buyer.

A coal shifted in the grate. A brief rush of heat crossed the left side of his face and was gone.

Livingston ran his finger down the deposit revenue column, then across to the bond service figure, and back again. His lips did not move.

He looked at the map on the wall.

'What,' he said, after a moment, 'are the eastern boundaries of Louisiana?'

Barbé-Marbois set his pen down and closed the ledger with a sound like a door shutting.

The question sat between them on the table, beside the two open volumes, and neither man reached for it.

## III.

The conference room had not been heated. By four o'clock the cold had established itself with quiet permanence, and Barbé-Marbois kept his coat on, his fingers stiff around the document case he carried under his arm. The three draft treaties lay on the cleared table, their pages squared, their seals unaffixed.

Monroe arrived before Livingston—itself a statement. He stood at the window, his back to the room, looking out at the greying courtyard. When he turned, his face carried the composure of one who has decided, in advance, precisely what he intends to say and has no intention of saying anything else.

Livingston followed two minutes later, said nothing, and took the chair furthest from the door.

'I must raise a matter before we proceed to any question of signature.' Monroe did not sit. 'The Minister of Foreign Affairs has communicated to me, informally, his opinion that the eastern boundary of Louisiana does not comprehend West Florida—that the cession of 1800 did not include it, and that the present instrument cannot convey what France does not hold.'

He produced a single folded sheet from his coat. 'I require a written clarification on this point before any signature is affixed.'

The cold was sharp enough that Barbé-Marbois could see, faintly, the condensation of Monroe's breath.

He had known the question was coming. Talleyrand's note had been two days in transit before Barbé-Marbois learned of it—sent the morning after the penultimate session, precisely timed to arrive at the moment of maximum disruption. If the boundary question forced a postponement, the Thursday deadline would expire. The offer would die. Talleyrand would have killed the transaction without ever appearing to oppose it. The man's instinct for the exact pressure point was, in its way, admirable.

Barbé-Marbois set his document case on the table and opened it. He did not hurry.

'Talleyrand is a man of considerable learning.' He drew out the Treaty of San Ildefonso—not a copy, the original French text, obtained from the archive through channels that had required some delicacy—and opened it to the relevant article.

He read aloud, his voice flat, each clause given its weight and no more:

*'His Catholic Majesty promises and engages on his part to retrocede to the French Republic, six months after the full and entire execution of the conditions and stipulations herein relative to His Royal Highness the Duke of Parma, the Colony or Province of Louisiana, with the same extent that it now has in the hands of Spain, and that it had when France possessed it; and such as it should be after the treaties subsequently entered into between Spain and other States.'*

He stopped. He did not close the document. He let the clause sit in the cold air.

*With the same extent that it now has in the hands of Spain.*

Livingston's eyes moved to Monroe.

'You will observe,' Barbé-Marbois said, 'that the founding instrument specifies no eastern boundary. No western. No northern. It transfers Louisiana as Spain held it, and as France held it before Spain.' He turned the page toward Monroe. 'If he possesses a clarification that this text does not itself contain, I confess I should be grateful to see it.'

Monroe's composure did not break. But he set Talleyrand's sheet down on the table without unfolding it.

The silence lasted perhaps four seconds. The wind moved through the courtyard outside, and the tall windows shuddered once in their frames—a dry, wooden sound, quickly gone.

'Then the boundary,' Monroe said at last, 'is as France understood it.'

'The boundary is as the treaty states it. And the treaty, Mr. Monroe, will state it no differently on Friday than it does today—but on Friday, the authorisation may no longer obtain.' He closed the San Ildefonso text and returned it to the case. 'Shall we proceed?'

Monroe looked at Livingston. Livingston gave a single, almost imperceptible nod.

'We shall proceed,' Monroe said.

The claims settlement required another hour. Barbé-Marbois held at twenty million francs for the American claims, refused Monroe's attempt to reduce it to fifteen, and conceded only that the claims commission would sit in Washington rather than Paris—a jurisdictional point that cost France nothing and gave Monroe something to report. The total stood at eighty million francs: sixty for the territory, twenty for the claims. The bond arrangements with Hope and Baring were confirmed in a side memorandum that Livingston, after reading it twice, pronounced acceptable.

When it was done—when the figures were agreed and the drafting instructions given to the secretary—Barbé-Marbois gathered his papers and stood.

'The fair copies will be ready by tomorrow evening,' he said. 'We sign on the second of May.'

He did not wait for their assent. He walked to the door, and only in the corridor, alone, did he permit himself to exhale—a long, controlled breath that carried the taste of coal smoke and cold air and the faint metallic residue of ink on his fingers.

## IV.

The night of the first of May. The room was too warm—he had kept the fire high to dry the ink on his annotations, and now the heat pressed against his face like a hand. Barbé-Marbois removed his coat and hung it on the back of his chair, rolled his shirtsleeves to the elbow, and bent again over the three treaty copies.

He had been through each document twice. The bond schedule in the English text matched the French. The claims figure—twenty millions—was carried correctly through both instruments. The principal articles bore the dates he had specified, the seals were properly affixed at the lower margin, and the blanks awaiting signature were accurately placed.

He found no error. He went through them a third time.

He set the last page down and pressed the heels of his hands against his closed eyelids. Five days of close reading had left his eyes raw, the lines of figures blurring when he held them too long, and he had to wait—hands over his face, the fire loud behind him, the smell of heated paper and tallow sharp in his nostrils—until the blurring cleared.

When he lifted his hands, the room returned: the map on the wall, the stacked volumes, the three documents with their unaffixed seals, and on the corner of the desk, the Tuileries authorisation, its edges softened from handling.

He looked at the map. The coastlines were tolerably accurate. The river was well-drawn. But the interior—the vast brown interior, reaching north and west past the frame into a space the cartographer had declined to invent—was nothing. A colour. A direction. A territory larger than France, larger than the whole of the Rhine's reach from source to sea, and neither he nor the Americans nor any official of any government in this building had seen more of it than its southernmost margin.

He knew what he was transferring. He knew what he did not know about it.

France did not, in the strictest construction, possess what the treaty proposed to alienate. Spain had transferred Louisiana under conditions that prohibited exactly this resale. The Americans could not, under any reading of their Constitution that satisfied a careful jurist, commit to this level of debt without prior Congressional sanction. The boundaries were no more defined in the instrument he had concluded than they had been in the Treaty of San Ildefonso three years prior—which was to say they were not defined at all.

He knew these things. They did not constitute errors in the document before him. They were its conditions—the native climate in which it had been conceived and in which it would be ratified or repudiated.

He picked up his pen, uncapped the inkwell, and wrote the date on the cover sheet of the first copy.

*2 Mai 1803.*

He moved to the second copy. Then the third.

He aligned the three documents on the desk—edges flush, the seals centred—and then, because the alignment was not perfect, adjusted them once more, a quarter-inch, and left them.

Livingston and Monroe would arrive at nine o'clock. In eleven hours, the transaction would be complete or it would not. If Livingston's hand faltered, if Monroe raised a new objection, if a courier arrived from the Tuileries with a countermanding order—any of these would undo three weeks of work and leave the Treasury precisely where it had been in March: empty, with an army to feed and a war to finance.

He reached for his coat. The opening sentence of his report to the First Consul had not yet come to him. He tried three formulations and abandoned each. The words that presented themselves were too large for the occasion, or too small, and he could not find the register that lay between.

He blew out the candle and sat in the dark, listening to the fire die.

## V. — *2 May 1803*

Barbé-Marbois arrived at the Ministry at seven o'clock and did not sit down.

The conference room had been prepared: the table cleared and polished, three inkstands set at measured intervals, the copies of the treaty arranged in a column at the centre. A secretary stood near the door. Two witnesses occupied chairs against the wall—functionaries from the Treasury's lower offices, men whose names he had not retained. The windows were open. The spring air came in carrying the smell of the street—horse dung and fresh bread from the bakery on the corner, the morning smell of Paris, indifferent to what was being accomplished inside.

From the Foreign Ministry: silence. No messenger, no attaché, no note of felicitation or obstruction. Talleyrand's absence was complete—the final mark of someone who would not dignify with his presence a transaction he had failed to prevent.

Barbé-Marbois stood beside the table. He checked the alignment of the inkstands. He opened the first treaty copy to the signature page and verified the blanks. He closed it. He opened it again. The secretary watched him from the door with the careful blankness of a subordinate who knows better than to speak.

At five minutes past nine, Livingston and Monroe arrived together—the two having decided, somewhere between their respective lodgings, that a unified entrance was worth the coordination it required. Livingston's colour was better than it had been at any point in the preceding week. Monroe carried his coat over one arm, as though the spring morning had surprised him with its warmth.

Barbé-Marbois placed the first copy before Livingston without preamble. He uncapped the inkstand himself, set the pen beside the document, and stepped back.

The room was very quiet. Through the open windows came the sound of the street—a hawker's cry, the clatter of hooves on stone, the low murmur of a city that had survived five revolutions and a terror and was now, on this Tuesday morning, buying bread. The smell of fresh ink rose from the uncapped stand, sharp and faintly astringent, mixing with the spring air.

Livingston looked at the pen.

He picked it up. He dipped it. He signed.

The scratch of the nib was the only sound. Then it stopped, and the silence that followed was not empty but full—the silence of a thing accomplished that cannot be taken back.

Monroe signed beneath.

Barbé-Marbois took the pen from Monroe's hand. The shaft was warm from two men's grip. He dipped it, and signed his own name beneath both of theirs with a steady hand.

He blotted it. He did not look up.

The second copy. The third.

When the last signature was dry, Livingston rose from his chair and stood facing the window—not the map, but the open window, the street, the city beyond.

'We have lived long enough,' he said quietly, 'to give this day to our country. I

't is the noblest work of our lives.'

Barbé-Marbois said nothing. He gathered the courier's copy and handed it to the secretary, who received it with both hands and withdrew.

*The noblest work.* He looked at the two remaining copies on the table—one for France, one for the republic across the sea—and considered what the First Consul would require: figures, not nobility. Confirmation that the sixty millions were secured, the bonds arranged, the English forestalled. The territory itself had never interested him. He had cared only what it cost to hold and what it would fetch in the selling.

Livingston remained at the window, still as a man who has arrived somewhere he spent years travelling toward and finds the arrival unexpectedly silent. Barbé-Marbois recognised it. He had felt something adjacent himself, three weeks prior, walking out of the Tuileries with the written authorisation folded in his coat—the weight of it not power but obligation.

He capped the inkstand. He squared the French copy against the table's edge.

Outside, the hawker's cry rose again—*pain frais, pain frais*—and dissolved into the general noise of the morning.

What could not wait—what pressed against him now with the insistence of a debt come due—was the letter he owed to Hope and Baring. The bond terms required confirmation; the transfer of funds from Amsterdam to Paris to the military treasury depended upon it, and the courier left at noon. He had perhaps four hours. The letter was not begun, and the first sentence of that letter would bind the banking houses to a schedule that no subsequent instruction could easily revise.

He had crossed to the writing desk in the corner and drawn a fresh sheet from the case when the door opened without a knock.

The secretary stood in the frame, his colour altered, a sealed dispatch held before him with both hands as though the wax itself were hot.

'Citizen Minister.' The man's voice was careful in the way of a man delivering news he does not wish to have carried. 'From the Tuileries. The courier rode through the night. They say the First Consul requires your attendance before the noon hour.'

Barbé-Marbois looked at the unsigned letter, the blank sheet, the clock on the mantel. Then he looked at the dispatch.

'Set it here,' he said. 'And send for my carriage.'
