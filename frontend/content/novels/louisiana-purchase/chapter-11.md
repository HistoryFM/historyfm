# Chapter 11: The Flags at New Orleans

## I.

The Mississippi came to him first as smell—not the clean mineral scent of rivers he had known in New York, but something older and more vegetal, a broad brown exhalation of soil carried from a thousand miles upriver, of rotting cane and river mud and the sweetish foulness of a levee where flatboats had been mooring since before the republic existed. Livingston breathed it in as the vessel's gangplank was run out and found nothing in it that resembled triumph.

The levee was crowded at this hour, though the December light had barely cleared the rooftops of the Cabildo. Dock workers moved along the wharf with the indifference of commerce—free men of colour alongside slaves alongside Irish and Kentuckians, their breath clouding in the cold air, their attention directed anywhere but at the party of Americans disembarking with their trunks and their documents and their expressions of bewildered possession.

Monroe was at the rail.

He had managed, somehow, in the press of the company, to arrive at the gangplank first—a fact so precisely accidental in its appearance, and so precisely arranged in its execution, that Livingston recognised it before his right foot touched Louisiana soil. Monroe's aides had worked the geometry of the deck in the final minutes before landing, and Monroe occupied the point of the triangle.

Livingston did not wait. He stepped forward, caught Monroe's elbow, and spoke low enough that only Monroe would hear.

'The commission names us jointly, Mr. Monroe. We shall descend together, or I shall descend first. Choose.'

Monroe's jaw tightened. A beat of silence in which the gangplank creaked under the weight of a trunk being carried down by two sailors. Then Monroe inclined his head—a fraction, no more—and shifted half a pace to the left.

They descended abreast. The gangplank was not wide enough for it; Livingston's shoulder pressed the rope rail, and the hemp bit through his coat sleeve. He did not adjust.

The French official was perhaps thirty-five, his coat good but not brilliant, his bearing precise and unhurried—a man who had his orders and would discharge them without embellishment. He came forward through the thin crowd and stopped before Monroe.

'Monsieur Monroe.' He inclined his head. 'Envoyé extraordinaire des États-Unis. Monsieur le Préfet Laussat vous présente ses compliments et vous souhaite la bienvenue en Louisiane au nom de la République française.' A pause—the fraction of a second that separates a greeting from an afterthought. 'Et Monsieur Livingston, naturellement.'

*Naturellement.* The word fell with the lightness of something dropped from a height sufficient to break it.

Livingston stepped forward and placed himself between Monroe and the official.

'Monsieur.' His French carried the accent of study rather than birth, but it carried also the precision of one who knew which words he needed. 'Je dois vous signaler une distinction que votre préfet voudra sans doute consigner avec exactitude dans le procès-verbal. Ma commission de ministre plénipotentiaire précède celle de Monsieur Monroe de près de deux ans. C'est moi qui ai conclu le traité dont nous venons aujourd'hui accomplir les effets. Le texte porte ma signature en premier.'

The official's face performed a small recalibration. He had not been given instructions for this.

'Certainement, Monsieur Livingston. Je le ferai noter.'

Not an apology. Barely an acknowledgment. But it was on the record now, and Livingston had made it so before the walk to the Cabildo had begun. If Laussat's report to Paris listed Monroe first, the correction would follow it like a shadow—and Livingston knew from two years in that city how carefully such shadows were read.

He fell into step beside Monroe, and the levee crowd closed around the American party, and the river smell came with them all the way to the square.

## II.

The sala capitular held sound the way a stone church holds cold: pervasively, without preference. Laussat's address had been going for some minutes before Livingston accepted that he was following perhaps two words in five.

He stood at the American side of the central table, Monroe at his right hand, and fixed his attention on Laussat's face with the concentration he had developed across two years in Parisian drawing rooms—reading the musculature around the mouth, the tightening at the eyes, the slight lift of the chin that preceded a rhetorical period. The candles banked along the walls gave the plastered room a yellow, subaqueous quality. The air was close with the smell of tallow and too many bodies in wool, and beneath it the faint sweetness of beeswax from the polished table where the documents lay.

*Ce territoire,* Laussat was saying—Livingston caught the noun, lost the verb, recovered on *les lois françaises*—*les droits*—then a long subordinate construction in which Laussat's voice dropped and the assembled Creoles seemed collectively to draw breath.

The prefect was not addressing the Americans. This required no translation. Laussat faced the gathered French Creole population with the bearing of a eulogist—measured, personal, directed entirely at the bereaved. The legal instruments on the table, the documents Monroe's secretary had arranged, the American soldiers visible through the tall windows—all peripheral. Laussat spoke to the people who would remain when the flags changed.

Livingston leaned toward Monroe's secretary, Clarke, and murmured: 'The substance. Give me the substance as he speaks.'

Clarke hesitated—a glance at Monroe, who gave no sign—then bent close to Livingston's good ear and began to translate in fragments. *He releases them from their oath to France... commends them to the justice of the new sovereign... the rights of property and religion to be preserved...*

So it was a farewell. Laussat was severing them, and the room knew it.

Livingston straightened. Somewhere to his left, a woman's breath caught audibly—a sound swallowed almost before it was made. He did not turn, but he marked it. He marked, too, the quality of the silence that followed: not the held breath of ceremony, but the compressed silence of people enduring what they had not chosen.

Monroe leaned toward him. 'He promises them their rights will be honoured. Under our governance.' The murmur was pitched for Livingston alone, but the ease of comprehension in it was not—it was the ease of someone to whom every syllable had arrived intact. 'You will wish to address that in your remarks.'

'I have prepared remarks, Mr. Monroe.' A pause of one beat. 'They address what requires addressing.'

When the American commission was called—he caught *commissaires américains* and took it as his cue—Livingston stepped forward to the table.

The quill was charged. He signed where the document indicated, with a deliberateness he had rehearsed. Then he held the quill for a moment after signing, the nib resting in the curve of his final letter, the paper slightly depressed beneath it. He was aware, in this interval, that the room was watching his hand and not his face, and that the hand was steady, and that steadiness was the only language this room would trust from him today.

He placed the quill in its rest.

He delivered his prepared address in French—acknowledging the character of the territory, the language and laws that had shaped it, the rights and customs of its inhabitants which the government he represented would receive with the solemnity they deserved and maintain with the fidelity owed to a people now joined to the republic.

The assembled witnesses heard him out. The faces that had not softened since Laussat began did not soften now. He had expected this. He had not expected how precisely it would feel to speak accurate words into a room that did not believe them—not because the words were false, but because the speaker had no power to guarantee their truth, and the room knew it, and he knew it, and the candle wax pooled silently in its bracket behind him, and the ceremony continued.

## III.

The tricolor came down.

Not in the silence of a crowd holding its breath—something colder than that. The ordinary silence of people who have decided not to perform. The French Creoles in their good wool and their mantillas stood without speaking, without weeping openly, without any demonstrative grief that might have served as release. They watched. A city watching itself be handed over, the way a patient watches a surgeon's hands and does not wish to see what they are doing.

The flagpole rope ran through its pulleys with a low, fibrous complaint. The bass drum struck—Livingston felt it through the soles of his boots before he heard it—and then the band was playing, and the tricolor was descending, and Laussat, twenty feet away, pressed his lips together and held them there.

Livingston reached into his coat and drew out the folded text of the transfer instrument—his own copy, the one he had carried from Paris. He opened it. He did not need to read it; he knew every clause. But he held it before him as the flag descended, because he wanted the assembled crowd to see that the American commissioner held the document in his hands at the moment of transfer, that this was an act of law and not of force, that the paper mattered.

The tricolor cleared the pole. The rope went slack.

In that interval—the dead seconds between one sovereignty and the next, the flag down and no flag yet ascending—Livingston looked at the crowd and the crowd looked back, and what he saw was not grief and not anger and not acceptance. It was assessment. These were people calculating what they had lost and what they might yet keep, and the calculation was not sentimental. A merchant near the front watched the American officers with the eyes of a man pricing new partners. A woman in a dark pelisse—well-dressed, her hat not bought cheaply—watched the bare flagpole with an expression that contained, he thought, the entire history of a city that had changed hands before and would remember this change as it remembered the others: with precision and without forgiveness.

Then something shifted in him—not revelation, not shock, but the particular cold of a thing understood too late to be useful. He had written, in April, that Louisiana was a commercial corridor—the language of cartography and navigation rights, of arpents and the breadth of navigable water. *The territory is, in its present condition, a geographic necessity and nothing more.* He had believed this, or had chosen to believe it, which was not the same thing but had served the same purpose. Standing now in the Place d'Armes with the rope slack and the pole bare, he understood that the sentence was not wrong so much as it was incomplete in a way that no subsequent amendment could repair, because the incompleteness was not accidental—it was the incompleteness of a man who had needed the simplification to get the thing done.

The American flag began its ascent.

Around him, the officers applauded. He heard it as rhythmic percussion, distant and slightly irregular. His own hands remained occupied—the document still open before him, the pages catching the river wind. He folded it carefully and returned it to his coat.

The woman in the dark pelisse turned away. She did not hurry. She simply turned, as one turns from a completed transaction, and walked toward the rue de Chartres, and Livingston watched her go and understood—with the clarity that comes not from thinking but from ceasing to avoid a thought—that the treaty contained no provision for what was to become of her. Of any of them. The instrument transferred title. Title was what he had concluded. He had been very precise about it.

Somewhere to his left a child said something in French. He could not make out the words.

The flag held the wind above the square, and the bare iron hooks on the Cabildo's façade, where the tricolor bunting had hung that morning, caught the grey light like small wounds in the plaster.

## IV.

Étienne de Boré was near the window, his coffee cup held at an angle that suggested he had forgotten it. Outside, through the watered glass, the grey afternoon was thickening toward dusk. The reception room smelled of chicory and candle smoke and the damp wool of thirty coats that had stood too long in the December air.

Livingston crossed to him directly. The Wilkinson aide near the punch bowl was holding forth about alluvial soil and the price of sugar in Baltimore; two Spanish officers occupied the far wall like men waiting for a coach that would not come. He had no time for either.

'Monsieur de Boré.' He spoke in French. 'I shall be direct with you. The administration in Washington wishes to govern this territory in a manner that respects its inhabitants—their customs, their legal traditions. But I have stood in that square today and observed a city that does not wish to be governed by us at all. I require your candour, not your courtesy.'

De Boré turned. He was lean, past sixty, his face carrying the weathering of the Louisiana climate and something harder beneath—a watchfulness worn into the bone by Spanish governance, French restoration, and now this. He regarded Livingston for a moment with eyes that gave nothing.

'You are direct, Monsieur le Ministre. It is refreshing.' He sipped his coffee. 'Very well. The people of this city have lived under the governance of Spain. Before that, of France. They will live under the governance of your republic.' He set the cup on the sill. 'As they have always accepted what they could not refuse.'

The word *refuse* dropped into the space between them and lay there.

Livingston pressed. 'I put it to you as a question of practical administration, not of sentiment. Property disputes will arise. Title questions from the Spanish period remain unresolved. The Code Noir governs the condition of the enslaved population under French and Spanish law—your American commissioner has received no instructions regarding its continuation or its replacement.' He held de Boré's gaze. 'When a planter of this territory is summoned before an American court, will he come? And when he comes, under what law does he expect to be judged?'

De Boré looked out at the street. A ladder was propped against the house opposite; someone had begun removing the iron hooks where bunting had hung. The work had stopped. The ladder stood empty.

'The courts of Spain served us adequately,' de Boré said. 'The courts of France, in the brief tenure we enjoyed, gave us every reason to expect the same.' He paused—not the pause of a man composing his thought, but of one who has held it long enough that releasing it requires deliberation. 'But you raise the question of the Code Noir, and that is a different matter. The Americans of Kentucky and Tennessee will bring their own customs regarding the enslaved. Our customs are not theirs. Whether your courts will reconcile the two—' He lifted one hand, palm upward, and left it there a moment before lowering it. 'That, Monsieur, is not a question I am able to answer. It is a question your treaty did not ask.'

Livingston opened his mouth.

De Boré had already retrieved his cup and turned to greet a Creole merchant approaching from the left, and the conversation was over with the swiftness of a door closed by someone who owns the house.

He remained at the window. The coffee he had accepted upon arrival sat on the sill, untouched and cooling. He picked it up and drank. It was sweet with chicory, and beneath the sweetness a bitterness that did not yield—the grounds had settled and risen again, and he tasted them on his tongue, gritty and dark, as he set the cup back down.

The ladder across the street still stood empty. No one had returned to finish the work.

## V.

The lamp had burned low enough that he could see the char on the wick. He worked the penknife with stiff fingers, his knuckles aching from the December chill that rose off the courtyard flagstones and found every gap in the shuttered window. Below, orange trees stood in the darkness, their fruit small and hard, barely visible. He had not noticed them until now. The courtyard smelled of wet stone and, faintly, of the river—that same vegetable rot he had breathed on the levee that morning, carried inland on the night air as though the Mississippi intended to remind him of the distance he had not traveled.

He set the penknife down. The lamp steadied.

The blank page had been before him for twenty minutes. Outside, a fiddle was playing somewhere toward the rue Royale—a quick tune in a minor key, its phrases cut off and resumed as if the player were working out an argument with an absent opponent. A night watchman's call answered it from a distance, the words indistinct, the cadence familiar in the way that human voices calling across dark streets are always familiar regardless of their language.

He dipped the quill and wrote: *The population of New Orleans received the transfer of sovereignty with expressions of general satisfaction, and the ceremony of the twentieth December was conducted with a dignity becoming the occasion.*

He read it back. Then he drew a line through it—not with anger, but with a slow, considered pressure—and set the quill in its rest and did not look up from the page for a time.

He knew what Madison required. He had written dispatches of this kind for two years: the optimistic summary, the reassuring subordinate clause, the careful subordination of what was actually seen to what the administration needed reported. It was not dishonesty, precisely. It was the grammar of the position—the institutional demand that a commissioner's account serve the policy it accompanied, that the truth be legible only to those who already knew it and could absorb it without alarm.

Monroe's name appeared at the top of the commission's official correspondence. He had noticed it when he set out the paper. He had said nothing about it and would say nothing, and this too was a form of dispatch—the kind that wrote itself in silence and was never crossed out.

He began again.

*The population received the transfer with the composure of a people long accustomed to the changes of sovereignty.*

He stopped. The quill rested in his fingers. It was true—the most precisely true sentence he had written since leaving Paris. It would reach Madison's desk and be read alongside Federalist petitions already in circulation, alongside whatever Pickering's allies could make of the constitutional question. It would be read, too, alongside the first dispatches from the western territories—fragmentary reports that the enslaved population of Louisiana, hearing of the transfer, had begun to move in ways the territorial admi

nistrators could not yet name, ways that no treaty provision addressed because no treaty provision had been written with those movements in mind.

If he wrote the truth, it would arm the Federalists. If he wrote the comfortable lie, it would leave the administration unprepared for a territory that was not the empty corridor he had once described but a living city with its own laws, its own language, its own careful and unforgiving memory.

He added a second sentence: *It is the composure of those who have endured much and have learned, through long practice, to distinguish between what may be changed and what must be survived.*

He read it once. Madison would understand. Madison would also understand what it cost to write it.

He continued—the dates, the formalities, the catalogue of officials present, the procedural record. The lamp guttered and he trimmed the wick, the penknife steadier now, a thin curl of smoke rising and flattening against the shuttered louvers.

The knock came without warning—not three strokes but four, the wood jumping in its frame, and before he had risen from the chair a second set followed, harder, as though the first had not been believed.

The voice outside was a young man's voice, or had been once: the French arrived first, bitten short, then the English overtaking it—*Monsieur le Commissaire*—there was a party on the levee, a flatboat out of Natchez, seven men visible by the torchlight and the count uncertain beyond that, in the dark where the river smell was strongest, and they were not petitioning. The speaker corrected himself. They were not requesting. They had come down fifteen hundred miles of river to put a question to someone with the standing to answer it, and the question was whether the new administration intended to honour the right of deposit or whether the flag now flying above the Cabildo was, in its practical operation upon a cargo of corn and salt pork and dressed hides, indistinguishable from the flag it had replaced. Their spokesman was in the courtyard below. He had declined, with some force of expression, to withdraw.

A second voice reached him then—lower, further from the door, the vowels flat and wide, Ohio country or Kentucky, a voice that had spent its winters outdoors and did not modulate for walls:

*Tell him we have a letter. Tell him it concerns the garrison at the fort, and the men who were turned back at the landing three weeks since, and he will want to read it before he sends us away.*

The ink on the last line of the dispatch had not dried. He could see it from where he stood—still wet, still capable of smearing—and the letter in the courtyard below was already on its way up the stairs.
