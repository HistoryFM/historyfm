# Chapter 11: The Flags at New Orleans

## Scene 1

The Mississippi reached them first through smell—river mud and rotting cane and the sweetness of a city that had burned tallow since before the republic was conceived. Livingston gripped the rail as the vessel worked toward the levee, the cold December air sharp against his face, and took his inventory of the place he had purchased without ever visiting: the pale Spanish façades of the Cabildo and the church of St. Louis rising beyond the Place d'Armes, the line of flatboats and keelboats moored along the bank, their lines creaking against the current. The river was high and brown and indifferent. It carried the smell of the upland continent—silt and pine resin and the cold mineral breath of mountains he would never see.

Monroe was already at the rail. He had been there, Livingston noted, for some time.

The gangplank was run out and found the levee. Monroe moved toward it with the ease of someone who had arranged his own arrival. Livingston straightened his coat with both hands and followed three paces behind—close enough to be counted in the party, far enough to make clear he had not been led.

The French official dispatched by Laussat—young, his uniform pressed to an excessive precision—was already moving through the press of dock workers and free men of colour who had gathered to observe the morning's proceedings. He reached Monroe and bowed.

'Monsieur Monroe. Monsieur le Ministre Extraordinaire. Le Préfet Laussat vous présente ses respects, et vous souhaite la bienvenue au nom de la République française—'

The cold river wind drove into Livingston's eyes. He pressed his handkerchief briefly to his face, his vision blurring at the moment he wished to appear most composed. He lowered the handkerchief. The official had turned, fractionally, toward him.

'—et Monsieur Livingston, bien sûr.'

Two words. The subordination lay not in the phrasing alone but in the inflection—a courtesy appended after the obligation had been discharged. The assembled witnesses on the levee received it: Monroe's aides, the military officers from Wilkinson's party, the Creole merchants at their respectful distance. Monroe's aide, a young Virginian whose name Livingston had already forgotten, suppressed a small adjustment of expression.

Livingston stepped past Monroe and placed himself directly before the official. He spoke in French, quietly, without heat.

'Monsieur. Je dois vous signaler une inexactitude dans votre protocole. Ma commission en tant que Ministre Plénipotentiaire des États-Unis date du mois de novembre 1801. Celle de Monsieur Monroe, en qualité d'Envoyé Extraordinaire, date d'avril 1803. C'est mon nom qui figure en premier sur l'instrument du traité que vous vous apprêtez à exécuter aujourd'hui. Je vous serais reconnaissant de bien vouloir en prendre acte.'

The official's composure held—he was too well-trained to show the recalculation—but his eyes shifted once, quickly, toward the Cabildo, as though measuring the distance to someone who could tell him what to do. No one came. He had executed his instructions. He had not been given instructions for this contingency.

'Monsieur Livingston.' A bow—correct, fractionally deeper than Monroe's. 'Je vous en fais mes excuses. Vous avez entièrement raison.'

The order of address on the levee would not appear in any official document. Every witness on the bank knew this, and Livingston knew it, and the official knew it, and the knowing changed nothing about what had just been established.

Monroe said nothing. He waited with the patience of a man who had decided that the morning's small theatre was beneath his notice—which was, Livingston understood, its own species of contempt. If Monroe chose magnanimity here, before the walk to the Cabildo, then the record would show his grace rather than Livingston's correction.

Livingston turned from the official and addressed Monroe directly, loud enough for the nearest witnesses to hear.

'Mr. Monroe. I trust you will permit me, as senior commissioner, to present the American party to Préfet Laussat in the order established by our commissions.'

Monroe met his gaze. A beat of silence—not theatrical, merely considered, two men who had been in each other's company long enough to know exactly what was being said. Then: 'As you wish, Mr. Livingston. The order of our commissions is a matter of public record.'

A concession that conceded nothing—the record being, as Monroe well knew, a document the newspapers in Virginia had already attributed chiefly to Monroe's mission. But it was spoken aloud, before witnesses, and Livingston received it with a nod and turned toward the Cabildo.

If he could not command the history, he would command the morning.

---

The sala capitular smelled of tallow and damp plaster and, beneath both, the older smell of the building itself—stone that had absorbed a century of Spanish governance and held it like an argument that refused to conclude. Candles in iron brackets threw light that moved when no one moved, the flames responsive to currents too faint for any human agency to have produced. Outside, through the tall windows, the Place d'Armes was full.

Livingston stood at the American side of the central table and fixed his attention on Laussat's face.

He had learned, across two years in Paris, that a deaf man who watches the speaker's face knows more than a hearing man who watches his hands. Laussat's face told him, before the prefect had spoken a dozen words, that the address now being delivered was not addressed to the Americans at all. It was addressed to the Creoles along the far wall—and through them, to the city, and through the city, to the France that was abandoning it.

The prefect spoke in the rapid formal French of the Conseil d'État. Livingston caught perhaps a word in four. *Louisiane. Nos lois. Notre caractère. Ce que nous abandonnons.* What we surrender. But the words mattered less than the faces receiving them: along the far wall, the assembled Creoles stood with a stillness that was itself a kind of ceremony—jaws set, eyes unblinking, hands held at their sides or clasped before them with the controlled rigidity of mourners who had resolved, before the first word was spoken, that they would not be seen to weep.

Monroe stood at Livingston's right hand and heard every syllable. Whatever the address contained, Monroe's expression gave nothing back—the careful neutrality of someone conserving his reactions for a later accounting.

A candle guttered in its bracket directly behind Livingston. The sudden flare of heat caught the back of his neck and he flinched, his shoulders pulling forward for one visible instant before he recovered his posture. He did not look around.

Laussat spoke for perhaps twelve minutes. His voice did not break. It thinned—toward the end, in the final passage—and he said something that changed the room's air, the way a room changes when a window is closed in it. One man along the wall—grey-haired, his coat cut in the fashion of a decade past—brought his hand to the arm of the chair beside him and gripped it, and did not let go.

Then Laussat fell silent. A translator leaned toward the American commissioners and delivered three sentences in English that were not, Livingston judged from their brevity, an adequate rendering of what had been said.

Livingston rose before his name was called.

He crossed to the table in six strides, his boot heels striking the stone floor with a deliberateness he had not planned but did not suppress—the sound carrying through the sala capitular, marking the space between Laussat's France and what came next. The transfer documents lay in order before him, their pages weighted at the corners with small brass discs. He took up the quill. The nib was freshly cut; he could feel the sharpness of it against the pad of his thumb as he positioned it. The document's surface was cool under his wrist.

He signed his name. Not the rapid signature of ordinary business. The full, unhurried hand—*Robert R. Livingston*—each letter formed with the deliberation of a man who understands that this page will outlast every person in the room, that the hand which signed it will be read by men not yet born, and that what they will read in it is not a name but a claim.

He held the quill a moment after signing. The ink was still wet on the page. He could smell it—iron gall, sharp and faintly metallic, cutting through the tallow and the damp plaster. The smell of the law. The smell of every document he had ever signed that had mattered.

He set the quill in the rest and stood.

He had composed his address aboard ship, three nights out of Baltimore, when he had still believed this moment would feel different from how it felt. His French was adequate. He spoke of Louisiana's character, its laws, its people. He promised fidelity to the rights and customs of those who now passed into the republic's governance. He said *fidélité* and *respect* and *les droits des habitants*.

The Creoles along the wall received these words with the same stillness with which they had received Laussat's. Which was not the same thing as believing them.

Livingston knew it. He finished his address and sat down.

Monroe rose. He delivered his remarks in English—warmer in their assurances, more specific in their promises—and the translator rendered them into French with a fluency that Livingston's own address had not received. The Creoles attended with the same composed attention, and Livingston understood, with the cold precision of a surveyor reading an elevation, that the ceremony had already passed beyond the question of which commissioner spoke better. The ceremony belonged to Laussat. The Americans were witnesses to a grief they had caused and could not console.

The documents were gathered. The hour appointed for the square was upon them.

---

The drum struck first. A single beat, then silence, then the brass—fragmented, arriving in pieces, as though the music were being assembled and the pieces were not quite finding each other. Livingston stood with his hands at his sides.

The tricolour began to descend.

He watched it come down. The cloth moved slowly against the grey December sky, the colours bleeding into each other as the wind pressed the fabric flat and then released it. Around the square, no one spoke. The crowd filled every edge of the open ground: French Creole families in dark wool and careful hats, the women's cloaks gathered at the throat; Spanish officers near the church of St. Louis, their uniforms still vivid, their faces arranged into the professional blankness of men who had already performed this ceremony once, in this same square, when the Spanish flag came down for France; American soldiers in two straight lines on the eastern side, their breath rising in small white columns.

At the crowd's outermost edge—not gathered, precisely, but present—stood the gens de couleur libres. They watched with an attention so complete and so withheld that Livingston, reading the square as he had learned to read every room his deafness had made foreign to him, felt the weight of it across the distance. These were property holders. Taxpayers. Creditors and debtors in the courts of this city. Not one provision of the treaty he had signed in Paris addressed what would become of them under American law.

The tricolour reached the bottom of the pole. A French sergeant detached it with steady hands and folded it against his chest.

The band struck the new measure.

The American flag began to rise.

Fifteen stars. The cloth snapped once in the river wind as it climbed. Livingston did not look away. He did not breathe. Around him he heard—imperfectly, through the constant low static of his deafness—what might have been a collective intake of breath from the Creole side of the square, or might have been the wind against the church façade, or might have been nothing at all.

He had written in Paris that Louisiana was a geographic necessity. A commercial corridor. The artery through which the western country's commerce must flow or cease to flow. He had written these words to Barbé-Marbois, and to Madison, and to himself in the margins of dispatches he never sent. He had cited the territory's function as one cites a statute: here is the provision, here is its application, here is the conclusion that follows.

The flag climbed. The provision was a city. The application was a hundred thousand souls. The conclusion—

Laussat wept. Across the square Livingston could see the prefect's shoulders held rigid, his face surrendered. There was no performance in it. This was a man transferring something he had held for three weeks and had already come to love with the intensity of the newly dispossessed.

A woman standing twenty feet to Livingston's right—middle age, dark pelisse, the quality of her dress indicating property and position—pressed a handkerchief to her mouth and turned from the pole. Not with drama. Simply away, as one turns from a view one has seen enough of. She looked at the church façade, its Spanish lines pale in the winter light, and her expression was that of a woman who has made a private accounting, found the figures correct, and wishes with quiet precision that she had not.

The flag reached the top of the pole. It snapped hard and held.

Livingston did not applaud. His hands remained at his sides.

He had purchased eight hundred and twenty-eight thousand square miles. He had not purchased a single one of these people's loyalties, or their trust, or their willingness to submit their property, their families, their names to courts that would conduct their business in a language most of them did not speak. If the Creole population chose to resist—not with arms, which would be simple, but with the slow, corrosive patience of a people who had outlasted two empires—then the republic would govern a city that refused, in every way that mattered, to be governed. The dispatch he must write to Madison tonight would be the first document to say so, or to fail to say so, and the failure would shape everything that followed.

The band completed its measure. The flag flew. The crowd did not move.

---

The chicory coffee sat untouched on the windowsill where Livingston had placed it. The reception room was close with candle wax and damp wool, and somewhere beneath both, the coffee cooling in its cups.

He had positioned himself near the window with his back to the wall—the habit of two years in Parisian salons where his deafness made every open room a theatre of missed cues. From here he could see the whole chamber: Monroe conferring with Wilkinson's officers near the fireplace, the Creole notables clustered in their own constellation by the far door, and between them, occupying the room's precise centre of gravity, Étienne de Boré.

The man was perhaps sixty, with the economy of movement that belongs to those who have learned to spend themselves carefully. He stood at an angle that was not quite confrontational and not quite companionable, watching one of Wilkinson's officers hold forth on Louisiana soil with the expansive confidence of someone three weeks arrived. His expression was one of serene inattention—which meant, Livingston understood at once, that he was attending to every word and filing it.

Livingston crossed the room, took his coffee cup from the windowsill as he passed it, and placed it on the table at de Boré's elbow—a small territorial act, a claim on the space and the conversation.

'Monsieur de Boré. I should value your candid assessment of the population's reception. Not as a question of sentiment—but as a practical matter of governance.'

De Boré turned from the officer. The movement was unhurried, as though Livingston had been expected and the only question had been when he would arrive.

'You are kind to distinguish them.' His English was careful, slightly deliberate—the speech of someone who has learned a language thoroughly enough to use it as a shield. 'The city has received what the day required.'

'With composure.'

'With composure. Yes.' He turned back toward the officer from Wilkinson's staff, who had not noticed the interruption and was still speaking, his voice carrying the satisfied cadence of someone who believes he is being listened to.

Livingston stepped into de Boré's sightline—a deliberate obstruction, unhurried but unmistakable. 'I ask something more specific, sir. The question of property rights. Titles held under Spanish and French grant, disputes that will now come before American tribunals.' He kept his voice level. 'Will the population bring their claims forward under American law? Or will they let those claims lapse and arrive in Washington ten years hence with grievances no administration can address?'

De Boré looked at him. A pause in which the officer's voice continued, oblivious, behind them. Then: 'You describe a situation, Monsieur Livingston, in which the population's silence would be the instrument of its own dispossession.'

'I describe a situation I wish to prevent.'

'Then you must ask yourself what you intend to offer them in exchange for their confidence.' He set his cup down. 'The courts of this territory have always administered justice according to the law that governed it. The population will continue to seek justice.' He paused—not for effect, but for precision, the way a man pauses when the next word must carry weight. 'Whether they will find it—that will depend upon what the courts of the United States choose to offer.'

He reached for his hat.

Livingston put a hand on the table between de Boré and the hat. Not blocking. Occupying.

'One matter more. The gens de couleur libres. Their standing before the new courts—their property titles, their civil status. I saw them in the square this morning.'

De Boré looked at the hand on the table. He looked at Livingston. Something shifted in his expression—not surprise, but recognition, the look of a man who has been waiting to discover whether his interlocutor is worth the trouble of candour.

'They have always watched.' His voice dropped a register—not for privacy, but for weight. 'It is what one does when one's standing depends upon the disposition of those who have not yet decided what they think of you.' He took his hat from beneath Livingston's hand—the movement so smooth it carried no discourtesy, only the quiet assertion that the hand had never been an obstacle. 'I would advise you to consider their situation with great care. Those who have watched many flags rise and fall develop a very precise understanding of what each new flag is likely to mean. They will know, before your courts have rendered a single judgement, whether the republic intends to honour what Spain granted.'

He settled the hat on his head.

'And if it does not, Monsieur Livingston—if it does not—then you will have made of this city not a possession but an adversary. And adversaries of long standing are not governed. They are endured.'

He inclined his head. 'Good evening, sir.'

He was gone before Livingston could find the next question. The hooks across the street were bare. The bunting was gone.

Livingston stood at the window. The officer from Wilkinson's staff was still speaking, his voice carrying the satisfied cadence of someone who believes he is being listened to. Monroe laughed at something—a short, genuine sound—and the room rearranged itself around the laugh, the Creole notables near the far door turning fractionally away, the American officers drawing closer. Two worlds, each pretending the other was not in the room.

Livingston picked up his coffee. It was cold. He drank it anyway.

---

The lamp had burned low enough that the wick was charring. Livingston set down the quill, took the penknife from beside the inkwell, and trimmed the wick with hands stiffened by the cold of the unheated room—the blade slipping once before he caught it cleanly. The flame steadied. Its wider circle fell across the page.

He picked up the quill.

He had written, in the first attempt, what the form required: *The population of New Orleans received the transfer of sovereignty with expressions of general satisfaction, and the ceremonies of the twentieth of December were conducted with a dignity becoming the occasion and reflecting credit upon all parties.*

He read it back. Through the shuttered window came the sound of a fiddle from somewhere down the street—thin, persisting, a French air he did not recognise. A night watchman's cry, distant, in the nasal French of the city. Then quiet, and the fiddle again.

He drew a line through the sentence. Slow, the full width of the page.

*Expressions of general satisfaction.* He had written that phrase, or its equivalent, in dispatches from Paris, from London, from every station where the truth had required translation into the usable. It was the grammar Madison would expect, that Jefferson required—the grammar of a republic that needed good news in order to govern, and that would mistake the silence of a subjected city for the consent of a contented one.

But if he sent it, Madison would read satisfaction where there had been endurance, and Jefferson would read consent where there had been silence, and the administration would proceed as though the city it had purchased were a willing bride rather than a captive whose compliance concealed a judgement not yet rendered. And when the courts opened and the property claims arrived and the free coloured population discovered what American law intended for them, the administration would have no warning, because the first dispatch from New Orleans had told them what they wished to hear.

He could not afford that. Neither could the republic.

He drew the candle closer and began again.

*The population received the transfer with the composure of a people long accustomed to the changes of sovereignty. It is a composure that should not be mistaken for contentment.*

He stopped. Read it. It was true. It would survive the historical record because it was accurate. It would survive Madison's reading because the first sentence could be received as praise—and the second would lodge in the Secretary's mind like a splinter, working deeper with each re-reading, impossible to extract without tearing the surrounding tissue.

He wrote on. Property titles and the question of the civil law. The standing of the free coloured population, rendered in language sufficiently oblique that Madison might choose to read it or to set it aside, as the administration's necessities required. He wrote of the western parishes and the reports—fragmentary, unconfirmed, reaching him through channels he did not name—that the enslaved population had heard of the day's proceedings and had begun to move. In what direction, toward what end, no one in the new administration had anticipated or prepared for.

He paused. Dipped the quill. The ink was cold and thick on the nib.

He did not know what those movements signified or where they would end. He knew only that the dispatch in his hand was the first official document of the republic to acknowledge their existence, and that what he chose to say—or to leave unsaid—would shape what the administration believed it faced, and therefore what it chose to do, and therefore what would follow for every soul in this territory whose fate the treaty had transferred without once mentioning their names.

He thought of de Boré's final words, which he had been turning since the man departed: *They will know, before your courts have rendered a single judgement, whether the republic intends to honour what Spain granted.* He had not answered. He had not known how to answer—which was itself an answer, and not one he could set before Madison in plain language.

He thought of the woman in the dark pelisse, turning from the flagpole.

He thought of the three men on the ladder, pulling down the French bunting with the efficiency of those who had decorated for many occasions and expected to decorate for many more, whatever flag flew above them.

He wrote carefully. The lamp burned. The fiddle played on somewhere in the dark streets of the city that was no longer French and not yet American—a French air, he was nearly certain now, the kind played at weddings and at wakes with equal facility, the melody serving whatever occasion required it.

He reached the final paragraph of the dispatch. He had written around the central question for six pages now, approaching it from the margins, naming its constituent parts without naming the whole. He set the nib to the page.

*It remains to observe that the territory now transferred to the United States contains within its population circumstances which the treaty, in its present form, has neither resolved nor addressed. The administration will, I respectfully submit, require to determine its disposition toward these circumstances before the courts of the territory open their proceedings. What is not determined now will be determined by events, and events in this city have a way of arriving before the administration's instructions.*

He read it back. It was, he knew, the most consequential sentence he had written since the treaty itself—not because it said more than the record could bear, but because it said precisely enough to make silence, hereafter, impossible to claim. Madison would read it. Jefferson would read it. And when the question came—as it would come, as it must come, as the city's ten thousand contradictions would eventually force it into the open—there would be, in the State Department's files, a dispatch dated the twentieth of December, 1803, in which the minister who signed the treaty had told the administration what it faced.

Whether they would act upon it was not his to determine.

He sanded the page, folded it, and reached for the sealing wax. The candle flame bent as he held the stick above it, a thin thread of smoke rising and dissolving into the dark above the lamp's reach. Outside, the fiddle had stopped. The night watchman's cry came again, closer now, moving along the street below the window—a voice calling the hour in French, in the city that was no longer French, in the language it would keep long after the flags had changed again.

He pressed the wax and held it. The seal cooled under his thumb. He set the dispatch to one side and drew a fresh sheet toward him. There was still the matter of Monroe—of the credit, of the dispatches already in transit to Washington that placed Monroe's name first in every account of the negotiation—and that letter, too, required writing before morning. He had been composing it in the margins of his attention all day, fitting sentences between the ceremony and the reception and de Boré's quiet warning, and now the room was quiet enough to set them down. He took up the quill.

He wrote the salutation. He stopped.

The fiddle had begun again, somewhere farther along the street—the same air, or a different one, it was impossible to say. The river moved beneath the city's foundations, carrying what it had always carried, indifferent to the name of whoever held the shore.

He wrote the first sentence of the letter. Then the second. The lamp held.
