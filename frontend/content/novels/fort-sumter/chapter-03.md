# Chapter Three: The Midnight Crossing

## Scene 1

The morning mail had arrived in three separate deliveries, and Lincoln had made no progress whatever in reducing it.

A Cincinnati dry-goods merchant urged immediate conciliation with South Carolina on the grounds that the river trade could not survive a disruption. Two lines were sufficient; he laid it aside. A New York assemblyman urged immediate firmness on the grounds that the river trade could not survive a surrender. One line was sufficient. A letter in a hand he did not recognise, postmarked Columbus, he consigned to the growing pile without opening at all.

The office was cold at the window and overheated near the stove — one of those arrangements that a borrowed room imposes without apology. He had pushed his chair six inches from the desk to find the narrow band of temperature that was neither. Outside, the frozen capitol grounds lay under a sky the colour of old pewter, and the elm trees stood without moving, as though they had decided some weeks ago to wait out whatever came next.

He was reading a letter from a Nashville attorney of his acquaintance — a man who had once argued a boundary case opposite him in circuit court, and who now wrote with the earnest conviction that Lincoln might be persuaded to support a national convention — when the door opened without a knock.

Judge David Davis filled a doorframe the way a courthouse fills a public square — not by height alone but by a settled conviction that the space had been arranged for his occupation. He was carrying his hat and his overcoat, which meant he had come directly from somewhere and intended to go directly somewhere else, and his boots were of a quality that Springfield did not ordinarily supply. Black calfskin, close-fitted, with a high gloss that the frozen mud of the capitol walk had not yet diminished. Lincoln looked at them a moment longer than the situation required.

"I have just come from downstairs," Davis announced, crossing to the near side of the desk without invitation, "and I want you to know that I have done you a considerable service."

Lincoln laid the Nashville letter on the stack to his left and drew a folded telegram from the stack to his right. "Tell me about this service," he said.

"There are two men from the *Journal* waiting in the lower hall. I spoke with them not thirty minutes past, and I told them that the President-elect regards Major Anderson's action as the conduct of a loyal officer performing his duty as he understands it." Davis placed his hat on the corner of the desk. "They were very gratified to hear it. I think you will find the morning edition takes a sound position."

Lincoln put the telegram down. He folded his hands on the desk and regarded Davis with the expression he reserved for witnesses who had answered a question other than the one he had asked. "What were your exact words to them, Judge?"

"I have just told you—"

"The exact words. As near as you can render them."

Davis shifted his weight. The stove popped — a sharp report from somewhere in the iron body of it — and spat a small ember onto the floorboards between them. Lincoln stamped it out with his boot heel.

"As near as you can render them," he said again.

Davis's certainty had begun to settle toward something less comfortable. "I may have said that the President-elect regards the Major's judgment as sound. That the transfer reflects well on the garrison's leadership."

"May have."

"Did say."

"And the reporters understood this to be my position."

"They understood it to be the position of a man close to your counsel, which I am."

Lincoln was still a moment. His hands remained folded. Then: "Judge, I want you to go back downstairs and tell those gentlemen that you spoke without authority, that the incoming administration has no statement to offer on military matters at the present time, and that they are to print nothing attributed to this office or to anyone connected with it."

"That will read as a repudiation—"

"It will read as nothing, because they will print nothing. You will see to it."

Davis reached for his hat. His colour had changed in a way that had nothing to do with the stove's warmth. "If you believe silence will serve you better than—"

"I believe," Lincoln said, "that a man who does not yet hold an office ought to be cautious about the opinions he holds in public. I am cautious, Judge. I would be grateful if you were cautious on my behalf." He rose, crossed to the stove, and opened its iron door to feed in a split log from the box beside it. The heat struck his face and hands. He closed the door and turned back. "There is also a dispatch here that wants your attention. A matter of patronage in the Fourth District — your particular province, I believe."

He held it out, and Davis took it, and the exchange was complete — the reprimand absorbed, the consolation offered, the balance of the relationship adjusted by a degree so fine that neither man would need to acknowledge it had moved.

Davis went out. Lincoln did not sit down at once. He stood by the stove, feeling the warmth spread through the wool of his coat, and listened to the Judge's heavy tread descending the stairs. If Davis had spoken to two reporters, the words were already loose in Springfield. By evening they would be in Chicago. By the end of the week, some version of them — distorted, embellished, attributed — would reach the telegraph offices and the Washington press. And from there, they would reach Charleston, where Major Anderson would read in a Northern newspaper that the incoming administration had endorsed his judgment, and the South Carolina authorities would read in the same newspaper that the new government had committed itself to holding the fort.

Neither conclusion was one Lincoln could afford. Not yet. Not until March, when the office was his and the consequences belonged to him rather than to the Republic's imagination.

He returned to the desk, but he did not resume the mail. He sat with his hands flat on the wood and considered the damage.

## Scene 2

The clerks had gone. The stove, freshly stoked before their departure, pushed heat unevenly across the room — fierce at Lincoln's left side where he sat nearest the iron, negligible by the window where the cold pressed through the frame's gaps with the persistence of a petitioner who will not be turned away. He had lit two candles. Their tallow smell, sharp and faintly rancid, mixed with the stove's iron heat and the dry-paper scent of a hundred opened envelopes.

The correspondence had been sorted into rough categories that now seemed less like categories than accusations: letters requiring answers he could not give, letters demanding positions he could not take, letters from men who believed the republic's salvation lay in their particular prescription and who would not forgive him for failing to concur.

Weed's letter was in his right hand. He had read it twice already. He read it again.

The language was Seward's in all but the handwriting — subordinate clauses stacked and interlocking, each one qualifying the last until the original proposition was so armoured against objection that objection seemed almost impolite. Weed had inquired, with characteristic indirection, whether the President-elect might be disposed to receive certain of the Senate's border-state members informally, in the spirit of the season, to discuss matters of mutual constitutional concern. He had expressed confidence in Lincoln's patriotism. He had expressed confidence in Lincoln's flexibility. He had expressed, in particular, the President-elect's *known flexibility on matters of detail* — the phrase occupying the middle of the third paragraph as though it had always been there, as though the man in Springfield had placed it himself.

He turned the letter over and held it near the candle, studying the paper's watermark — a New York mill, expensive stock, the kind Weed used for correspondence he expected to be preserved. This was not a private communication. It was a document composed for circulation, and somewhere in a Senate cloakroom or a Washington drawing room, a copy of it — or a careful summary — was already doing its work.

Lincoln put the letter down and pressed the heels of his hands against his eyes. The candlelight swam in red and black behind his lids. He thought of his father, who had moved the family three times across the frontier and who had understood, in the way that unlettered men sometimes understand better than educated ones, that a promise extracted under pressure is not a promise but a trap. Thomas Lincoln had never signed a contract he had not read aloud to himself twice, moving his lips over every word, and he had taught his son that the most dangerous sentence in the English language was the one that sounded as though you had already agreed to it.

*Known flexibility on matters of detail.*

He had expressed no such thing. Not to Weed, not to Seward, not to any living soul. He had written to Seward directly and in plain language that the tug must come, and that he preferred it sooner. He had refused the Crittenden proposals in terms that left no interpretive room. And yet here was Thurlow Weed — Seward's instrument and the most accomplished political mechanic in the Republic — attributing to him a position he had never occupied, committing it to paper, routing it toward Washington, where it would circulate as though it carried Lincoln's own seal.

If he did not answer — if he let the letter sit for a week, or two, as the press of business might excuse — Weed's characterisation would harden into fact. Seward would present it to the border-state senators as settled ground. By the time Lincoln reached Washington, the compromise would have been built around him like a scaffold, and he would be asked not whether he agreed but why he had changed his mind.

He could not permit it. But neither could he afford to break with Seward before the administration had begun. The man was to be Secretary of State. He commanded loyalties Lincoln did not yet possess and understood a Washington that Lincoln had visited exactly once, for a single undistinguished term in the House, twelve years past.

Lincoln took a fresh sheet from the stack at his elbow and uncapped the inkwell. He held the pen above the paper for a long interval, then wrote a single sentence, read it back, and drew a line through it. The second attempt ran three lines before he stopped, crossed those out as well, and sat with his chin resting on his free hand, listening to the stove's low complaint and the faint creak of the building settling into the cold.

He thought of what it would cost him to be wrong. Not the political cost — he had calculated that already, carried the figures forward the way he carried any sum, to their necessary conclusion. The other cost. The one that came at three o'clock in the morning when the house was dark and the boys were asleep and the only sound was the wind working at the window frames. The cost of knowing that eighty-odd men in a fort in Charleston Harbor were eating reduced rations because the government they served could not decide whether to feed them or abandon them, and that he — the man the country had chosen to resolve the question — could not yet so much as send them a barrel of flour without exceeding an authority he did not hold.

Then he began a third time, and this time he did not stop.

He wrote to Mr. Weed with expressions of regard for the Senator's distinguished service and his known devotion to the Union's preservation — phrases that were true in their way and useful in their deniability. He wrote that he had given careful consideration to the Senator's thoughtful communication. He wrote that he trusted the Senator would understand that a man in his present position must be especially scrupulous in distinguishing between his personal sentiments and his public obligations.

He then wrote, in the careful hand of a draughtsman who has spent two decades preparing contracts that could not be disputed after the fact, that the platform upon which he had been elected was not a collection of sentiments but a binding declaration of principle — that the people of the free states had voted, in November last, upon the proposition that slavery shall not be extended into the territories of this Republic, and that this proposition admitted of no qualification, no arrangement of detail, no flexibility of any variety, known or unknown, which a gentleman in Springfield had expressed or could express consistently with his duty to those who had elected him.

He praised Senator Seward's patriotism in the next paragraph at some length.

The compliments were genuine enough. They were also as close to a warning as courtesy permitted — the kind a man might deliver with a firm hand on the shoulder and a pleasant expression, so that the recipient could not afterward say he had been threatened, only that he had been made to understand something he had previously chosen not to understand.

He read the letter through twice. Then he folded it, pressed the crease flat with his thumbnail, and addressed it to Mr. Thurlow Weed, Albany, New York.

He held it up and studied the address in the candlelight. If it arrived too late — if Weed had already circulated his version to the border-state men — then this reply would serve only as evidence that Lincoln had objected after the damage was done. A protest, not a prevention. He would need to send it by the morning express, and even then the margin was narrow.

He placed it with the outgoing correspondence and blew out the nearer candle. The room contracted around the single remaining flame. Outside, the January dark was absolute, the grounds invisible, the world reduced to this desk and its freight of paper and the smell of cooling wax.

## Scene 3

The packet from the State Capitol arrived after supper, delivered by a boy whose boots left wet prints across the parlour threshold and who smelled of horse and frozen wool. Lincoln thanked him, pressed a five-cent piece into his palm, shut the door against the January cold, and stood with the bundle in his hands while Mary settled back into her chair by the fire, her needlework returned to her lap with the patient efficiency of a woman who has learned not to ask too quickly.

He broke the string himself. Most of it was the usual accumulation — office-seekers, editors requiring his endorsement of their papers, two senators whose letters he recognised by the seals and placed aside without opening. Then the letter from Charleston, forwarded through three hands, the outermost envelope much handled, its corners soft from a coat pocket or a mail pouch. The handwriting on the inner envelope was spare and vertical. Military.

He read it standing.

The fire snapped in the grate, and from upstairs came the sound of Tad's voice raised in some argument with Willie, the elder boy's response too low to make out — a murmur of authority, or the attempt at it. Then quiet again.

Major Anderson wrote as soldiers write when they have practised restraint until it has become the only language available to them. He did not say he was frightened. He said the garrison was in good spirits. He did not say the walls were inadequate. He said the fortification afforded certain defensive advantages, and certain others that the position at Moultrie had not. He did not say his men might starve. He said that the present stores of salt pork would sustain the command for approximately six weeks, and that the flour supply was somewhat less than that, and that he had reduced daily rations by one-third as a precautionary measure, and that he wished to apprise the proper authorities of these facts so that appropriate decisions might be made in good time.

Six weeks. Less for the flour.

He carried the numbers forward the way he had learned to do it in a courthouse, to their conclusion, before he allowed himself to consider what the conclusion meant. The fourth of March was sixty-three days distant. The flour would not last to the inauguration. If Anderson's men ran out of provisions before Lincoln took the oath, the garrison would be compelled to surrender — not by Confederate cannon but by hunger — and the flag would come down from Sumter while James Buchanan still occupied the Executive Mansion and Lincoln stood in Springfield with no more authority than any other citizen of the Republic.

The chair by the fire received him without any evident instruction from his mind, and he sat, and continued.

Anderson had added a postscript in a smaller, tighter hand — the writing of a man who had returned to the letter after setting it aside, perhaps after dark, when the sounds of the harbour carried differently. He reported that the South Carolina batteries on Morris Island and Fort Johnson were being strengthened daily. That he could observe, through his glass, gangs of labourers working by torchlight. That the channel was being measured and staked. He estimated, with the professional detachment of an officer who has spent his career studying fortifications, that within thirty days the harbour approaches would be commanded by sufficient firepower to prevent any vessel from reaching Sumter without sustaining crippling damage.

Thirty days to close the harbour. Six weeks of food. The two figures did not align. They collided.

The fire shifted. A log rolled against the iron dogs and sent a scatter of sparks across the hearthrug. Mary was on her feet before he looked up, stamping them out with her slipper — three quick, precise stamps, the sound sharp against the wool. She straightened.

"What is it?"

"A letter."

"I can see that it is a letter, Mr. Lincoln." She crossed toward him, her needlework abandoned on the chair. "You have been reading it for ten minutes and you have not moved."

He folded it and put it in his breast pocket.

"Anderson writes of difficulties," he said. "Nothing that cannot be managed."

Mary stood close enough that he could smell the faint cedar from the chest where she kept her good wool. She studied his face with the attention she had given it for twenty years, which was a different order of attention from what anyone else brought to the exercise.

"That is not an answer."

"It is the answer I have."

"Abraham." She said it quietly, without rising inflection. Not a reproach. Something nearer to a verdict.

He met her eyes directly, which was itself a form of evasion.

"The situation at the fort is delicate," he said. "There are men who depend on decisions not yet made, and the making of those decisions requires that certain things remain — contained. For now."

"How many men?"

The question surprised him. Not its substance — Mary had always understood that politics was, at its foundation, a matter of bodies in rooms and bodies in the ground — but its precision. She was not asking about policy. She was asking about lives.

"Eighty," he said. "Perhaps a few more."

She held his gaze for a long moment.

"And when will they cease to require containment — these decisions?"

"When I hold the authority to act on them."

"And if the authority comes too late?"

The fire had settled back to its steady work. From upstairs came the creak of a floorboard, Tad's heavier footfall moving toward his bed.

"How are the boys?" Lincoln said. "Was Tad's cough any better this afternoon?"

Mary looked at him — through him, perhaps — and returned to her chair. She took up her needlework.

"It was somewhat better," she said. "The camphor helped."

The needle moved through the cloth with small, regular sounds, each stitch a tiny puncture in the silence. She did not look up again.

## Scene 4

He had arrived before Nicolay, before the doorman, before the building's fires had been laid. The second-floor office held the January dark at a remove — the single window giving onto the capitol grounds showed nothing but the faint shapes of bare trees against a sky that had not yet decided whether to admit the morning — and the cold had a weight to it, a physical pressure against his hands and face, as though the room itself were resisting occupation. Lincoln had not bothered to light the stove. He sat at the desk in his overcoat, his breath visible in the thin light of a single candle, and spread the papers before him.

The map was from Blunt's *Coast Pilot*, a published survey of the approaches to Charleston Harbor, the kind of thing any merchant captain might own. He had found it at the bookseller on Adams Street three days prior, paying from his own pocket, paying in coin, saying nothing to the proprietor beyond a remark about the weather. It lay open now, the harbour's geometry rendered in careful ink: the channel, the shoals, the three fortifications at their respective points — Moultrie to the northeast, Castle Pinckney nearer the city, and Sumter at the harbour's throat, where Anderson's eighty men were quartered on reduced rations and counting the days.

He placed Anderson's letter beside the map and aligned the postscript's reference to the Morris Island batteries with their position on the chart. The distances were small. A ship entering the main channel would pass within eight hundred yards of those guns — close enough that even poorly served artillery could not miss. And Anderson had written that the guns were being improved daily, by torchlight, with the urgency of men who understood that the window for a relief expedition was closing.

Lincoln took up the pen. He wrote *Dear General Scott* and stopped.

The candle guttered. A draught from the window frame — he could feel it against the back of his neck, a thin blade of cold air — had found the flame and set it trembling. Shadows lurched across the map. He cupped his hand around the candle until it steadied, then returned to the page.

What he required from the General was information: the depth of the main channel under the bar at full tide, the range and calibre of the batteries Anderson had described, the capacity of a steam-driven transport to approach under covering darkness. He did not require the General's opinion on the propriety of the inquiry. He would not ask for it. Scott was an old man, vain of his authority and jealous of his prerogatives, and he might well refuse to answer a private communication from a civilian who held no commission and commanded no army. That refusal was a risk Lincoln could measure. The risk he could not measure — the one that had kept him awake since reading Anderson's postscript — was the risk of not asking, of arriving in Washington on the fourth of March to discover that the harbour was sealed, the garrison starving, and no one in the incoming administration had thought to inquire whether relief was even possible.

His back seized as he bent over the desk — the old circuit-riding complaint, a deep spasm along the left side of his spine — and he pushed his chair back and stood with both hands pressed flat against his lower back, jaw set, waiting for the muscle to release. It did, after a moment, grudgingly. He sat down and resumed writing.

The door opened behind him.

Nicolay came in quietly, as was his habit — the young man had a secretary's instinct for making himself small in doorways — and then he stopped. Lincoln heard the footstep halt, heard the quality of the silence that follows a man who has registered something unexpected and is deciding what to do with it.

"Mr. Lincoln." A pause. "Forgive the hour. I thought I might begin on the morning correspondence before—" Another pause, shorter. "Is that Charleston Harbor?"

Lincoln turned. Nicolay was twenty-eight years old, slight, with the careful eyes of someone who had taught himself to observe without appearing to — a skill Lincoln recognised because he had spent thirty years cultivating the same one. The young man's gaze moved from the map to Anderson's letter, which lay beside it, unfolded.

"Close the door, John. Then sit down."

Nicolay closed the door. The latch clicked — a small, definite sound in the cold room. He sat.

Lincoln slid the map across the desk and placed Anderson's letter on top of it. "Read that. All of it, including the postscript."

Nicolay read without comment, without the small sounds men make when they wish to signal their comprehension or their sympathy. When he reached the postscript, his breathing changed — a single sharp intake through the nose, quickly controlled. He finished and placed the letter on the desk with care, as though it were evidence in a proceeding whose verdict had not yet been rendered.

"How long do they have?"

"Six weeks of salt pork. Less for the flour. And Anderson believes the harbour will be closed to relief within thirty days." Lincoln completed the salutation on the sheet before him and continued writing, the sentences forming as he had rehearsed them through the sleepless hours. "I am writing to General Scott. Privately. The letter goes by your hand and no other, and you will say nothing of it to anyone who arrives at this office today, regardless of their station or their friendship with me."

"Yes, sir."

"If Scott refuses to answer — and he may — we will have lost nothing but time. If he answers, we will know whether relief is possible before I take the oath. That is the only question that matters now, John. Not whether we ought to relieve the garrison, but whether we can."

He wrote for several minutes. The cold in the room had deepened; his breath hung above the page, and the ink thickened on the nib so that he had to dip more frequently, the small sound of the pen entering the inkwell marking a rhythm against the silence. Nicolay sat opposite and said nothing, which was what Lincoln required of him.

When he had finished, he read the letter through once, made a single emendation in the third paragraph — striking *might be practicable* and writing above it *is practicable*, because the distinction was the letter's entire argument — and laid the pen down.

He folded the letter, fitted it into the envelope, and held the stick of wax over the candle flame. The wax softened, then ran — a dark red thread falling onto the paper's join. He pressed his seal into it and held the pressure until the wax firmed.

He held the sealed envelope out toward Nicolay.

Nicolay took it. He put it inside his coat, against his chest, and buttoned the coat over it.

"Before the visitors arrive," Lincoln said.

Nicolay rose and went to the door. He paused with his hand on the latch.

"Mr. Lincoln." The young man's voice was careful, measured in the way a man's voice is measured when he has weighed the cost of speaking and decided to speak anyway. "If Scott does answer — if relief is practicable — you will still require the authority to order it. And that authority does not exist until March."

Lincoln looked at him. The candle had burned low; the light caught the underside of his jaw and left his eyes in shadow.

"Well, now." A pause, longer than comfortable. "I reckon Anderson's men had better be careful with the flour."

Nicolay held his gaze a moment, then went out.

The door did not quite close. It stood a half-inch ajar, and through that gap came the sound of the building waking — a door below, the scrape of a coal scuttle on stone, a voice calling a name Lincoln did not recognise. The day was arriving whether he was prepared for it or not.

He turned back to the map. The harbour's geometry lay before him in the candlelight — the channel, the shoals, the converging arcs of fire from batteries being strengthened by torchlight while he sat in a frozen office in Springfield and composed appeals to old men who might not answer. He traced the channel with one finger, from the bar to the fort, measuring the distance against the scale with the span of his hand. Eight hundred yards under the Morris Island guns. Perhaps a thousand under Moultrie's. A ship would need to pass through both fields of fire to reach Sumter, and Anderson's postscript had made clear that the fields were narrowing daily.

He was still studying the chart when he heard, on the stairs, the unmistakable tread of Judge Davis — heavier than Nicolay's, deliberate, the footfall of a man who considers himself expected. A moment later came another set of steps behind the first, lighter, quicker, and then the low exchange of voices on the landing, two men conferring in the manner of those who have agreed on something before they knock.

Lincoln did not move. He kept his eyes on the map, on the small black square that marked Sumter's position at the harbour's throat, and waited.

The knock came. Firm, unhurried, proprietary.

Whatever Davis had brought with him — whatever fresh damage required managing before the morning was an hour old — would arrive through that door in thirty seconds, and the letter to Scott was already gone, already beyond recall, already committed to a course of inquiry that could not be undone. If Scott refused to answer, the garrison would run out of flour before Lincoln held the power to send so much as a supply boat. If Scott answered and the harbour was already closed, then the letter had been sent too late and the question it asked was moot. The window, Anderson had written, was thirty days. The letter had been dispatched this morning. The arithmetic was not favourable.

The knock came again.

"Come," Lincoln said.
