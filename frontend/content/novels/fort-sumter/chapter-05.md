# Chapter 5: The Farewell

## Scene 1

The trunk at the head of the stairs had been corded twice and still did not sit level, one brass fitting catching the lamplight at an angle that nagged at Lincoln each time he passed. He had passed it three times. The hallway smelled of cedar shavings and the cold that enters a house when half its furniture has gone out of it — a thinned air, rooms stripped of their own dimensions.

He carried the carpetbag into the bedroom and set it on the bare mattress ticking. Mary stood at the window with her back to him, her wrapper drawn close, looking out at the street below where nothing moved in the February dark. The boys were in the next room. Willie's breathing came through the plaster, slow and regular. Not yet seven o'clock.

'The smaller trunk is not labelled,' she said, without turning. 'I told Harriet to label it and she did not.'

'I will see to it.'

'You will not.' She did not raise her voice. She did not need to. 'You will say you will see to it and then you will stand in the hallway with that expression and Nicolay will arrive and the trunk will go onto the car unlabelled and we will not find our things until Cincinnati.'

He opened the carpetbag and pressed his hand flat against its contents — shirts, the shaving kit, the small Testament Mary had placed there the evening before without comment. He pressed down until he felt the spine of the book.

'I have labelled every trunk in this house that wanted labelling for sixteen years.' He pulled the buckle tight, testing the strap. 'I will label this one.'

She turned then. Her hair was not yet dressed and her face in the lamplight had gone unguarded, all the careful arrangements dissolved. She had been weeping, or had been close enough to it that the distinction was not material.

'The boys' coats.' She moved to the nearest trunk and lifted the lid, though she did not look inside it. 'Tad's coat has a button missing from the left cuff and I have not —' She stopped. Her hands rested on the lid's edge. 'Do you believe we shall ever come back to this house?' She did not turn to face him, which was its own kind of mercy — for him, or for her, he could not have said. He opened his mouth and found nothing there that was not a lie.

From the next room, Tad cried out — sharp, brief, the sudden waking of a child surprised by his own dream. Lincoln turned toward the wall. The sound rose and diminished, the boy muttering himself back under. Silence. Then Willie's breathing resumed its slow measure.

Lincoln turned back.

Mary had not moved. Her hands were folded at her waist and she was watching him, and the complaint about the coat had not been finished and would not be. He knew that expression. He had seen it across a dinner table in this house for sixteen years, and across a fire, and across the silence that descends at two in the morning when a man has come in from the cold and a woman has waited up and neither of them has found the right thing to say.

'Abraham.' Not a question. Not a complaint. His name, set between them like something placed on a table and left there.

'Do you believe,' she said, 'that we will ever come back to this house.'

The carpetbag was in his hands and the question stood in the room and there was nowhere to go with it. He set the bag on the floor. He crossed to her and took both her hands in his — cold at the fingers, cold at the wrists — and her grip tightened by degrees until she was holding him as much as he was holding her.

'I do not know,' he said.

Her breath caught, once, and steadied.

'What I know,' he said, 'is that I cannot do what is required alone. Not the office. Not the journey to it.' The lamp on the dresser threw their shadows against the bare wall, elongated and motionless. 'I need you beside me. Not to make it lighter — I am not asking for that. Only to bear it alongside me.'

She did not answer at once. Her hands in his were warming slowly. Outside, the street held its breath — no wheel, no voice, only the February dark pressing against the glass.

She drew one hand free and pressed it flat against his chest, over the breastbone. The words, when they came, were very quiet. 'There is no house without you in it. You understand that.'

He covered her hand with his own.

He understood it. He had understood it since November, and the understanding had settled into him the way cold settles into stone — not as a shock but as a permanent condition, altering the substance rather than merely the surface.

'The trunk,' she said at last. Her voice had changed — steadied, the domestic machinery reasserting itself over the thing neither of them could govern. 'It wants a label. Go.'

He went. He trailed his fingers along the doorframe as he left, feeling the wood — rough where the paint had thinned, worn smooth where her hand had passed over the years — and then he was in the hallway, reaching for the lamp, and below in the street a horse went by at a walk, its iron shoes distinct and unhurried on the frozen ground.

The Chenery House lobby had the quality of a courtroom gallery in the minutes before the judge enters — men pressed together who would not ordinarily share a room, the air close with their competing purposes. Lincoln came through the door at half past seven and the noise swelled, then fractured into a dozen separate approaches.

Lamon was already moving, broad-shouldered and watchful, steering the worst of the crowd into a horseshoe rather than a press. It helped, somewhat. Lincoln turned his stovepipe hat in his hands before settling it back onto his head, and then he was in it — a congressman from Tazewell County who needed a word about the collectorship, a minister from the Second Presbyterian who offered a blessing Lincoln accepted with a bow, two women who pressed forward to touch his coat sleeve.

He shook hands. He spoke the names he knew and invented warm generalities for those he did not. Nicolay moved at the edge of the room with a leather satchel, managing documents Lincoln would not examine until the train was rolling.

Near the staircase, where the wallpaper had begun to separate at the seam, a man in a grey coat was writing in a small notebook. The Chicago Tribune, Lincoln guessed, or possibly the Herald. The man had the look of a correspondent whose editor had given him an instruction he found distasteful but intended to execute faithfully.

The question, when it came, was not subtle.

'Mr. Lincoln.' The voice cut across the room with the practised projection of a man accustomed to being ignored. 'Will you reinforce the Southern forts once you take your office?'

The lobby did not go entirely quiet — there were too many people for that — but the conversations nearest Lincoln ceased, and the cessation spread outward in widening rings. The correspondent had positioned himself where Lincoln could not pretend not to hear. Answer plainly, and the words would reach Charleston by telegraph before his train reached Decatur; every wavering border-state legislator would read them over breakfast. Decline to answer, and the refusal itself would be reported as evasion, and evasion would be read as weakness, and weakness in a president-elect was a commodity the Montgomery authorities would be pleased to advertise.

Lincoln looked at the man. Not at his notebook. At the man.

'Well, now,' he said, and the room heard him because he had spent thirty years making himself heard in courthouses where the acoustics were worse than this. 'That puts me in mind of a fellow I knew down in Sangamon County — farmer by trade, though he was not what you would call an industrious one. A neighbour came by one morning and found him sitting on his porch, and the neighbour said to him, "I hear you have been offered a position on the county road commission." And the farmer said, "That is so." And the neighbour said, "And do you intend to accept it?" And the farmer studied on this for a considerable while, and finally he said, "I intend to find out what it pays before I decide what I intend."'

The laughter assembled itself in stages — a sound from the back first, then the men closest to Lincoln who had caught the pivot before the others, and then the room gave way to it, the question dissolving in the crowd's relief at having somewhere to put its tension. The correspondent wrote in his notebook without looking up.

Lincoln was already moving. He had not waited for the laughter to finish — through the horseshoe, one hand briefly on the Tazewell congressman's shoulder, a word exchanged with the minister, Lamon opening a channel toward the street door with the quiet authority of a man whose mind was made up and whose body had simply arrived at the conclusion first.

The cold hit him before he reached the door — sharp in the back of the throat, tasting of coal smoke and frozen earth. Behind him, the lobby reassembled itself into the noise of men who had witnessed nothing consequential and would report accordingly — except for the correspondent near the staircase, who was still writing, and who would report exactly what had occurred: that the President-elect of the United States had been asked a direct question about the disposition of federal garrisons in a seceded republic and had answered with a story about a lazy farmer.

Lincoln stepped into the February morning.

The train was waiting.

## Scene 3

The locomotive's breathing shook the platform boards beneath his boots — a low, rhythmic concussion felt in his knees and in the hollows of his chest. Steam rose in slow white columns above the depot roof and dissolved into grey sky. Coal smoke settled on his tongue, acrid and particular.

He had not expected a thousand. But they had come out in numbers that pressed against the wooden fencing and spilled back toward the freight house, their breath rising in small separate clouds that merged overhead into a single vapour. He stood upon the platform and they stood before him — three feet of planking between his boots and the front rank — and he felt the whole weight of what was coming press against him as surely as the crowd pressed against the boards.

Nicolay waited at the edge of the platform, pencil ready.

Lincoln removed his hat.

He did not plan to. His right hand simply rose and took the hat from his head, and then he held it against his chest, and the drizzle began immediately to wet his hair, and he did not replace it.

The crowd quieted at that. Not wholly silent — children moved at the edges, and somewhere a horse shifted against its traces — but the murmur subsided into something attentive and still.

He looked at them. He had known many of these faces for twenty-four years. A man near the front had argued a case against him in the Tazewell County court in 1847, and had argued it well, and Lincoln had told him so afterward. A woman in a grey bonnet had brought her husband's will to the office on Fifth Street the spring before last, wept without apology, and Lincoln had drafted the document with care and charged her nothing. He did not know the names of nine in ten. What he knew, looking at them now, was that he had been among them — had moved through their streets and eaten at their tables and pleaded before their juries — and that the life he had made here was as real and rooted a thing as he had ever possessed, and that he was leaving it, and that the leaving might be permanent.

He gripped the hat's brim and began to speak.

He told them he did not know how soon he should see them again. He told them he had lived among them for a quarter of a century, and that they had given him all that he had and all that he was. He told them that what lay before him was greater than what had rested upon any man since the time of Washington — and then he heard himself say it, and felt the full weight of the statement, and did not retract it, because it was the truth as he understood it, and he had not come to this platform to manage the truth.

The cold rain found the gap between his collar and his neck and ran down between his shoulder blades.

*I now leave, not knowing when, or whether ever, I may return.*

The silence came down like a hand laid flat upon the crowd. He watched it move through them the way a wind moves through standing grain: a slight shift, a settling, the faces going still. They were not waiting for the next sentence. They understood that the next sentence could not be what they wanted. He had told them he might not come back, and they believed him, and the believing was in their faces, and some of those faces belonged to men who would enlist before summer, and some of those men would not return either, and this was the knowledge that passed between them — not spoken, not speakable, but present in the rain and the steam rising from the locomotive behind him.

He would not give them policy. He would not give them reassurance that tasted of falsehood.

He commended himself to the Divine Providence that had guided the nation from its founding. He asked for their prayers — not as a formula but as a man who had reckoned, in the hours before dawn, the distance between what was required of him and what he was able to supply from his own resources, and had found the gap considerable.

Affectionately, he said. Farewell.

A woman in the front row was weeping openly. The man beside her put his hand on her arm and she shook it off. She did not look away from Lincoln's face.

He replaced his hat. He turned toward the car. Behind him, the crowd did not cheer. It did not call out. It released him into the sound of the locomotive's breathing and the rain on the depot roof, and he climbed the iron steps and did not look back, because if he looked back he would see their faces and he was not certain he could carry their faces and also carry what was waiting for him at the other end of the rail.

## Scene 4

The car rocked. Springfield was gone.

Rain streaked the glass. The last of the town's buildings had dissolved into bare ground and bare sky, fence posts ticking past at intervals, a farmhouse chimney trailing smoke that the wind pressed flat before it rose. Lincoln sat at the small writing desk fitted into the forward end of the private car, his carpetbag on the floor beside him, his hat on the rack above. Nicolay had taken the seat opposite, notebook open, pen charged and waiting. Lamon occupied the rear bench — still, watchful, occupying no more of the world than the bench required.

The wheels found their rhythm. The car smelled of coal smoke and damp wool, the locomotive's exhalations pressing back through every gap in the window frames.

'Begin,' Lincoln said.

Nicolay's pen touched the page.

Lincoln picked up the pen that had been set out on the desk. He held it for a moment, then tried to write — the words he had spoken not forty minutes ago, the words he could still feel in his chest, where they had originated before any act of will had arranged them into sentences. *My friends —* Two words. The pen moved no further. His hand was not shaking — it was something quieter than that, a refusal at the root of the motion, as though the body understood that what had been spoken aloud in the rain could not be reduced to ink without losing the thing that had made it true.

He set the pen down. He looked at his hand for a moment, then slid the pen across the desk.

'You write. I will speak it.'

Nicolay took it without comment. He was twenty-eight years old and had learned, in these past months, that Lincoln's silences were not invitations to fill.

Lincoln folded his hands on the desk and began to dictate. The words came back with the fidelity of a thing spoken from the deepest part of understanding — not memorised but remembered, the way a prayer is different from a recitation. He spoke into the rocking car, his voice level, pitched just above the wheel-noise, and Nicolay's pen moved steadily, and the Illinois plain ran away outside the glass toward a horizon that held nothing.

The train lurched over a rough joint in the rails. Lincoln's elbow struck the desk's edge and the inkwell jumped, scattering a dark spray across the top of the page. Nicolay reached for the blotting cloth, pressed it to the damage, turned the sheet, smoothed a fresh one beneath his palm, and looked up.

Lincoln had not stopped dictating.

When he finished, the car was quiet except for the wheel-sound and Lamon's breathing. Nicolay read back the last several lines. One nod.

Then Nicolay set down the pen and produced, from the inside breast pocket of his coat, a folded telegram. He extended it across the desk without a word.

Lincoln looked at it for a moment before he took it. 'When did this arrive?'

'Last evening, sir. I judged it best to wait until you were aboard.'

'You judged correctly.' Lincoln unfolded the paper. He read it once, quickly — the manner of a man confirming what he already suspects rather than discovering what he does not. He read it a second time, his thumb pressing the crease flat against the desk's surface.

Jefferson Davis had been elected provisional President of the Confederate States of America by the Congress assembled at Montgomery, Alabama. The election had occurred on the ninth of February. Two days past.

Lincoln set the telegram flat on the desk. Outside, a stand of bare oaks moved past the window, their branches black and intricate against the pewter sky. His hands were folded. His eyes had gone to the middle distance, fixed on some point beyond the glass, beyond the oaks, beyond the grey dissolving horizon — the stillness his intimates had learned to read not as vacancy but as arrival: the stillness of a man who has already reached a conclusion he has not yet chosen to speak.

'Well,' he said. 'There are two of us now.'

Lamon shifted on the rear bench. 'Two presidents. One army. And that army is scattered across thirty forts from Maine to Texas, half of them commanded by officers whose loyalties are not yet declared.'

Lincoln picked up the telegram and folded it once along its original crease. He placed it beneath Nicolay's transcription — the farewell address and the rival inauguration lying together on the small desk, the private grief and the public fracture joined by proximity and by the fact that both were now irreversible.

'Every stop between here and Washington,' Lincoln said, 'every platform and hotel lobby and statehouse reception — they will want to know what I intend. The newspapers will parse every sentence for a signal. The Southern papers will report what I did not say as loudly as what I did.' He turned to Nicolay. 'We will need to draft remarks for Indianapolis. Something that holds firm on the Union without furnishing Montgomery a pretext.'

Nicolay opened his notebook to a fresh page.

'And Nicolay —' A pause. The train was accelerating, the wheel-rhythm quickening beneath them, the car swaying with the urgency of a thing that has committed itself to a direction. 'If any further telegrams arrive concerning the Montgomery government, I am to see them at once. Not after the next stop. Not after I have slept. At once.'

'Yes, sir.'

Lincoln turned to the window. The fields were darkening as clouds massed to the south — pewter shading toward iron, the horizon dissolving into a smear of rain that had not yet reached them but would. Indianapolis by evening. Cincinnati by morning. Then the long arc east — Columbus, Pittsburgh, Philadelphia, the corridor narrowing toward a capital where half the population wished him well and the other half wished him gone, and where the man he had chosen for Secretary of State was already conducting a foreign policy of his own devising, feeding assurances to Southern intermediaries of things Lincoln had neither sanctioned nor been consulted upon, building an architecture of promises that would require, sooner or later, a reckoning with the man who held the office those promises presumed to dispose of.

The ink on Nicolay's transcription was still damp. Outside, the rain reached them — a sudden percussion against the glass, the fields erased, the world reduced to the rocking car and the sound of the wheels eating the distance between what Lincoln had been and what he was required to become.
