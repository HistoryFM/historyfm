# Chapter Four: The Star of the West

## Scene 1

The first dispatch had arrived before six, and Seward had been composing his reply to Judge Campbell for the better part of two hours — the letter growing, by the careful accretion of subordinate clauses, into something that might serve simultaneously as reassurance, as misdirection, and as the opening position in a negotiation he had not yet named. The coal fire had burned low without his noticing. Outside the curtained windows of the F Street study, Washington was conducting its morning under a sky the colour of old pewter, and somewhere in the street below a dray horse's iron shoes struck the frozen cobbles in a slow, arrhythmic report that carried through the glass like a muffled drum.

He had reached the letter's critical passage — the one in which Buchanan's dispatch of the vessel was to be characterised as a gesture of loyalty to the garrison that the incoming administration could not be held responsible for and would not be compelled to repeat — when Frederick knocked and entered without waiting for an answer. His son carried a telegraph form folded between two fingers, and something in the set of his jaw, the deliberate steadiness with which he held the paper, told Seward that the contents had already been read and found to admit no charitable construction.

"The vessel has turned back," Frederick said. He read from the dispatch without preamble, his voice level in the manner of a clerk reading a court finding: South Carolina batteries on Morris Island had opened fire upon the *Star of the West* as she attempted the main channel at approximately half past seven that morning. The vessel, unarmed and carrying provisions only, had taken fire from the batteries without returning it, had come about, and was now standing out to sea. She would return to New York without having delivered her cargo. Major Anderson's garrison remained unsupplied.

The fire popped — a dry, hard sound, not loud — and a scatter of ash sifted through the grate.

Seward set down his pen.

"A second dispatch confirms it entirely," Frederick continued. "There is no ambiguity in the account. South Carolina's commissioners are already preparing a statement. It will be in the afternoon papers."

Seward pushed back from the desk and stood. He crossed to the window — not for the view, the curtains were drawn — but because movement was a thing he could do while his calculations ran. The horse had stopped somewhere to the east. In its absence he could hear the house: the tick of the mantel clock, the thin whistle of a draught through the casement's lower edge.

"You believe," Frederick said, his voice careful now, "that men such as Judge Campbell will read this morning's events as you intend them to be read."

Seward turned from the window.

"I believe," he said, "that Campbell is a man of considerable intelligence who will read events as the most plausible available interpretation presents them."

"And if the most plausible interpretation is that the firing vindicates South Carolina's position rather than yours?" Frederick set the telegraph form on the corner of the desk, placing it with the deliberation of a man producing evidence. "That it demonstrates federal overreach, not Southern aggression? The commissioners will say exactly that before noon. They will say the vessel entered a sovereign harbour under false colours, and that the batteries acted in lawful defence. Your letter, as presently composed, argues that Buchanan's gesture was a blunder the next administration will not repeat. But it does not argue that the blunder was the vessel's dispatch. It argues that the blunder was the *manner* of it." He paused. "Campbell will see the seam. And if he sees it before your letter arrives, the whole architecture collapses — every assurance you have given, every commitment those men in Richmond are holding against you. Gone before noon."

The word *gone* sat in the room between them.

Seward's hand moved to the letter. He took up the two completed pages, aligned their edges, and dropped them into the grate. The paper caught at the corner, browned, and then took the fire whole, the ink darkening before it vanished.

He coughed — coal smoke sharp in the back of his throat — and pressed the window open two inches until the cold air cut the smoke. His eyes watered. He stood there until the spasm passed, one hand on the sash, the January air cold against his wrist where his cuff had ridden up.

"Take up your pen," he said, when he could speak cleanly. "We are not writing to Campbell about Buchanan's blunder. We are writing to Campbell about the inescapable conclusion that only evacuation can now prevent the next incident from being the one that cannot be managed." He turned from the window. "The firing does not refute our position. It proves it. Begin."

Frederick did not move immediately. He stood with his hands at his sides, looking at the grey ash where the pages had been.

"And if Campbell has already drawn his own conclusion?" he said. "Before your letter arrives."

"Then we shall be confirming what he already believes." Seward drew a fresh sheet from the drawer and uncapped the inkwell himself. "Confirmation is never wasted."

## Scene 2

The cloakroom smelled of wet wool and the sweetness of cigars left to die in their holders — a smell that clung to the low ceiling and the horsehair cushions and to the coats of the men who stood in clusters, speaking in the urgent, half-voiced register of those who have news but no policy to match it. Three hours had elapsed since the first dispatches from Charleston. The walls of the Senate had absorbed the information the way stone absorbs cold — slowly, thoroughly, and without visible sign.

Seward moved through the room with the unhurried ease of a man arriving at his own table, nodding as he passed, touching an elbow here, a shoulder there, reading the room's geometry as he went. He counted three men already in Chandler's orbit — Senator Wade, Senator Trumbull, and a third he could not immediately place — and felt a sharpening of attention that had nothing to do with alarm and everything to do with the recognition that the board required immediate attention.

He had paused briefly beside the bench where Charles Francis Adams sat alone with a newspaper folded to its inner pages, reading with the absorbed, slightly superior attention of a scholar who believes he has already understood what the others are only now discovering. Adams had looked up. Seward had offered the small, cordial nod of one statesman acknowledging another — the sort of nod that costs nothing and concedes less — and had moved on without stopping. He knew it for a slight. Adams was the better classicist, the more polished diplomatist, the man whose name carried the full freight of two presidential generations; and Seward had been hearing for a fortnight, from sources he trusted, that Mr. Lincoln found in Adams a certain gravity of intellect that he found — the word used had been *reassuring.* Seward had not forgotten the word. He did not stop.

Chandler saw him coming and did not bother to arrange his expression.

"Senator Seward." He held a sheaf of papers folded once, the way a man holds a document he means to produce at the right moment. "We were discussing whether the government of these United States retains the capacity to respond to an act of war."

"A stimulating inquiry," Seward replied. "Though I confess the vessel was an unarmed merchant ship, and the question of what precisely constitutes a *casus belli* under those circumstances admits of some deliberation."

"The guns of South Carolina did not deliberate." Chandler unfolded the papers. "I have here a resolution. It calls upon the President to dispatch naval vessels to Charleston Harbor with orders to cover any future resupply attempt and to make clear to the Montgomery authorities that this Republic will not suffer the seizure of its property by force." He looked at Seward with the flat, unwavering attention of a man who has decided to be satisfied with nothing less than a full declaration. "Senators Wade and Trumbull have indicated their support. Senator Foster has also expressed agreement in principle. I require a fourth commitment before I bring it to the floor this afternoon. I would be gratified to record yours."

Seward took Chandler's arm.

His fingers closed above the elbow with a pressure that was also a direction, and he steered Chandler three steps away from the cluster of senators — far enough that the conversation became private, close enough that the others could observe without hearing.

"Zachariah," he said, and the use of the given name was a register shift, a door opened to a smaller room. "I will tell you something I have not told this chamber, and you will understand why I have not told it."

The smell of someone's extinguished cigar drifted past them. Chandler said nothing.

"I have had, within the past fortnight, communication from men of standing in the Virginia convention — men whose names I will not commit to public record for reasons you are intelligent enough to appreciate. What those men have told me, with a consistency I find persuasive, is that the Unionist delegates hold a working majority at present. A working majority, Senator — not a comfortable one, not a permanent one, but sufficient to the purpose if the purpose is to keep Virginia in this Union for the next sixty days." Seward released the arm and straightened his own coat. "Your resolution, if it reaches the floor today and is telegraphed to Richmond by evening, will be read as a declaration of coercive intent. I do not say it will fail. I say it will reach those delegates before the ink is dry, and it will give the secessionists in that convention the argument they have not yet been able to make successfully."

Chandler's jaw moved, not quite speech. His hand tightened on the folded resolution.

"Sixty days," Seward continued. "I am asking for forty-eight hours. Two days, Zachariah, to see whether my intelligence holds. If it does not — if those delegates have shifted, if the convention moves before my information is tested — I will not only withdraw my objection to your resolution, I will second it from the floor myself."

"Forty-eight hours." Chandler's voice was flat. "And if in that span Buchanan has still done nothing? If Anderson sends another dispatch saying his men are on half-rations and the batteries are drilling daily? What then is your intelligence worth to Virginia or to anyone else?"

The question hung between them, and Seward felt its weight — not as a rhetorical challenge but as a genuine accounting, the kind that does not wait for an answer so much as watch to see whether one is attempted.

"Then," Seward said, "you will have your resolution and my name upon it."

From the Senate chamber beyond the door came the low percussion of a gavel calling order, and the muffled sound of a clerk reading a bill that had nothing to do with Charleston.

Chandler held the resolution a moment longer. Then he folded it again, along its original crease, and placed it inside his coat.

"Not forty-nine," he said.

He turned back toward Wade and Trumbull without further ceremony.

Sumner, who had been standing near the door throughout, caught Seward's eye for a single moment and then looked away — the look of a man keeping his own ledger, and finding the current entry instructive.

## Scene 3

The anteroom to Buchanan's private office smelled of candle tallow and, beneath it, the close, faintly sour odour of a room where anxious men had waited through many such afternoons without resolution. A young lieutenant sat with his hands folded on his thighs — rigid, patient, his uniform collar too tight at the throat, a small dark stain of perspiration at his temple despite the cold. He had been there, by Seward's reckoning, since mid-afternoon. No orders had come.

Seward removed his gloves upon entering and laid them on the corner of the President's desk without being invited to do so. He did not sit.

The iron stove in the corner threw heat in uneven waves — close and oversweet with it — the curtains drawn against the January dusk so that the office existed in a kind of amber suspension: lamplight on mahogany, on the pale oval of Buchanan's face, on the characteristic tilt of the President's head that gave every audience with him the quality of a man listening through a wall. Seward felt the warmth press against the front of his coat while his back, facing the door, remained untouched by it.

"Senator." Buchanan gestured toward the chair across the desk without rising. "I had not expected you."

"The day has been full of the unexpected, Mr. President." Seward remained standing. "I will not keep you long. I have come to ask a direct question, and I should be grateful for a direct answer."

The President's hands rested flat upon the desk, atop a document bearing the seal of the War Department. He said nothing.

"Have you determined upon a course of action in response to this morning's events?"

"The vessel was fired upon," Buchanan said, his voice carrying the careful neutrality of a man reciting an unpleasant legal finding. "She has turned back. Anderson remains without resupply."

"I am aware of the facts, Mr. President. I am asking what you intend to do about them."

A silence settled — not the companionable silence of shared understanding but the specific silence of two men each waiting for the other to move first. A coal shifted in the stove with a low, grinding sound, and a thread of grey smoke curled briefly from the damper before the draught took it.

Buchanan drew a slow breath. "I should value your judgment," he said at last, "as to whether the administration that succeeds mine would countenance a retaliatory expedition. Whether Mr. Lincoln, in your estimation, would support such a course." He paused. "I ask because any measure I undertake now will fall to him to sustain. It would be imprudent to act without some assurance of continuity."

There it was.

Seward crossed to the desk — not to sit, but to stand directly before it, so that Buchanan was required to look up at him. He planted his hands on the desk's edge and leaned forward two inches.

"Mr. President," he said, his voice low and entirely without inflection, "you have this afternoon a garrison of federal soldiers on federal soil who have been fired upon by men in rebellion against this government. You have a War Department document beneath your hands. And you are asking me — a Senator-designate with no executive authority whatsoever — to tell you whether another man, who does not take office for seven weeks, will ratify whatever you choose to do or not to do." He straightened. "I will not tell you that. I will tell you this: if you do nothing, you will hand to your successor not a crisis but a catastrophe, and history will record which of you held the pen when the moment required it."

Buchanan's head tilted further. The lamplight caught the moisture at the corner of one eye.

"You are very precise," the President said.

"I have found precision a useful habit in difficult seasons." Seward took his gloves from the desk's edge and held them without putting them on. "The commissioners from South Carolina have this afternoon issued a public statement. They call the firing an act of sovereignty. Self-defence." He let the words rest a moment. "Jefferson Davis has been informed. He will draw his own conclusions from whatever you do next — and from whatever you do not."

Buchanan said nothing. His hands did not move from the document.

"If you order a retaliatory expedition," Seward continued, "and Mr. Lincoln's administration declines to sustain it upon taking office, the humiliation falls to you alone. I understand why you seek assurance. But I cannot give it, and no man in this city can give it honestly." He moved toward the door, then stopped with his hand on the frame and turned back. "What I can tell you, Mr. President, is that those who will write of this period have never yet shown mercy to paralysis. They are constitutionally incapable of it."

He left Buchanan with his hands still flat upon the War Department seal, the lamplight steady, the room close and warm and very quiet.

In the anteroom, the young lieutenant had not moved. He sat in the same rigid posture, hands folded, eyes forward, waiting for orders that had not come and, Seward now understood, would not come tonight. He did not look at Seward as he passed. He had learned, in whatever anteroom had preceded this one, that looking at the men who come and go only sharpens the waiting.

## Scene 4

The carriage had set him down on F Street at half past nine, and the house was quiet in the way that houses become quiet when the servants have been told to withdraw rather than merely having retired of their own accord. Frederick had taken himself upstairs after a silence at the door that said more than he would permit himself to say aloud. Seward had not detained him.

Now the study held only the sound of the coal settling in the grate and the scratch of his pen against paper that kept arriving at the same sentence and stalling there.

It is my considered judgment, conveyed with the full confidence of intelligence from Richmond that admits of no contrary interpretation, that the federal garrison at Fort Sumter will not be maintained beyond such time as a peaceful transfer of authority may be arranged to the satisfaction of all parties.

He set the pen down.

The intermediary would depart on the six-fifteen train to Richmond. He had perhaps seven hours.

He rose and went to the sideboard, poured two fingers of brandy into a glass, and stood holding it without drinking. The room smelled of coal smoke and old paper and the faint resinous scent of the pine boards beneath the carpet where the floor had never fully dried after last winter's flooding. He had lived in this house for three years and had never noticed that smell until tonight. He set the glass down untouched.

He returned to the desk.

The paper was cream-coloured, good stock, the kind that held ink without bleeding at the edges. He had purchased it specifically for correspondence of this character — correspondence that would be read more than once, held up to lamplight, perhaps compared against future events. He smoothed the page with the flat of his hand, feeling the slight tooth of the grain against his palm.

The letter to Campbell required one thing and one thing only. Everything else he had written — the reassurances about Virginia, the careful parsing of Buchanan's inaction as a harbinger of Republican restraint, the oblique references to his own standing within the administration he was about to join — all of it was scaffolding. The structure it was meant to support was a single sentence. Without that sentence, Campbell would read the letter as the pleasantries of a man who had lost his nerve, and would carry that reading to the men in Montgomery who were, at this moment, deciding whether Virginia was worth the patience of waiting.

He picked up the pen again.

His hand cramped around it without warning — a sudden seizing of the fingers, the tendons pulling tight from somewhere in the wrist. He set it down, flexed his fingers once, twice, a third time, working the knuckles until the grip loosened. Then he took it up again.

He wrote the sentence he had already written once that morning, before the dispatches arrived, before the letter went into the fire: *I am in a position to assure you, with a confidence I do not extend lightly, that the federal garrison at Fort Sumter will not be maintained beyond such time as a peaceful transfer of authority may be arranged to the satisfaction of all parties.*

He did not read it back.

He wrote the closing sentences with the speed of a man finishing something before he can think about it clearly, and then he folded the pages — three of them, covered on both sides — pressing the crease with the edge of his thumbnail until each fold was sharp and flat. The paper was warm from his hand where he had been resting his wrist against it.

He lit the wax over the candle and let it pool on the envelope's flap, then pressed his seal into it with more force than the task required, driving the brass into the soft wax until he felt the resistance of the paper beneath. He held it there a moment longer than necessary. When he lifted the seal, the impression was deep and clean — deeper than usual, the device rendered in every particular.

He held the envelope at arm's length.

It weighed nothing. He had expected something more.

He crossed to the bell-pull and rang for Josiah, who arrived from the back passage with a quickness that betrayed no sleep.

"This goes to Mr. Aldrich tonight," Seward said. "He is to have it before six."

"Yes, sir."

The envelope passed from his fingers.

He stood at the desk after Josiah had gone, listening to the retreating footsteps on the back stairs, then the soft percussion of the kitchen door, then nothing. The coal in the grate shifted once and went quiet.

He had told Buchanan, not four hours past, that those who write of such periods have never shown mercy to paralysis. He had said it with the easy authority of one who believed himself to be acting rather than waiting. Now, in the stillness of the study, with the wax cooling on a letter he could not recall having sealed, he understood that there was more than one variety of paralysis — and that the kind he had just committed to paper was the more dangerous sort, because it wore the costume of decision.

In Springfield, a man he had never adequately measured was working by lamplight on an address that would be delivered to a nation he believed he could hold together by the force of its own founding logic. That man did not know his name had just been signed to a promise he would never have sanctioned, made to men who would hold it against him, in a letter now moving through the January dark toward a morning train.

Seward sat down.

He did not reach for the brandy.

But the intermediary would be at the depot before dawn, the letter already warm in his coat, and by the time any second thought could be dispatched to overtake him, he would be across the Potomac and the promise would be irrevocable — held by men who collected such things the way a creditor collects notes, and who would present it, in due course, for payment.
