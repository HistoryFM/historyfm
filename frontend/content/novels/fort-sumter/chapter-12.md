# Chapter 12: The Virginian

The telegram boy crossed the lobby at a run, and Seward tracked him without appearing to.

Willard's conducted its morning as it always did — office-seekers banked against every upholstered surface, correspondents from the *Tribune* and the *Herald* and a dozen lesser sheets working the pillars with the patient industry of those who had learned that proximity to power was itself a form of intelligence. Cigar smoke hung in horizontal strata beneath the gas chandeliers. Beneath the smoke, the percussion of a city that had not resolved its condition: boot-heels on marble, the sharp crack of a telegram boy's heels, voices pitched at the register of men who wished to be overheard.

Seward had been here since half past nine. It was approaching eleven, and he had not yet breakfasted.

Frederick's note had arrived before the coffee — four lines, handwriting precise, information contained within it rather less so. Lincoln had summoned John Baldwin of Augusta County, delegate to the Virginia convention and among the steadiest Unionist voices in Richmond, to a private meeting at the Executive Mansion. The summons had passed through John Hay's office. Not through the State Department. No notification. No consultation sought.

He moved to the newspaper rack and lifted the morning's *National Intelligencer*, opened it, and read nothing. Through the paper's edge he watched the entrance.

The channel he had spent six weeks constructing — Campbell to the commissioners, Summers to the convention men, the careful triangulation of assurance and implication that had kept Virginia's Unionist caucus from forcing an early vote — that channel ran through him. He had built it to run through him. He had made himself indispensable to it, which was a different article from being indispensable to the President, but close enough, he had judged, for present purposes.

Lincoln had apparently formed a different judgment.

A fit of coughing seized him — the smoke, which at this density was less an atmosphere than a substance — and he stepped into an alcove between a marble column and the rack, pressing his handkerchief to his mouth until it passed. The entrance remained clear.

What Lincoln had offered was the question he could not yet answer. That something had been offered seemed probable; the man did not summon Unionist delegates for the pleasure of their company. The troubling possibility — the one he had been turning since Frederick's note arrived — was that Lincoln had offered precisely what Seward had been promising through his own channels. Evacuation. A withdrawal from the harbour. The concession that would hold Virginia and let the secession fever break of its own insufficient heat.

If that offer had been made and accepted, the back-channel was not merely redundant. It was exposed. Judge Campbell would learn. The commissioners would learn. The men in Richmond who had staked their political lives on the assurance that evacuation was coming — that Seward was the man who would bring it — would learn that the assurance had been given without authority, by a Secretary of State whose government had been simultaneously speaking for itself.

He folded the *Intelligencer* and returned it to the rack with care.

Frederick appeared from the corridor toward the writing rooms, threading through a cluster of War Department clerks with the apologetic efficiency of one trained to move quickly and to look as though he were not.

"Well?" Seward said, low enough that the Baltimore correspondent three feet away could not have caught it.

Frederick's face held the careful neutrality it always held when the news was not good. "He left for the Mansion before nine. I spoke with the desk. He took no breakfast and left no word of return." A pause, in which Frederick looked not at his father but at the middle distance, as though the news required a moment's additional clearance. "He has his bag with him."

His bag. The evening train to Richmond. Five hours, perhaps.

Seward produced a cigar and did not light it. "Then we wait."

---

He had Summers's letter in his coat pocket. He had read it twice on the walk up from the lobby, and once more after Frederick left, and he had understood it the first time. The understanding had not improved upon repetition.

The parlour Willard's had given him was not built for occupation — two armchairs angled toward a cold grate, a side table bearing a brandy decanter, curtains drawn against the afternoon so that the single lamp did all the work of a window and managed a quarter of it. The smell of the room was stale wool and old smoke and the faint sweetness of the brandy's cork, which had not been replaced with care. He stood at the window and then moved away from it, the carpet muffling his steps, and found himself before the mantelpiece.

He drew the letter out and spread it across his palm.

Summers wrote in the manner of a man conscious of the historical record: full sentences, careful qualifications, the prose of a Virginia gentleman trained to believe that elegance of expression conveyed weight of judgment.

*The delegation resolved, after considerable deliberation, to seek direct conference with the President upon the matter of the state's posture toward the federal government. I communicated this determination to the appropriate parties at the Executive Mansion on the thirty-first ultimo, and received assurance that such conference might be arranged through Mr. Baldwin at the President's earliest convenience.*

The thirty-first. Three days before the meeting. Three days during which the Virginia Unionists had been arranging direct access to Lincoln through channels that did not pass through F Street, through Seward's study, through Judge Campbell, through any part of the architecture he had been constructing since the first week of March.

He set the letter on the mantelpiece. Then he picked it up and folded it — once, twice, pressing each crease flat with his thumb — and placed it back in his pocket. The paper was heavy stock and resisted the fold. He pressed harder, the nail leaving a white line that darkened slowly as the pressure released.

Summers had not written to inform him. The letter was a record — a notification, filed with the care one files a deed of transfer, establishing that the transaction had occurred and that Seward had been notified, which was not at all the same as having been party to it. He was being documented. The distinction arrived with the quality of a door closing in a room one had believed oneself to occupy.

Baldwin had not been Seward's man. This was the difficulty he had been declining to state plainly. Baldwin was Summers's man, which Seward had taken to mean the same thing, since Summers was his conduit and his conduit was, by extension, his instrument. He had made the error of confusing the channel with the water it carries.

He crossed to the side table and poured two fingers of brandy. He did not drink it. He set the glass down and looked at it — amber, still, the lamp's reflection floating in it like something trapped — and then he turned back toward the cold grate and began to arrange his questions.

The easy ones first, to establish the ground. What did Lincoln say, and in what order, and where were you sitting, and what was the hour. Then the harder ones. Then the question that mattered.

Baldwin would return by four. Possibly before. If he did not return before the evening train, the arrangement — whatever remained of it — would depart with him for Richmond, and Seward would be left with Summers's letter and a glass of brandy and no account of what had been said in the room where the last negotiable hour of this crisis had been spent.

He sat in the nearer chair. The chair was built for a shorter man; the angle was wrong, and a dull complaint ran across the base of his spine. He did not move.

---

The light through the curtain gaps had shifted to a lower angle by the time Baldwin's step sounded in the corridor — that quality of late afternoon that makes a room feel both smaller and more final, pale bars falling across the floor to within a foot of Seward's boots. A servant had come and gone without speaking, leaving the lamp lit. The brandy glass stood where it had been left, untouched.

Baldwin moved through the doorway with the deliberate unhurriedness of a man who had settled, somewhere between the White House and Willard's, how much he intended to give away. He was still in his overcoat — charcoal wool, the lapels cut with a precision that belonged to a Richmond tailor of the first rank, the stitching at the collar so fine it caught the lamplight. He set his hat on the occasional table by the door and did not sit.

Seward rose before Baldwin could take the room's measure. He moved to the mantelpiece and positioned himself with his back to the lamp, so the light fell on Baldwin's face and not his own.

"Mr. Baldwin."

"Mr. Secretary." The Virginian's eyes moved once around the room — the drawn curtains, the cold grate, the decanter — and returned to Seward with the composed attention of one who has been received in difficult rooms before and knows that the difficulty is rarely in the furniture.

"The President received you this morning."

"He did."

"And you will tell me what passed between you."

Baldwin's chin lifted, fractionally. "The meeting was private, Mr. Secretary. I should think the President himself—"

"The President is not in this room." Seward kept his voice even. "You are. And I will tell you plainly what is at stake in the next five minutes, so that you may weigh it against whatever scruple is presently detaining you." He did not move from the mantelpiece. "I have spent six weeks constructing a channel through which this administration's better intentions might reach Virginia's Unionist delegates. I have done so at considerable cost to my standing within a cabinet that contains men who would gladly see that channel closed and your delegation left to the mercy of the fire-eaters. If the President has spoken to you directly, I do not quarrel with his prerogative. What I require is to know whether what he said to you is consistent with what I have been saying on his behalf — because if it is not, then the men in Richmond who have trusted my word are exposed, and the Unionist cause in your convention is finished before the week is out."

Baldwin's hands were clasped before him. He was silent for a moment. Somewhere in the corridor, a door slammed — hard, sudden — and both men held still, the silence between them stretched across that interval, each waiting for the other to resume.

"He raised the question of Sumter," Baldwin said.

Seward waited.

"He put it as a possibility. Contingent." The word was placed with care. "He suggested the garrison might be withdrawn — as a gesture — if Virginia's convention were to adjourn without a vote on secession."

"And you told him."

"I told him it was insufficient." Baldwin met his eyes. "A gesture is not a policy, Mr. Secretary. The convention will not adjourn on the strength of a possibility. Virginia requires settled assurance — on Sumter, on the disposition of federal forces in the border states, on the whole posture of this administration toward Southern sovereignty. A fort evacuated in the dark of the night, with no public declaration, no formal communication to Richmond—" He paused, and something shifted in his expression — not contempt, but its near neighbour. "That is not a peace offering. That is a stage direction."

A sound reached them from the street below — iron-shod wheels on cobblestone, a horse at a quick trot, fading south toward the avenue.

"You refused him," Seward said.

"I declined to accept what he offered as sufficient."

Seward moved then — away from the mantelpiece, two steps toward the centre of the room, close enough that Baldwin would have to hold his ground or yield it. "Let me describe what you have done. The President made an offer. It was imperfect — I grant you that freely, and I would have told him so myself, had I been consulted, which I was not. But an imperfect offer, properly received and returned with conditions, is a negotiation. An offer declined outright is a door shut." He did not wait for Baldwin to answer. "Virginia's Unionist caucus has three friends in this cabinet. I am the first. The Postmaster General is not among the three. The Secretary of the Treasury is actively hostile to everything your delegation represents. If I cannot return to those men with something — some thread of continued engagement — then the question before your convention will be answered by Blair's faction and Chase's faction between them, and you know what answer they will give."

Baldwin looked at him steadily. Then, with the quietness of a man setting down a weight he has carried some distance: "I know what you are suggesting, Mr. Secretary."

"I am describing arithmetic."

"Then permit me to offer some in return." One step forward — not a retreat, not a concession, a repositioning — and his voice dropped a register, the sound of it flattening in the curtained air. "Your assurances have been arriving in Richmond for six weeks. And alongside them, contradicting them, there have been other assurances. From other sources within this administration. Different in their substance — different in what they promise and what they threaten." He paused. "The delegates no longer know which voice speaks for the President. They have begun to wonder, Mr. Secretary, whether your channel carries water, or whether it is simply a channel." He retrieved his hat from the side table. "Good evening."

He was through the door before Seward could frame a reply — or had decided not to wait for one, which in the event amounted to the same consequence.

The door did not slam. It closed with the soft, definitive sound of a thing that cannot be reopened from the outside.

---

The lamp had burned low. Seward sat in the wrong-angled chair and did not move to correct it.

He had poured the brandy sometime after Baldwin's step faded down the corridor. He held the glass without drinking — the amber catching what remained of the light, the smell of it reaching him, warm and faintly medicinal. He drank it. Set the glass on the floor beside the chair, where it would not catch the light and remind him it was empty.

The April first memorandum returned to him, as it had been returning at intervals all afternoon. *If this must be done, I must do it.* Lincoln's voice, stripped of its prairie courtesies — the voice of a man who had settled on the one punishment that cannot be appealed: clarity. Three days ago, in that anteroom, Seward had been shown the precise dimensions of his own authority. Not the authority he had imagined, not the authority he had spent three months constructing through implication and back-channel and the patient cultivation of men who needed to believe he spoke for the administration — but the actual authority, which was narrower than he had permitted himself to examine, and more conditional, and entirely dependent on a President who had just demonstrated that he could act without consulting his Secretary of State.

He had walked out of that anteroom believing he had absorbed the lesson.

Baldwin's departure had clarified that he had not.

What he had built — the careful triangulation of assurance and implication, the six weeks of correspondence and cultivation — had rested, all along, on the assumption that he was the indispensable intermediary. Baldwin had just delivered a surveyor's report on that assumption: the delegates in Richmond did not know which voice spoke for Lincoln. Which meant they had concluded, as Baldwin had concluded, that Seward's voice might not.

He rose from the chair — the spine's complaint returning, duller now, a familiar grievance — and went to the writing table in the corner. He drew a sheet of paper from the case, dipped the pen, and wrote at the top: *For the attention of the Acting Secretary, Navy Department.* Then he stopped.

The Powhatan was at the Brooklyn Navy Yard. Fox's expedition was being assembled. Within days, the relief ships would sail — carrying the administration's first direct assertion that the garrison at Fort Sumter belonged to the United States of America and that the United States intended to sustain it. One order, routed through the right channel to the right officer, could redirect the Powhatan to the Florida operation and leave Fox's mission without the naval force it required. The expedition would proceed. It would simply be insufficient. The fort would not be relieved. The crisis would continue — and in that continuation, some further negotiation might yet be possible, some further delay purchased, some thread of the arrangement that Baldwin had just severed might be retied by other hands.

He could do it. He had done comparable things. It was the work he had done all his life — the management of events through the careful placement of orders in the spaces between authorities.

He set the pen down.

He sat for a time with his hands flat on the table, the paper before him, the lamp's heat against the left side of his face. The room smelled of old smoke and the brandy's ghost and the cold ash in the grate, and beneath those smells, faintly, the particular smell of a city in early April — mud and horse and the green edge of something beginning, indifferent to the uses men were making of the season.

What he had told himself, these three months, was that he understood the situation better than Lincoln. That the President was a man of the interior — capable, perhaps, in ways that had not yet been demonstrated, but untested in the specific discipline of managing a constitutional crisis at the edge of war, where the difference between a negotiation and a provocation was a matter of days and the placement of a single vessel. He had told himself that his own experience — thirty years of it, the Senate, the governorship, the long patient cultivation of the Republican coalition — constituted a form of authority that the President's commission did not automatically supersede.

*They have begun to wonder whether your channel carries water.*

He had believed himself to be the voice that carried water. He had believed it with the certainty of long habit, which is the most dangerous certainty of all — the kind that does not announce itself as certainty but simply as the shape of things.

He took up a fresh sheet — clean, unaddressed — and dipped the pen again.

*My dear Frances,*

He wrote it and stopped. His hand cramped without warning, the muscles seizing between the second and third knuckle, and he set the pen down and worked his fingers against the table's edge, pressing each joint in turn. The pain passed. He picked up the pen.

*I write to you tonight from a room I have occupied for some hours and am not yet prepared to leave.*

He stopped again. Through the window — the curtains had not been drawn on this side — the avenue was visible in the last of the daylight, a carriage moving at an ordinary trot, a woman crossing with her skirts held clear of the mud, a boy selling papers at the corner with the mechanical persistence of the very cold. The city continuing. The city entirely without interest in whatever was being decided in the parlours of Willard's Hotel.

*The matters before us are of the gravest consequence. I find that I have been, in certain particulars, wrong — and that the cost of being wrong is not mine alone to bear. I do not yet know what I shall do with that knowledge. I know only that I am sitting with it tonight, and that it is heavier than I had anticipated, and that I am glad you are not here to see me sitting with it.*

He did not read back what he had written.

The knock came at the third repetition — he had not heard the first two. A porter, apologetic, with a sealed document on a small tray. The seal bore the Executive Mansion's mark.

He looked at it for a moment without taking it. Then he took it, and turned it over, and felt the weight of it — not heavy, a single sheet within, perhaps two — and set it on the table beside the letter to Frances.

The Navy Department sheet was still there. Still addressed. Still blank below the heading.

He had not opened the Mansion's letter. He had not yet decided about the other. But he understood, with a cold and sudden clarity that had nothing to do with the lamp's warmth, that both decisions were the same decision — that the man who countersigned the Navy Department order and the man who did not were not the same man, and that he was about to discover, before this night was finished, which one he was.

Outside, the paper-boy's voice carried up from the corner, thin and persistent in the cold April air: *Extra! Extra!* — the words without content yet, the cry that preceded the news, the moment before the thing itself arrived and could not be taken back.
