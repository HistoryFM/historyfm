# Chapter Sixteen: The Notification

The circular had been going badly since nine o'clock.

Seward had the text of Lincoln's notification before him — the copy Robert Chew had carried south, now returned through the department's channels as a matter of record — and beside it the half-drafted circular intended for American ministers abroad. The circular was meant to explain. It was meant to place the notification within a framework of continued negotiation, to suggest to the courts of London and Paris and Vienna that the administration's action represented not a provocation but a patient extension of its constitutional authority, consistent with the possibility of a peaceful resolution. He had written three paragraphs in this vein. They were, he recognised, accomplished paragraphs. They were also, in a precise and unambiguous sense, false.

He read the notification's central sentence again.

*An attempt will be made to supply Fort Sumter with provisions only; and that, if such attempt be not resisted, no effort to throw in men, arms, or ammunition will be made.*

Somewhere below on Fifteenth Street a cartman's wheel struck a loose stone, the crack of iron on cobble carrying cleanly through the glass. Then the ordinary morning resumed: the scratch of the department clerk's pen through the anteroom wall, the smell of coal smoke from the grate mixing with the stale coffee that had sat untouched since half past eight, the cold working upward through the carpet's weave from the floorboards beneath. He had not rung for the boy to tend the fire. He had not rung for anything.

The sentence was extraordinary. He did not want to think so, but the thought arrived whole and would not be revised. In twenty-three words, Lincoln had constructed a position from which no diplomatic arrangement could extricate the Confederacy. If the Montgomery authorities permitted the provisions to reach Anderson's garrison, they acknowledged the garrison's legitimacy and surrendered their claim that the harbour was sovereign Confederate territory. If they resisted — if Beauregard's batteries opened on an unarmed supply vessel carrying bread — they became, before every court in Europe, the authors of the war. The choice of first fire rested entirely in Southern hands, and Lincoln had placed it there not through cunning but through a simplicity of moral logic so plain that Seward's own months of elaborate manoeuvring now appeared, by comparison, as the work of a man who had confused complexity with intelligence.

His pen was still in his hand. He set it down.

He looked at the three paragraphs of the circular. The first argued that the notification was consonant with the administration's desire for a peaceful resolution. The second suggested that the resupply attempt, being limited to provisions, represented a gesture of restraint rather than coercion. The third — and here the pen had faltered, had produced a subordinate clause that contradicted itself, had been scratched through and begun again — attempted to characterise the notification as an invitation to further negotiation rather than a terminus of it.

He drew a single line through the half-finished page and turned it face-down.

The crick in his neck from two nights of poor sleep chose this moment to reassert itself, a sharp complaint along the right trapezius that forced him upright and then out of his chair entirely. He stood, rolled his head once to the left, felt nothing give, and began to pace — six steps to the window overlooking the street, six steps back. The April morning outside was pallid, the sky the colour of unbleached linen. A dray made slow progress northward, its driver hunched against a wind that did not know the season had changed.

He stopped at the window. He pressed one hand flat against the glass and felt the cold come through it, the city's indifferent chill against his palm.

Lincoln had ended four months of uncertainty with twenty-three words. He had given the South a clear choice and made the terms of that choice legible to every foreign observer who might otherwise have been persuaded that the federal government was the aggressor. Seward's strategy had always required the other party's cooperation in remaining uncertain — had required the Confederacy to continue believing that time might bring them what they wanted without the necessity of force. Lincoln had not negotiated that uncertainty. He had dissolved it.

Seward returned to his desk. He stood over the overturned page of the circular, then crossed to the drawer beneath the blotter, folded the three paragraphs once along the horizontal, and placed them inside. He would not send it. Whatever he told London and Paris, it would not be those paragraphs. He closed the drawer with more force than he intended, and the sound of it carried through the anteroom wall, and the clerk's pen went briefly silent.

He was still standing there when Frederick came in.

---

Frederick came in carrying the cold of the street on his coat and a folded letter in his hand, and Seward read his son's face before the boy had crossed half the room — the particular compression around the mouth that Frederick had inherited from his mother and deployed, as she had, only when he was uncertain whether the news he bore would be received as news or as accusation.

'From Richmond,' Frederick said. 'Private conveyance.'

Seward did not move toward him. 'How many signatories?'

'Summers leads. Three others whose names you will recognise.' Frederick held the letter out. When his father did not immediately take it, he crossed the remaining distance and placed it on the desk's edge. 'There is also a note from the President. It arrived while you were with the circular. I left it on your blotter.'

Seward looked at the note — a single folded sheet in Lincoln's hand, the letters formed with the unhurried deliberateness of a man who writes slowly because he means every word. He set his palm upon it without opening it and took up the letter from Summers instead.

The paper was good Virginia stock, heavier than government issue. He broke the seal and read it standing. The language was formal — these were men who understood what they committed to paper — but beneath the formality the accusation was as naked as any he had received in thirty years of public life. *We had understood from repeated assurances conveyed through channels we now see reason to question that the administration's tendency was toward evacuation. The notification delivered to Governor Pickens has rendered our position in the convention untenable. We do not accuse you, Mr. Secretary, of deliberate misrepresentation. We ask only whether the representations made to us in your name reflected any authority you possessed to make them.*

He folded it along its original crease.

'Read the passage beginning *we ask only*,' he said.

Frederick took the letter, found the place, and read it aloud. His voice was careful and even, carrying no editorial weight. When he finished, he held the letter at his side and waited, and Seward was aware — with the acuity that attends moments of humiliation — that his son was not waiting for instruction. He was waiting to see whether his father would break.

Seward turned his chair away from the desk and faced the window. The light on Fifteenth Street had changed since morning — lower now, catching the upper stories of the buildings opposite, leaving the street itself in early shadow.

'Father.' Frederick's voice had dropped a register. Not the voice he used in the anteroom, not the voice for clerks and messengers. The voice from the house on Auburn's hill, from evenings when the fire was low and the boys were meant to be asleep and were not.

'Is it not possible,' Seward said, to the glass, 'that they are right?'

A silence. Not the clerk's performed silence — a real one, the kind that costs something to hold.

'What do you wish me to write to them?'

Seward turned. Frederick was watching him with an expression that was not pity — his son had too much of his father in him for pity — but something adjacent to it, something that acknowledged the distance between what a man intends and what he brings about. Seward crossed to the desk and took the letter from Frederick's hand. He held it a moment, feeling the weight of the Virginia paper, and then set it beside Lincoln's note.

'The truth,' he said. 'That the policy has changed. That I represented a tendency I could not guarantee. That I regret they are exposed.' He paused. 'And that there is no recourse remaining to them. Tell them that plainly. They are grown men and the courtesy of honesty is the only courtesy that remains to offer.'

'They will ask why the policy changed without your knowledge.'

'They already know why.' Seward sat. He pulled the blotter toward him and looked at the two documents side by side — Summers's letter and Lincoln's note, the accusation and whatever the President had chosen to say on this particular morning. 'I told them what I believed to be true, and what I believed I could make true, and those were not the same thing. I did not examine the difference with sufficient care.' He looked up at Frederick. 'Write that, in terms that do not require them to publish it in the *Enquirer*.'

Frederick did not move immediately. He was still watching his father with that careful, measuring attention — the look of a son who has spent years learning to read a man who does not easily permit himself to be read, and who has, on this particular afternoon, succeeded.

'Send the reply to Summers first,' Seward said. 'Then come back.'

'And the President's note?'

'After.' He placed his palm over it again. 'Go, Frederick.'

Frederick went. At the door he paused, and for a moment Seward thought he would say something further — some word that the occasion seemed to require, some filial acknowledgment of what had just passed between them. But Frederick only pulled the door closed behind him, quietly, as one closes a door on a room where someone is sleeping, and Seward sat alone with the two documents and the smell of coal smoke and the diminishing afternoon.

He did not open Lincoln's note. Not yet. He had perhaps an hour before Rutherford arrived, and he intended to use it.

---

The intermediary's name was Rutherford. He had been a Richmond lawyer before the conventions made such distinctions impossible to maintain, and he carried himself with the particular erectness of someone resolved that bearing should serve where authority no longer could. He arrived at ten minutes past four, unannounced, the anteroom clerk having been told to admit him without delay.

Seward heard him before he saw him — the decisive report of boot heels on the corridor's oak boards, a cadence that communicated neither haste nor deference. He was already on his feet when Rutherford entered, and he had moved to the window, putting the desk between them and the door, so that Rutherford would have to cross the room to reach him.

'Mr. Secretary.'

'Mr. Rutherford.' Seward gestured toward the chair across the desk. 'Will you sit.'

Rutherford sat, drawing his coat closed with one hand and placing a leather case across his knees with the other. He was perhaps fifty, with careful eyes and the unhurried manner of someone long accustomed to rooms where the wrong word costs something irreplaceable. The smell of tobacco clung to his coat — not the cheap Virginia leaf of the enlisted man but the darker, denser variety that comes from a case kept close to the body. The lamp on the desk had been burning since before two o'clock; whatever oil remained was insufficient to the hour, and the light it cast reached no further than the desk's edge, leaving Rutherford's face in the amber half-dark beyond.

'I will not detain you long,' Rutherford said.

'You may detain me as long as the matter requires.' Seward came around the desk and sat. He did not offer refreshment. He folded his hands on the blotter and waited — which was not his customary posture in this room, and which he suspected Rutherford noted.

Rutherford opened the case. He produced a single folded sheet and held it, not yet extending it.

'Judge Campbell has asked me to convey his personal regard,' he said, 'before conveying his official communication.'

'I receive both with equal appreciation.'

'I wonder,' Rutherford said, 'whether you will.' He placed the sheet on the desk's surface and pushed it forward with two fingers — deliberate, unhurried. 'Judge Campbell requests that you read it in my presence.'

Seward took it. He knew the weight of Campbell's paper before he opened it, the cream stock the judge favoured, the precise fold. He opened it and read.

The words were his own. He knew them before he reached the end of the first sentence, knew them the way a man knows the sound of his own voice in an unexpected room. *Faith as to Sumter fully kept; wait and see.* Four words and a fragment, rendered in Campbell's meticulous hand, dated the first of April. Below them, in Campbell's formal notation, a brief accounting of the circumstances under which they had been delivered — the hour, the meeting, the understanding conveyed to the commissioners that the garrison's evacuation remained the settled tendency of the administration.

Below that, a single question in Campbell's hand: *What answer is Mr. Secretary now prepared to give?*

Seward set the paper down. He did not set it face-down.

'The circumstances,' he began, 'which obtained at the time of that communication have since—'

'Mr. Secretary.' Rutherford had not moved, had not altered the quality of his attention by so much as a degree. His voice carried no anger. That was the worst of it — not anger, not contempt, but the flat patience of someone who has heard this particular opening before and knows precisely where it does not go. 'The commissioners transmitted your assurance to President Davis on the third of April. It was used as the basis for restraining military preparations around the harbour. Three days of restraint, purchased at the cost of Judge Campbell's personal credibility and the commissioners' formal representations to their government.' He opened the leather case again and produced a second document — thicker, bound with a ribbon. He set it beside the first. 'The commissioners have prepared the full correspondence for publication. Every communication. Every assurance. Every date.' He left his hand resting on the bound pages. 'Before they release it, Judge Campbell wished you to have the opportunity to offer some explanation. That opportunity exists at this moment, and it will not exist again after I leave this room.'

The word *explanation* fell into the room and stayed there.

Seward looked at the ribbon-bound correspondence. He looked at Rutherford's hand resting upon it — large, steady, the hand of someone who has argued cases before juries and understands the weight of physical evidence. He looked at his own hands on the blotter.

The machinery that had served him across thirty years — the subordinate clause deployed at precisely the right moment, the graceful pivot, the digression that arrived, somehow, at a more defensible position — all of it assembled itself and found nothing to work with. There was no formulation that was not either a lie compounded upon the original lie or a confession that would follow him for whatever remained of his career. He was standing at the edge of a geometry that contained no third option.

He said nothing.

The silence extended. Rutherford did not fill it. He waited, his hand still resting on the correspondence, and the lamp burned at its contracted height, and somewhere in the building below a door opened and closed, and the smell of the tobacco from Rutherford's coat reached across the space between them — dark and sweet and entirely without mercy.

Seward's hand moved toward the bell on the desk — the habitual reach toward the mechanism of a summoned clerk — and then withdrew, coming to rest open-palmed upon the mahogany, as though he had set down something he no longer possessed the right to carry.

Rutherford rose. He collected Campbell's sheet, folded it along its original crease, and returned it to the case. The bound correspondence he left where it was.

'I shall convey your response to Judge Campbell,' he said.

He collected his hat from the side table, settled it with the unhurried precision of someone who has completed the errand he was sent to complete, and walked to the door.

'Mr. Rutherford.'

The man paused, his hand on the frame.

'Tell the Judge,' Seward said, 'that I will write to him directly. Before the week is out.'

Rutherford turned. His face held nothing that could be called satisfaction, and nothing that could be called mercy. 'Mr. Secretary, the week may not afford you that interval. Beauregard's batteries have been trained on the fort since Monday last. The commissioners have received word from Montgomery that the notification will be treated as a declaration of war.' He let that settle. 'The correspondence' — he indicated the pages on the desk — 'will be published when the first gun fires. Not before, and not long after.' He settled his hat a final degree. 'I thought you should hear it said plainly, sir. From someone who was present in those rooms.'

He left. His boot heels marked his progress down the corridor, diminishing, and then the outer door opened and closed, and the sound was gone.

On the carpet, perhaps eighteen inches from the leg of the chair he had occupied, a small deposit of ash lay where it had fallen from his coat — a grey crescent, barely visible in the lamp's contracted circle. The tobacco smell reached Seward now, as it had not while the man was present.

He looked at the ribbon-bound pages sitting on his desk. Rutherford had left them deliberately. Not an oversight. A reminder of what would be published, and when, and in whose words.

He reached across and turned the pages face-down on the blotter. Then he picked up Lincoln's note and opened it.

---

He read it twice. Then he set it beside the commissioners' record and sat with both documents before him and the night pressing at the windows and the lamp burning at its reduced, honest height.

Lincoln's note was brief. It did not gloat. It did not instruct. It said only that the notification had been delivered as planned, that the relief expedition was proceeding, and that the President would be grateful for the Secretary's continued attention to the diplomatic correspondence with London, Paris, and Vienna, which would require, in the coming days, the most careful management the department could provide. The final sentence read: *I have no doubt that you will find the right words, Mr. Secretary, as you always have.*

He folded the note once and laid it flat upon the desk.

He had said something very like what he was thinking now to Thurlow Weed, in the study on F Street, in the weeks before the convention — something about having built this party, having earned this office, having no intention of playing second to a man who had never administered so much as a county court. He could not recall the precise words, but the substance was accurate enough to serve as a verdict on the man who had spoken them.

He drew a fresh sheet of paper from the drawer. He held it a moment, feeling the weight of the stock, the slight roughness at one edge where the ream had been imprecisely cut. From the corridor outside, the night clerk's footsteps moved past and receded. The building settled around him into its nocturnal quiet.

He set the pen to the paper.

*To the President of the United States — Sir:*

He stopped. He looked at the two words of salutation and understood that whatever followed them would constitute, in some sense he could not yet fully articulate, the most consequential document he had composed in thirty years of public life — not because it would be read by foreign courts or entered into the record of the Senate, but because it would be read by one man, in private, and that man would know whether it was true.

He began again.

*Having reflected upon the notification delivered this day to Governor Pickens, and upon its consequences — which I had predicted would be grave, and which I now believe may prove otherwise — I write to offer what I could not, until this afternoon, bring myself to offer: an acknowledgment that I was wrong.*

His hand had cramped from the tension of holding the pen, the knuckles stiffened. He laid it aside, pressed his fingers flat against the desk's surface until the joints protested, and released them. Then he continued.

*The language of the notification is such that the burden of the first act of violence rests entirely upon the Confederate authorities. This is a construction I did not foresee, and did not counsel. I had believed the object was achievable by other means — by the delay of confrontation, by the cultivation of Southern Unionist sentiment, by the preservation of a negotiating space that force must necessarily close. I argued for these means with conviction, and I do not recant the sincerity of that argument.*

He paused. The lamp guttered once, recovered, burned on.

*But the President's method has achieved what my method could not: it has placed the question of war or peace in Confederate hands, and in doing so has established the moral ground upon which any subsequent action must be justified. For this I was not prepared to give him credit, and for this omission I can offer no defence that a man of candour would accept.*

He put the pen down. He read what he had written. He did not cross it out.

The commissioners' record sat at the corner of the desk. If it were published before he could reach Campbell directly, the account would be entirely theirs: the assurances given, the assurances broken, the Secretary of State's silence in the face of a question he could not answer. There would be no version of events in which that silence read as anything other than guilt, because it was guilt, and Campbell's meticulous hand had dated it and folded it into cream stock and sent it south by private conveyance.

He needed to write to Campbell tonight.

He folded the letter to Lincoln once, along the horizontal, and then again along the vertical, making it a compact square. He did not place it in the drawer. He set it on the blotter where he would see it when he returned.

He drew another sheet from the drawer.

*My dear

Judge — he had written three words when the sound reached him: the telegraph key in the communications room three doors down the corridor, beginning its rapid and unceasing percussion against the night's silence. Not a test transmission. Not the routine close-of-day traffic. A second key commenced, overlapping the first, both instruments running together in the manner that obtained only when the traffic was urgent and the operators had been instructed not to wait for the line to clear.

His hand went still upon the paper.

He did not rise immediately. He sat listening to the doubled percussion fill the corridor, and he understood — before any messenger came, before any door opened, before any word was spoken — that the letter to Campbell would not be written tonight. That whatever remained of his position in this administration would be settled not by correspondence but by the arithmetic of artillery.

He looked at the unfinished letter to Lincoln on the blotter. *I am, sir, with full acknowledgment of his superior judgment in this matter —*

The sentence had no ending he was yet capable of writing.

Then from somewhere below came the sound of boots upon the staircase — not the night clerk's measured tread but something faster, someone taking the steps two at a time — and Seward's hand came off the paper and he was already turning toward the door when Frederick appeared in the frame, his coat still on, his face carrying the particular pallor of a young man who has read something he cannot unread.

'The wire,' Frederick said. 'From Charleston.'

He held the transcript at arm's length, as though proximity to it were a thing to be managed. Behind him, in the corridor, a third key had joined the first two.
