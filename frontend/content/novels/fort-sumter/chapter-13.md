# Chapter 13: Provisions Only

## Scene 1

The clock in the anteroom had struck ten before Lincoln found what he was looking for, and he was not certain, even then, that he had found it.

Fox's memorandum lay before him on the second-floor desk, flanked by Anderson's dispatch on his left and the five cabinet opinions stacked on his right — five separate men, five separate arguments, arriving at the same verdict with the unanimity of a jury that has agreed upon its answer before the foreman rises. Seward's was the most polished: three pages of subordinate clauses, each one reinforcing the last, building toward the conclusion that any attempt to provision the garrison would drive Virginia from her present course and manufacture the very war the administration sought to avoid. Cameron's was the shortest, four sentences, its brevity its own form of finality. Welles had written at length about naval capacity and political consequence, the two subjects braided together by someone who wished his objection to read as expertise rather than caution.

He did not read them again. He had read them twice already, and the words had not changed.

He turned Fox's memorandum over in his hands, creasing the corner with his thumb — the paper softening at the fold, the edge going faintly warm from the repeated contact — before finally setting it flat on the desk. The lamp threw its light across Fox's careful figures: the *Baltic*, the *Powhatan*, the tugs, the distances, the tidal windows. A workmanlike document, the product of an officer who had measured a problem and described his measurements without ornamentation.

Anderson's dispatch lay beneath his left palm. He could feel the slight ridge of the major's pen strokes through the paper, the pressure of a man writing in a cold room at the edge of the continent, measuring his words the way a quartermaster measures flour. *I am compelled to say that I am not yet prepared to decide what course to pursue.* And then the figure that sat in every calculation Lincoln attempted: twenty thousand men. Twenty thousand to hold Fort Sumter against a determined assault.

He pressed two fingers to the back of his neck and stood.

The crick had been building since eight o'clock, the muscles along his right shoulder seizing from hours bent over the desk, and when he straightened it came on sharply — a hard, specific pain that demanded his attention and got it. He crossed to the window. Below, Pennsylvania Avenue lay in silence, the gas lamps throwing their pale intervals across the stone. He stood there long enough for the pain to ease, watching a single lamp gutter and recover in the wind, and then he turned away from the glass and went back to the desk and sat down.

He found the passage he wanted in Fox's memorandum. Tugs running the harbor entrance at night, provisions off-loaded directly to the wharf. Not a military operation. A delivery.

He set the memorandum down and did not take it up again.

What the cabinet had not asked — what Anderson, God bless him, had not thought to ask — was the prior question, the one that sat beneath the rest like bedrock beneath clay. Not *how do we hold the fort?* Not *how do we hold Virginia?* But simply: *what does the garrison actually need to survive the next two weeks?*

Not twenty thousand men. Not reinforcement. Bread.

And if the Confederacy chose to fire upon bread — if they trained their guns on flour and salt pork and the men who carried them — then the choice of war would belong entirely to Charleston, and the record would say so, and the North would read that record, and the border states would read it too, and the question of who had chosen this catastrophe would admit of only one answer.

Lincoln reached for the pen. His thumbnail left a pale crescent on the memorandum's margin as he positioned the nib. He wrote two words in the white space beside Fox's third paragraph, pressing the letters down with the deliberate economy of long reckoning arrived at last.

*Provisions only.*

He set the pen in its stand. He looked at what he had written.

The lamp hissed faintly. Somewhere in the house a door settled in its frame. He sat with the silence and the smell of ink drying and the full weight of what he had just committed himself to, and he did not move for a long time — not from indecision, but because the decision had arrived and he wanted to feel its full dimension before the morning came and made it irreversible.

## Scene 2

The Cabinet Room held the particular brightness of a space that has not yet earned its morning — sunlight through the tall south windows falling too sharply across the long table, leaving the far chairs in shadow that two unlit lamps did nothing to remedy. Lincoln had arrived before either man, which was as he intended.

Welles sat across from Blair with the careful posture of someone who has prepared remarks. He was a methodical New Englander, white-bearded and watchful, and he had brought a memorandum — Lincoln could see it squared precisely before him on the blotter, its edges aligned with the table's edge in the way of a man who arranges his materials to signal that he has thought the matter through. Blair had brought nothing but himself, which, in Blair's case, was generally sufficient.

"Gentlemen," Lincoln said, and placed both hands flat on the table, fingers spread. "I am going to tell you what I have decided. Then I am going to tell you what I need from each of you. In that order."

A cart horse passed in the street below, iron shoes deliberate on the paving, the sound coming through the window's slight opening — unhurried, indifferent to whatever was being resolved above it.

"The expedition will carry provisions only. No arms. No reinforcements beyond the men required to manage the vessels. If Charleston fires upon bread, the burden of that choice belongs to Charleston alone." Lincoln looked at Welles. "Secretary Welles — the *Pawnee*, the *Pocahontas*, the *Baltic*, and the *Powhatan*, readied at Brooklyn within the week."

Welles did not consult his memorandum. He had already consulted it. "Mr. President," he said, his voice measured, precise rather than quarrelsome, "I cannot issue preparatory orders to the Brooklyn Navy Yard on verbal instruction alone. The department requires written authorization specifying the expedition's scope, its chain of command, and — I must be plain about this — a clear statement that the Navy Department is not to be held responsible should the mission provoke hostilities. Without that document in hand, I have no ground to stand on before a congressional committee, and congressional committees, in my experience, are the first consequence of any enterprise that does not go as planned."

Blair made a sound in his throat. Lincoln raised one hand slightly from the table — a small motion, but Blair went quiet.

"Mr. Secretary." Lincoln's voice did not change. "I hear you. The written order will come, and you will have my hand on it within forty-eight hours." He paused. "But I want to put a question to you directly. If Major Anderson surrenders from hunger while ships sit idle at the Brooklyn yard because this department waited upon a document — who bears the responsibility for that?"

The room absorbed the question. A door opened somewhere in the corridor, then closed.

"The greater dereliction," Lincoln said, "is not action taken on a President's verbal authority. It is inaction taken while a garrison starves and the Union's claim upon its own property dissolves without a shot." He looked at Welles steadily. "Begin preparations this morning. I will sign the written order before you need to recall a single vessel."

Welles looked at his memorandum. Then he looked at Lincoln. Something in the room had shifted its weight, and both men felt it.

"Forty-eight hours," Welles said at last.

Blair leaned forward. "And reinforcements — if the provisioning is resisted, we ought to—"

"That is the order, Mr. Blair." Lincoln did not raise his voice. "Provisions only. We will not give them more than they require to fire first."

Blair sat back. He looked at the table. He said nothing further, which was its own form of assent.

## Scene 3

The fire in the grate had burned to coals by the time Seward finished speaking, and the room had grown cold at the walls. Lincoln rose from behind the desk without ceremony, crossed to the hearth, and added a log himself — the birch catching after a moment with a faint hiss, throwing orange into the room's amber gloom. He brushed his hands together once and returned to his chair.

Seward drew from his breast pocket four folded pages and set them on the desk's edge, pushing them forward with two fingers.

"I have set down my thoughts with some care," Seward said. "The substance, I believe, will repay the President's attention."

Lincoln took up the pages and read. He did not read quickly. The room settled around the silence — the birch snapping once in the grate, rain pressing at the window glass. Seward sat with his hands folded, his posture composed, though his right thumb moved in a slow, repeated arc across the knuckle of his left forefinger — the only visible evidence that the composure was maintained rather than native.

Four pages in a clear, formal hand. Lincoln read every clause. He read the proposal for a foreign quarrel with Spain or France as a mechanism of national reunion. He read the observation that the administration had, as yet, no domestic policy worthy of the name. He read, without visible alteration of expression, the passage which proposed that if the President were unwilling to direct such a policy himself, the task might devolve upon some member of the cabinet — that member's name conspicuously absent from the page though present in every syllable.

Lincoln folded the memorandum once and placed it in his breast pocket.

"The essential point, as I have endeavored to convey—"

"I have read it."

A beat of silence.

"Mr. President, the memorandum represents six weeks of—"

"I said I have read it, Mr. Seward." Lincoln set his elbows on the desk and leaned forward, the lamp between them throwing his shadow up the wall. "Now I will put you a question. If the Sumter expedition sails without a policy — without, as you have written, any domestic policy worthy of the name — and if it fails, and if Anderson surrenders, and if this administration is then asked by the Congress and by the country who bears the responsibility: what name appears in that accounting?"

Seward's thumb had ceased its movement.

"Yours," Lincoln said. "If the cabinet governs and the cabinet fails, it is the cabinet that answers. But if the President governs and fails, it is the President who answers — and the cabinet that endures." The birch shifted in the grate, sending a brief column of sparks up the flue. "I am offering you the better position, Mr. Seward. I am asking only that you allow me to occupy the worse one."

Seward regarded him with an attention that was not concession but recalculation. He reached across the desk and extended one hand, palm upward. "The memorandum, if you please."

"It is in my pocket."

"Mr. President, that document contains—"

"If this administration is to have a policy, the making of it belongs to the President." Lincoln assembled the sentence with a deliberateness that left no interval for interruption. "If that policy requires a man to answer for it before this country, or before history, that man must be the President. The executive power of these United States does not reside in the cabinet. It does not reside in the Secretary of State. It resides here."

He did not touch the desk. He did not gesture toward himself. He let the sentence stand.

Seward's hand withdrew. He looked at Lincoln for a long moment — the look of a chess player who has staked a great deal on a single combination and watched it turned aside with a courtesy so complete it admitted no appeal.

"Then the President," Seward said, with measured evenness, "will of course direct as he sees fit."

"He will."

Outside, the rain had steadied into something more purposeful. Seward rose and gathered his hat from the side table. He did not ask for the memorandum again.

Lincoln watched him go. The door closed. The fire crackled once and fell quiet, and Lincoln sat alone with the coals and the rain and the knowledge that genuine defeat announces itself—some small signal of compliance offered on the way out the door, a yielding visible in the set of the shoulders or the pace of the step. Seward had offered nothing. The quiet that followed him down the corridor was not the quiet of surrender. It was the quiet of calculation already resumed.

## Scene 4

The War Department smelled of tobacco ash and damp wool and something older beneath both — the mustiness of files accumulated through administrations, wars, and peacetimes without once being culled. Cameron sat behind his desk with the bearing of an officer who had arrived early specifically so that he would not appear to have hurried. Fox stood near the window with his charts rolled under one arm and his hat in the other hand, neither sitting nor quite at attention, inhabiting the uncertain territory between officer and petitioner.

Lincoln did not sit. He moved to the desk's near edge and stopped there, his full height making the room seem lower than it was.

"Mr. President." Cameron's voice carried the warmth of one who has resolved to be agreeable until the precise moment he is not. "I had expected this conversation some days ago."

"Then we are both gratified it has arrived." Lincoln drew a folded document from his coat and placed it on the desk. "The expedition sails from New York within the week. Commander Fox commands it. The *Baltic* carries the provisions. Three armed escorts. Two hundred regular Army to accompany."

Cameron did not touch the document. He looked at it the way a man looks at a bill he disputes the amount of.

"I was not consulted," he said. "This department was not consulted. I sit in this cabinet, Mr. President, as the officer responsible for the Army, and I am presented with a requisition for two hundred of its men as though I were a regimental quartermaster."

"You are consulted now."

"That is not consultation. That is notification." Cameron's colour had risen. He was a Pennsylvania man in his build and his grievance both, thick through the shoulders, accustomed to the room's deference. "I have been Secretary of War for less than five weeks. My authority here requires establishment, and it cannot be established if the President circumvents it at the first occasion requiring military cooperation."

Fox shifted slightly at the window. The charts made a dry sound against his coat.

The stove in the corner had been fed too generously; the room pressed its heat against every surface. Lincoln felt the warmth at the back of his collar and did not acknowledge it.

"Mr. Secretary," he said, "I will tell you plainly what I require. I require those two hundred men requisitioned this morning, so that Commander Fox may depart Washington before nightfall and reach New York in time to assemble his squadron. Major Anderson's flour will not outlast a prolonged discussion of departmental prerogative. That is what I require."

"And I require acknowledgment that this department was not—"

"What you require," Lincoln said, "is a decision." He pressed two fingers against the document's edge, not pushing it, simply marking it. "You may sign the requisition and serve as Secretary of War in this matter. Or you may decline, and I will find a man who will sign it, and you may consider what that signifies for your tenure."

The stove ticked against the iron. Cameron's jaw moved once. He looked at the document, then at Fox, then at Lincoln's hand at the paper's edge. He reached for his pen.

Then he set it down.

"I will sign it," Cameron said, "when the President acknowledges, in writing, that this requisition was made at his personal direction and that the department acted under executive compulsion. I will not carry the consequence of this expedition upon my record without that protection."

Fox did not move at the window.

Lincoln looked at Cameron — looked at him with the patience of a man who has anticipated this exact obstacle and prepared accordingly. He reached into his coat and produced a second sheet, already folded, and placed it beside the requisition.

"That document bears my signature and states precisely what you have described. I prepared it this morning." He let that settle. "I ask only that you sign before Commander Fox loses the afternoon tide."

Cameron unfolded the presidential letter. He read it. He read it again. Something crossed his face — not quite surprise, not quite relief, but the expression of a man who has demanded a concession and received it whole and is now uncertain whether he has won or been managed. He took up his pen and signed the requisition with the slow care of a man ensuring the record will show he was not hurried.

Fox stepped forward and collected both documents before the ink had finished drying.

Lincoln turned toward the door. In his mind he was already counting: the *Pawnee*, the *Pocahontas*, the *Harriet Lane*, the *Baltic*. Four ships. And the *Powhatan* — the largest of them, the one whose guns would matter most if guns were needed — still at Brooklyn, her orders not yet countersigned, waiting on a silence from Seward that had now extended three days without a word.

Four ships. He needed five.

He walked out into the corridor, where the smell of old paper gave way to March air coming through an ill-fitted door, and Fox fell into step behind him without speaking.

## Scene 5

The rain came against the windows in pulses, irregular, as though the sky were undecided, and Lincoln sat alone at the second-floor desk with the draft before him and the lamp at his right hand and the sound of water finding its way through the night.

The notification letter to Governor Pickens had been four things already, and none of them right.

The first version had carried the word *demand* — he had struck it before the sentence was finished. The second had offered assurance where assurance implied concession, the diction of a man acknowledging a sovereignty he intended to deny. The third had been better, cleaner, but its conditional structure had inverted on him: *unless such attempt be resisted* placed the resistance first, as though resistance were the expected condition and passage the exception, and a hostile reader in Charleston would take that framing and use it.

He crossed out the third version with three slow strokes.

The spring rain on the glass was the only sound. Somewhere above him Robert was asleep, and Mary, and the house held its own counsel. Nicolay had left a lamp burning in the anteroom, but Lincoln had sent him to bed hours ago, and the silence since had been complete.

His writing hand had cramped around the pen. He set it down, pressed his palm flat against the desk's surface — felt the grain beneath his skin, the slight roughness where the varnish had worn — and worked the fingers open one by one until the spasm relented. Then he took up the pen again.

The difficulty was this: the letter must do two things that pulled against each other. It must announce the expedition's character — provisions only, no men, no arms, nothing to alarm a man of good faith — and it must also establish that the federal government retained every right to proceed. Concede too much and the letter read as a petition, a suppliant's request for Confederate permission to feed its own soldiers. Assert too much and it read as a declaration, a notice of coercive intent that would reach Charleston before the fleet and give Beauregard's batteries their justification already composed.

He had been a lawyer long enough to know that the language of conditions was the language of responsibility. The question was whose.

He wrote: *I am directed by the President of the United States to notify you to expect an attempt will be made to supply Fort Sumter with provisions only —*

He stopped. Struck *to expect an attempt will be made* and wrote above it: *that an attempt will be made.* Cleaner. More direct. Less the language of supplication.

He continued: *— and that if such attempt be not resisted, no effort to throw in men, arms, or ammunition will be made without further notice, or in case of an attack upon the fort.*

He read the conditional clause again. *If such attempt be not resisted.* Not *unless the attempt meets resistance.* Not *provided no opposition is offered.* The difference was the placement of the agent. His formulation said: the attempt will come; what happens next depends entirely on what you do. The trigger for escalation sat in Confederate hands. The choice for war or peace sat in Confederate hands. The federal government had announced its purpose and conditioned its further action on Confederate response — which meant that if Confederate guns opened on bread-carrying vessels, the opening would be Confederate, and no subsequent argument could relocate it.

He read the sentence aloud to the empty room, his voice barely above a whisper. Then again, slower, as a judge reads a finding he intends to stand behind.

He set down the pen.

He was thinking of the *Powhatan*. Fox had pressed him on the ship twice in the past week, and twice Lincoln had given his word. Without her, the squadron was a gesture. With her, it was a fleet. And Seward had sent nothing — not a note, not a messenger, not a word — since their confrontation three nights past. The countersignature on the *Powhatan*'s orders remained unsigned. Lincoln had not pressed Seward directly, had told himself that compliance would come in its own time. He was no longer certain he had been right to wait.

He reached for the sand-caster and blotted the notification with a slow, even motion, watching the words hold their shape as the sand fell.

The letter was complete. The expedition order lay beside it, signed. Fox would leave for New York in the morning. The notification would travel by special messenger to Charleston, and if the fleet was what Lincoln believed it to be — what Welles had promised, what Fox had built it to be — then the choice of war or peace would rest where he had placed it. He had arranged the pieces with the care of a man who understands that he cannot compel events, only dispose them. The rest belonged to Charleston.

He gathered the papers and set them parallel to the desk's edge. He rose, crossed to the window, and stood looking out at the avenue below — the wet stone dark and empty, the gas lamps burning in the rain.

He heard it before he understood it: wheels on the paving stones below, moving fast — faster than a man going home at this hour, faster than a messenger on ordinary business. The carriage came into the lamplight and was gone again in the space of a heartbeat, heading east toward the State Department, its iron wheels loud and then swallowed by the rain. Lincoln stood at the cold glass and watched the darkness where it had been.

The *Powhatan* sat at Brooklyn without her countersignature. Seward had not written. And a carriage was moving through the wet toward the State Department at this hour, at this pace, carrying something — a dispatch, a visitor, a decision already taken — that Lincoln had not been told was coming.

He pressed his hand flat against the glass. The cold came through at once, sharp against his palm.

He had three hours before Fox departed for New York. If the *Powhatan*'s orders were not countersigned by morning, the largest gun in his fleet would sail without him — or would not sail at all.
