# Chapter 7: The Guns at Palo Alto

## Scene 1

The *National Intelligencer* lay open across the centre of the desk, its ink still sharp in the lamplight, and Polk had read it four times.

He had been precise about it. The first reading was for the argument's shape. The second was for its evidence. The third was for the names it deployed and the names it conspicuously withheld. The fourth was with the pen in his hand, and that was the reading that mattered.

*The President of the United States*, the editorial declared, in the rolling, prosecutorial cadence that Gales and Seaton had perfected across thirty years of Whig agitation, *having determined that the Mexican Republic would not yield California by purchase, resolved that she should yield it by compulsion. To that end, he ordered Brevet Major General Taylor across the Nueces River into territory whose ownership no instrument of law has established as American, there to await the provocation that the administration required. That the Mexican cavalry obliged him does not transform a manufactured occasion into a just one. The blood shed upon that disputed bank was shed, in the most consequential sense, by presidential design.*

Polk drew a line beneath *no instrument of law* and wrote in the margin, in small, deliberate letters: *See joint resolution of annexation, Dec. 1845 — Texas boundary as presented to this govt. extends to Rio Grande. Not a question of law but of prior recognition.*

The editorial continued. *The claim that American blood was shed upon American soil must be understood, by any dispassionate reader of the documentary record, as a fabrication — a pretext dressed in the language of national honour, composed by a president who had exhausted every honest means of provoking the conflict he required.*

He underlined *fabrication* twice. Beside it he wrote: *Slidell mission — Mexico refused to receive. Diplomacy exhausted by Mexican refusal, not by this administration's preference.* Then, below that, smaller still: *Thornton incident — Mexican cavalry crossed north of the river, 25 April. First aggression theirs.*

This was how he worked through opposition — not by dismissing it but by dismantling it, clause by clause, until what remained was a structure he could rebuild on his own terms. The *Intelligencer* was not wrong that Taylor had crossed the Nueces. It was wrong about everything that followed from that fact.

He pushed the supper tray to the far edge of the desk without looking at it. The smell of the cooling food — roasted meat gone greasy and cold, bread beginning to harden at its crust — had been at the edge of his awareness for an hour, and he had ignored it with the same methodical indifference he brought to everything that was not the work before him.

The map on the board showed the river in its brown course, bending southeast toward the Gulf. He could trace the line from where he sat — the disputed ground between the Nueces and the bank where Thornton's dragoons had died. The distance was not large. The argument over it would be enormous, and it would be fought not on that ground but here, in the press, in the galleries of Congress, in the dispatches that reached London and Paris before the truth of the matter had been properly established.

The *Washington Union* went to press before midnight. He had perhaps three hours.

He drew a fresh sheet toward him and commenced the memorandum. The administration's position on the Texas boundary was not a construction of convenience but a recognition of what the Republic of Texas had herself claimed and what Congress had affirmed in the act of annexation. The Slidell mission had been refused — not by this government's impatience but by Mexico's categorical rejection of American credentials. The Thornton patrol had operated north of the Rio Grande on ground that Taylor's orders designated as American territory, and it was there, on that ground, that Mexican cavalry had struck the first blow.

He wrote steadily. The window was open against the night's thickness, and the air that came through it was warm enough to feel like pressure against his forearms where his sleeves were rolled back — a damp, insistent heat that accomplished nothing. From somewhere below on the avenue, a horse's hooves struck the cobblestones in an uneven canter, and then faded, and the city returned to its close, breathing silence.

He was midway through the third paragraph when a sharp cramp took hold in his lower bowel and bent him forward in the chair. He stood, pressing his hand flat against his side, and walked the length of the room twice, his breath careful, until the thing passed.

Then he returned to the desk.

The memorandum would reach the *Union*'s compositors before the forms were locked. The editorial's version of events would appear in tomorrow's editions regardless. But the counter-argument would appear alongside it, and members of Congress would read both before they had formed their opinions, and that was what he required. If the *Intelligencer* held the field unopposed until morning, the Whig account would be the only account, and a dozen uncommitted votes in the House would harden against him before the week was out.

A knock came at the study door.

He did not answer immediately. He finished the sentence he was writing — *the cavalry of the Mexican Republic, having crossed north of the Rio Grande in force, did on the twenty-fifth of April last engage and destroy a patrol of United States dragoons* — and set down the pen.

"Come."

Knox Walker entered with the particular care of a young man delivering something he was uncertain how to characterise. He held a folded note, the seal already broken, the paper slightly creased from having been handled more than once on its journey upstairs.

"From Congressman Roane, sir. His man said it came by way of a steamboat captain out of Point Isabel." Walker paused. "He said the general has fought a battle, sir. At a place called Palo Alto. He said the general won."

Polk looked at the note. Then he looked at the map.

"The source."

"A captain named Whitfield, sir. He put in at the navy yard this afternoon."

Polk extended his hand and Walker placed the note in it. He read it in ten seconds. The details were sparse and the provenance was precisely what Walker had described — a steamboat captain, not a military courier, not an official dispatch. A man who had been near the fighting and had heard things and had repeated them.

He could not act on it. He could not ignore it.

"Get word to Secretary Marcy tonight," he said. "I want him and Secretary Buchanan at the Executive Mansion at seven o'clock tomorrow morning." He set the note on the desk beside the annotated editorial. "And Walker — this memorandum is to reach the *Union*'s editor before midnight. Take it yourself. If the editor raises any question of its provenance, you will tell him it comes from the President's hand and that he is to run it entire, without abridgement."

Walker gathered the pages and was gone before Polk had turned back to the map.

Palo Alto. He did not know the ground. He traced the river's curve with his eye, estimating the position — somewhere north of Matamoros, somewhere in the flat coastal plain where Taylor had been waiting since March. If the captain's account was accurate, Taylor had engaged a Mexican force in open country and driven it from the field.

If it was accurate.

He sat down. He took up the pen. There was still a paragraph to finish, and the *Union* would not hold its forms past midnight, and the *Intelligencer*'s accusation would be in every congressional hand by morning regardless of what he did now.

He resumed writing. The map stayed at the edge of his vision — the brown river bending toward the Gulf, and somewhere along that curve a general who had just won a battle, and who would not remain merely a general for long. The *Intelligencer* had called the war a fabrication. If Taylor had truly won, the fabrication had acquired a momentum of its own — and a victorious general was something no administration had ever found easy to govern.

The pen scratched on. The lamp burned. The night pressed at the open window, warm and without mercy.

## Scene 2

The cabinet room held the morning's glare without softening it. Light came through the tall windows in flat, uncompromising planes, falling across the long table and the three men arranged around it, and the coffee before each of them had been poured and left long enough to acquire a skin.

Buchanan had arrived first, as he always did when he wished to establish that he was not summoned. He stood at his customary place, a sheaf of papers squared before him, his coat immaculate, his expression arranged into the aspect of a man attending to a routine matter of state. Marcy sat to Polk's right, his large hands resting on the table's surface, already committed to whatever direction the morning required.

Polk set the congressman's note on the table before him and did not unfold it.

"The intelligence from the south," he said, "requires a decision before the morning's editions are locked. I will have your views."

Buchanan drew breath — audible, measured.

"It must be acknowledged," Buchanan began, "that the intelligence in question derives from a steamboat captain's account, transmitted through a congressional intermediary whose reliability in such matters is — that is to say, the provenance is not such as to warrant public commitment on the part of this administration. To announce a victory that may prove exaggerated, or that may not have occurred at the scale reported, would expose us to precisely the species of ridicule the *Intelligencer* is already positioning itself to deliver. I would counsel, Mr. President, that we await official dispatch before making any public declaration."

A fly had come in through the door when Marcy entered, and it moved now in slow circuits above the table, dropping toward the rim of Buchanan's coffee cup and lifting away. Polk watched it. Buchanan did not appear to notice.

"And in the interval," Polk said, "the Whig papers compose the story themselves."

"The Whig papers will compose whatever story serves them regardless of our timing." Buchanan's voice carried the cadence of a man who believes he is being misunderstood. "I confess that my concern extends beyond the tactical. In my recent correspondence — I have been in communication with certain friends in Pennsylvania who follow these matters closely — I have found it necessary to acknowledge that the question of whether continued diplomatic engagement might have produced a different outcome is one that thoughtful men are already raising. It would ill serve us to appear to be rushing past that question by celebrating military action before the full picture—"

Polk lifted his coffee cup. He held it for a long moment, the porcelain warm against his fingers, and then set it down untouched and placed both hands flat on the table.

The fly landed on the cup's rim.

"Pennsylvania friends," Polk said.

Buchanan's forefinger moved once against the table's edge.

"Gentlemen of standing who share my concern for the administration's long-term—"

"You have written to Pennsylvania counsel that this war might have been avoided."

"I have expressed, in private correspondence, the view that—"

"I will have that letter before the afternoon." Polk's voice did not rise. "You will produce it, or I will have Secretary Marcy draft the diplomatic circular you have been declining to finalise, and your name will appear on neither document."

Buchanan set his sheaf of papers on the table. The gesture was too careful — the movement of a man buying himself a moment's concealment behind a routine action. "Mr. President, I must protest that the characterisation of a private communication as—"

"The letter," Polk said. "Before the afternoon."

The fly lifted from the cup and circled once and was gone toward the window.

Polk turned to Marcy. The movement was complete, final — the turning away from a matter that had been settled.

"All military dispatches originating from the southern theatre will pass through the War Department before transmission to any member of this cabinet or any officer of the press. Acknowledgment of receipt, summary of contents, full text — your office first. You will prepare the routing procedures today."

Marcy's hands shifted on the table. "Yes, Mr. President."

"The rumoured victory will be confirmed by official dispatch within the week. When it arrives, the *Washington Union* will have our account ready." Polk gathered the congressman's note and rose. "We are agreed."

He did not wait for affirmation. His hand was upon the latch when Buchanan's voice came — not loudly, not with any theatrical quality, but with the careful, carrying clarity of one who has chosen his ground and planted himself upon it.

"Mr. President."

Polk stopped. His hand rested on the door frame.

"I should observe," Buchanan said, "that the Pennsylvania gentlemen to whom I wrote are not without their own correspondents in this city. If the letter is produced, as you require, it will not be the only document in circulation by the week's end."

The room held the sound of that — the flat morning light, the smell of cooling coffee, the distant creak of the building settling in the heat. Behind Polk, Marcy had not moved.

Polk did not turn. He stood with his back to the room, his hand on the frame, and let the silence do what silence could do — which was, in his experience, rather more than most men credited it.

Then: "Good morning, Mr. Buchanan."

He went through the door. Behind him, he heard Buchanan's papers being gathered from the table — a sound like the slow, considered rearrangement of a hand that had not yet been played.

## Scene 3

The War Department smelled of sealing wax and old paper and the sweat of men who had been sitting in the same rooms since before dawn. Polk noted it the moment he crossed the threshold — a close, institutional odour, the smell of a place that processed other men's urgencies without ever quite resolving them. Marcy's office was at the end of a corridor lined with correspondence boxes, each labelled in a clerk's neat hand: *Rio Grande Theatre. Quartermaster Returns. Adjutant General — Received.* The courier stood near the door, his boots still chalked with road dust, his expression the careful vacancy of a man trained not to register what he carried.

Marcy rose from behind his desk. "They arrived an hour past, sir."

Polk did not sit. He crossed to the desk and took up the official dispatch first — Taylor's report of the engagements at Palo Alto and Resaca de la Palma, eighth and ninth of May respectively. He read it in three minutes. The prose was a soldier's prose: brisk, factual, the names of the dead listed without ceremony. Mexican artillery had opened the action on the plain. The American line had held. The rout at Resaca had been complete. Casualties were enumerated with the economy of a commander who understood that the number was the argument.

He set it aside.

The second document was thinner. Addressed to the Adjutant General's office, marked *private* in Taylor's own hand. Polk loosened his cravat — the heat in the room was extraordinary, a compressed, airless thing that bore down from the low ceiling — and worked a finger under the linen until it lay loose at his collar. Sweat gathered at the hollow of his throat. He read.

Taylor had named the requisition number. He had named the promised delivery date, then the actual delivery date, then the deficit in artillery rounds expressed not as an estimate but as a count arrived at with deliberate care: four hundred and twelve rounds short of operational minimum for sustained engagement. He had named the infantry regiments requested and not received. He had stated, without ornament, that the twin victories had been achieved *in spite of* the War Department's management of the campaign's logistical preparation, and that the men who fought them deserved acknowledgment of that fact from those in Washington who would now take credit for the result.

Polk read the final paragraph again. Then he folded the document in thirds and placed it inside his coat.

"He names the requisition number," Polk said.

"He does."

"He names the delivery date. The deficit in rounds." Polk turned from the desk. "He has written this for a congressional committee, Marcy, not for the Adjutant General's files."

Marcy's jaw worked briefly. "Some of his supply complaints are — the Department acknowledges that the transition to a war footing introduced delays—"

"I am not interested in the Department's acknowledgment." Polk moved to the window. Below, on Seventeenth Street, a cartman was arguing with a soldier over right of way. Neither would yield. "I am interested in who reads this. Where does it go from that office?"

"It would ordinarily be filed, with notation, and copies circulated to—"

"It will not be circulated." Polk turned. "You will sequester it. File it in your office, under your hand, and it does not leave without my knowledge."

Marcy reached for his pen. The scratch of it against paper was the only sound in the room for a moment — then, from somewhere deeper in the building, the muffled thud of a correspondence box being set down on a stone floor.

"Beyond that," Polk continued, "all future correspondence from that theatre — official dispatches, private letters, quartermaster requisitions, any communication whatsoever from General Taylor's headquarters — passes through this office before any other destination. You will acknowledge receipt, summarise contents, and bring to me what I determine to require my attention."

"And Taylor's requests for reinforcement?"

"Grant him two of the three regiments." Polk moved back to the desk and stood over it, his hands braced against its surface, his voice dropping to the register he used when he wished to be remembered with precision. "Full ammunition complement for operations at the current scale — not for independent offensive action beyond his present lines. He will have what he requires to hold his position and to demonstrate that this republic supports its army in the field. He will not have what he requires to march on Monterrey without Washington's authorisation."

Marcy wrote. The courier stood near the door with the stillness of a man who had learned that stillness was the one service he could render.

"There is one matter further," Polk said. "Senator Crittenden."

Marcy looked up.

"I am told — I will have this confirmed — that General Taylor has been corresponding with Senator Crittenden of Kentucky on matters that are not strictly military in their character." He said it flatly, as one states a calculation already completed. "I want to know whether any such correspondence has passed through this department."

"I have no knowledge of such correspondence."

"Then you will acquire it." Polk straightened. "The courier departs south in two days. Have the routing instructions written before then, and the partial reinforcement order signed. I will review both before they leave this building."

He took up his hat from the corner of the desk. He was at the door when Marcy spoke.

"Sir." A pause — long, deliberate, the silence of a man who has held something back through the entire length of a conversation and chosen, at last, to release it. "There is a matter I should have raised at the outset."

Polk did not reach for the handle. He waited.

"One of the clerks in that office — a man named Fessenden — has already made a copy of the private letter." Marcy set his pen down. "Before I was aware of its contents. Before I could direct otherwise."

The room contracted. Polk felt it — the walls, the low ceiling, the airless heat pressing inward from every surface.

"Where is the copy."

"I do not know, sir. Fessenden has not been at his desk since yesterday afternoon. His supervisor reports he has not been seen."

Polk walked back to the centre of the room, slowly, until he stood directly before Marcy's desk. He did not sit. He looked at Marcy's face — the face of a man caught in the moment after an irrevocable utterance, still feeling the weight of it leave his mouth — and held him there for a long moment.

"If that copy reaches Senator Crittenden," Polk said, "the missing rounds will be read aloud on the Senate floor before the month is out. The figures Taylor chose to record will become the record of this administration's conduct of the war. Do you understand what I am telling you?"

"Yes, sir."

"Then find him." Polk put on his hat. "Not before the week is out. Before tomorrow's post departs. Find him, Marcy, and bring me the copy before it leaves this city."

He left Marcy already on his feet, the pen abandoned, calling through the open door for his deputy.

## Scene 4

The house had settled into its night sounds — the tick of the hall clock, the creak of old timber adjusting to the dark — by the time Polk came upstairs. Sarah had drawn her chair close to the desk without being asked, as she always did when she knew he would come to her rather than to sleep, and the single lamp threw its circle across her hands where they worked at a kid glove, the needle moving through a split seam at the thumb with the small, regular sound of thread pulled taut against leather.

He sat down heavily. He did not speak immediately. He pulled off his coat and hung it on the chair back, and the motion brought with it the smell of the day — the War Department's sealing wax, the cabinet room's cold coffee, the particular close heat of rooms where consequential things had been decided in the morning and their consequences carried through the afternoon.

Sarah did not look up from her work.

"You have not eaten," she said.

"I have not."

She set the glove aside. She rose without ceremony, crossed to the sideboard, and poured him a glass of water from the pitcher there — not wine, not whiskey, but water, because she knew his stomach — and set it before him and returned to her chair. The small competence of the gesture, its absolute absence of performance, settled something in him that the day's calculations had wound tight.

He drank. He set the glass down.

Then he drew Taylor's letter from the coat where it had rested against his chest through the long afternoon, and laid it on the desk between them.

"I will read you something," he said.

Sarah folded her hands over the glove.

He found the passage and read aloud, his voice flat and even: *"That the victories at Palo Alto and at Resaca de la Palma were achieved through the courage of the officers and men under my command, and through that courage alone, I do not hesitate to affirm — it having been demonstrated that the material support promised by the Department of War, and specifically the artillery ammunition and the two additional companies of the Fourth Infantry, were not present upon the field and had not been present at any point during the operations, their absence being a matter of documented requisition and documented failure of delivery—"*

He stopped. The clock in the hall measured the silence.

"He names the figures," Polk said. "The promised date and the actual date and the count of rounds short." He set the letter on the desk. "He names them all."

Sarah looked at the letter. Not at him — at the letter, as though taking its measure independently.

"Tell me," he said. "A man who writes in that manner — is he writing as a soldier, or as a candidate?"

"The letter is addressed to the Adjutant General's office," she said. "But it is not written for the Adjutant General." She lifted the glove again. The needle moved. "A man who catalogues his grievances with that precision — the figures, the dates, the deficit in rounds — is composing testimony." She drew the thread through. "For whom, Mr. Polk, does a general compose testimony?"

He rose from the desk and crossed to the window. The night beyond the glass was close and dark, the canal a black seam at the city's edge, its smell reaching even here — river mud and something green and rank, the smell of standing water in summer. He stood with his back to the room.

"They will say I punish him for winning," he said.

"They will say it regardless." A pause. The needle. "The question is whether what you do is warranted."

A moth had found its way through a gap in the window frame and began circling the lamp, its wingbeats audible in the close air. Polk watched it without intention. Sarah reached over without pausing in her mending, cupped it gently in her free hand, crossed to the window, worked the latch, and released it into the dark. The night air came in briefly — thick and warm, carrying the smell of the summer city, horse dung and river mud and something green and rank from the direction of the canal — and then she shut the window again and returned to her chair.

He watched her settle. He watched her hands resume their work.

"Buchanan threatened me this morning," he said. "Not in terms he could be held to account for — he is too careful for that. But he made it plain that the Pennsylvania letter, if produced, would not be the only document in circulation."

Sarah's needle paused. A single beat — then resumed.

"He has something," she said.

"Or he wishes me to believe he has something."

"The effect is the same."

He returned to the desk and sat. He took up the letter and held it — the paper gone soft at the folds from a day of carrying, warm still from his body's warmth — and then set it down again. "Fessenden," he said.

Sarah looked up. Her eyes found his with the directness she employed when she wished him to understand that she had already arrived where he was going.

"A clerk," he said. "He made a copy of this letter before Marcy could sequester it. He has not been at his desk since yesterday afternoon."

"And you do not know where he has gone."

"No."

"But you know where he is going."

He did not answer. She already knew the answer, and they both knew she knew it, and the acknowledgment of that — the small, private economy of a marriage in which certain things did not require saying — was the only comfort the evening had offered.

"If the copy reaches Crittenden," he said, "the missing rounds become the administration's record. Not Taylor's complaint — our record. Every figure he chose to document becomes the evidence at a congressional inquiry, and the inquiry becomes the platform, and the platform—" He stopped. "You understand what the platform becomes."

"A nomination," Sarah said. She did not say it with alarm. She said it the way she said most things — plainly, as a fact that had been established and required only acknowledgment. "You have given him the material for a nomination, Mr. Polk, and now you must recover it before he uses it."

He looked at her across the desk. The lamp between them burned steadily, its light falling across her hands, across the letter, across the glass of water she had poured for him. The house breathed around them — the old timber, the settling dark, the clock in the hall.

"Marcy is looking for Fessenden," he said.

"I know." She picked up the glove. "Such a man does not linger."

He rose, moved to the window again, and stood with his back to the room. If Fessenden had moved yesterday afternoon, he had twelve hours on Marcy's men, and twelve hours was sufficient to reach Baltimore, or to pass a letter to a rider already bound for Kentucky. The figures and the dates and the four hundred and twelve missing rounds — Taylor's testimony, composed for a committee that did not yet exist, for a campaign that had not yet been declared — might already be moving north through the Maryland dark.

"Go to bed," he said. "I have writing to do."

She did not argue. She set the glove aside, smoothed her skirts, and touched his shoulder once as she passed — the brief pressure of her hand through the broadcloth, there and then gone — and the door closed behind her.

He sat for a moment in the lamp's diminished circle. Then he drew a fresh sheet toward him, dipped the pen, and wrote at the top: *Memorandum — for the President's eye alone — concerning the matter of a clerk named Fessenden, and the disposition of certain correspondence from the southern theatre, and the persons who may have had occasion to receive it.*

The lamp guttered once in a draught from somewhere — the old house breathed, even in summer — and steadied.

Someone had known to look for Taylor's letter before it could be sequestered. That was not the act of a clerk working on his own initiative. That was the act of a clerk working on instruction — and the question of whose instruction opened outward in two directions simultaneously: toward Crittenden in Kentucky, and toward Buchanan, who had this morning demonstrated that he held something he had not yet chosen to produce.

Two men. Two documents whose contents he could not fully account for. One city, in the dead of a June night, with the canal stinking at its edge and the press already setting type for a battle he had not yet officially acknowledged. Fessenden had twelve hours. The post rider south departed in less than two days. And somewhere in the building Marcy had vacated an hour ago, a routing order sat unsigned on a desk, waiting — as all such instruments waited in this city — for the moment when the men who needed it most would discover they had moved too slowly to use it.

He set the pen to the page and began to write.
