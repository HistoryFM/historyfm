# Chapter 4: The Disputed Bank

## I.

The circular wanted a first sentence, and Buchanan could not give it one.

He had the structure clear enough in his mind — the periodic architecture of a diplomatic assurance, its subordinate clauses arranged to prevent any single reading from becoming too definite. He had composed such documents before, in circumstances that were merely difficult rather than impossible, and the form came naturally to a man trained in the Pennsylvania bar, where the art of saying a great deal while committing to very little was taught not as duplicity but as professional excellence. What stopped him now was not the form. It was the page itself, white and unrelenting in the afternoon light, which seemed to require of him a candour that the occasion could not bear.

*The government of the United States, in the present posture of its relations with the Mexican Republic, entertains no design—*

He dipped the pen. Held it above the paper. A drop gathered at the nib, trembling with the weight of its own indecision, and he watched it until it fell — a dark bloom on the word *design* that spread and ruined the line.

He blotted it. Drew the sheet aside and began again.

*No design inconsistent with the peaceful settlement of all matters in dispute between the two governments.*

Which was, as a statement of fact, not so much false as a representation of a country that had not yet been surveyed and might, upon survey, prove to contain none of the features promised. And if Pakenham filed it with the Foreign Office, and if within the month Taylor's guns spoke from the contested bank — then the representation would be measured against the ground, and the ground would win, and James Buchanan's name would stand upon the discrepancy.

The grate at his back had burned down to ash and orange-veined coal, its warmth theoretical now. He had been at the desk since two o'clock, and the afternoon had passed through its pale February stages without his noticing until the light changed and he was writing by lamplight without having lit the lamp himself — his clerk, Milligan, having attended to it with the wordless efficiency that Buchanan usually found a comfort and today found a reproach. The scratch of his own pen was the only sound, and it had acquired the quality of accusation.

He set the pen down. Pressed both palms flat against the desk's surface and pushed himself to standing, then crossed to the window and opened it three inches. Cold air entered like a blade. He breathed it, tasting the faint sulphurous bite of coal smoke from the chimneys along F Street, and let it sharpen him.

He was on his fourth attempt at the opening when Milligan knocked and entered without waiting for an answer — itself a communication.

"Dispatch, sir." The young man held a sealed packet. "From Mr. Black, in Mexico City. Arrived by the evening post."

John Black, the American consul. Buchanan took the packet, broke the seal before Milligan had cleared the doorway, and read it standing at the window where the cold still pressed against his face.

Black's prose was plain to the edge of bluntness, a consular style unadorned by diplomatic pretension, and the plainness served him here because what he had to report required no ornament. The Mexican Congress had acted. President Paredes — Herrera's successor, installed by the faction that considered Herrera too accommodating — had received from the legislature authorisation to repel, by force of arms, any encroachment upon national territory. The resolution had passed with the character of a popular demonstration rather than a deliberative act. The galleries had been full. There had been cheering.

Buchanan folded the dispatch and slid it into his breast pocket. He returned to the desk, took his unfinished draft, and held it beside the dispatch in his mind — the two documents occupying the same diplomatic universe yet describing different planets.

*No design inconsistent with the peaceful settlement of all matters in dispute.*

If he transmitted that language now, and if Paredes made good on the congressional resolution, then the circular would become not merely false but a provocation in its own right — evidence, in the hands of any European minister, that Washington had been constructing the architecture of reassurance while the army marched south. Pakenham would not need to manufacture outrage; the documents would supply it.

He locked the draft in the upper left drawer with the small key on his watch chain. Then he drew a fresh sheet and wrote, in his clearest hand, a request for a private audience with the President at the Executive Mansion on the following morning at nine o'clock, on a matter requiring immediate personal direction.

He sealed it and called for Milligan. "Before the hour grows too late," he said, and added, because the thought had been forming since Black's report arrived: "And bring me the file of Mr. Slidell's correspondence. The complete file. I shall need it before morning."

## II.

The President's study was insufferably warm. Someone — Rucker, most likely, following standing orders — had piled fresh coal onto the grate until it glowed an angry orange, and the heavy curtains had been drawn against the March light as though against a siege. The air tasted of ash and the faintly medicinal odour of whatever tincture Polk had been taking for his cough. Buchanan felt the heat on his face before he had fully crossed the threshold — a dry, pressing heat that had nothing of comfort in it.

Polk did not look up immediately. He was bent over the map, one finger tracing a line that ran southwest from the Nueces, and the desk lamp threw his shadow large across the wall behind him. Buchanan noted the grey at his temples, the skin beneath his eyes gone thin as onion paper. The man was burning from within — not with doubt, which would have been legible, but with certainty, which left no residue.

"You have seen the dispatch from Black." Not a question.

"I have." Buchanan did not take the chair. He remained standing and placed Black's report on the desk between them, atop the map, covering the line of the Nueces. "The Mexican Congress has authorised armed resistance. Whatever language I employ in the circular must account for this, or it will not survive ten days before the British legation produces the contradiction."

Polk's finger stopped. He looked at the report where it lay on his map, then up at Buchanan. His hands remained flat on the table.

"You are here to tell me the circular cannot be written."

"I am here to tell you it must be written with sufficient care that it does not become, in Pakenham's hands, the instrument of our own embarrassment." Buchanan drew Slidell's file from under his arm and opened it on the desk's edge. "One sentence. A single clause acknowledging that this republic, in advancing its legitimate claims, does not pursue the dismemberment of the neighbouring government. Such language costs us nothing we have not already spent."

"It costs us what I will not spend." Polk straightened. In the lamplight his eyes had the quality of polished flint — hard, unreflecting, admitting no light from without. "A sentence disclaiming territorial ambition becomes, in the hands of a Whig senator or a London editorial, a treaty obligation. You know this."

"It becomes a record," Buchanan said. "Evidence, should the question arise before Congress or before history, that this administration proceeded in good faith. One clause, Mr. President. The omission, to a reader of Pakenham's experience, is itself a declaration."

"And if I do not write it," he continued, before Polk could answer, "then the absence becomes its own statement. He will cable Aberdeen within the week. Aberdeen will raise the question in Parliament before the month is out."

Polk was seized then by his cough — a hard, raking thing that knocked Black's report from the map's edge to the floor. Buchanan did not retrieve it. He waited, watching the President's shoulders heave, hearing the wet rasp from somewhere deep in the chest, a sound like cloth being slowly torn.

The cough stopped. Polk pressed his handkerchief to his lips, examined it briefly — Buchanan caught the flicker of his eyes downward — and folded it away.

"The interests of this republic," Polk said, his voice scraped thin, "do not require the Secretary of State's personal indemnification against future criticism. They require a circular that reassures the British and French sufficiently to prevent interference, while committing this administration to nothing." He placed both hands on the desk and leaned forward. "If you find yourself unable to produce such a document, I will draft it myself. Secretary Marcy has indicated a willingness to assist with the diplomatic correspondence, should the burden have grown excessive."

Marcy. The name landed where it was aimed.

Buchanan closed the Slidell file. He held it against his chest and met Polk's gaze with the steadiness of a lawyer who has heard the opposing counsel's best argument and found it wanting — though in truth the argument was not wanting at all, and both men knew it.

"The Secretary of War has no standing with the European legations," he said. "A circular transmitted through channels other than my department would signal to every foreign minister in Washington that this government's diplomacy has fractured. You would hand Pakenham the very weapon I am endeavouring to keep from him." A pause — deliberate, measured, each syllable of what followed already weighed and found sufficient. "I will draft the circular. The language will serve your purposes."

"Every purpose."

"Every purpose you have stated to me, Mr. President. But I will ask you to remember — when the language is tested, as it will be — that I counselled a different course."

Polk regarded him. The coal hissed and shifted in the grate.

"I do not require my cabinet to counsel," Polk said. "I require them to execute."

He picked up Black's report from the floor himself, set it aside without reading it again, and returned his attention to the map. His finger found the Rio Grande and pressed down, tracing its course toward the Gulf — hard enough that the paper dimpled beneath his nail, as though the line required his weight to hold it in place.

Buchanan turned and left the room. The cooler air of the corridor struck his damp collar like a reprieve he had not earned.

## III.

The Madeira stood at his elbow, poured and untouched, its colour in the lamplight the precise amber of old varnish.

He had commenced the third draft at half past seven. It was now, by the mantel clock whose tick he no longer heard, well past nine. The sheets lay before him in a hierarchy of failure: the first two reduced to crossed lines and marginal cancellations, the third half-formed, its opening clause suspended like a question no one had yet consented to answer.

*It being the sincere desire of this Government to maintain relations of amity with all the nations of the earth—*

He read it back. Pakenham would read it as a lawyer reads a contract presented by opposing counsel, which was what it was. *Sincere desire* — a phrase that made no commitment. *Relations of amity* — a phrase that described the wished-for end without specifying the means by which that end would be pursued, or abandoned. Whether the British minister would file a protest or merely note the ambiguity in his despatch to Aberdeen was a question this desk at this hour could not resolve.

He pushed the chair back and stood. The fire had died to a faint orange glow while he worked, and he had not called for Milligan to rebuild it. The room held the cold now — not the blade-cold of the open window but the settled cold of a space from which warmth has been gradually withdrawn, seeping in from the walls and the floor, patient and thorough. He crossed to the sideboard, lifted the Madeira decanter, and poured a second measure into a fresh glass. He did not drink from it. He set both glasses side by side and looked at them: two vessels of the same wine, both untouched, both waiting.

He was fifty-five years old. He had served in the Senate, in the legation at St. Petersburg, in the cabinet of a president whose ambitions exceeded his own by a margin that Buchanan found, on certain evenings, vertiginous. He had drafted treaties, argued before the bar of the Supreme Court, managed the correspondence of a republic that spanned half a continent. And he was here, alone, in a room that smelled of cold ash and candle tallow, trying to write a sentence that would be believed by men who would not believe it — and who would be right not to.

The difficulty was not the words. Every construction he attempted resolved itself, under examination, into one of two varieties of failure. The first was language sufficiently direct to pass Pakenham's inspection — and therefore, in Polk's estimation, a sentence that bound the administration's hands. *This republic disclaims any intention of acquiring territory by force of arms.* Plain, defensible, legally coherent. Polk would strike it before the ink dried, and Buchanan would receive a note from the Executive Mansion — curt, unsigned, delivered by a clerk who would not meet his eyes — inquiring whether he required assistance with his correspondence. The second variety satisfied Polk, preserved every option, committed the republic to nothing beyond the vague aspiration toward peace — and was therefore, to any reader of intelligence, a confession dressed in evening clothes.

He returned to the desk. He took up the pen.

What a lawyer required, when the facts would not serve, was structure.

The construction he needed was not one that disclaimed territorial ambition, nor one that preserved it. He needed a sentence whose main clause said nothing while its subordinate clauses created, in the reader's mind, the impression of having said a great deal. He had encountered such sentences in treaties. He had, on occasion, drafted them for lesser purposes — boundary commissions, commerce disputes, matters where the parties required the appearance of resolution without the inconvenience of its substance.

He wrote:

*The Government of the United States, entertaining no purpose inconsistent with the principles of justice and the law of nations, and desirous that the existing questions between the two republics shall be resolved by such means as are consonant with the dignity and mutual interest of both peoples, does not contemplate any course of action that would not be warranted by the clearest obligations of national honour.*

He set the pen down and flexed his cramped fingers, one by one, hearing the small crack of each knuckle in the silent room.

*Does not contemplate any course of action that would not be warranted.* A double negative enclosing a standard no reasonable nation could object to meeting. *Clearest obligations of national honour* — a phrase that could justify conquest as readily as restraint, since national honour, in the event of Mexican cavalry firing upon American dragoons at the river, would warrant the very response Polk intended.

He had built a door that opened both ways and called it a wall.

He looked at the sentence again. It was, he recognised with a clarity that arrived not as pride but as something closer to nausea, the most accomplished piece of legal draftsmanship he had produced in thirty years of practice. The kind of sentence written once in a career and spent the remainder hoping no one parses too carefully.

He reached past the inkwell, took the nearer glass of Madeira, and drank it in a single draught. The wine was sweet and faintly oxidised, coating his tongue with a warmth that did not reach his chest. He took a fresh sheet and began to copy the circular from the beginning, his hand steady now, the letters precise, the conscience deferred.

## IV.

The drawing room at Senator Allen's residence had accumulated, by ten o'clock, that density of masculine conversation and cigar smoke that makes a crowded space feel conspiratorial simply by existing. Candlelight flattened the faces of Democrats into amber uniformity. Beneath the smoke drifted the smell of roasted fowl still escaping from the dining room, and the sharper note of Allen's claret — a Bordeaux of modest vintage, the kind purchased in quantity rather than by deliberate selection.

Buchanan had positioned himself near the east fireplace, performing the easeful posture of a gentleman with nothing particular to accomplish, his back to the warmth, a glass of the indifferent claret held at chest height. He had noted at table that Allen's cellar ran to quantity rather than discernment; his own held three cases of a Haut-Brion that would have made this dinner a different occasion. The thought arrived without ceremony, set itself down among weightier concerns, and refused to leave — which told him something about the state of his nerves that he preferred not to examine.

Across the room, the British chargé d'affaires — a pale, attentive fellow named Crampton — was receiving the measured attentions of Senator Allen himself, who possessed the chairman's instinct for placing himself between foreign ears and inconvenient mouths. Buchanan watched the pairing and calculated: Crampton would report the evening's conversation to Pakenham; Pakenham would read the report alongside the circular drying on his desk on F Street. The two documents would be compared. The arithmetic was inescapable.

He was turning this over when Calhoun arrived beside him — not approached, arrived, moving through the room the way water finds the low ground, without apparent direction, presenting himself as though by coincidence.

The Senator from South Carolina was older than Buchanan remembered from their last extended conversation. The hair had gone entirely white, the face a topography of deliberate lines. But the eyes retained their quality — settled, patient, carrying the particular stillness of conclusions long since reached, awaiting only the world's confirmation. Calhoun had been awaiting that confirmation for forty years. He had grown comfortable in the posture.

"Mr. Secretary." Calhoun's voice was pitched below the room's general noise. He accepted a glass from a passing servant and did not drink from it. "I trust the evening finds you well."

"It does, Senator. Senator Allen's hospitality is, as always, generous." Buchanan turned his glass slowly by the stem. "I had not expected the pleasure of your company this evening."

"Nor I yours. Yet here we are." Calhoun surveyed the room with an expression that suggested he had catalogued its contents and found them unsurprising. "I have been reading, of late, the circular your department transmitted to the European legations."

"The department endeavours to address the concerns of foreign ministers with appropriate thoroughness."

"*Appropriate thoroughness.*" He repeated the phrase as one might turn a coin to examine its obverse. "There is a passage — I confess I have committed it to memory — in which the circular asserts that this republic, in the exercise of its established rights upon territory recognised by the joint resolution of annexation, harbours no design upon the sovereign dominion of the neighbouring republic beyond the recovery of what is, in justice and in law, already ours." He paused. "I have that substantially correct, I believe."

A burst of laughter from Allen's end of the room broke across the space between them. In the pause that followed, Buchanan was aware of the sweat cooling beneath his collar, and of Crampton's slight turn in their direction — the attentive half-rotation of a man who has caught a change in the room's temperature without yet identifying its source.

"The language speaks for itself, Senator."

"It does. Remarkably so." Calhoun's gaze settled on him — unhurried, immovable. "What it does not speak of, naturally, is Mr. Slidell's instructions. Which I have reason to believe left no margin for reception under any title that Mexico's government could accept without dissolving itself." A beat. "The mission was composed to be refused."

"Senator, the instructions to Mr. Slidell were drafted in accordance with established protocols of diplomatic appointment — the precedents in such cases are well established, and the department's conduct throughout has been—"

"Mr. Buchanan." Not a rise in volume. Something more effective — a subtraction of courtesy, so slight as to be almost imperceptible, and therefore impossible to address directly. "I have read Slidell's private letter to your department. I will not say by what means."

Buchanan set his glass on the mantelpiece.

"Then I must ask what the Senator intends." He kept his voice level, his gaze direct, and permitted himself a single step closer — not confrontational, merely the closing of a distance that had been, until this moment, comfortable for both of them. "Because if that letter reaches the Senate floor, it will not merely embarrass this administration. It will hand the British a pretext for intervention in a theatre where we cannot afford a second adversary. Whatever the Senator's constitutional objections — and I do not dismiss them, sir, they deserve the most serious consideration — disclosure would serve no interest but Britain's. And Mr. Crampton is fifteen feet away."

Calhoun glanced toward Crampton with the unhurried care of a man who had already accounted for Crampton's position before entering the room.

"I do not intend to publish the letter," he said. "I intend something more durable." He turned back, and his voice dropped to the register of a man who has no need to raise it. "When the blood is shed — and it will be shed, sir, within the month, on ground that Mexico has never ceded and that no treaty has confirmed as ours — you will find me upon my feet in the Senate chamber. I will cite the circular your department transmitted. I will cite it as the document by which this administration assured the civilised world of its peaceful inten

tions whilst marching an army to the contested bank." He paused. "Your name is upon it, Mr. Secretary."

Buchanan did not speak.

"I do not say this as a threat," Calhoun continued. "I say it as one who has watched this republic for forty years and who recognises the machinery when he sees it in motion. Taylor has reached the river. Reports of Mexican cavalry on the northern bank are expected before the week is out. And when the collision comes, the circular will be read not as diplomacy but as evidence — evidence that this government knew precisely what it was doing and did it with deliberation." He inclined his head, a gesture that contrived to be simultaneously respectful and final. "I shall be waiting. As shall history."

He moved away without haste, accepting a word from a Virginia congressman with the appearance of a gentleman whose evening had been entirely pleasant.

Buchanan retrieved his glass from the mantelpiece. The claret had gone to room temperature, flat and faintly sour on his tongue. Crampton was still at Allen's elbow, still performing his attentiveness with the disciplined patience of a man who had been trained to outlast every room he entered. Buchanan watched him and understood, with the cold precision of a man reading a verdict, that Crampton had heard enough — not the words, perhaps, but the register, the proximity, the quality of stillness that surrounds two men who have just said something irrevocable. By morning, Pakenham would know that Calhoun had sought out the Secretary of State at a private dinner. By the week's end, Aberdeen would know it too.

The circular was already insufficient. And Calhoun had not yet risen to speak.

Buchanan set the glass down.

The drawing room doors opened.

A servant entered at the measured pace that announces not a domestic errand but a message — the kind that does not wait for the conclusion of an evening. He crossed to Allen, bent, and delivered something in a voice too low for the room to catch. Allen's expression did not alter, which was itself an alteration. He looked up, found Buchanan across the candlelight, and held his gaze for a moment that lasted precisely as long as it needed to.

"Gentlemen," Allen said, and the room quieted with the instinct of men who have learned to read a host's voice for the thing beneath the word. "I am informed that a courier has arrived from the War Department."

Buchanan did not move. His right hand rested against the marble edge of the mantelpiece, and he felt the cold of it travel up through his palm as Allen crossed toward him, the folded paper already extended — already, Buchanan saw, broken open and resealed, which meant it had passed through at least one other hand before this one.

"For you, Mr. Secretary," Allen said. "He rode through the night."
