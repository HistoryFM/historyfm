# Chapter One: The Four Points

## I.

By the time Polk reached the Mexican dispatches, the study had grown cold along its margins — cold where the plaster breathed through the walls, cold at the baseboards where the floorboards met the wainscoting — while the grate pushed its diminishing warmth across the desk's surface in a shallow arc that barely reached his hands. He did not call for the porter. He turned up the gas lamp himself, drew the nearest chair closer to the heat, and resumed reading.

Tyler's correspondence was a ruin. That was the word that presented itself and he permitted it. Not a disaster — disasters had a single, identifiable cause — but a ruin, the accumulated consequence of two years of a man governing without a party, without a mandate, and without, so far as Polk could determine from the evidence before him, any sustained conception of what he intended. The dispatches to Mexico City ran in three directions at once. The dispatches from the Texan congress suggested that the annexation terms had been communicated to Austin in at least four different versions. And beneath everything, like a fissure running beneath a building's foundation, there was the matter of what John C. Calhoun had apparently promised the Mexican minister — a promise that, if honoured, would undo the work of the last Congress before Polk had drawn his first full breath in office.

He set down the letter and lifted the draft beneath it. Unsigned — Calhoun had not signed it, which meant either that it had never been sent or that a fair copy existed somewhere bearing his hand, the unsigned version left here as the residue of a man covering himself in both directions simultaneously. The paper was good stock, the hand a clerk's, the language unmistakably Calhoun's: that cadence of elevated scruple, principle deployed as a weapon while its author stood carefully behind it.

*The Government of the United States,* the draft read, *being solicitous of the most amicable relations with the Republic of Mexico, and regarding the question of Texan annexation as one admitting of further deliberation between the interested parties, does not consider the congressional resolution to foreclose negotiation regarding the precise disposition of boundaries and conditions of transfer—*

He pressed his handkerchief to his mouth. The cough came without warning, dry and hard, shaking him forward over the desk for a full half-minute before it relented. When it passed, his ribs ached and the taste of iron sat faintly on his tongue. He folded the handkerchief without examining it and laid it aside.

*—does not consider the congressional resolution to foreclose negotiation—*

There it was. If this language had reached the Mexican legation — and Polk had to proceed on the assumption that it had — then Paredes or Herrera or whoever next seized the presidential chair in that convulsed republic possessed documentary grounds to claim the question of Texas remained open. Every month's delay strengthened that claim. Every week that passed without a definitive American assertion of the boundary allowed the European courts to treat the matter as unsettled. And if Britain chose to guarantee Mexican sovereignty over the disputed ground between the Nueces and the river — the harbour at San Francisco lay at the end of that chain of reasoning, and Polk could see it as plainly as a surveyor sees the terminus of his line.

He read the draft a third time, his eyes moving from clause to clause with the attention of a man examining a deed of property for provisions that would require breaking.

He gathered the Tyler correspondence — the dispatches, the memoranda, the letters of instruction to ministers whose instructions had since been superseded — and aligned the stack against the desk's edge, pressing each corner into true until the pile sat square and even. Then he opened the lower left drawer, placed the unsigned draft inside, turned the small brass key, and pocketed it.

The lock was stiff. He noted this for the morning — the mechanism wanted oil, or replacement. He would not have documents of this nature secured by hardware a determined clerk could defeat with a penknife.

He drew a fresh sheet toward him and dipped the pen.

The memorandum he intended would not be long. It needed to accomplish one thing: to inform Buchanan, before he arrived the following evening with his own assumptions about the inherited diplomatic landscape, that every assurance, undertaking, or representation made to any foreign power by any officer of the preceding administration was to be regarded as null — not merely superseded but null, as though it had never been uttered — unless it bore Polk's explicit ratification. The Department would begin from first principles. The first principle was that this republic's policy was now determined by one man, and that man had not yet spoken.

He wrote: *It is my determination that no diplomatic communication, whether formal or informal, transmitted by officers of the preceding administration, shall be construed as expressing the policy of this government on any matter now before the Executive, unless such communication shall have received my explicit written endorsement—*

He paused. The gas lamp hissed faintly in the quiet. From somewhere below, in the body of the mansion, came the sound of a door closing — Sarah, perhaps, or one of the servants moving through the unfamiliar rooms, learning the geography of a house not yet settled into.

He continued writing.

The memorandum took twenty minutes. It was plain. It said what it meant. He blotted it, set it at the desk's centre, and sat back.

Through the window, the grounds of the Executive Mansion were a dark geometry of frozen mud and bare trees, the lights of the city beyond them sparse and indifferent. The half-raised column of the monument stood against the sky like a broken promise — this republic's tribute to its first president, abandoned for want of funds, the scaffolding black against the stars.

The Secretary of State would arrive the following evening expecting a consultation.

He would receive his instructions.

## II.

Buchanan's coat announced him before he spoke — the fine-milled broadcloth of a Philadelphia tailor, the buttons set with the close precision of a man who understood that dress was argument, the collar lying without effort against a neck that had spent thirty years tilted at its characteristic angle. Polk registered it in the moment Buchanan settled into the chair opposite: such care for an evening meeting described as private was the preparation of a diplomat who knew that no encounter with a president was ever truly private, and who had dressed accordingly.

The gas lamps threw their amber half-light across the room. The air held coal smoke and the chalky sweetness of fresh plaster, and the cold that had retreated from the grate's warmth still gripped the window glass, the panes faintly luminous against the dark outside.

'I am grateful you came at this hour,' Polk said.

'The honour is mine, Mr. President.' Buchanan's head inclined at its habitual angle — the slight tilt his mismatched eyes required — and he set his hat on his knee. His voice carried the measured warmth of long practice: cordial, precise, and costing him nothing.

Polk did not offer water. He placed one hand on the memorandum and drew it toward him.

'I will not detain you with preamble, Mr. Buchanan. There are four matters before this republic that I regard as the whole business of this administration, and I have set them down in the order of their urgency.'

He lifted the sheet and read from it — Texas, Oregon, California, the tariff — each point rendered in the plain declarative of a man reading a bill of lading rather than a programme for continental transformation. When he finished, he set the sheet down and waited.

'The tariff I understand,' Buchanan said, after a moment. 'And Texas is, as you say, substantially accomplished. Oregon presents difficulties that are manageable, I believe, through patient diplomacy — Lord Aberdeen has given no indication that Britain desires a rupture over the forty-ninth parallel.' He paused. His forefinger found the brim of his hat and rested there. 'It is California, Mr. President, on which I find myself in need of your further guidance.'

'Then ask.'

'Is it to be acquired by purchase, or by other means?'

The question arrived without inflection, as though Buchanan were inquiring about the conveyance of a parcel. But the phrasing — *by other means* — was not innocent. It was the construction of a man who wished to hear the word *purchase* and who had left the alternative unnamed so that Polk might name it first, and thereby own it.

Polk let the silence hold for three beats. Then, not answering the question: 'I have in my possession an unsigned draft, in Mr. Calhoun's hand, assuring the Mexican minister that the annexation of Texas remains open to negotiation. Are you aware of this document?'

Buchanan's forefinger stilled on the hat brim. 'I am not.'

'Then you are now.' Polk opened the lower drawer, produced the document, and laid it on the desk between them. 'Read the third paragraph.'

Buchanan leaned forward. His mismatched eyes moved across the page. When he straightened, the tilt of his head had shifted — no longer the habitual compensation for imperfect vision, but the involuntary angle of a man recalculating something he had believed settled.

'This is — Mr. President, if this language reached Mexico City, it constitutes a representation by the officers of the United States that—'

'That the question is open. Yes.' Polk drew the paper back. 'Which is why General Taylor's command will advance from Corpus Christi to the river. The movement will commence upon my order, within the fortnight.'

'You mean to answer Calhoun's letter with bayonets.'

'I mean to occupy the territory this republic has annexed, before any government in Mexico City or any minister in Whitehall can convert that indiscretion into a legal position. If we do not assert the boundary now — visibly, with troops on the ground — we shall spend the next two years arguing it in diplomatic notes while the British consolidate their interest in California. I will not argue. I will occupy.'

'Mr. President.' The subordinate clauses began to multiply — the sign, Polk had already observed, of a man purchasing time. 'It must be acknowledged that the boundary as recognised by prior treaties and as understood by the Mexican authorities presents a construction — I confess I cannot, in good conscience, draft correspondence to the European courts that contradicts the established legal position on the Nueces. To do so would expose this administration to the charge of deliberate misrepresentation before the civilised world, and if that unsigned document should come to light—'

'Mr. Buchanan.' Polk returned the paper to the drawer. The brass key turned with a sound like a small bone breaking. 'The joint resolution recognises the annexation of Texas. Texas claims the river as its boundary. It is therefore the position of this administration that General Taylor's advance constitutes the occupation of American soil. The correspondence of the Department will reflect that construction, and no other.'

'A construction that Mexico will regard as an act of war.'

'Mexico may regard it as she pleases. The question before you is whether you accept the position I have described, or whether you do not.'

The half-light gathered between them. From somewhere in the street below came the sound of iron-shod wheels on cobblestone, receding into the dark.

Buchanan turned his hat in his hands. His eyes moved from the locked drawer to Polk's face.

'I would urge, Mr. President — as a matter of statecraft, not constitutional objection — that Mexico's factions are presently divided, and that a generous purchase offer, made through proper channels, might find reception among those elements who understand their republic's financial condition. To advance troops before that avenue is exhausted will unite those factions against us and furnish the European powers with grounds to intervene.'

Polk heard it — the way a man hears a well-constructed argument for a course of action he has already closed off. He let Buchanan finish. Then he leaned forward, and when he spoke, his voice was lower than before, and slower.

'Mr. Slidell will carry a purchase offer. I am not unreasonable. But I will tell you what I have not yet told any man in this city, and I will tell you because you are to be my Secretary of State and a minister must understand the policy he serves.' He held Buchanan's gaze. 'The purchase offer is not the policy. The policy is California. If Mexico receives Mr. Slidell and accepts the offer, I shall have California by negotiation. If Mexico refuses him — and I believe she will refuse him, because no government in Mexico City can survive the appearance of selling territory to a foreign republic — then General Taylor's presence on the river will have created a condition from which war may follow. And if war follows, I shall have California by conquest. Do you understand me?'

The wheels were gone. The street below had gone quiet. The grate ticked once as iron contracted in the cooling ash.

Buchanan sat very still. The hat had stopped turning.

'You are describing,' he said, with the care of a man setting his foot on a bridge he does not trust, 'a sequence in which diplomacy serves as the preface to a military action already determined upon.'

'I am describing a sequence in which this republic acquires California. The method is contingent. The outcome is not.'

'And the role of the Department — my role — is to construct the diplomatic record that will justify whichever contingency obtains.'

Polk said nothing.

Buchanan drew a long breath. The smell of coal smoke and plaster filled the room, and beneath it something fainter — the stale sweetness of old paper from the drawer where Calhoun's draft lay locked.

'If this construction should become known — if the sequence you describe should be perceived by the Senate or the press as having been arranged rather than suffered — the charge will not be misrepresentation, Mr. President. It will be conspiracy to manufacture a war. And the first name attached to the correspondence will be mine.'

'Yes,' he said. 'It will.'

Another silence. Longer than the first.

'I will require,' Buchanan said at last, 'that the correspondence be drafted in terms that acknowledge the joint resolution as the governing instrument. I can defend that construction before the Senate, provided the resolution itself is the ground on which we stand.'

'It is the only ground I have ever occupied, Mr. Buchanan.'

Buchanan looked at the memorandum — four points, written in Polk's hand, in sentences that admitted no qualification, no avenue of retreat. His forefinger moved once against his knee, a single tap, and was still.

'Then I am at the President's service,' he said.

He rose. Polk did not. Buchanan set his hat on his head with the deliberate care of a man reassembling himself after a fall, and crossed to the door. His hand found the latch. He paused there — the span of a single breath — and then he opened the door and passed through it without looking back.

The latch clicked shut.

## III.

Buchanan had been gone perhaps ten minutes. The room had grown too warm — the coals banked too high by whoever had prepared the study, the heat pressing close against the back of Polk's neck, gathering in the angles of the ceiling like something with weight. He loosened his stock and unfastened the top button of his waistcoat, a concession he permitted himself only in solitude.

He sat at the desk and looked at the four-point memorandum and the blank page beside it.

*You are describing a sequence in which diplomacy serves as the preface to a military action already determined upon.*

Buchanan had named it precisely, with that Pennsylvanian gift for reducing a complex manoeuvre to its essential shape. And Polk had not denied it, because denial would have been a lie, and lies required maintenance, and he did not intend to spend the next four years maintaining a fiction with the man who would draft his diplomatic correspondence. Better that Buchanan understand the architecture. Better that he build within it knowingly than discover it later and claim betrayal.

But Buchanan had also laid his marker. *The first name attached to the correspondence will be mine.* Not a protest — a price, named aloud so that both parties understood the terms of the arrangement. Buchanan would serve. He would draft the dispatches and manage the legations and perform the elaborate courtesies of statecraft that Polk himself had neither patience nor temperament to perform. And in return, Buchanan would hold, somewhere in his private papers, a record of the evening's conversation — not verbatim, but sufficient — that would establish, should the enterprise collapse, that the design had been the President's and the compliance reluctant.

Let him have it. A secretary who believed himself to possess a record of principled reservation was a secretary who would continue to function. The reservation changed nothing. The dispatches would still go out over Buchanan's signature. The instructions to Slidell would still bear the Department's seal. And when Mexico refused Slidell — as she would refuse him, as any government would refuse a minister whose credentials were an insult dressed in protocol — the record would show that the United States had extended the hand of negotiation and been rebuffed. That was all the record needed to show.

Polk reached for the pen and wrote.

*You will proceed with your command to the banks of the Rio Grande.*

He did not dress it. The page was private, the draft preliminary, and he was alone, and so he wrote the order as it was — a line of instruction that named the river and named the command and left the rest to be inferred by any officer capable of reading a map. He continued without hesitation, setting down the particulars: Taylor at Corpus Christi with approximately thirty-five hundred men, the advance to be conducted with due regard for the security of the column, the establishment of a position on or near the northern bank. He wrote it as one writes a deed of conveyance — the property described, the parties named, the instrument executed.

When it was done, he set the pen in its stand and pressed the heels of his hands against his eyes. The pressure brought a brief, dark relief. His back ached. His chest ached where the cough had seized it. He was forty-nine years old and he felt, in this moment, the full weight of every year — not as fatigue but as a kind of gravitational compression, the sense that the office was pressing him into a shape from which he would not recover.

He lowered his hands and looked at what he had written.

The order was not yet dispatched. It would go to Marcy at the War Department, and Marcy's machinery would carry it south through the relay stations to Corpus Christi, and Brevet Major General Taylor would receive it and do what soldiers did with orders from the government they served. That machinery had not yet turned. The ink was still drying on the page.

But the machinery would turn. He had set it in motion tonight — not with the order, which was only paper, but with the conversation. Buchanan knew. Buchanan had accepted. And a policy known to two men was no longer a private intention; it was a conspiracy, exactly as Buchanan had named it, and conspiracies possessed their own momentum. Buchanan would draft the Slidell instructions tomorrow. Slidell would depart within the month. Taylor would march. And somewhere on that river, in the heat and dust of a country that did not yet know it was contested, the sequence Polk had described would move from diplomatic theatre to the thing itself.

He thought of the men at Corpus Christi — thirty-five hundred of them, encamped in the sand and salt grass, waiting for orders from a president most of them had never heard of six months ago. They would march south because he told them to march south. Some of them would not return. He did not flinch from this. He held it in his mind the way he held a column of figures — a cost to be weighed against the value of what it purchased. The harbour at San Francisco. The Pacific coastline. A continent entire, from the Atlantic to the western ocean, governed by one republic under one constitution. That was the purchase. The cost would be paid by men whose names he would never learn, in a country he would never visit, for a cause they would be told was defence of American soil.

It was not false. But it was not the whole truth, and Polk, alone in the overheated study with the draft order cooling beneath the lamp, understood the distinction with a clarity he would never permit himself to express — not to Buchanan, not to Sarah, not to the diary he had begun keeping on the first day of his presidency. The whole truth was that he intended to provoke a war, and that the provocation would be dressed in the language of territorial defence, and that the language would be Buchanan's, and the war would be his, and the continent — if God and the army between them provided — would be the republic's.

He had not, in the course of the evening, once thought of Jackson. He noticed this now, and was satisfied by it. He did not require the old general's approbation. He required only the result.

A knock at the outer door. He covered the draft with a blank sheet — the gesture automatic, the instinct of a man who had learned that no document was private in this house.

The porter entered with a folded note on a small tray. A dispatch from the Navy Department, addressed in Secretary Bancroft's hand, marked in the upper left corner with a single word: *Immediate.*

Polk took it from the tray. He held it between his fingers and felt the weight of the paper — heavier stock than ordinary correspondence, the seal thick and freshly applied, still faintly warm from the courier's coat.

He thought of the Pacific. The long coastline he had not yet possessed. The harbour that every naval officer who had seen it described in the same terms — deep water, protected anchorage, the finest port on the western ocean. He thought of the British squadron that was, by the latest intelligence, somewhere in those waters. If Aberdeen moved before Polk's own orders reached the Pacific squadron — if the British flag rose over San Francisco while Taylor was still marching through the chaparral toward the river —

He broke the seal and read.

Three lines. Bancroft's hand was steady, the language stripped to its essentials: a British man-of-war had been sighted off Monterey, California, flying the ensign of the Pacific Station. The American consul reported unusual activity in the harbour. The squadron commander awaited instructions.

Polk set the dispatch on the desk beside his draft order to Taylor. Two documents. Two theatres. The continent spread between them like a map unrolled, and at its western edge, a British warship rode at anchor in the harbour he had claimed in his mind before he had claimed it in law.

He reached for a fresh sheet. His hand did not pause above it.
