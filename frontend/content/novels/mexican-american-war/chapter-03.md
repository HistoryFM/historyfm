# Chapter 3: The Closed Door

The dispatches had arrived before dawn, but Polk did not read them until the fire had been built up and the room reached something approaching warmth. He had learned early in his tenure that cold made him hasty, and haste was a cost he could not afford this morning.

He broke the first seal at half past seven.

The official communication from Mexico City ran to four pages in a clerk's careful hand, the Spanish rendered into English by the legation's translator with the flat fidelity of a man who understood words but not their consequence. Polk read it through once, then set it aside and pressed two fingers against the bridge of his nose. The cramp in his bowels had been at him since before rising, a low persistent grinding that the morning's coffee had done nothing to quiet, and he stood and moved to the fire, one hand flat against his abdomen, remaining there while the heat from the coals worked against the wool of his trousers. The coals smelled of sulphur and iron — the particular smell of Washington in January — and the warmth they produced was grudging, sufficient, nothing more. When the cramp passed, he returned to the desk.

He read the document again.

The difficulty was plain. Mexico's foreign minister — Peña y Peña, whose name Polk had learned to pronounce with the flat American vowels that stripped it of its musicality — had not refused to negotiate. He had refused to receive Slidell's credentials. The distinction was narrow, and before the bar of international opinion it was not nothing. Slidell had presented himself as Envoy Extraordinary and Minister Plenipotentiary of the United States, a title that carried within it the implicit recognition of Texas annexation as a settled matter, and Mexico's government had declined to accept a man bearing that designation without prejudice to its position on the boundary question. It was procedural. It was careful. It was the work of a foreign ministry that wished to appear willing while ensuring nothing was conceded.

Polk set the document down.

He had anticipated this. He had, in fact, required it — the instructions had been drafted to make any other outcome nearly impossible. But the language of the refusal, as translated and transmitted, gave him less than he needed. A procedural objection was not an insult. Buchanan would say as much, and he would be correct, and the Whigs in the Senate would say it after him with considerably more volume.

The second dispatch lay beneath the first. It bore Slidell's private seal.

Polk opened it.

Slidell wrote as he always had — with the fluency of a man accustomed to Louisiana courts, where argument was a form of performance. He described his reception in Mexico City, the social atmosphere, the factional pressures bearing upon President Herrera's government from the more bellicose elements of the press and the military. And then, in the letter's third page, he set down what Peña y Peña had communicated to him privately, through an intermediary, the evening before the formal refusal was transmitted: that the foreign minister was not personally opposed to discussions, that the question of California was one he believed the two governments could approach with mutual benefit if the title question could be — the word used was *ajustado*, adjusted — and that Herrera's government was constrained not by principle but by the arithmetic of faction, by the certainty that any negotiation conducted under Slidell's current designation would be used by the opposition to destroy Herrera before the first proposal was exchanged.

Polk read the letter a second time. Then a third.

He set it face-down on the desk. He drew the Revised Statutes from the shelf at his right hand and placed the volume over it, pressing the book's spine flush against the desk's corner with the care of a man composing himself for what comes next.

He understood, with the cold clarity that had served him from the Tennessee bar to the Speaker's chair to this room, that Slidell's private letter was more dangerous than the formal refusal. The refusal was a weapon. The letter was a key that unlocked the very lock the weapon was designed to force. If Buchanan saw it — if the Senate saw it — the whole structure became something else entirely: not a nation defending its dignity, but a government that had received an opening and chosen not to walk through it.

He drew a fresh sheet toward him. Outside, the January morning pressed against the heavy curtains — no sound from the street yet, the city still gathered in its cold — and the coal fire behind him had overbuilt itself into a steady, almost aggressive heat that warmed the back of his neck and left the far side of the desk in comparative chill. He dipped his pen.

The memorandum took him twenty minutes. He wrote it as he composed all documents of this kind: in the declarative, without subordination, each sentence arriving at its conclusion before the next commenced. He described the formal refusal in the language of national dignity — the government of Mexico had declined to receive the duly appointed representative of the United States, had refused to open communications on matters of legitimate concern to both republics, had treated the American envoy as a man without standing before the bar of nations. He used the word *affront* once, then used it again, and did not cross it out.

He did not mention Peña y Peña's private communication.

The memorandum was not a lie. It was a summary, and every summary was a selection, and every selection was a judgment about what the essential facts required. Polk had been a lawyer before he had been anything else, and he understood that documents shaped events not through what they fabricated but through what they chose to contain. The formal refusal was real. The private letter was also real. He had determined which reality the cabinet required.

He blotted the memorandum and set it with the official dispatches in the order in which he intended to present them. Then he reached for the morning's remaining correspondence — among it, he noted without opening it, a letter in the hand of Senator Calhoun, its seal intact. He set it to one side. The senator's questions, whatever they were, could wait until the cabinet had done its work.

He took up the next letter and broke its seal. The fire crackled once, sharply, as though something in the coals had given way.

---

The cabinet room had not been warm since morning. Whatever heat the fire had generated by noon had long since surrendered to the January draught moving steadily through the window casements, depositing itself along the floor and rising, degree by grudging degree, toward the waists of the seven men seated at the mahogany table. The maps on the standing board — Texas, the Nueces Strip, the country running south and west to the river — had been pinned with the precision of someone who wished to forestall all argument about geography. Polk had seen to that himself, before the others arrived. He had stood before those maps for ten minutes, alone in the cold room, and felt something that was not quite satisfaction and not quite impatience — a tightening in the chest, the sensation of a mechanism under load.

He presented the dispatches without preamble. Mexico's government had declined to receive Mr. Slidell in the capacity of Envoy Extraordinary and Minister Plenipotentiary. The refusal was an affront to the dignity of this republic. The path of patient diplomacy having been exhausted, the administration must now consider what measures the situation required.

He did not read from notes.

Secretary Walker of the Treasury nodded at the word *exhausted* — the small affirmative of a man who has already decided. Bancroft, at the Navy, kept his hands folded on the table and his eyes on the map. Cave Johnson was writing something in his memorandum book. Mason, the Attorney General, had not yet spoken and showed no sign of intending to.

Then Buchanan rose.

Not precipitously. He set both hands on the table's edge first, steadying himself before a crossing, and when he stood his coat was perfectly arranged and his voice, when it came, was measured to the last syllable.

'Mr. President,' he said, 'I must register my dissent from the proposed order, and I must do so with the fullest candour the occasion permits.'

Polk placed both palms flat on the cabinet table.

'The advance of General Taylor's forces from Corpus Christi to the Rio Grande,' Buchanan continued, 'would constitute a movement into territory whose sovereignty remains, before the law of nations, a matter genuinely in dispute. Whatever this government's construction of the Texas boundary, that construction has not been ratified by treaty, nor confirmed by any tribunal to which Mexico is a party. To advance under these circumstances is not to defend American soil. It is to assert a claim by force of arms — which is to say, Mr. President, it is an act of war. An act of war, by the settled understanding of constitutional practice, requires the authorization of Congress.'

The draught moved through the room. Somewhere in the passage beyond the door, a clock ticked.

'I would add,' Buchanan said, his voice acquiring the elevation of a man citing the authority he himself represents, 'that the European powers — Britain above all — will not read this movement as a defensive occupation. They will read it as aggression against a sovereign republic with whom they maintain commercial relations of considerable consequence. We have not yet settled Oregon. We cannot afford to present ourselves, at this moment, as a nation that resolves territorial disputes with infantry.'

He paused. His right forefinger tapped once against the table's edge — a single, involuntary stroke, quickly stilled.

'And I must put to you, sir, directly —' He looked at Polk. 'Was it ever the intention of this administration that Mr. Slidell should produce a negotiated settlement? Or was the mission designed, from its inception, to furnish this government with the appearance of exhausted diplomacy?'

The room went very still. Cave Johnson had stopped writing. Walker's earlier nod had been retracted; his face was now a study in careful neutrality. Bancroft did not shift his gaze from the map.

Marcy, the Secretary of War, said nothing. He had said nothing since the meeting commenced, and his silence had long since ceased to resemble the silence of a man reserving judgment. It resembled, instead, the silence of a man who has already been told what he will be asked to sign.

Polk's palms remained on the table. He let the question stand in the air between them — let it stand until Buchanan's certainty about having asked it began, visibly, to curdle.

Four seconds. The clock in the passage supplied the count.

'Mr. Buchanan.' His voice carried no elevation and no heat. He addressed the table. 'The authority of the President of the United States as commander-in-chief over the disposition of the military forces of this republic is not a matter that admits of cabinet deliberation. It is established by the Constitution, in terms that require no construction from this body, and it has been exercised by every president since Washington without reference to prior congressional authorization for the movement of forces already in the field.'

He paused — not for effect, but to allow the sentence's weight to settle.

'General Taylor's command is in the field. It is in the field because Congress authorized and this government completed the annexation of Texas, whose boundary as presented to this republic extends to the river. The order I contemplate is not a declaration of war. It is the ordinary exercise of executive authority over forces already deployed within the territory of this republic. If Mr. Buchanan holds that such an order requires congressional sanction, he holds a position that no attorney general of this republic has ever sustained, and I will say plainly that I do not propose to be the first president to submit the movement of his own army to a legislative committee.'

He lifted his eyes then, and they found Buchanan across the table.

'As to your second question — I take it as a question that has been answered by events, and I do not propose to improve upon that answer here.'

Buchanan's jaw was set. He sat down.

'The order, Mr. Secretary.' Polk turned to Marcy.

Marcy reached for his pen and drew the draft toward him across the table. The scratch of the nib was audible in the room's silence — a small, industrious sound, the sound of a thing being made that could not thereafter be unmade.

Buchanan watched the pen move. He watched it with the fixed attention of one who understands that what is being written will one day require his countersignature, or his public repudiation, and that the interval between this moment and that reckoning is the only time remaining to him. He might yet speak. He might rise again, might say: *I cannot remain in this cabinet if this order is issued without congressional sanction.* The sentence was available to him. It had been available since before he entered this room. He could feel its weight, the way a man feels the weight of a stone he has decided not to throw.

Marcy's pen continued its small, industrious work.

Buchanan did not rise.

Polk removed his palms from the table.

---

The corridor was cold in the manner of stone that has not been warm since autumn. Gas lamps burned at intervals along the wall, their light the colour of old tallow, and the shadows between them were absolute. Buchanan walked half a pace behind the President, as protocol required, as habit had long since made involuntary.

He stopped.

Polk took two more steps before he registered the silence behind him and turned.

'Mr. President.' Buchanan's voice was level. 'Did you ever expect Mexico to receive him?'

The question occupied the corridor. Somewhere deeper in the house, a clock measured the silence.

Polk looked at him. Not through him, not past him — at him, with the fixed attention of a man who has just been handed the instrument he required.

'No,' he said. 'It is the best possible outcome.'

The words arrived with the same unhurried authority as everything else Polk said — and that was the thing that struck Buchanan, not the admission itself, which he had suspected, which some part of him had known since the instructions were drafted, but the complete absence of concealment. No lowered voice. No glance toward the far end of the corridor. He said it the way a surveyor states the elevation of a ridge, as though the moral character of the calculation were simply not a category he recognised.

A servant emerged from the cabinet room behind them, bearing a tray of used coffee cups, the china rattling faintly with each step. Both men moved apart to let him pass. The footsteps receded toward the servants' stair, and the sound of the cups diminished, and then there was nothing but the gas and the cold and the smell of tallow smoke.

'Mexico's refusal establishes the insult,' Polk said. 'A negotiated settlement would have required us to purchase what is already ours by right of annexation. A refusal requires us to take it. The one would have cost us twenty-five million dollars and the patience of the Senate. The other will cost us considerably less, and considerably sooner.'

Buchanan said nothing. He was thinking of Slidell's letter — the word *ajustado* in its italicised Spanish — and of the fact that he had not seen it, had not been permitted to see it, and of what that omission signified about the degree to which his own participation in this enterprise had been, from the first, a performance staged for his benefit.

'You drafted the war message before the dispatches arrived,' he said. 'On the basis of the commercial claims alone.'

'The commercial claims are legitimate.'

'They are insufficient, and you know it. The Senate would never —'

'The Senate,' Polk said, 'will receive what the situation provides.' He turned and walked two steps toward his office, then stopped with his back still to Buchanan. 'The commercial claims are the foundation. Slidell's reception was to have been the architecture. The river will be the roof.' He turned then, and his face in the gas-light was without expression. 'The structure requires all three. I have the foundation. I have the roof. Mr. Slidell has given me what stands between them.'

'When the war is concluded — what terms do you intend to demand?'

'California. New Mexico. The Rio Grande as the boundary of Texas. In exchange for which this republic will pay the Mexican government fifteen million dollars and assume all outstanding claims of American citizens against that nation.'

'Fifteen million dollars,' Buchanan said. 'For half a continent.'

'It is a generous settlement for a government that will have lost the capacity to refuse it.'

The lamp nearest Buchanan's shoulder gave a faint, irregular hiss. The cold pressed in from the plaster on both sides, the kind that settles into a building's bones in January and does not yield until March. He could smell the gas now, faintly acrid beneath the tallow smoke, and the cold itself had a quality he associated with nothing so much as old paper — the smell of the archive, of things set down and preserved against the day they would be required as evidence.

'You will require diplomatic justification,' he said. 'For the European powers. Particularly Britain.'

'I will require a circular letter from the Department of State assuring the foreign ministers of Great Britain and France that this republic prosecutes the war in defence of its recognised boundaries and entertains no designs upon the sovereign territory of the Mexican Republic.'

The silence this time was different from the silence before.

'You are asking me,' Buchanan said, 'to write a letter that is false in its essential character, to be transmitted to governments whose goodwill we require, and whose continued neutrality will depend upon their believing it.'

'I am asking you,' Polk said, 'to perform the functions of your office.'

He walked the remaining distance to his office door, placed his hand on the frame, and spoke without turning.

'General Taylor will have his orders within the week. The men at Corpus Christi will march to the river before the spring rains make the roads impassable. When Mexican forces engage them on ground that Mexico considers its own, this republic will have its provocation.' A pause, the length of a drawn breath. 'I trust this presents no difficulty.'

He went in. The door did not slam. It simply closed, with the soft, decisive sound of a latch finding its seat.

Buchanan stood in the corridor. The gas lamp hissed. He looked at his gloves in his hand, then put them in his coat pocket.

He had, he understood, just been made an instrument of the thing he had objected to. Not by compulsion — no man had compelled him. By the ordinary operation of office, by the logic of position, by the fact that the circular letter Polk required could not be written by any other hand without first passing through his. He could refuse. The refusal was available to him; it had been available at the cabinet table, available in this corridor, available at every juncture since the instructions for Slidell were first laid before him. He had not used it. He was not certain, standing now in the cold and the tallow light, that he would find it at his desk tomorrow morning when the blank sheet lay before him and the pen was in his hand.

He turned and walked toward the entrance hall, his heels striking the floor in the even cadence of a man going somewhere he has already decided to go.

---

In the office behind the closed door, Slidell's letter lay face-down beneath the Revised Statutes. Beneath it — placed there by Polk's own hand that afternoon, its seal still intact — lay the note from Senator Calhoun, requesting in the measured language of a man who suspects he already knows the answer whether the administration possessed any private communication from Mexico City bearing upon the question of Mr. Slidell's reception.

Taylor's orders would go out within the week. The dragoons at Corpus Christi would break camp in the cold before the month was out, their boots already wearing against the chaparral, their officers already studying the ground between the Nueces and the river. Calhoun's letter would remain beneath its weight of statutes until those orders were already riding south — until American soldiers stood on the northern bank of the Rio Grande, their muskets loaded, watching the far shore for movement. By the time the senator received his answer, if he received one at all, the question of what the administration had known, and when, and what it had chosen not to say, would belong to a different season entirely.

And in that different season, there would be blood on the ground, and the President would have his war, and the circular letter would be drafted in Buchanan's careful hand, and none of it — not the omitted letter, not the manufactured insult, not the army already moving through the brush toward a boundary that existed nowhere but in the President's declared intention — none of it would be retrievable.

The lamp in the corridor guttered once and held.

Somewhere to the south, in the cold dark of a Texas winter, a column of infantry was already being told to make ready.
