# Chapter Eighteen: The Halls of Montezuma

The dispatches arrived before nine o'clock, three separate packets laid across the desk by Derrick with the efficiency of a man who understood that this morning required no ceremony. Buchanan broke the first seal — the War Department's red wafer, pressed with more force than the occasion demanded — and read.

Scott's forces had entered Mexico City on the thirteenth. The National Palace flew the American flag. General Santa Anna had withdrawn south of the capital with the remnant of his army. Chapultepec had fallen after a bombardment of six hours, the cadets at its walls fighting with a tenacity that Scott's own dispatch acknowledged in a subordinate clause and then passed over. The war, by any military calculation, was finished.

He set the dispatch down and did not reach for the second.

The map on the wall — a surveyor's rendering, pins marking Scott's advance with a thread running from Veracruz inland — told him what the dispatches confirmed. The thread ended at Mexico City. The conquest was complete. And yet he remained here, behind this desk, in a capital three thousand miles from the consequence, with no authorised negotiator in the field and a recall order already travelling south toward a man who did not yet know he had been dismissed.

He rose and went to the window.

Pennsylvania Avenue lay below in the early September light, the dust still unsettled from a morning's traffic, a cart horse standing in the traces with its head low. The city moved with its customary indifference to the magnitude of what had occurred three thousand miles to the south. He pressed two fingers against the glass and felt the warmth already gathered there — the sun barely above the rooftops, the day already promising a heat that would not relent before evening. From the street below came the sound of iron wheels on stone, the cry of a vendor, then silence settling back over the avenue like sediment.

He had begun turning back toward the desk when the fly found the window.

It struck the glass once, retreated, struck again. He stepped aside without looking at it, and in that interval his eye fell on the third packet, the one he had not yet opened: grey-sealed, the wax stamped with the anchor of the naval station at Veracruz, its edges carrying the damp of paper that had crossed the Gulf in summer. He had nearly passed it over. The War Department communiqués had been placed on top, their red wafers and the vocabulary of Marcy's office arranged to command attention, and they had succeeded.

He picked up the grey packet and broke the seal.

A routine communication from Commodore Perry's squadron — ordnance inventories, coastal intelligence, the disposition of vessels along the Gulf approaches. He read it standing, turning the pages with the brisk economy he brought to documents that required acknowledgment but not thought, and he was nearly through when the paragraph stopped him cold.

*Confirmed at General Scott's field headquarters as of the eighteenth instant: the person of Mr. N.P. Trist, Chief Clerk, Department of State, who continues in residence and remains in communication with intermediaries of the Mexican government. No official recall packet has been received at this station or, to the knowledge of this command, delivered to the above-named individual.*

He read it again. The fly struck the window a third time and was still.

Trist had not received the recall order.

The arithmetic assembled itself without ceremony. The recall had been dispatched from Washington in mid-August. Transit to Mexico City required three to four weeks under ordinary circumstances. The Commodore's dispatch was dated the twenty-eighth of August. The recall packet was somewhere on the road between Veracruz and the capital — delayed, misdirected, or still aboard a vessel that had not yet made port. Trist remained in the field. He remained authorised — technically, entirely authorised — to negotiate on behalf of a government that had just told him he was no longer its instrument.

The warmth of the window pressed against his back. He did not move.

Here was the thing that could not be spoken aloud in any room of this building: the war was won and the peace was unowned. Scott's army held a capital with no treaty to show for it. The occupation would harden into something permanent if nothing intervened — an army of ten thousand men governing eight million people by the authority of the bayonet alone, while Congress sharpened its knives and the Whig papers composed their editorials and the slavery question, which had not gone anywhere simply because the guns were firing, waited with its customary patience for the moment the shooting stopped. The Republic had taken Mexico City and had no legal instrument to give the taking any shape. Trist was the only man alive who could provide one. And Trist had not yet been told he was dismissed.

He crossed back to the desk. He opened the routing log to the morning's entries, found the line for the Commodore's dispatch, and wrote against it: *Received. Acknowledged. Filed.* Then he slid the packet beneath that edge, set the War Department papers squarely on top, and pressed his palm down upon the stack.

The door opened.

Young Hendricks — nineteen, the nephew of someone whose name had not needed retaining — entered with the careful posture of a man delivering something he would prefer not to be associated with. He carried a single folded note on a small tray, and his expression had that quality of studied blankness the young adopted when they sensed the air of a room was charged with something they could not name.

'From the Executive Mansion, sir. The President requests your attendance in the cabinet room at one o'clock.' A pause, just long enough to constitute something other than a pause. 'Secretary Walker asked me to convey that the President anticipates you will be prepared to affirm the War Department's position regarding the administration of occupied territories.'

Buchanan looked at him steadily. 'Thank you, Hendricks.'

The boy waited a moment longer, then withdrew.

Three hours. Walker had already written the script for the afternoon, and somewhere south of the Rio Grande a recall order was travelling toward a man who had not yet received it, and the question before him now — the only question that mattered — was whether that gap in the record was an accident of distance or an opportunity the Republic had placed in his hands without knowing it had done so.

He retrieved the naval dispatch from beneath the stack and read the relevant paragraph a third time. Then he folded it precisely along its original creases and placed it in the inner drawer of his desk, which locked with a key he kept on his person.

The War Department papers remained on top.

---

The cabinet room of the Executive Mansion held September's heat without mercy. The curtains had been drawn against the afternoon glare, and the effect was not of shade but of compression — the same air recycled through the same lungs, the smell of hot wool and candle tallow thickening with each quarter-hour. Polk sat at the head of the long table, gaunt in a way that had progressed since July, the hollows beneath his cheekbones deepening into something that no campaign portrait would have acknowledged. The map of Mexico lay unrolled before him, its corners weighted by a candlestick, a leather portfolio, an inkwell, and a glass.

Walker had arrived first. He had chosen his chair with care.

Buchanan took his place across from Marcy, who studied the table's grain with the concentrated attention of someone who had determined, before the meeting commenced, that his most useful contribution would be silence. Attorney General Clifford sat to the left, marginally more present. Between them the map showed Mexico City as a small cross of streets in a vast brown interior, the territory spreading southward past any line this room had yet agreed to draw.

'The Secretary of the Treasury,' Polk said, without preamble.

Walker lifted a document of perhaps twelve pages. 'Mr. President, gentlemen.' He did not wait for acknowledgment. 'The Army has achieved a complete and unconditional military success. I have prepared a memorandum on what that success now entitles this administration to demand.'

He began to read. The original treaty terms — California, New Mexico, the Rio Grande boundary, fifteen million dollars — had been calculated, he argued, under conditions of anticipated pressure, not demonstrated supremacy. Scott had taken Chapultepec. He had taken the capital. Under such circumstances, to present Mexico with terms drafted before a single engagement had been fought north of Veracruz was to *surrender* — his word, delivered with the flat precision of long premeditation — the fruits of every soldier who had fallen on the causeways, on the castle heights, at the gates of the city itself.

He arrived at Buchanan without naming him.

'Certain counsel within this administration,' Walker read, in the even tones of a quarterly account, 'has consistently advocated the moderate position.' He turned a page. 'The men who counselled restraint did not stand at Chapultepec. They did not bury their dead in Mexican soil. They propose that those deaths purchase California — which this administration intended to acquire before the first shot was fired — and nothing further.'

Sweat gathered at Buchanan's collar. He did not touch it.

Walker concluded with the new territorial demands: all land north of the Sierra Madre Occidental, Sonora, Chihuahua's silver districts, Baja California. He set the memorandum on the table with the deliberateness of a man placing a wager he expects to collect.

Polk's hands lay flat on the map. He said nothing.

Buchanan removed his spectacles. He set them on the table before him — not a pause for thought, but a declaration that he intended to be seen clearly — and stood.

'Mr. President.' He waited until Polk's eyes came to his. 'I have a question for this administration, and I put it directly. Does the President believe the Congress of the United States will vote to govern eight million Catholic Mexicans as an organised territory?' He did not wait for an answer. 'And does he believe the Whig opposition — which has called this war a manufactured aggression from its first hour — will decline the opportunity to place that question before every voter in every Northern state before the election of eighteen forty-eight?'

Walker's chair scraped. 'Mr. Secretary—'

'I have not finished, sir.' The words came out level, not raised, which was worse. 'The gentleman from Louisiana speaks of what the soldiers at Chapultepec deserve. I share that obligation entirely. What those men deserve is a peace their republic can sustain — a treaty that can pass the Senate, a boundary that can be defended in law and not merely in arms. What they do not deserve is to have their sacrifice rendered the predicate for an occupation without end, of a population that no act of Congress can constitutionally absorb and no administration can politically govern.'

He sat. He replaced his spectacles. He folded his hands before him and said nothing further.

Walker's mouth opened. He closed it.

The room was very warm. Somewhere in the building a door was opened and shut, and the brief draught it produced moved the curtains fractionally before the heat pressed back in.

Marcy had not moved. Clifford's pen had stopped.

Polk's silence was not the silence of deliberation. It was the silence of a man who had already arrived at a conclusion and was now determining the form in which it would be delivered. His eyes moved from Buchanan to the map, and from the map to the middle distance, and the middle distance was not the room.

'I will want a memorandum,' Polk said at last, 'from the Secretary of State. On treaty options. On the constitutional questions as he has framed them. Before any instruction issues to the War Department.'

Walker looked at Polk. Then at Buchanan. Then he drew his document back across the table toward himself — not quickly, not with any visible feeling, but with the particular deliberateness of a man who understands that a motion has been tabled and intends to bring it again.

Buchanan noted the direction of the movement and said nothing.

What he could not say in this room, before these witnesses, was that there was a man in Mexico City who had not yet received his recall, and that the memorandum Polk had just requested would take three days to prepare, and that three days was time enough — if the channels existed and the will was present — to ensure that the gap in the record became something more than an accident of distance.

He gathered his papers. He rose when Polk rose. He exchanged the necessary courtesies with Clifford in the corridor, accepted Marcy's handshake with the warmth he kept in reserve for men whose allegiance was contingent, and descended the steps of the Executive Mansion into the September afternoon, where the heat rose off the paving stones in visible waves and the dust of Pennsylvania Avenue hung suspended in the air like something that had been waiting for him.

---

The supper tray Mrs. Drury had left on the sideboard breathed through its linen cover — roasted fowl gone cold, the sweetness of boiled onion working its way across the study in slow insistence. He had moved the tray twice, once to the windowsill and once back, and the smell persisted regardless. It had settled into the fabric of the room now, into the curtains and the spines of the books, and he had ceased to attend to it.

Three sheets of paper lay before him on the writing desk. The lamp was turned high. From the street below came the intermittent sound of carriage wheels on stone, and from somewhere further along F Street, a pianoforte — slightly out of tune, the notes arriving with the indifference of a city that had no knowledge of what was being composed three streets away.

The first draft had taken twenty minutes and was, upon reading it back, a document that would destroy him. Too explicit in its encouragement, too transparent in its purpose — the kind of letter a congressional committee would read with the particular satisfaction of men who have been waiting for precisely this. He held the corner to the lamp flame and watched the paper curl and darken, and he held it until the heat reached his fingers and dropped it into the grate, where it blackened and broke apart without drama.

He thought of Walker's face in the cabinet room — the way the memorandum had moved back across the table, that fraction of an inch, the small deliberate withdrawal of a man blocked but not finished. Walker would return to Polk tonight. He would argue that Buchanan's constitutional objections were the objections of a presidential aspirant protecting his own prospects, not the Republic's interests, and he would not be entirely wrong, and Polk would know he was not entirely wrong, and the memorandum Buchanan had been asked to produce would be read, when it arrived, through that lens. Whatever he wrote in the next three days would be weighed against Walker's counter-argument before the ink was dry.

The second draft was more careful. He spent forty minutes on it, choosing each phrase with deliberateness. But reading it over, he found that the caution had produced its own kind of visibility — the hedges too numerous, the qualifications arranged with an architecture so deliberate that any man of intelligence would understand precisely what the hedges were concealing. Caution, in excess, was its own form of confession.

He burned the second draft as he had burned the first. The smell of singed paper rose and thinned into the room's existing odours — cold fowl, old ink, the faint char of the grate.

His writing hand cramped then — a sudden seizing between thumb and forefinger, the quill dropping. He put his hand flat on the desk and waited, working each finger in turn against the wood's resistance, the small joints protesting the cold of the surface. When the cramp released, he flexed his hand three more times before he trusted it again.

The third draft would have to accomplish what the first two had not: it would have to mean two things at once. Legible to Trist, who was not a fool and who would understand an oblique instruction from a superior; opaque to anyone else, who would find only the kind of general counsel a Secretary of State might send to any agent in the field — encouraging, cautious, and entirely without actionable content.

He wrote for thirty minutes without stopping. The pianoforte outside had ceased. The carriage traffic had thinned to nothing. The smell of the cooling fowl had long since settled into the room's fabric.

The critical sentence came late in the second paragraph, positioned where a reader moving quickly would take it as part of the surrounding observation about diplomatic patience in foreign capitals.

*The interests of the Republic may require patience beyond the letter of your instructions.*

He held the page under the lamp. The sentence fell neither way without the reader's own weight tipping it. Trist would read it as authorisation. A congressional committee would read it as platitude. Polk — and it must never reach Polk, which was why it travelled outside official channels — would find nothing actionable.

It would have to do.

The wax ran quickly in the candle's heat, pooling across the fold in a small, even spread. He pressed his personal signet into it — not the State Department seal, which would have made the communication an official instrument and therefore traceable, but the small oval device he used on private correspondence — and held the pressure steady until the wax firmed. The impression came up clean.

He set the letter on the corner of the desk. Tomorrow it would go to Commander Breese at the Navy Yard, and from Breese to a courier vessel, and from the vessel to the capital of a republic whose government had not yet decided whether to abandon the one man still positioned to negotiate its peace.

Outside, the September dark had settled fully over the city. The pianoforte had resumed somewhere along the street — faint, slightly out of tune, reaching him through the open window with the indifference of a place that did not know what had just been sealed three streets away.

He did not sleep for some time.

---

The former Mexican Legation had been made festive by French hands, which is to say it had been made festive by concealment. Tricolor bunting draped the mantelpiece, and where it fell short — precisely at the coat of arms above the lintel — the eagle and the serpent bled through the silk in dark outline, patient and unreduced. Candles burned in the chandeliers above the assembled company, their light falling onto epaulettes and décolletage and the raised stems of champagne glasses, and the sound of the gathering was the sound of a city celebrating a victory that had cost it nothing.

Buchanan had arrived at half past seven with the sealed letter in the breast pocket of his evening coat. It rested against his sternum now as he moved through Pageot's reception, exchanging pleasantries with men whose names he remembered and whose opinions he had long since catalogued and found wanting. The letter was the fixed point of the evening. Everything else was performance.

Commander Breese stood near the east windows, a glass in hand, watching the room with the mild surveillance of an officer accustomed to reading weather. Compact, tending to stillness. Buchanan had known him through two administrations and through the particular fraternity of men who understood that the republic's real work was conducted not in the speeches but in the silences between them.

He crossed toward Breese without hurry, pausing to accept a greeting from the Portuguese attaché, and arrived at the windows as though the prospect of the street below had drawn him there.

'A fine evening,' Buchanan said.

'The city is pleased with itself,' Breese replied.

Neither looked at the other. The room's noise pressed around them — a French quadrille from the hired musicians in the anteroom, the clink of glass on glass, the murmur of men congratulating each other on events they had witnessed from a considerable distance.

'I have a communication,' Buchanan said, low, 'that requires conveyance to the south. Outside the ordinary channels.'

Breese turned his glass once in his hand. 'Outside.'

'Through your squadron. The courier departs at first tide.'

A pause. The chandelier above them swayed fractionally in a draught from the far door.

'Mr. Secretary.' Breese's voice carried the same even quality he might have used to report a change in wind direction. 'I cannot carry unofficial correspondence to a recalled diplomat. The exposure, if discovered, would constitute grounds for court-martial.' A beat. 'I would also require some account of the letter's contents.'

'The contents are not your responsibility.'

'They become mine the moment I place them in my coat.'

Before Buchanan could answer, a small, precise figure detached itself from the nearer cluster of guests and moved toward them with the unhurried purpose of a man who happens to find the east windows interesting. Monsieur Pageot, the French minister, was a diplomat of the old school: his pleasantries arrived with the punctuality of artillery, and his curiosity was never casual.

'Secretary Buchanan.' Pageot inclined his head. 'You grace our modest celebration.'

'The occasion merits it, Monsieur Pageot. The cause of peace advances.'

'Indeed.' A fractional pause — the length of a held breath. 'One hopes it advances in directions already agreed upon.' Pageot's gaze moved, briefly, to Breese, then returned, the movement so slight that only a man watching for it would have caught it. 'Commander. I trust the Gulf remains hospitable to American vessels.'

'Entirely,' Breese said.

Pageot lingered a moment longer than courtesy required — long enough to establish that he had lingered — and withdrew toward a knot of guests near the fireplace.

Buchanan did not watch him go. He turned back to Breese.

'You said court-martial,' he said quietly. 'I will tell you what the alternative looks like. There is a man in that capital who is this republic's sole remaining instrument of peace. He has received no instruction in weeks. He does not know whether his government has abandoned him or merely failed him, and in the present circumstances the distinction is not material.' He paused. 'If no word reaches him — if he concludes he has been forgotten, or if the recall arrives before any counsel does — he will depart. He will come home. And when he does, there will be no one with standing to negotiate anything, and Scott's army will govern that capital by bayonet alone until Congress decides what to do with it, which may be never.' He held Breese's gaze. 'That is what you decline to prevent, Commander, if you decline.'

Breese said nothing immediately. The quadrille in the anteroom resolved into something slower, the hired musicians finding a new key.

'I am not asking you to read it,' Buchanan said. 'I am not asking you to answer for it. I am asking you to place it in the hands of a man who still believes this republic is capable of conducting itself with honour. If that belief is misplaced, it will do no harm. If it is not misplaced, it may be the difference between a treaty and an occupation without end.'

A champagne glass was pressed into his hand by a passing French attaché — young, oblivious, already moving on — and Buchanan accepted it without looking away from Breese.

The silence between them had its own texture. Behind them the slow music continued, and the candles in the chandelier above shifted in a draught, and the light moved across Breese's face in a way that showed nothing.

Then Breese said, very quietly: 'The ship departs at four.'

Buchanan turned. The windowsill was empty.

He raised his glass toward the assembled company and drank. The champagne was indifferent, slightly warm, with the faint sweetness of something bottled too early. Across the room, Marcy was accepting congratulations from the British minister — Sir Henry Bulwer, who had arrived only last month and was still determining who in Washington was worth cultivating. Bulwer's attention was directed principally at Marcy, who stood with the comfortable solidity of a man occupying ground he had not earned and intended to keep.

Buchanan moved, gradually, toward the room's centre. The movement required perhaps six steps, accomplished across two brief exchanges about the weather and one about the reliability of the coastal packet service. By the end of it he stood equidistant between Marcy and the French minister's wife, which placed him — by no geometry he had consciously arranged — at the room's approximate centre. Bulwer's gaze found him there, briefly, before returning to Marcy.

He was still positioned there, glass in hand, when he became aware that Pageot had not rejoined the guests near the fireplace. The French minister stood instead near the far wall, speaking with his secretary, and his attention — offered with the complete naturalness of a man absorbed in conversation — was directed, at an angle that wanted accounting for, toward the windows where he and Breese had stood.

Pageot's secretary was a young man who carried dispatches to Paris.

The letter was in Breese's coat. The courier ship sailed before dawn. And Pageot, who had approached twice and withdrawn twice, and who had looked once at the windowsill with eyes that missed very little, was now speaking quietly with a man whose profession was the conveyance of information to foreign capitals.

Buchanan raised his glass again — to no one in particular, to the room, to the candles and the bunting and the eagle and the serpent bleeding through the silk above the mantelpiece — and found that his hand was entirely steady.

Whether it would remain so depended on what Pageot had seen, and what Pageot intended to do with it, and whether the ship reached Veracruz before the recall order reached its destination, and whether Trist, upon reading a single sentence about the patience the Republic required, would understand that the man who had written it was asking him to risk everything on the proposition that conscience and ambition, on this one occasion, pointed in the same direction.

He drank. The champagne was no better than it had been. Around him the celebration continued, and the city outside went about its evening, and somewhere across the room Pageot's secretary accepted a fresh glass from a passing tray and laughed at something his minister had said, and the laugh carried across the noise of the gathering with the ease of a man who has nothing to conceal — or who has already concealed it.
