# Chapter 8: The Contraction

## Scene 1

The fire had been built too high. Dr. Holt's orders, relayed through Donelson with the apologetic precision of a man conveying a sentence he had not himself pronounced. Jackson lay against the banked pillows with the counterpane pulled to his chest, the smell of camphor thick in the close air, the medicine bottles ranked on the side table catching the firelight in amber and brown, a basin half-screened by a cloth that did not quite conceal what it contained.

September still. The oaks beyond the window had not yet turned.

Papers from the Treasury lay spread across the counterpane, corners weighted by a small Bible and Jackson's own fist. He had been reading since first light — or what passed for first light in a room where the curtains were never fully opened — and his eyes had not troubled him as much as his legs, which had swelled again through the night and now sat beneath the wool like something that had ceased to belong to him.

Donelson came in without knocking, carrying a fold of newsprint, his step carrying the compressed urgency of a man who has decided what he will say and is not yet certain of its reception.

"The Girard Bank has confirmed receipt of the first transfer," Donelson said, setting the newspaper on the bedside table before Jackson could reach for it. "Sixty thousand in specie, as Taney arranged."

"And the others?"

Donelson's hand did not move from the paper. "Portsmouth is willing. Cincinnati is deliberating."

"Deliberating." Jackson said it the way a physician says *gangrene*.

"There are reports from Philadelphia, sir." Donelson lifted the newspaper, unfolded it, and extended it at the angle of a man presenting evidence he would rather not have gathered. "Biddle has curtailed discounts. The figure reported—" He paused. "Two million dollars. In a single week."

The fire popped. A coal shifted and settled.

Jackson reached out and took the paper. He read the passage Donelson had folded to the outside, the column of figures below the dateline, the names of the Philadelphia counting-houses that had presented notes for renewal and been refused.

Two million. One week.

He had expected Biddle to contract — had told Taney to expect it, had told Kendall, had said so plainly in August when the plan was still confined to the four men who knew it. But he had expected a slow tightening over months, a thing that could be answered in kind with time to place the answer. Not this. Not a fortnight after Taney's signature was dry.

He pushed the counterpane aside and swung his legs toward the floor. Vertigo rushed up through him at once, and he caught the bedpost with both hands and gripped until the room steadied.

Donelson had not moved. Had not offered his arm. Knew better.

Jackson released the post and sat on the edge of the bed. The fire had burned lower in the last hour, and in its diminished light Donelson's face was all shadow and hollow — a young man carrying news he had not chosen to carry, waiting to learn what it would cost them both.

"Biddle means to frighten the pet banks off before the transfers are complete," Jackson said. "He means to show them what it costs to do business with this government."

"That is what it appears, sir."

"It is not appearance. It is declaration." He found Donelson's eyes. "Sit down and take this. Write to Taney — tonight, not tomorrow. He is to accelerate every transfer still pending. Every one." He drew a breath that caught briefly in his chest before he controlled it. "And write a second letter. To Blair. Tell him the Bank has contracted two millions in discounts in seven days, that it has done so not from necessity but from malice, and that every merchant in Philadelphia who cannot renew his note this week is to understand who holds the knife." He paused. "Those are not my words for publication. Blair will find his own. But that is the argument he is to make."

Donelson had his pen out. The scratch of nib on paper was the only sound besides the fire.

"The deliberating banks," Jackson said. "Cincinnati."

"Yes, sir."

"Send word that the General has taken note of their deliberation." He said nothing further on the subject.

Donelson wrote.

The coughing came without warning — deep and racking, doubling him forward, his fist driving against his sternum. When it released him he took the handkerchief from his coat pocket, looked at it a moment, then folded it and set it beside the medicine bottles.

"Biddle has struck first," he said, his voice stripped level again. "Good. Let him strike. Every note he refuses in Philadelphia is a witness for us when the time comes to name what he has done to this country."

Donelson blotted the page and looked up.

"Is there anything else, sir?"

"Tell Taney," Jackson said, "that the contraction is faster than we reckoned. He will know what that means." He turned back to the papers spread across the counterpane, then stopped. "And Donelson — if Cincinnati does not answer by Friday, they will lose the deposits to a bank that will. Biddle cannot have them, and a coward cannot keep them. Make that plain."

Donelson's pen scratched again. The fire settled lower. Outside, the oaks stood dark and unmoving against the September sky, and the transfers that had not yet cleared were forfeit the moment Biddle's agents reached those cities — which, if the contraction continued at this pace, might be a matter of days rather than weeks.

## Scene 2

The East Room received them in November half-light, the chandeliers dark above, the single fire producing more smoke than warmth. The Baltimore men stood in a rough semicircle — twenty of them, broadcloth coats damp from long travel, their breath faintly visible in the cold — and the room around them was too large, too silent, too indifferent to their suffering. Jackson had positioned himself near the hearth, his cane before him, and he did not speak as they arranged themselves.

He waited.

The spokesman was a broad-shouldered shipper named Harrington — a man who had built his yard with his own hands and believed that fact entitled him to plain speech with any man in the republic. He held his petition with both hands, not as a document but as a relic, and when he began to read his voice carried the steadiness of someone who has rehearsed against the possibility of breaking.

'Mr. President.' He paused. Let the address settle. 'We come not as partisans but as men of commerce — men who have employed their neighbours, paid their debts, met their obligations, and asked nothing of this government save the ordinary protections of law and contract.' He lifted the petition slightly. 'This document bears the signatures of four hundred and thirty-seven citizens of Baltimore and the surrounding counties. It speaks of ships lying idle at the wharves for want of credit to provision them. It speaks of workers turned away. It speaks of notes protested that in ordinary times would have been renewed without question.'

A gust came down the flue and sent a sheet of grey ash across the hearthstone. Several of the men shifted. Harrington did not. Something had altered in his face — a tightening around the jaw, a compression of the prepared cadence into something rawer.

'There is a man known to several here present.' Lower now, the oratory gone. 'A shipwright. A veteran of the late war with Britain, who kept his yard on the Patapsco for nineteen years. The Bank called in his note in October. He could not meet the demand.' A long pause. 'He has lost the yard. He has lost his house. His wife is living with her sister.'

The mantel clock ticked. From outside came the sound of a horse's hooves on gravel, unhurried, going somewhere else entirely.

Jackson stood very still. The firelight moved across the hollows of his face. He was looking at Harrington, but what he saw was not Harrington — it was a yard on the Patapsco, a man's nineteen years, a wife carrying her belongings to her sister's house in the early cold of an October morning. He had known men like that. Had been, in certain stripped-down essentials, something near that man himself, once, when the notes came due and the land went and Rachel had stood in a doorway with her hands at her sides.

He let the silence hold a moment longer than was comfortable.

Then he drew himself up, and what had moved behind his eyes withdrew.

'Mr. Harrington.' Quiet, and the quiet was not gentleness. 'I have heard every word you have spoken. I do not dismiss a syllable of it.' He paused. 'The man who lost his yard on the Patapsco — I know what that costs. I know it in my bones.' One beat, two. 'And I tell you plainly: the Bank of the United States has done this to him. Not this government. Not this office. The Bank contracted its credit deliberately — not from any pressure upon its obligations, not from any necessity of its condition, but as a weapon, wielded with full knowledge of the suffering it would produce, to bring this government to its knees.'

Harrington's mouth opened, then closed. He turned his face away — only a degree, only enough that Jackson would not see what had moved there.

Jackson raised one hand from his cane. 'Carry your petition to Chestnut Street, sir. Nicholas Biddle holds every remedy you have described. The specie is in his vaults. He has the power to restore every farthing of credit he has withdrawn, and he has withdrawn it not because his institution required it but because he has chosen to make the people of this republic hostages — every ruined merchant, every idle ship, every wife carrying her belongings to her sister's house — hostages to his recharter.' He held Harrington's gaze. 'I cannot in conscience reverse a lawful act of this government to reward that calculation. Present your petition where the power to answer it actually resides.'

Harrington held the document at his side. He did not speak.

At the back of the semicircle, one of the younger merchants — prosperous coat, heavy gold watch-chain looped across his waistcoat — stood with his hat pressed to his chest and his eyes fixed on the middle distance. Jackson's gaze moved to that chain. The man had ridden from Baltimore to plead poverty, and there was enough gold on his person to renew the shipwright's note and leave change.

'Gentlemen,' Jackson said. 'You have my answer.'

## Scene 3

He gave up on sleep well before the clock struck three.

He sat at his desk in the study, the single lamp throwing its circle across the papers, and beyond that circle the room was dark and close, smelling of the camphor preparation someone had refreshed on his chest without asking — he could taste it at the roof of his mouth, medicinal and faintly sweet. The fire had been banked low; the physician had relented on that point, at least, and the room held a bearable chill that he preferred to the sickroom's suffocating warmth. He pressed his stockinged feet flat to the floor. The cold worked up through the boards and into his ankles with a patience that no grate could answer.

The newspapers were spread before him in the order Donelson had arranged them. He read as a general reads terrain — not for comfort, not for persuasion, but for the shape of the ground.

*Hezekiah Morse, merchant, Pearl Street, New York. Three notes protested. Yard closed. Eleven men dismissed.*

*The counting house of Whitmore & Sons, Philadelphia. Unable to meet obligations of fourteen thousand dollars. Assets surrendered to the Bank's agent.*

*The family of one James Reardon, ship chandler, whose note of eight hundred dollars was called the fourth of December, the premises now vacant—*

He turned the page.

More names. More figures. Baltimore. Boston. The same arithmetic in different hands, the same ruin in different handwriting.

Rachel looked out from her portrait above the mantel. He had hung it himself, six years ago, and had not moved it since. Without looking up from the newspaper, he reached among the papers and found the small oval miniature — she was younger in it, her gaze direct and without apology. He held it for a long moment. Then he set it face-down and turned back to the columns of ruin.

A sudden deep pain seized his left side — the old ball shifting as it always did in the cold — and he rose and pressed his fist hard against his ribs, standing with his forehead nearly to the dark window glass until the spasm passed. Outside, a fine dry snow had begun; he could see it in the lamplight from the lower windows, falling without wind, settling on the gravel in silence. He straightened, returned to the chair, drew his coat across his lap.

He read for another quarter hour. Then he stopped.

Beneath the *Intelligencer*, beneath the *Courier and Enquirer* with its columns of ruined merchants, lay a letter in a plain hand, postmarked Nashville, the seal already broken by Donelson's preliminary sorting. He had set it aside earlier in the evening. He picked it up now.

*General — we have heard the reports from the Eastern cities and I will tell you what the men here say. They say the Bank has never lent us a dollar we did not beg for, and when we begged they named their terms. The contraction is felt in New York and Philadelphia because those cities live on Bank paper. In the western counties we raise what we eat and sell what we raise and the Bank's notes have been of little use and less comfort. Hold firm, General. We are with you.*

It was signed by a man he had known since the Creek campaign. He read it twice.

He set the letter down and sat with his hands flat on the desk, looking at nothing. Then he pulled a fresh sheet toward him, uncapped the inkwell, and began.

*My dear Taney — the newspapers cry ruin from the seaboard cities, and I do not dismiss the suffering. But I have tonight a letter from a Tennessee farmer who reports the contraction scarcely felt in the western counties, where the Bank's paper has never run. Mark this well. The distress is not universal — it is concentrated precisely where the Bank's reach is deepest. This is not a natural consequence of removal. This is a map of the Monster's dominion. The suffering proves the tyranny. It does not prove our error. Use this argument — use it in every memorial, every brief, every letter to the floor managers. The geography of ruin is our evidence. I want it before the public before Clay opens his mouth on the floor.*

He wrote steadily, the lamp warm against his forearm, the snow continuing in silence beyond the glass. When he finished he read what he had written, made one correction, and found it sufficient.

He folded the letter and set it with the morning's dispatch.

Then he picked up the miniature, looked at it once more, and placed it in the desk drawer. He did not set it face-down again. He put it away.

## Scene 4

The New York men arrived on a Thursday, forty of them, and they filled the reception room until the air was close with cold wool and the particular tension of men who have crossed several hundred miles to deliver a verdict they believe unanswerable.

James G. King stepped forward from the rest. He was not a slight man, and he carried the bearing of a name — Rufus King's son, Federalist blood, the whole weight of New York commerce in his straight back. He did not wait for Jackson to speak. He unfolded the memorial and began.

*To the President of the United States, the undersigned merchants and citizens of New York, suffering under a general distress of commerce occasioned by the withdrawal of public deposits from the Bank of the United States—*

Jackson stood behind the writing table. He had not sat. He would not sit.

King's voice was trained for this — measured, grave, each clause set down like a stone in a wall. He documented failures by name: three importing houses on Pearl Street, a shipyard in Brooklyn, a textile concern in lower Manhattan that had employed two hundred workmen and now employed forty. He read figures — notes protested, credit refused, vessels idle at the East River wharves with their cargoes unsold. He cited the constitutional ground with the precision of a brief prepared in consultation with men who understood exactly where to drive the wedge: the Secretary of the Treasury's authority under the charter, the rights of Congress, the limits of executive discretion under a government of laws.

The room was quiet except for the faint sound of the street below — a cart, a horse, the ordinary world continuing its business without apparent awareness that it was being throttled.

A bead of sweat ran from Jackson's temple into his collar. He swallowed against a sudden rise of nausea, the room tilting fractionally before it steadied. His hands were on the edge of the table, and he gripped harder, the wood biting into his palms.

King reached the conclusion. He demanded restoration of the deposits to the Bank as the sole and sufficient remedy for the nation's distress, looked up from the memorial, and waited.

The room waited with him.

Jackson straightened. He released the table's edge. He came around it.

'Mr. King.' Not quiet now — controlled, which was a different thing entirely, the voice of a man who has decided to be heard rather than merely answered. 'I have listened to your memorial. I have attended to every name, every figure, every constitutional argument your counsel has furnished you. And I will tell you what I observe.'

He stopped three feet from King. King did not step back.

'Your memorial attributes this distress to the withdrawal of public deposits. It does not ask why Nicholas Biddle chose to contract two millions in discounts in a single week — a contraction the Bank's own condition did not require, a contraction no pressure of its obligations necessitated. It does not ask why the specie sits in the vaults on Chestnut Street while your ships rot at the East River wharves. It asks me to restore the deposits. It asks me, in plain terms, to surrender.' He drew a breath. 'I will not.'

King's jaw tightened. 'Mr. President, the Constitution—'

'The Constitution, Mr. King, does not authorise a private corporation to manufacture a national panic in order to compel a presidential reversal. That is not banking. That is coercion.' Jackson held his ground. 'You have ridden from the city to lay this before me, and I respect the journey. Now I ask you to make another. Carry your memorial to Philadelphia. Present it at the counter on Chestnut Street. Ask Mr. Biddle — with the same gravity and the same figures — why he has chosen to visit this suffering upon the merchants of your state rather than restore the credit that lies idle in his vaults. The answer he gives you will instruct you better than anything I can say.'

King held the memorial at his side. Something had shifted in his expression — not defeat, but recalculation: the set jaw, the briefly hooded eyes of a man whose prepared argument has met ground it did not anticipate.

Thomas Hart Benton, at the far wall, unfolded his arms.

Jackson turned back to the table. 'Gentlemen. This government will not be moved by a panic of Mr. Biddle's manufacture. If you wish an end to your distress, you know where to apply.'

He had nearly reached the table when King spoke again, his voice lower, stripped of its prepared register.

'General. There are men in that memorial who will not survive another month of this.'

Jackson stopped. He did not turn immediately. When he did, his expression had not changed, but something behind it had — a compression, as though the words had found a place that was not armoured.

'I know it,' he said. 'God help me, I know it. And the man who has made them so is Nicholas Biddle, and he will answer for it before this republic is done with him.' He held King's gaze for a moment. 'Go to him, sir. Tell him what you have told me. Tell him what it costs. And watch his face when you do.'

## Scene 5

The cedar smoke reached Taney before he cleared the doorway — thick and resinous, layered over the laudanum smell that had settled into the curtains and the wool of the bedclothes. He sat in the straight-backed chair with his coat still buttoned, hands folded across his knee, and waited while Jackson gathered himself against the pillows. The fire crackled and spat. A log had been added recently; the bark was still catching, and heat pressed against Taney's collar, the back of his neck.

From outside came the sound of iron wheels on frozen gravel — a late carriage, moving quickly, going away.

'They have the votes,' Taney said.

'How many?'

'Sufficient. Clay intends to call the debate before the month is out.' A pause. 'Grundy has gone over. Two others from the Western states are expected to follow — they cannot hold against the petitions from their districts.'

Jackson drew the blanket higher over his legs with slow, deliberate movements. Then he fixed Taney with a gaze that burned in the hollowed face.

'Coerced,' he said. 'Every man of them.'

'The Bank holds their constituents' notes. Yes.' Taney met the gaze without flinching. 'The coercion is real. It does not, however, change the count.'

'No.' Flat, stripped to its bone. 'It does not.'

The fire spat again. The laudanum smell came through the cedar smoke in a thin, sweet thread.

Taney reached into his coat and produced several folded sheets — his own hand, the lines close and precise. He laid them on the counterpane near Jackson's right hand without ceremony.

'Benton writes that the floor managers need a document,' he said. 'Not a speech. A constitutional argument they can rally to. Something that does not merely answer the censure but attacks it — establishes the censure itself as the constitutional violation.'

Jackson's right hand moved to the sheets, rested there, fingers spread.

'Say it plainly.'

'A formal Protest to the Senate. Not a defence of the removal — an assertion that the Senate has exceeded its authority. That censure without trial, without charge, without any of the forms of impeachment, is itself an usurpation.' Taney's voice slowed, each word set down with the care of a man who knows he is drafting doctrine rather than argument. 'The executive power is vested in the President alone. The Senate may advise. It may refuse confirmation. It may impeach, through the House, by the prescribed method. What it may not do is pronounce judgment upon an executive act by resolution. That is not legislation. It is not trial. It is nothing the Constitution authorises or contemplates.'

'And you will write it.'

'If you authorise me to.'

Jackson took up the pen. His fingers seized around it at once, and he had to flex them twice — open, close, open — before the tendons released enough to hold the quill. He made a short mark at the top of Taney's first sheet. Not a signature. A line.

'Write it,' he said.

Taney took the sheets back without comment.

'It will not stop the vote,' Jackson said.

'No, General. It will not.'

'Clay will pass his censure.'

'He will.'

The fire snapped. Outside, frozen gravel crunched once under a boot — a sentry making his round — and then silence came back.

'And your confirmation,' Jackson said. 'What do you reckon they will do with that?'

Taney folded the sheets and replaced them in his coat. His expression gave nothing. 'I reckon,' he said, 'that is a question for another evening.'

He rose, buttoned his coat to the collar, and reached for his hat. At the door he paused with one hand on the frame, his back to the room.

'The Protest will be ready when you need it.'

He was drawing on his second glove when the footstep came — quick, deliberate, from the end of the corridor — and Donelson appeared behind him, a sealed dispatch in hand, his breath coming short as though he had taken the stairs at a run.

'A rider from the Capitol, sir.' He looked past Taney to Jackson. 'Senator Clay has moved the censure resolution to the calendar. The debate opens Monday.'

Taney's hands went still on the glove. He did not turn.

In the bed, Jackson said nothing. The fire burned. The cedar smoke thickened in the close air, and the room held both men in their separate silences — Taney at the threshold, one hand raised, the other at his side; Jackson against his pillows, his eyes open and fixed on something that was not in the room.

Monday. Four days. And somewhere on the road between Philadelphia and Washington, Biddle's agents were already moving — carrying instructions, calling notes, drawing the noose another increment tighter before the Senate could even open its mouth to pronounce the knot legitimate.
