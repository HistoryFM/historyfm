# Chapter 11: The Chief Justice

## Scene 1

The letter from Colonel McHenry arrived on a Thursday, folded with care, and Taney did not open it at once. He set it on the corner of the writing desk and returned to the window, where the frost had worked its way inward along the casement, each crystal a small, precise argument for the season. Frederick lay grey and still below. A carter moved his horses toward the mill, their breath rising in pale columns against the cold air. Taney watched them until they were gone.

He was not well. He had not been well since September, when the intestinal complaint had returned with the patience of a creditor who knows the debt will be honoured in time. The fire had been burning since before dawn, but the warmth did not reach him the way it once had — it stopped at the surface of things, at the wool of his coat, at the skin of his hands, and went no further.

He opened McHenry's letter.

It was thorough. Three pages in a close hand, each sentence a delivery of bad intelligence arranged in ascending order of its severity. Webster had been seen at Clay's rooms the previous Tuesday. Three senators whose votes had been considered reliable were now described as wavering. The Committee of Ways and Means would move to open the confirmation debate before the month was out. Then the particulars: the veto message's doctrine of coordinate review, characterised without euphemism as *constitutional heresy*; the deposit removal, to be placed before the Senate as executive usurpation; and the rejection of June 1834, which would be read into the record as proof that the Senate had already rendered its judgment on Roger Brooke Taney's fitness for high office.

He read it twice. The second reading was slower — not because he required the additional time, but because the principle, properly understood, demanded that a man look at the thing he intends to answer before he answers it.

He heard Anne on the stairs — her step distinct, unhurried, the step of a woman who had learned to move through a house in which bad news arrived with regularity and required no theatrical acknowledgment. She came in carrying a second letter, held close, as though its weight required the nearness of the body to be properly assessed.

'This came with the morning post,' she said. 'The seal is the General's.'

He took it. The handwriting was Jackson's — that forward lean, the tremor in the downstrokes worsened since the spring. Inside, a single line:

*I have kept my promise. Now keep yours.*

Anne stood near the fire, her hands folded before her, and the firelight moved across her face.

'Roger,' she said. Only that.

He turned to the desk. He pulled McHenry's letter toward him and turned it face-down, so that the intelligence lay against the wood and could not be consulted again. His hand trembled as he took up the pen. He brought his left hand across and steadied the writing wrist against the desk's edge until the tremor subsided. Then a cramp seized him low in the bowels, sudden and clenching, and he set the pen down and breathed through his teeth, gripping the desk's edge until it passed.

He picked up the pen again.

'The Committee moves before the month is out,' Anne said. She had read McHenry's letter — he had not told her she might, but he had not folded it, and she was Anne. 'Webster and Clay together. Three votes uncertain.' A pause in which the fire shifted and settled. 'You are not well enough to go to Washington.'

'I am not well enough to remain in Frederick,' he said.

'Roger.' Her voice was different now — stripped of its careful management. 'If you go to Washington and the vote fails, you will have gone ill and come home defeated. The physicians have not cleared you for travel. I have read what they wrote.'

He dipped the pen. 'Then you have read more than I asked you to.'

'You left it on the desk.'

He looked at the nib. A drop was forming at its tip, threatening the paper. He held it steady over the inkwell until the drop fell back.

'I am going,' he said. 'The question is whether I go having written this letter or having failed to write it.'

The fire shifted. Anne did not answer. After a moment he heard her cross to the window — the sound of her skirts against the floorboards, the slight creak of the board near the casement that had needed attending to since autumn. She stood there while he wrote. When he had finished and sanded the page, she was still at the window, her back to him, looking out at the grey road where the carter had gone.

He folded the letter, sealed it, and set it apart from McHenry's pages. Two documents on the same desk: one that described the cost of what he was about to do, and one that committed him to doing it. He looked at them both for a moment. Then he rose and rang for the boy to carry the acceptance to the post.

## Scene 2

The gallery was full by half past nine, the benches packed so close that winter coats pressed against Taney's arms on either side, warm and faintly damp, two hundred spectators' exhalations rising toward the coffered ceiling. Below, the Senate chamber arranged itself in its familiar geometry — curved rows of desks, the presiding officer's chair, the galleries on three sides holding the republic's citizenry in a posture somewhere between tribunal and theatre. The air smelled of wet wool and tallow and the sourness of a room sealed against the January cold too long. Someone had left a window insufficiently latched; it ticked against its frame in the draught.

Clay rose at eleven.

The room did not fall silent — it contracted. Conversations concluded themselves in lowered voices, heads turned, and the gallery's murmur subsided into attention. Henry Clay of Kentucky did not require silence. He required only that a room understand he was prepared to fill it.

Taney sat forward in his bench, elbows on his knees, and fixed his gaze on the chamber below. He had come to the gallery as a witness, which was the only posture available to him — but a witness was not a supplicant, and he intended Clay to feel the distinction, even from thirty feet above.

'The Senate is asked,' Clay began, his voice carrying to the gallery's highest row without apparent effort, 'to confirm to the highest judicial office in this republic a man whose principal distinction is his willingness to execute the orders of the executive against the expressed will of this body and against the settled determinations of the very Court he is now proposed to lead.'

A murmur in the galleries. Clay let it run its course.

'It must be observed — and I speak not to impugn the private character of the nominee, which has never been my purpose — that Mr. Taney is a man of considerable legal attainment, which renders what he has done and written the more alarming rather than the less.'

Taney's right hand, resting on his knee, slowly closed into a fist. He felt the pressure of his own knuckles. Then, finger by finger, he opened it and laid it flat — not because the anger had passed, but because he would not give Clay the satisfaction of a clenched hand in the gallery, visible to anyone who cared to look up.

Clay had the paper now. He held it as an advocate holds a document he has studied many times and no longer requires.

'The President,' he read, his voice shifting into the measured cadence of quotation, 'is independent of both the Senate and the House as to his interpretations of the Constitution. The authority of the Supreme Court must not, therefore, be permitted to control the Congress or the Executive, when acting in their legislative capacities.' He paused. 'These words were written by the nominee. And this Senate is now asked to elevate their author to sit in judgment upon the very instrument he has claimed the right to construe without reference to the Court's authority.'

A cough seized Taney — the damp air had been working at him since morning — and he pressed his handkerchief to his mouth, his shoulders drawing inward with the effort of suppression, fighting it down through four, five seconds until it subsided. The man two seats to his left shifted away with the small, instinctive courtesy of a person who has decided not to notice. Taney straightened, folded the handkerchief, and returned his attention to the floor below. He would not be seen to flinch, and he would not be seen to be ill.

Clay had not paused.

'The principle, gentlemen, if principle it may be called, cuts in every direction. If the President need not defer to this Court, why should this Court defer to the Congress? Grant Mr. Taney his doctrine, and you have granted every branch the right to construe the Constitution according to its own convenience, its own appetite, its own political interest of the moment. You have not made three co-equal branches. You have made three sovereigns.'

The murmur that followed was different. Lower. Distributed differently across the chamber. Taney tracked it by ear — it was moving through the Democratic benches, not only the Whig, and that was the thing that mattered.

He kept his gaze moving across the rows below, reading the room the way a lawyer reads a jury — not for what men said but for what they did with their hands and their eyes when they believed themselves unobserved. He was aware, at the periphery of his attention, of Thomas Hart Benton on the Democratic side — a broad, still figure — and aware that Benton had not turned to look up at him.

Clay's voice rose for his peroration. 'The Senate rejected this man once. It did so upon grounds that have not diminished in the interval but multiplied. I ask only that this body remember what it knew eighteen months ago, before the composition of its membership was altered to suit the convenience of those who found that knowledge inconvenient.'

He sat.

The gallery exhaled.

Taney remained where he was, palms open on his thighs. Below, in the moment Clay read the passage aloud, he had seen two Democratic senators lean fractionally toward one another — not away, toward — and in the row behind them, a third senator had set down his pen and folded his hands on the desk with the stillness of a man who has decided to wait before committing himself to any posture.

That was Bedford Brown.

The murmur after *three sovereigns* had not been the murmur of men persuaded; it had been the murmur of men not yet certain they were not. Brown's folded hands were not a refusal. They were an invitation, and Webster's people would be at his lodgings before the afternoon session concluded. If Brown moved before Thursday, the others would have their excuse, and the vote would fail, and Taney would have come to Washington ill and returned to Frederick defeated, precisely as Anne had warned.

He rose from the gallery bench, excused himself past the packed shoulders on either side, and descended the stair. He had seen what he needed to see. Now he had work to do.

---

The anteroom held no fire. Someone had meant to lay one — the grate was set with kindling, a birch log balanced across the andirons — but the tinder had not been struck, and the cold had settled as cold does in small rooms, not as moving air but as a property of the walls themselves, radiating inward from the stone. Taney kept his coat on and stood at the table and waited.

Benton arrived without knocking. He was a large man — broad through the chest, with the unhurried movements of someone accustomed to occupying space without apology — and he set his hat on the window ledge and did not sit.

'Brown,' Taney said, before Benton could speak.

Benton looked at him. 'You saw that from the gallery.'

'I saw it. What do you know of Webster's approach to him?'

'A man named Forsythe — not the senator, a functionary — sat with Brown for better than an hour on F Street this afternoon.' Benton's voice was a low, unhurried instrument, tuned by decades for carrying information across distance without heat. 'Brown owes the General a debt from thirty-two. Nullification. Jackson intervened personally on a matter touching his district, saved him an embarrassment before his legislature. Brown has not forgotten it. But Webster's people are telling him that the time for old debts has passed — that a man of his standing need not bind himself to a nomination the Senate rejected eighteen months past.' Benton said *standing* with the flat inflection of someone who finds dignity, as a political currency, somewhat overvalued. 'They are telling him that voting against you costs him nothing and voting for you costs him the friendship of every Whig committee chairman he will need for the next six years.'

'And the others?'

'Softer. Brown is the one who matters. If he goes, the rest have their excuse.'

Taney set both hands flat on the table. 'Then we do not permit Brown to go.' He held Benton's gaze. 'Can you reach Donelson without going through the physicians?'

Benton's expression did not alter, but a stillness came into it — the stillness of a man doing arithmetic he would rather not show. 'I can reach Donelson.'

'Then do so tonight. I require a letter from the President to Senator Brown. Personal, not official. One paragraph. Jackson need only sign it — Donelson can draft the language if the General is not equal to the composition. Brown must have it before he breakfasts tomorrow, before Webster's man reaches him again.'

Benton looked at him steadily. 'You understand what you are asking. If the General's condition is worse than reported and this letter becomes known — the timing, the circumstances of its composition — Clay will use it on the floor. He will say the executive is reaching from a sickbed to purchase a confirmation vote.'

'Clay will say whatever serves him whether we act or not.' Taney did not move his hands from the table. 'Proceed tonight.'

A silence in which the cold worked through both their coats. Benton took his hat from the window ledge and turned it once in his large hands.

'There is one thing more,' he said. 'Forsythe was seen this morning at the offices of the *National Intelligencer*. He carried a document case in, and he did not carry it out again.' Benton's gaze was level. 'If there is anything in the record of the deposit removal — any correspondence, any memorandum in your hand that Webster has not yet placed before the Senate — you should know of it before Thursday.'

The chill found the back of Taney's neck where his collar had loosened during the gallery session. He thought of every letter he had written in the autumn of 1833, every memorandum, every instruction to the sub-treasurers. He thought of what he had kept and what he had not.

'There is nothing,' he said, 'that has not already been read into the record.'

'Then we proceed as discussed.' Benton set his hat and went out.

Taney stood at the table after the footsteps had receded. The unlit grate. The walls holding their settled cold. He did not know whether Jackson could hold a pen, or whether Donelson would carry the letter, or whether what Forsythe had left at the *Intelligencer* was nothing, or whether *nothing* was the answer a man gave when he could not afford to discover otherwise. He had four days and one uncertain senator and a President who might not be able to sign his own name.

He put on his hat and went out after Benton.

## Scene 3

The clerk's voice had no quality to recommend it — a municipal sound, competent and unvaried, naming each senator in the order the roll demanded. From the gallery, Taney could see the crown of Clay's head, the silver hair dressed with its usual precision, and beneath it the rigid set of shoulders that had not shifted since the session opened. Clay did not look up. He had made his speech. What remained was arithmetic.

The air in the chamber was warmer than it had been in January — a suggestion of spring in the tallow-smell, in the slight ease of wool-clad men who had ceased to hunch against the cold. The gallery was full. Women in the upper tier made a row of pale ovals above the railing. Someone nearby smelled of pipe tobacco and perspiration held too long under heavy cloth. No one spoke.

*Aye.* Then again: *Aye.* The word accumulated with the regularity of a slow drop from a leaking roof.

Taney kept his gaze moving across the chamber rather than fixing it. He had counted the votes three times in the preceding days, and the count had not changed, but counting and hearing were different operations. He had no memorandum book today. He had only his hands, flat against his legs, and the clerk's voice.

*Nay.* Preston. *Nay.* Poindexter. Each refusal landed with its own small weight, and he counted them without moving his lips.

Brown's name was called.

The pause was not long. Three seconds, perhaps four — the time it takes a man to draw breath and commit to what he has always known he would do. Every throat in the gallery seemed to close at once.

*Aye.*

He did not permit himself anything. Not the easing of a muscle, not the release of air. He kept his hands where they were and waited for the remaining names to be discharged until the clerk set down his paper and the presiding officer spoke the tally aloud.

Twenty-nine to fifteen.

The gallery broke into sound — not quite applause, the chamber's dignity still restraining it — a general exhalation, a shifting of bodies. Benton, below on the Democratic side, turned in his chair and looked up toward the gallery. Taney inclined his head. Once.

Webster rose before the chamber had settled. He gathered his papers with the economy of a man who had been prepared to depart since before he arrived, and walked toward the aisle with his back straight and his face arranged into an expression that conceded nothing — not fury, not defeat, not even the formal acknowledgment that he had witnessed his own defeat. He did not look toward the gallery. He did not look toward Clay. He walked to the door, and the sound of it closing reached the gallery a half-second after he had passed through.

Clay remained in his seat. He understood the theatre of stillness — knew that rising too soon conceded more than the vote itself. But his hands lay open on the desk before him, the fingers slightly spread, as a man's hands lie when he has set down a weight he has been carrying for a long while and is not yet certain he is glad to be rid of it.

Taney looked at the presiding officer's desk, where the clerk was already entering the tally in the journal — in ink that would not wash out.

The Bank's federal charter had expired that same month. He had not thought of it until now.

He sat with that for a moment. Then he rose, and the women in the upper tier shifted their bonnets to follow him, and he descended the gallery stair into the corridor below, where a boy was waiting with his coat and hat and the intelligence that a carriage had been arranged. He put on the coat. He put on the hat. He did not ask who had arranged the carriage.

Outside, the March air was raw and smelled of the river. He stood on the Capitol steps before descending. Somewhere in the city, Clay was still in his seat. Somewhere, Webster was walking fast, the kind of walking a man does when he has business that will not wait and cannot be conducted in a chamber where he has just been defeated. And somewhere, in a room Taney had not been permitted to enter, Andrew Jackson lay in his bed at the President's House, and the physicians were not saying what they were thinking.

The carriage was at the foot of the steps. Taney descended and got in.

## Scene 4

The Supreme Court chamber occupied the Capitol's lower storey with the deliberate modesty of a room that had never expected to become what it was. Low vaulted ceilings. Columns of modest proportion. A single lamp, left burning on the clerk's table by some attendant who had expected the incoming Chief Justice might wish to see the place before morning made it official. The flame stood straight in the still air. The cold came as a settled thing, not moving, simply present, as cold is present in stone that has not been warmed since autumn. The smell of the place reached him at the threshold — old paper, old wood, the exhalation of a room where argument had been conducted for decades and the arguments had accumulated in the walls like sediment.

Taney entered alone.

He stood for a moment near the entrance and let his eyes adjust. The lamp illuminated the table, the bench, the arc of the associate justices' seats, and left the gallery and columns in a darkness not quite absolute.

The bench was longer than he had imagined. He had argued before it in other years, other capacities — had stood at the bar and constructed his periods for men who sat where he would now sit. The geometry of the room reversed itself in his mind, slowly, the way a figure in a mirror is recognised as oneself only after a brief, disconcerting interval.

He moved toward the centre of the bench.

John Marshall's chair had been empty since July — eight months in which the Court had conducted its necessary business from the associate positions, the centre seat conspicuous in its vacancy. He put his hand on the back of it. The wood was cold against his palm, cold enough that he felt the grain through the skin, the slight ridges left by decades of handling, the places where Marshall's hands had worn the finish away until the oak beneath showed through. He ran his fingers along the arm, then withdrew his hand.

He sat.

The chair was slightly lower than he had expected — lower, certainly, than the Treasury Secretary's chair. He was momentarily displeased by this.

The displeasure passed, and he was left with the room.

Very quiet. The marble held the cold the way marble always did, pressing upward through the chair's frame. He breathed and watched the lamp flame, which had not moved.

He thought, as he had not permitted himself to think in the weeks since Jackson's letter arrived in Frederick, of the doctrine.

*The principle, properly understood* — and here his mind moved as it always moved, sequential, building — *does not confine itself to the executive. It is not a fence erected around one branch's pasture. It is a proposition about each branch's sovereign relation to the instrument itself.*

He had written it for a particular purpose. The President, he had argued, was not bound by the Court's constitutional determinations; each branch interpreted the instrument as its own oath required. He had written it against the Bank, against Marshall's *McCulloch*, against the presumption that a single court's reading could foreclose all other readings permanently.

He had written it, and it had served, and the Bank was dead.

But the chair was here. And the doctrine, seated in this chair, produced a different proposition than the one he had intended.

*If the President may disregard the Court's construction of what Congress may enact, then the Court may equally disregard the Congress's construction of what the Constitution permits.*

He sat with it. The lamp flame did not move. Stone pressed its cold upward through the chair's joints and into his bones.

The territories. The question of what Congress might legislate in the territories — who might inhabit them, under what conditions, with what legal standing — had not yet presented itself as a judicial question requiring resolution. But it would. The pressures already moving through the republic, managed through silence and the careful avoidance of precision, did not resolve themselves. They accumulated. And when they reached a court armed with the doctrine of coordinate review, applied not against executive overreach but against an act of Congress —

A draught from the basement corridor guttered the lamp, and Taney cupped his hand around the glass to keep it lit, hunching over the table, his breath fogging the air above the flame until it steadied and held.

He straightened.

The same intellectual structure he had built to restrain one branch from dominating another could be turned, by any sufficiently determined court, into a weapon of precisely that domination. He had fashioned it as a shield. It would serve equally as a sword, and the hand that wielded it would be his own — not today, not in any case yet filed or conceived, but in some future session when the silence the republic was maintaining on the question of the territories finally broke, as silences of that magnitude always broke, under the accumulated weight of what had been left unspoken.

He had written it carefully, and it was sound, and the Bank was dead.

He rose from the chair.

He stood looking at the bench — at the arc of the associate seats, at the columns receding into the dark, at the single lamp that was the only warm thing in the room. Somewhere above him, the night watch was making his rounds, and the fires in the committee rooms were burning low, and the journal of the Senate was drying in the clerk's office with the tally of twenty-nine to fifteen entered in its permanent column. And somewhere, cases were accumulating — in the dockets of the circuit courts, in the offices of attorneys who had not yet filed their writs, in the minds of men who had not yet conceived their grievances into the form of legal argument. The territories question was not before the Court. It was before the country, managed through silence, and the country's silence would not hold indefinitely.

He had built the instrument. He had not yet determined what it would be used to construct. He was not certain, standing in this cold chamber with the lamp guttering at his back, that the determination would be his to make.

He took up the lamp from the table. He looked once more at Marshall's chair at the bench's centre. Then he turned toward the door — and stopped.

On the table, beneath where the lamp had stood, lay a folded paper he had not noticed upon entering. The direction was written in a hand he knew: the same forward lean, the same tremor in the downstrokes — but the letters larger now, less controlled, formed against the resistance of a body that would not hold steady.

He broke the seal.

Inside, a single line:

*They will come for the doctrine next. Be ready.*

He read it twice. Then he turned the paper over. The reverse was blank.

He folded it and placed it inside his coat, over his breastbone.

He did not know who had carried this to the table — whether Jackson had written it before the vote or after, whether it was counsel or command or merely the last coherent thought of a man who felt his time running short and wished to spend it usefully. He did not know whether *they* meant Clay and Webster, who had already come and been turned back, or some future adversary who had not yet taken the field. He stood in the cold chamber with the lamp in his hand and the paper folded against his ribs, and the not-knowing settled over him the way cold settles — not as a sensation but as a condition, present in the walls, in the stone, in the chair that was now his to occupy.

He extinguished the lamp.

The dark was immediate and complete. He felt for the wall with his free hand, found it — cold plaster, faintly damp — and moved by memory toward the door. His footsteps were the only sound in the chamber. Behind him, in the dark, Marshall's chair stood empty at the bench's centre, waiting, as it had waited for eight months, for a body to fill it and a hand to take up what Marshall had set down.

He found the door. He opened it. The corridor light fell across the threshold, and he stepped through.

He stopped.

At the far end of the corridor, just rounding the corner toward the Capitol's east stair, a figure moved — a man in a dark coat, carrying a document case, walking with the purposeful economy of someone who had concluded his business and wished not to be observed concluding it. The figure did not look back. In three seconds it was gone, and the corridor was empty, and the only sound was the distant percussion of the night watch's boots on the floor above.

Taney stood in the doorway.

The document case. Benton had told him two days past that Forsythe had carried one into the *National Intelligencer* and had not carried it out again. He had carried it out tonight — from somewhere in this building, at this hour, the night before Taney was to be sworn.

He took three steps into the corridor. The corner was twenty yards distant. He stood and looked at it and did not move further, because whatever had been in that case had already left the building, and because the morning would require him to be Chief Justice of the United States.

Whatever Forsythe had carried out, Webster now had it. And Webster, who had walked from the Senate chamber that afternoon without looking at Clay, without looking at the gallery, without conceding by so much as a glance that he had witnessed his own defeat — Webster had not been walking away. He had been walking toward something. Toward whatever was in that case, and toward whatever use he intended to make of it, and toward whatever morning he had chosen to make his move.

The corridor was cold and silent. Somewhere above, the night watch moved on.

Taney set his jaw and went toward the east stair, and the Capitol, and the morning.
