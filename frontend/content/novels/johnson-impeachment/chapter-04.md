# Chapter 4: The Instrument of the Dead

## Scene 1

The Meridian dispatch lay at the centre of the desk, separated from the surrounding correspondence by no more than two inches on each side, yet as distinct from those other papers as a brand mark from the hide around it.

Stanton read it for the third time.

Captain Aldous Fitch wrote plainly — the plainness of a soldier who had learned that ornament delays action, and that in the Bureau's district stations, delay cost lives. His account occupied three pages in a careful regimental hand, and its subject was the fourteenth of September, 1865, at a settlement of freedpeople on the Hargrove tract outside Meridian, Mississippi. Twelve armed men had arrived before dawn. By the account of survivors — those who had not fled into the cane breaks, those who had not been taken down in the yard — the men were known to several residents as former overseers, and at least two bore militia insignia whose authority derived from the provisional governor. Fourteen men had been flogged. Three were dead. Some forty souls, including eleven children, had been driven from the dwellings they had occupied since the Bureau's establishment of the settlement in the spring.

Fitch's final paragraph requested guidance. He had addressed his request to the War Department on the eighteenth. He had heard nothing.

Below the final paragraph, in the lower right corner of the first page, was a notation in a hand Stanton had seen before — the cramped, backward-slanting script of a secretary in the executive office. *Received — Exec. Office — 22 Sept. Filed per standing instruction.*

He pushed the pages aside and pressed both palms flat upon the desk. The wood was smooth and faintly warm from the iron stove behind him. Fourteen men flogged. Three dead. He had processed those words as evidentiary fact, as the raw material of a chain of proof. Now, in the stillness, he permitted them to arrive as they were: the sound of it, the yard before dawn, the children in the cane breaks. He sat with it for a moment — not long, he could not afford long — and then he took it back. He could afford only what was provable.

*Standing instruction.* He had been present in August when Johnson issued it. Bureau field reports describing property disputes — the phrase the President had used, with the precision of someone selecting vocabulary to perform a conclusion — were to be routed through the relevant provisional governor before any federal response was contemplated. The order had been framed as administrative efficiency. Every man at that table had understood what it was.

He went to the correspondence logs in his left drawer and confirmed what he had already suspected: the notation was consistent in date and hand with three other executive filings he had catalogued since August. Johnson's office had received Fitch's dispatch on the twenty-second and had done with it what the standing instruction required. Which was to say: nothing.

The difference was provable. That was the only thing that would hold.

He drew the foolscap paper from his right drawer — thicker than his correspondence stock, rougher at the edge, distinguishable by touch alone — and arranged it to the left of the dispatch. He took up his pen and began to copy.

He had completed half the first page when the coughing took him. It arrived without warning: a seizing at the top of his chest, and then the full contraction, his body folding forward over the desk, his right hand gripping the edge while his left pressed against his sternum. The pen rested against the page, the nib touching the foolscap, and ink spread in a slow dark pool where the letter *f* had been interrupted. He gripped the desk and waited while his lungs negotiated with themselves, and when the last of it had worked through him he straightened, breathed with care, and drew a fresh sheet from the drawer.

He began again.

He was on the second page when the door opened without a knock — Cadwallader's particular entry, not rude but compressed, carrying the authority of news that would not wait for courtesy. Stanton did not look up.

'Mr. Secretary.'

'Leave it on the corner.'

'It is from the executive office, sir. Rathbone's hand.' A pause. 'The cabinet meeting has been advanced.'

Stanton looked up. Cadwallader stood in the doorway — a young man whose face had been schooled into that particular blankness which makes bad news bearable to the bearer.

'Advanced to what hour.'

'Nine o'clock, sir. Morning of the twenty-seventh. The North Carolina matter, Rathbone writes.'

Stanton received the note, read it, placed it face-down on the corner of the desk. He had arranged to meet Stevens at half past nine. At nine o'clock, that arithmetic failed entirely. And Stevens, who had been waiting three days for this meeting, would not wait again.

'Tell Rathbone I will attend,' he said. 'I am informed of the hour.'

'Yes, sir.'

'And wait.'

Cadwallader waited. Stanton took a sheet of correspondence paper — not the foolscap, which he turned face-down beneath his blotter — and wrote eight lines in his own hand. He folded and sealed it himself, without wax, the fold tight enough to require deliberate effort to open.

'This goes tonight. Not by the Department's ordinary post.' He extended the note. 'You will carry it yourself to the address on the cover and place it in no hand but his. If he is not in, you will wait.'

Cadwallader took the note without looking at the address.

'Yes, sir.'

'One further matter.' Stanton kept his voice level. 'If anyone from the executive office inquires after my movements this evening — and I have reason to believe someone may — you were engaged with departmental correspondence. You did not see me leave.'

Cadwallader's expression did not shift. 'I have been engaged with departmental correspondence all evening, sir.'

When the door had closed, Stanton drew the Meridian dispatch toward him and then, with two fingers, left the stack at a slight angle, as though it had accumulated in the ordinary drift of an unread pile. He paused. Then he retrieved the foolscap from beneath the blotter and began again at the top of the second page. The stove behind him gave its slow, settled warmth against his coat. He had perhaps four hours before he must be in his place at the cabinet table, and Fitch's letter was three pages long, and Stevens was waiting, and somewhere in Mississippi a man named Fitch was still waiting for an answer that the standing instruction had ensured would never come.

He wrote on. Then stopped.

He set the pen down and opened the lower left drawer — not the correspondence drawer, the other one, kept locked, whose key he carried on his person — and withdrew a single folded sheet received that morning through a channel he would not record. He unfolded it and read it again, though he had already committed its contents to memory. It was unsigned, in an unfamiliar hand, and its substance was this: that Rathbone had been observed in private conference with General Lorenzo Thomas on the afternoon of the twenty-fourth, and that Thomas had been heard to remark, in the hearing of a departmental clerk whose name the author declined to supply, that the present arrangement at the War Department could not be expected to persist beyond the new year.

*The present arrangement.* The phrase carried the careful vagueness of someone instructed to say nothing directly who had obeyed the letter of that instruction.

Stanton folded the note, returned it to the drawer, and locked it. He picked up his pen.

If Thomas was already being sounded — Thomas, vain enough to be flattered by the approach and pliant enough to be managed afterward — then the cabinet meeting on the twenty-seventh was not merely a scheduling inconvenience. It was a survey. Johnson would sit at the head of that table and take his measure, and every word Stanton offered on the North Carolina matter would be weighed not for its administrative merit but for what it revealed of his allegiances. And if the measure came up short, the letter dismissing him would not wait for the new year.

He had perhaps three days to ensure it did not come up short. Three days, and a meeting with Stevens he could not now attend at the appointed hour, and a copy of Fitch's dispatch that had to be in the Senator's hands before the session opened.

He wrote on.

## Scene II

Sumner arrived late.

The three candles on the committee table had burned perhaps a quarter of their length by the time his step sounded in the corridor, and Stanton had spent those minutes in a silence broken only by Stevens's breathing and the small, occasional complaint of the old man's chair. Stevens had not remarked on the rescheduled hour. He had simply been there when Stanton arrived — coat still buttoned, his crabbed hands resting on the table — as though he had never expected any other arrangement. Stanton had noted the readiness and said nothing.

The room was cold. Not the cold of neglect but the cold of a space kept shut by design, its single window sealed against the October draught, the air inside carrying the residue of old tobacco and the particular mustiness of varnished wood through many seasons — a smell like the inside of a sealed argument. No fire had been laid. Stanton could feel the chill through his coat, and he did not remove it.

When Sumner entered, he did not apologize. He removed his hat, set it on the sideboard, and took his chair with the deliberate composure of a man arranging himself for an occasion he had already rehearsed. He did not look at Stanton. He looked at Stevens.

One of the candles threw a guttering light across the table's surface and steadied again, and in that moment of steadiness the old man spoke.

'You should resign, Mr. Secretary.' His voice was low, almost conversational — the voice of someone who has made this argument before and has learned to make it without raising it. 'You should resign, and you should say, publicly and in terms that admit no equivocation, that the President of the United States has made himself the instrument of the re-enslavement of four million people.'

Stanton did not answer at once. He held Fitch's dispatch in both hands, its edges pressed between his thumbs and forefingers, and he did not extend it across the table.

'The moral weight of such a declaration,' Stevens continued, 'would be felt in every Republican district before Congress convenes. A man of your standing, resigning in protest — it would not be a resignation. It would be testimony.'

'Testimony,' Stanton said, 'given once, and then done.'

'It would galvanize—'

'It would galvanize, Senator, for perhaps a fortnight.' He set the pages on the table before him but kept his hands upon them. 'After which I am a private citizen with opinions and no office. What Johnson's executive office received on the twenty-second of September, filed without action per a standing directive I myself heard him establish in August — that is not opinion. That is a record. A private citizen cannot obtain records of that character. A Secretary of War can.'

Stevens regarded him. The candlelight did nothing for that face — the deep lines of it, the hollows beneath the cheekbones, the compressed mouth of a man who has argued the same cause for thirty years and long since ceased to expect gratitude for the arguing.

'We have heard,' Stevens said, 'from two sources that the President is considering your replacement. A loyalist in the department. Someone who will not trouble him with inconvenient dispatches.'

Sumner, who had not yet spoken, shifted in his chair.

'I have heard the same,' Stanton said.

'Then you understand,' Stevens said, 'that your strategy of remaining inside the cabinet may not be yours to pursue indefinitely.'

Stanton placed Fitch's dispatch flat on the table and slid it across. 'Fourteen men flogged. Three dead. The governor's office received an identical account on the eighteenth of September and took no action. Johnson's office received it four days later and filed it. The notation reads: *Filed per standing instruction.* That instruction was Johnson's, given in my presence, directing that Bureau field reports of property disputes be routed through the relevant provisional governor before any federal response is contemplated. The governor is the same man who received the first report and did nothing.' He held Stevens's gaze across the table. 'A private citizen cannot demonstrate that chain. I can. You cannot use what I have gathered if I am removed before the session opens.'

Stevens took the pages. He read them in the thin, unsteady light, his eyes moving without haste. Sumner leaned to see, and Stevens tilted the sheets slightly toward him without looking up.

The silence stretched. The tallow smell thickened as one of the candles began to gutter in earnest.

Then Sumner spoke. His voice, which in the Senate chamber could fill a gallery, came out low and precisely aimed. 'Mr. Secretary. The minister who led the delegation to the President — Reverend Greer, from the Sea Islands — I am informed he has been beaten. His church has been burned.'

Stanton looked at him.

'He stood in that office and asked for protection of his people's land claims,' Sumner continued. 'The President told him to trust his former masters. And now he has been beaten and his church burned.' He set his hands flat on the table. 'I put it to you plainly, sir: can you look at what this costs and tell me that remaining in that cabinet — attending those meetings, lending your name and your office to the administration's proceedings — is worth the price that others pay for your access?'

A beat of silence. Then:

'No.' Stanton's voice did not change register. 'I cannot make it worth the price. I cannot make three dead men and a burned church worth any price.' He paused. 'What I can tell you is what the record shows. And what the record shows is that without someone inside that department, you will have accusations and no evidence. You will have the Bureau agents' dispatches and no proof that they were received and suppressed by design rather than neglect. Neglect is a political embarrassment. Design is an impeachable fact.' He held Sumner's gaze. 'I am not arguing that my continued service is without cost. I am arguing that the alternative costs more.'

Sumner looked at Stevens. The look was brief.

Stevens set the pages down and reached into the breast of his coat, producing a folded sheaf, the edges worn at the crease, the script upon them small and angular, pressed hard into the page as though each letter had been formed with a separate decision. He placed it on the table and did not push it toward Stanton.

'I have consulted three colleagues in the Senate,' Stevens said, 'regarding a constitutional principle. The principle is this: whether the Congress may require its own consent for the removal, by the President, of an officer confirmed by the Senate. Whether the power of appointment, shared between the executive and the legislative branches, necessarily implies a shared power of removal.'

Stanton looked at the papers. He did not reach for them.

'No person holding any civil office,' Stevens said, as though reading from memory, 'to which he has been appointed by and with the advice and consent of the Senate, shall be removed from such office by the President without the concurrence of the Senate.'

The candle that had been guttering went out. The room contracted by a fraction.

'You have already discussed this with three Senate colleagues,' Stanton said.

'I have.'

'And you came to this meeting to propose my resignation.'

'I came,' Stevens said, 'to determine whether you would refuse it.'

The room held that.

Stanton looked at Sumner. Sumner's expression did not change — and that stillness was its own answer about how long he had known.

He looked back at Stevens. 'You drafted this bill before tonight.'

'Yes.'

'Then you did not need my answer about resignation. You needed to know whether I understood my situation.'

Stevens said nothing. His hands, resting on the table, were entirely still.

Stanton reached across and took the sheaf himself, without waiting to be offered it. He could feel the indentations where the nib had worked, each letter pressed deep as a conviction. He read the opening clause, then the second, then set it down.

He understood now what the meeting had been. Not a consultation. Not even a negotiation. A fitting — the kind a tailor conducts when the coat has already been cut and the man inside it has not yet been measured for it. His continued tenure in the cabinet was no longer a choice he was making. It was a position being constructed around him by other men, for other purposes, and the door of it had been closing on the outside since before he arrived.

'You will want the dispatch returned,' he said, 'or a copy made for the committee's use.'

'The original will serve.' Stevens drew a breath — the slow, deliberate inhalation of a man who has learned to conserve what the body will not replenish. 'The bill will be introduced in the first week of the session. The President will be informed of it, as he must be. He will have perhaps a fortnight to act before the vote is called.' His eyes had not left Stanton. 'If he moves to remove you in that fortnight — before the bill passes — there is nothing we can do to prevent it. The law does not yet exist. You would be a private citizen with a grievance and a draft bill that died in committee.'

The tallow smell was sharp now, the extinguished candle trailing its thin thread of smoke toward the ceiling.

'Then I had best ensure,' Stanton said, 'that he has no cause to move before the vote is called.'

He rose, tucked the bill beneath his arm, and buttoned his coat. He was at the door when Stevens spoke again — the old man's voice carrying in the dark room with the economy of someone who has learned that volume is a young man's instrument.

'One matter more, Mr. Secretary. The cabinet meeting on the twenty-seventh.' A pause. 'We have been informed that General Thomas has been invited to attend as an observer. At the President's personal request.'

Stanton's hand rested on the door frame. He did not turn.

'I was not informed of that,' he said.

'No,' Stevens said. 'I did not suppose you were.'

## Scene 3

The committee room door closed behind him and the corridor received him in darkness.

He stood a moment. The passage ran perhaps forty feet before it bent toward the main stair, and the only light came from a single lamp at the turning, its flame shrunk to a blue filament against the draught that moved through the Capitol's lower precincts at this hour, finding every seam in the stonework, carrying the cold smell of lime and old mortar.

Thomas. At the cabinet table. As an observer.

He took out Stevens's draft bill. In the corridor's near-darkness he could not read it, but he had read it twice already, in the final minutes before Stevens and Sumner gathered their coats and went. He folded it now into precise quarters and placed it inside his waistcoat, between the wool and his linen. The paper was cool against his sternum.

He walked toward the lamp.

The watchman was at the foot of the main stair, a large man with a lantern and the unhurried posture of someone who had nowhere better to be. He straightened when Stanton appeared at the turning.

'Mr. Secretary.' A note of genuine surprise, quickly controlled. 'Good evening, sir. I did not know we had visitors in the building.'

'Good evening.' Stanton descended the final three steps without altering his pace. 'The ventilation in the large committee chamber is inadequate for a full session. I wished to satisfy myself as to the arrangements before the Joint Committee convenes.'

'At this hour, sir?'

'My days are occupied otherwise.' He reached the bottom and turned toward the east entrance. 'Good evening.'

The watchman did not fall back. 'Shall I light you out, Mr. Secretary? It is no trouble.'

'It is no trouble for me, either.' He kept walking, the dispatch case in his left hand, the latch cold through his glove. 'The night air will serve.'

He heard the man shift behind him — the scrape of a boot on stone, the small calculation of a subordinate weighing whether to press further without impertinence. Stanton did not slow. After a moment, the scraping stopped.

The east door was heavy, iron-banded oak, and it resisted him briefly before the latch gave. The night came in: damp earth and horse from the avenue below, and beneath that the faint mineral trace of the Potomac, two miles south, carrying its cargo of autumn cold inland. He descended the Capitol steps without looking back.

The carriage was where he had left it, the driver on the box with his collar raised. Stanton climbed in without speaking and pulled the door shut.

Washington at this hour was largely silent — a dog somewhere, the distant rhythm of a sentry's boots on the Treasury portico, the wheels on the macadam. He could feel the city's vacancy as a physical thing, the absence of its usual press and noise.

He took out the draft bill again. As they passed beneath the first street lamp, he read the opening clause: *No person holding any civil office to which he has been appointed by and with the advice and consent of the Senate shall be removed from such office by the President without the concurrence of the Senate.* The lamp moved past. Darkness again.

He had not chosen to remain in the cabinet. He had been retained there. And the law that was meant to protect him did not yet exist — would not exist for a fortnight, if it passed at all — and in that fortnight Johnson would sit across a cabinet table from him and look at him with the flat, assessing gaze of a man who suspected and could not yet prove, and Stanton would return it with the measured patience of a loyal officer, and every day that passed without the bill's passage was a day on which the President could act with perfect legality and leave him with nothing.

And now Thomas would be at that table. Watching. Taking his measure in the way that men take the measure of rooms they intend to occupy.

The carriage struck a rut in the macadam and a violent shiver seized him, his whole frame contracting, his teeth finding one another. He gripped the seat with his right hand and waited it out. His lungs, when the shiver passed, did not immediately cooperate. He breathed through it, slowly, until the tightness relented and his chest resumed its ordinary, imperfect labor. The draft bill crackled against his ribs.

He put it back inside his waistcoat.

The War Department came into view, dark above and lit below, the night clerks visible through the ground-floor windows. He entered by the back stair, spoke briefly to the duty officer — nothing, no dispatches, a quiet night — and climbed to his office, which the stove had kept at a temperature just short of bearable. The iron radiated its dry warmth at him as he hung his coat and settled into his chair.

The lower desk drawer held what he had placed there before leaving: Johnson's letter, dated the fourteenth of September, addressed to the provisional governor of Mississippi. He had obtained it through a channel he did not intend to record, from a man who had his own reasons for the transaction. He set it to his left, smoothed it flat, and drew a clean sheet of foolscap from the right drawer.

He dipped his pen and began.

The letter was three pages in Johnson's secretary's hand, and Stanton copied it in his own — the script close and compressed, each word set down with the deliberate economy of a man who had spent twenty years in documents and knew the cost of a misplaced syllable. He did not permit himself to pause on the content. He had read it already; he knew what it contained. *A political enterprise designed not for the benefit of the freedman but for the perpetual subjugation of the Southern states to Northern faction.* He wrote it as he had written field orders during the war: accurately, without ceremony, because accuracy was the only thing that would hold.

In four hours, he would sit across a cabinet table from the man who had dictated these words. He would attend to the business of North Carolina's provisional government. He would offer his considered views when asked, and nothing when not asked, and Johnson would look at him with that flat, assessing gaze — and beside him, or near him, or somewhere in that room where Stanton had not been invited to expect him, General Lorenzo Thomas would be present as an observer. Which was to say as a measurement. Which was to say as a verdict already forming.

He marked his place and continued copying.

Outside, the city was beginning to grey. Somewhere in the executive office a man named Rathbone had already, in all probability, drafted the letter that would end his tenure — and Thomas had been told, or would be told, or was even now being told, what posture to assume when he entered that room and looked at the chair that was not yet his.

The copy had to be finished before dawn. It had to be in the hands of a man whose name he would not write down, who would carry it to a place he would not record, before he entered that cabinet room and sat across from the man who had already decided what he was.

He wrote on. The stove gave its dry warmth at his back. The grey at the window deepened, and the morning came toward him — not like a sentence waiting to be delivered, not like anything so composed. It came like the next thing that would happen, which was the only way mornings came to men who had run out of choices and had not yet run out of time.
