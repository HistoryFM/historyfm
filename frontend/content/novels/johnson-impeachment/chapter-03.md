# Chapter 3: The Pardon Broker

The petitions had arrived before dawn.

Browning had stacked them in four towers on the corner of the desk — planter families from Georgia, merchant houses from Virginia, a former brigadier from the Carolina piedmont whose name Johnson recognized from a courthouse skirmish in 1842. Each petition bore the same general architecture: the preamble of repentance, the middle passage of constitutional argument, and the final paragraph where pride reasserted itself in the form of assurances that the petitioner had always, at bottom, been a Union man at heart. Johnson had read three of them through before the sun cleared the rooftops on Pennsylvania Avenue. He did not read the fourth to its conclusion.

He picked it up — a thick sheaf from a Mississippi planter named Beaumont, the paper of good quality, the hand of a paid amanuensis — and weighed it a moment, gauging the density of the thing, before setting it on the approved stack without turning to the final page.

"The Fenwick petition," he said. "What is the acreage?"

Browning consulted his ledger. "The family claims some four thousand acres in Beaufort County, sir. The petition was received on the twenty-sixth."

"Four thousand acres." Johnson turned in his chair and regarded the map. The Southern states spread across it in pale ink, unlined, the military districts not yet drawn — the country as it had been, or as it would be again, which in Johnson's view amounted to the same proposition. "Write the standard notation. Approved pending loyalty oath. Have it ready for signature before the first audience."

"Yes, sir." Browning's pen moved. Then he paused.

Johnson noticed the pause the way a man notices a loose board underfoot — not alarmed, merely attentive. The morning heat pressed against the drawn curtains. Somewhere below the window, a cart horse stamped twice on the cobbles and was still.

"There is also the Bureau communication, Mr. President. Filed yesterday from the district superintendent in Beaufort. I thought you would wish to —"

"Read it."

Browning unfolded the document with the deliberateness of one who has rehearsed the gesture and still finds it insufficient. He cleared his throat.

"*The superintendent reports that in the week of the twentieth instant, no fewer than eleven freedpeople, including one Hester Ann Coles and her four minor children, were compelled to vacate the cabin and kitchen garden they have occupied since January last under the authority of General Sherman's Special Field Order Number Fifteen. The compulsion was effected by the return of the land's former owner, one Mr. Elias Croft, who presented his pardon documentation and demanded immediate possession. Mrs. Coles, who could not read the document, was removed at the point of a rifle and her children's possessions thrown into the yard. The superintendent requests guidance as to the disposition of —*"

The heat at Johnson's collar was a physical fact, linen against damp skin. He looked at the window, at the hard white May light pressing through the curtains, at the dust moving in the shaft of it. A fly circled the lamp chimney in slow, indifferent arcs.

"— *the Bureau's authority in such cases, and whether the freedpeople's claims under the Field Order retain legal standing subsequent to the restoration of title.*"

Browning folded the document and waited.

Johnson straightened his collar. "Property restored under the terms of a presidential pardon reverts to its legal owner. That is not a Bureau question. That is a settled matter of law."

"The superintendent is asking for written guidance, sir. To protect the Bureau's position, should the matter be taken before —"

"Write the following." His voice carried the flatness of a conclusion reached before the conversation began. "The Bureau superintendent, Beaufort District, is directed to file the attached report without further action. Questions pertaining to the disposition of property recovered under the Amnesty Proclamation of May twenty-ninth fall outside the Bureau's administrative purview and are governed by the terms of the pardon instrument itself. File it with the general correspondence."

Browning's pen did not move at once. The scratch, when it came, was audible in the close room.

"There is one further matter, sir." Browning kept his eyes on the ledger. "Senator Wade's office has requested — informally, through a clerk — a full accounting of the pardons issued in the preceding fortnight."

Johnson was still. "Senator Wade is welcome to submit his request through the proper channels of congressional inquiry, and to await its consideration in the proper order." He set the Beaumont petition squarely on the approved stack. "Have the Fenwick pardon on top."

---

The afternoon petitioners came in order of their waiting, and their waiting was itself a ceremony Johnson had composed with deliberation. The anteroom held perhaps a dozen men at any given hour — former officers, former legislators, former proprietors of former thousands of acres — and the sounds that reached the reception room were the sounds of consequence deferred: the creak of a chair shifted for the fourth time, the low murmur of men long accustomed to being heard who had not yet accepted that the order of speaking was no longer theirs to set.

The smell of them preceded each man through the door. Pomade and tobacco, the sweetness of a summer coat taken from cedar storage and not yet fully aired. One or two carried the faint iron trace of men long absent from their own houses. Johnson marked these things without appearing to mark them, his hands resting flat on the arms of his chair, his face composed in the expression of a magistrate before whom the proceedings have not yet formally opened.

It was a ceremony, and he was its officiant.

General Beaumont Cahill — the name was not famous, but the family was, and the family's acres in the Shenandoah valley were more than sufficient to place him among those exceptions to the Amnesty Proclamation who must petition in person — entered with the bearing of someone arriving at a club where he had held membership for thirty years. He was perhaps sixty, silver-haired, broad through the chest, his coat of a grey-blue wool so finely woven that it caught the light through the tall windows and held it. Johnson saw the cloth. He said nothing. He settled his own coat with the small, exact adjustment of one who has dressed himself since boyhood without assistance.

Cahill took the offered chair without waiting to be invited.

The room received this. Long rectangles of light lay across the Turkey carpet. From the anteroom came the muted sounds of men in suspension — the creak of a bench, a low cough, the rustle of papers consulted for the fourth time. The ritual weight of the occasion pressed down like summer heat through glass.

'Mr. President.' Cahill's voice was measured, warm even, the practiced instrument of a man who had addressed courts and companies and saw no essential difference between them and this room. 'I am grateful for the opportunity to present myself before you in this matter of constitutional reconciliation. The late unhappy conflict has, as I need not tell you, exacted its toll upon men of every station in these states, and it is in the spirit of the restoration you have so wisely proposed — the restoration of our sovereign commonwealths to their rightful place in the Union — that I come before you today. I believe we share, sir, a common understanding of the constitutional order, and it is in that spirit of shared understanding that I offer my submission to the authority of —'

'General.' Johnson did not raise his voice.

Cahill paused.

'You will stand, if you please.'

Three seconds. The curtain at the far window hung motionless in the dead afternoon air. Cahill rose from the chair with the deliberateness of someone choosing, at every increment of the movement, to interpret it as his own idea.

'Now.' Johnson let him stand. 'You have traveled some distance. I should like to understand precisely what it is you are here to request.'

'As I was saying, sir, the matter of constitutional —'

'I am familiar with the Constitution, General. I have read it rather more recently than most.' A pause. 'What is it you are requesting of this office?'

Cahill's jaw set. His eyes moved once to Browning standing near the window, then back. He was not accustomed to this quality of silence — not hostile, not theatrical, simply present, waiting with a patience that had no apparent bottom.

'I am requesting' — and the word cost him something visible only in the slight forward motion of his chin, as a man pressing into a wind — 'a pardon.'

The word sat in the room.

Johnson took the pen from its stand. He signed the document before him without reading the final lines of the petition, the nib moving in the quick, decisive strokes of finality. He turned the signed paper face-down on the desk.

Cahill reached for it.

Johnson's hand came down flat on the document. 'Before you take that' — his eyes did not move from Cahill's face — 'you will tell me the name of your Bureau contact in the Shenandoah district.'

Cahill's hand withdrew. The light lay across the desk between them, warm and indifferent.

'I am not certain I take your meaning, Mr. President.'

'I think you take it precisely.' Johnson lifted his hand and left the document lying there, face-down, between them. 'There have been reports from Bureau officers of certain arrangements on properties lately returned under the Amnesty. Arrangements that bear examination.'

'The arrangements on my property are those of an owner restored to what is lawfully his and setting it in order. I am not aware that the terms of the pardon require me to answer to Bureau officers for the conduct of my own household.'

'They do not.' Johnson picked up the document and held it a moment without extending it. 'The terms require only the oath. Which you will administer within thirty days.' He set the paper on the near edge of the desk. 'My secretary will instruct you in the particulars.'

He looked past Cahill toward the door.

The next man was already waiting.

---

The gas lamps had been lit an hour since, and the windows stood open to the June evening, admitting the sounds of Pennsylvania Avenue — a dray horse's slow progress over the cobbles, the distant call of a vendor closing his stall, the irregular percussion of a city settling into its routines. The air that came through the windows was warm and carried the smell of horse and dust and, beneath it, the faint sweetness of the linden trees along the avenue, their blossoms going over in the heat.

Johnson sat at the desk and broke the seal on the topmost Bureau report.

The letter was from a Captain Elias Morse, attached to the district office in Natchez, Mississippi, composed with the spare, declarative precision of an officer who has learned that his dispatches are read in Washington by persons disinclined to credit what they contain. The prose was plain. It described, without embellishment, the circumstances of one Ezekiel Price, a freedman who had worked the same cotton ground near Natchez for the whole of his life, first as property and then, since the previous January, as a man in the legal sense. Price had refused to affix his mark to a labor contract tendered by his former owner, a Mr. Cornelius Hatch, lately pardoned by the executive office. Three days after Hatch's return to his property, Price had been taken at night by four men, beaten in a manner the letter specified with clinical thoroughness, and left in a ditch a quarter-mile from the cabin he had occupied since February under Bureau authority. He had not died. He had signed the contract.

Johnson read this passage twice. He set the letter on the desk.

He had known men like Ezekiel Price — not known them, but been in proximity to them, in the counties of eastern Tennessee where a white man who owned nothing and a Black man owned by someone else occupied the same rung of the same ladder by different mechanisms of descent. His own father had died in the service of a planter who would not have received him socially. He understood, with the knowledge that lives in the body rather than the mind, what it meant to be compelled by someone who held the title to everything beneath your feet.

He understood it. He set that understanding beside the letter and left both of them there.

A moth came in through the open window and found the gas lamp. It circled the glass shade with a rapid, dry ticking — wings against glass, once, twice, three times. Johnson watched it. Then he reached across the desk and took up the letter from the provisional governor of Virginia — a man named Pierpont, whose reports arrived with the orderly confidence of one who has been given his instructions and understood them. Pierpont wrote of progress: the courts resuming their functions, commerce reviving, the freedpeople settling into the labor arrangements that the season required. He wrote of gratitude. He used the word twice.

Johnson set Pierpont's letter down. He aligned the two accumulations of correspondence — Bureau reports to the left, governors' letters to the right — pressing their edges square with the flat of his hand.

The Bureau officer's account was not false. He had not told himself it was false, and he would not permit himself that particular comfort. What it was, was incomplete. Property recovered under the terms of executive pardon carried with it the full authority of law. The labor arrangements that followed were a consequence of legal order, not its violation. That Ezekiel Price had been compelled by four men in the dark — Johnson did not finish the sentence. He read Pierpont's letter instead, and found in its measured paragraphs the version of the South that his Reconstruction required: a country returning to itself, its natural order reasserting after the interruption of war, its people — all of its people — finding their proper stations.

The moth struck the glass shade once more and then was gone.

He sat for a moment with his hands open on the desk, palms up, the lamplight crossing them.

Then he drew the Bureau stack back from the edge, opened it, and removed the Morse letter from the top. He turned it over and wrote across the back, in his own hand: *File. No further action.* He placed the Pierpont letter on top of it and pressed it flat.

Now Browning appeared in the doorway — he had a talent for appearing precisely when Johnson had resolved something, as though the resolution itself were a sound audible from the anteroom.

'The South Carolina delegation, sir.' He did not enter the room. 'They have confirmed for nine o'clock tomorrow morning.' A pause. 'I should mention — they were received yesterday afternoon by Senator Sumner. At some length, I am told. A correspondent from the *Tribune* was observed departing the Capitol building shortly afterward.'

Johnson looked at him.

'I thought you would wish to know the full circumstance,' Browning said.

'You thought correctly.' Johnson turned back to the desk. 'Close the door.'

Browning closed it.

The lamp burned. The avenue sounds continued below — the dray horse at its unhurried work, the last vendor's call fading toward silence. Johnson understood what Sumner had done: received the delegation with ceremony, listened at length, and dispatched a correspondent to ensure that whatever passed at the White House the following morning would be measured against whatever had been said at the Capitol the afternoon before. The delegation would arrive already knowing they had an audience beyond this room. Greer — Browning had given him the minister's name — would arrive knowing that his words, and Johnson's, would be set in type before the Thirty-ninth Congress convened at noon.

He could not refuse the audience. Refusal would be reported as fear.

He could not grant what they asked. Granting it would undo every signature he had affixed since May.

He had until nine o'clock to determine what a third course might look like, and whether it existed.

He reached for the next petition on the stack and read it through to the final line.

---

They had dressed with care.

Johnson saw this at once — the seven of them arranged before him in the reception room, their best clothes pressed and brushed, a Sunday gravity to their bearing that cost them something in the June heat. The minister stood at the center: a man of perhaps forty-five, with the upright carriage of someone long accustomed to a congregation's attention. He wore a black coat of decent wool, the collar damp at the edges from the walk up Pennsylvania Avenue in the morning's already considerable warmth. He had removed his hat. The others followed his example.

Johnson settled into the chair behind the desk. Browning stood near the door. Another aide occupied the corner with a pen and a folio.

'You are welcome,' Johnson said. 'I am informed your delegation has traveled from South Carolina on behalf of the freedpeople of the Sea Islands. I will hear you.'

The minister inclined his head. 'We are grateful for the audience, Mr. President.' His voice carried the measured cadence of a preacher who has spoken through grief and jubilation alike, who knows the distance a voice must travel and has long since mastered it. 'I am the Reverend Thomas Greer. We come on behalf of some forty thousand souls who have worked those islands and whose right to remain upon them was secured, as we understand the matter, by the authority of the United States government.'

Johnson set down the pen he had been turning between his fingers and looked at the minister directly. 'The matter of General Sherman's field order.'

'Yes, sir. Special Field Order Number Fifteen, issued in January of this year.' Greer's voice did not rise; it settled, the way a man sets down something heavy with deliberate care. 'Forty acres, the promise of a deed. Men and women who have worked that ground for generations now hold government certificates. They have planted. They have built. They have buried their dead in that soil. And they are now being told that the certificates are void — that the same executive authority which issued them has recalled them — while the pardons issued to the men who once held those people as property are honored without condition.'

Johnson folded his hands on the desk. 'The question of those certificates is one of property law, Reverend Greer, and it is receiving proper consideration.'

Greer did not sit. None of them sat — Johnson had not invited them to, and Greer had understood the choreography before he entered the room.

'Mr. President.' The address came with a fractional pause before it, as though Greer were selecting from among several possible openings and had chosen the plainest. 'I have read your Amnesty Proclamation of the twenty-ninth of May with considerable attention. It is a generous instrument. It restores property to men who bore arms against this government — it restores lands, it restores standing, it asks in return only the oath and the petition. We do not quarrel with mercy.' He paused a single beat. 'But the freedpeople of the Sea Islands ask only what your own proclamation establishes as the principle: that a man's lawful claim to land, secured by the authority of the United States, ought not to be voided by executive discretion. The planter's claim and ours rest on the same foundation — the government's word. We ask that the government honor both, or explain why it honors only one.'

The room was close. The heat pressed through the tall windows, and the smell of it — warmed wool, the faint mineral trace of the ink drying in the corner aide's folio — filled the air between them.

Johnson looked at Greer.

The argument was sound. He had known it was sound since the night before, since he had pushed that accumulation of Bureau letters to the edge of the lamplight and picked up the governor's reassuring pages instead. It was built from his own words, mortared with the government's own promises, and it stood before him now with Sumner's hand somewhere behind it and the *Tribune*'s correspondent somewhere behind that, and forty thousand people behind all of it.

'Reverend Greer,' Johnson said, his voice settling into the register he had used from the stump for twenty years — rolling and certain, the voice of a man who has carried rooms by the sheer ballast of his conviction — 'I understand your concern. But I would counsel you, and those you represent, to place your trust in the men who know your people best. Your former masters — those who have labored beside you, who know your capacities and your needs — they are your best friends in this matter. They will not see you want. The civil order of the late Confederate states is returning to its proper condition, and in that order there is provision for the colored man who works faithfully and keeps to his station.'

Silence.

Greer did not look at the men beside him. He looked at Johnson.

'Mr. President,' he said, 'we have had those friends for two hundred and fifty years.'

The quietness of it was complete.

'We know them rather better than you do.'

Johnson's face changed — a small alteration, the way a door settles in its frame when the wind shifts outside. His hands, which had been moving across the desk's edge in their habitual smoothing motion, went flat and still.

In the corner, the aide's pen had stopped moving.

'The audience is concluded,' Johnson said.

They filed out with the same dignity they had carried in. Greer last, his hat not yet replaced, his spine as straight at the door as it had been at the center of the room. When the door closed, the room held the shape of their presence for a moment — the faint warmth of seven bodies, the ghost of damp wool and the June heat they had walked through to reach this place.

Johnson stood behind the desk. He did not move.

He had conducted the audience correctly. He had been courteous, measured, presidential. He had said nothing that could be quoted to his disadvantage in the morning edition of the *Tribune* — nothing that Sumner could read aloud from the Senate floor as evidence of malice or contempt. He had offered the delegation the answer his Reconstruction required, and the answer was not wrong. The civil order of those states was returning to its proper condition. That was the design. That was the purpose of every name he had signed since May, every provisional governor he had commissioned, every Bureau report he had filed without action.

It was not wrong.

*We know them rather better than you do.*

He reached down and took up the topmost petition from the stack on the desk. The preamble of repentance, the middle passage of constitutional argument, the final paragraph where pride reasserted itself in the form of assurances

that the petitioner had always, at bottom, been a Union man at heart. He signed it. Set it aside.

The folio in the corner aide's hands contained a verbatim record of the exchange. Browning would file it. It would exist. And Sumner's correspondent, wherever he had stationed himself on Pennsylvania Avenue, had already noted the hour at which the delegation emerged — and whether they emerged with a promise or with the polite conclusion of an audien

ce that had changed nothing.

Johnson signed the second petition without reading it to the end.

He signed the third.

Then Browning knocked once and entered without waiting for an answer — a liberty he took only when the matter was urgent — and set a folded sheet on the corner of the desk. 'This arrived from the War Department within the hour, sir. Captain Morse's superior has forwarded a second communication. It concerns the same property in Natchez.' He withdrew.

Johnson did not reach for it at once. He finished squaring the signed petitions against the desk's edge, pressing them flat, aligning them with the care of a man who finds order in small exactitudes when larger ones are not available.

Then he unfolded the sheet.

Morse's superior had written in the clipped, reluctant prose of a man who understood that his report would be unwelcome and had resolved to make it brief. The freedman Ezekiel Price, having signed the labor contract under the circumstances previously described, had been found that morning in the field he was contracted to work — alive, the report was careful to note, though the qualifier carried its own weight — with a document pinned to his coat. The document was a copy of the presidential pardon issued to one Cornelius Hatch, annotated in an unknown hand with a single line: *The President has spoken for us.*

Johnson set the sheet down.

Outside, the last of the evening vendors called his wares into the darkening air, the sound carrying through the open window — ordinary, unhurried, the commerce of a city that did not yet know what had been transacted in this room, or what it would cost.

He did not file the Morse report. He did not write across the back of it, nor fold it into the correspondence folio, nor call Browning to record its receipt. He placed it beneath the signed petitions, face down, where it would require a deliberate hand to find it.

The lamp had burned to its last quarter-inch of oil when boots struck the outer corridor — not Browning's measured tread, but two sets, moving with the clipped urgency of men who had not come to wait. The door opened before the knock had finished sounding, and the aide who entered was not Browning at all but a young man Johnson did not recognise, in a coat still damp from the evening air, holding before him an envelope that bore, in the upper left corner, the printed designation of the Senate Committee on the Judiciary.

'From Senator Wade, Mr. President.' The young man did not lower the envelope. 'I am instructed to convey that the Committee requires your written acknowledgment of receipt, and that a second messenger stands at the Pennsylvania Avenue gate with instructions to proceed to the *Chronicle* should none be forthcoming before the clock strikes nine.'
