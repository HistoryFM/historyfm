# Chapter 4: The Landing

## Scene 1

The first dispatch arrived at half past nine in the morning, and Armstrong read it standing, which was his custom with intelligence he expected to be unreliable. Captain Thornton's cavalry scout — a corporal, in fact, the captain having apparently delegated even his reconnaissance to subordinates — reported the British fleet moving upriver on the Patuxent, the lead vessels observed at a bearing consistent with Benedict or some point north of it. Armstrong set the paper flat on his desk and read it again with the same attention he gave to documents he fully intended to discard.

The map lay spread across the desk, weighted at its corners by a brass inkwell, a spent cartridge he kept for no reason he had ever examined, and two volumes of Thucydides. Baltimore lay marked in his own hand, a circle drawn in red chalk, the roads to it traced in deliberate strokes from every practicable landing point on the upper bay. He put his finger on Benedict and drew it north along the Patuxent's eastern bank, then east toward Annapolis, then north again to Baltimore. The distance was navigable. A force landing at Benedict was a force positioned to threaten Baltimore from the south and rear, drawing American defenders away from the harbour approaches before the main body struck from the water.

He traced it again. Then he moved his finger west from Benedict, along the road toward the Wood Yard, and stopped.

Thirty-five miles. A column of seasoned infantry could cover it in two days — less, if they marched through the night. Winder had perhaps fifteen hundred men there, most of them militia who had never loaded a musket under fire, and they would not hold. They could not hold.

He lifted his finger from the map.

The walls of the War Department building had absorbed six weeks of direct sun and now radiated it inward with the patient malice of a kiln. Armstrong had long since ceased to acknowledge August in this city as anything other than an inconvenience of climate. He had sent for water at eight o'clock; the pitcher on the sideboard sat empty, its pewter surface filmed with condensation that had long since evaporated, leaving a pale ring on the wood.

A horsefly landed on the back of his writing hand. He slapped at it, dragging a long arc of ink across the corner of the dispatch. He regarded the smear, blotted it with the cuff of his sleeve, and returned to the map.

The door opened. He did not look up.

'Mr. Secretary.'

Mason's voice. John Mason had served the War Department through three secretaries and possessed a quality Armstrong had once valued: the man understood that his function was to make the machinery run and not to inquire too closely into what it produced. Armstrong had valued this. He was reconsidering it.

'I did not send for you, Mason.'

'No, sir.' Mason crossed the room with the dispatch already extended. 'This arrived from Commodore Barney's flotilla within the hour. I thought it required your immediate attention.'

Armstrong took the paper without altering his posture. Barney's hand — blunt, practical, the penmanship of a man who wrote at sea in rough weather and had never troubled to improve it since. The Commodore reported British longboats putting troops ashore at Benedict: not a party for watering or foraging, but a formed body, moving inland under regular officers. He estimated the number at four thousand, possibly more. He named the units he had observed from the river. The 85th Regiment of Foot. Elements of the 4th and the 44th.

Peninsula men.

Something tightened across Armstrong's chest — not pain, but a sudden compression, as though the room had contracted around him by an inch. He pressed his thumbnail hard against the edge of the paper. Congreve rocket batteries, Barney had written, with the flat certainty of one who had seen them used in the Atlantic, in the Channel, in the narrow waters off Bermuda — who knew, from that experience, what they did to militia who had never heard them.

Four thousand veterans with rocket batteries against Winder's fifteen hundred. If those men reached the Wood Yard before Winder had consolidated his position — before a single regiment had been ordered to march — the road to Washington would be open. Not threatened. Open. And every hour Armstrong held Winder's orders in abeyance was an hour the column gained.

He set the dispatch beside the map and looked at the two documents together, the way a physician looks at a wound and a blade and understands the relationship between them.

Armstrong straightened. He placed Barney's dispatch beneath Thornton's and aligned the edges.

'Shall I prepare new orders for General Winder, sir?'

Mason's expression was that of a man asking a question he already knew the answer to.

Armstrong picked up the first dispatch and held it toward the window, tilting it so the light fell across the corporal's uneven hand. 'This landing at Benedict,' he said, 'is a feint. I have considered the disposition and I am satisfied. The object is Baltimore.'

'Sir.' Mason did not step back. He remained at the desk's edge. 'Commodore Barney names the 44th Foot. Those are the men who stormed Badajoz. They do not march forty miles inland to feint.'

Armstrong set the dispatch down. He looked at Mason directly for the first time since the man had entered the room. The clerk's face was carefully composed — not quite impassive, not quite alarmed — and it was the carefulness of it that Armstrong found, in this moment, most objectionable.

'Hold any new orders for General Winder pending further intelligence from the field. When we have confirmation of movement toward Annapolis, we will know the design.'

'And if confirmation comes too late, Mr. Secretary? If they march tonight and Winder has not moved—'

'That is my order, Mason.'

A pause. The horsefly had found the windowsill and was moving across the glass with the aimless persistence of a creature that does not know it is trapped. The sound of it — a dry, intermittent scrape of legs on pane — was the only sound in the room.

Mason inclined his head.

'Yes, sir.'

He departed. Armstrong did not look up from the map. He drew his finger once more along the road from Benedict to the capital and then set the quill down and did not pick it up again for several minutes.

## Scene 2

The avenue was a furnace.

Armstrong had known August heat in a dozen cities on two continents, had sweated through dress coats in the antechambers of Paris and New York, but Washington in its peculiar geography contrived something worse than mere temperature: a wet, enveloping weight that pressed against the face and chest and made each breath a small labour. The unpaved street threw dust at every footfall, and the dust carried with it the smell of horse dung, of animal labour, of a city that had not yet resolved what it was.

He adjusted his cravat as he walked, pulling the linen tighter at his throat, then doing it again a hundred yards further on.

The President's House was perhaps a quarter-mile ahead. He had time. Madison would wait — Madison always waited, with the patient precision of a man who had constructed the machinery of deliberation and felt himself obligated to honour its slower motions. Armstrong had attended enough cabinet meetings to know that punctuality was the one form of deference he could withhold without being accused of anything.

Near the Capitol grounds, a company of militia was attempting to drill.

He saw them from two hundred yards: perhaps eighty men in various states of uniformity, some in blue coats, some in the plain brown of farm work, executing a wheel that had gone wrong at its pivot point and was now producing something less like a military manoeuvre than like a flock of geese interrupted at their grazing. A junior officer — a lieutenant, by the look of him — was shouting corrections that the rearmost files could not hear. The smell of unwashed wool and open ground reached Armstrong before the sound did: sweat and dry grass and something sour underneath, the odour of men who had been standing in the sun since morning without the discipline that makes such standing purposeful.

He did not slow his pace.

But a figure detached itself from the edge of the formation and came toward him at a jog — a man of perhaps forty, thickened through the shoulders, wearing a captain's epaulette that had been sewn on with more enthusiasm than precision. The coat was dark with perspiration.

'Your pardon, sir.' The captain pulled up and removed his hat. 'You are the Secretary of War, sir, are you not? I have seen you at the department.'

'You have.' Armstrong did not stop walking. The captain fell into step beside him.

'Captain Jennings, sir. Third company, District militia. We've been waiting on our marching orders these three days past.' The man recollected himself, steadied. 'Sir, if the British come up from Benedict, as they're saying — where do we march? Where do we take our position?'

Armstrong looked at the eighty men on the field — their mismatched coats, their botched wheel, the lieutenant still shouting into the heat — and saw them as the advancing column would see them: an obstruction of no consequence, to be brushed aside in the first quarter-hour of contact. If those veterans moved through the night and found these men standing in a field with no orders and no position, they would break before the first Congreve rocket had finished its shriek. And whatever stood behind them — which was nothing, which was the open road to the Capitol — would be forfeit.

'Your orders come through General Winder's office, Captain. The chain of command in this district runs through the general, not through this department directly.'

Jennings stepped in front of him. Armstrong stopped.

'Three days, sir.' The captain's voice had dropped, and his hat was crushed in his fist. 'The men have families in this city. Their wives. Their children. They are asking me what is to become of them if the British come, and I have nothing to tell them because no one in this government has seen fit to tell me.'

A cart rattled past on the avenue, its iron-rimmed wheels grinding against the packed earth. The driver did not look at either of them.

Armstrong held the man's gaze for a moment. Then he stepped around him.

'When the Secretary of State arrives in the field,' he said over his shoulder, 'you may address him as well. He is riding toward the Patuxent and will carry what intelligence there is.'

'The Secretary of State, sir?' Jennings's voice carried the bewilderment of a man who has been handed a document in the wrong language. 'We are to take our orders from — sir, is the War Department not —'

Armstrong pulled his cravat tighter against his throat and walked on toward the President's House. Behind him, the militia company continued its broken wheel in the dust and heat, and the sound of the lieutenant's shouting grew small and then disappeared entirely.

## Scene 3

The oval drawing room was not cool. No room in Washington was cool in the third week of August, and the open windows served only to admit the particular sulphur of horse traffic on the avenue below, which mingled with the smell of warmed wool and the faint acidity of men who had been alarmed since morning. Armstrong took the chair to Madison's left, noted that Monroe had positioned himself at the table's far end with the studied neutrality of one who had already chosen sides and wished to conceal the moment of choosing. Jones sat with his hands flat on his thighs. Rush had his pen out, which was either diligence or theatre.

Madison had the dispatches before him. The President's hands rested on the table's edge — small, precise hands — and he was looking at Armstrong with the expression he brought to documents that required a second reading.

Armstrong spoke before Madison could establish the meeting's architecture. He placed his own copy of Thornton's dispatch on the table, face up, and set his palm flat upon it.

'The force at Benedict. I have examined the reports with care.' He aligned the dispatches before him into a precise stack, squaring each edge. 'The landing is not inconsistent with a design upon Baltimore. Benedict presents no material obstacle to a column turning northward. Ross is an officer of method — he will not expose his line of march to the Patuxent road if his object is the capital. The terrain alone argues against it.'

'Mr. Secretary.' Madison's voice was not loud. The room had to settle to hear it, and the room did. 'I am to understand, then, that four thousand five hundred Peninsula veterans, disembarked at a point sixty miles from Baltimore and thirty-five from this city, constitute evidence for Baltimore.'

'The geometry of a campaign is not always its most legible feature, Mr. President.'

A bead of sweat ran from Armstrong's temple into his eye. He blinked, wiped it away with the back of his hand.

'I have considered Baltimore at length,' he continued, 'and I maintain that a commander of Ross's experience, with Cockburn's squadron available for naval support, does not elect the shorter march when the longer march offers him the greater prize. Baltimore is a city of thirty-five thousand, a functioning port, a military objective of consequence. Washington is a village of public buildings and unpaved avenues. Ross did not bring Peninsula veterans across the Atlantic to burn a symbol.'

'And yet,' Monroe said, from his end of the table, 'the Commodore's report places them already twelve miles inland and moving northwest. Not northeast toward the bay road. Northwest, Mr. Secretary.'

Armstrong turned his head. Monroe held a sheet of paper — not one of the dispatches on the table, but a separate document, folded once, the seal already broken. He had been holding it since before Armstrong sat down.

'I was not furnished with that report.'

'It arrived this morning.' Monroe set it on the table and slid it three inches toward the centre — not far enough to reach Armstrong, far enough to be seen. 'Through the Commodore's own rider. I took the liberty of having it copied for the President's review.'

Rush's pen had not moved in some time.

Armstrong did not reach for the document. He reached instead for the dispatch he had brought and turned it so the text faced Monroe.

'Mr. President.' He chose the register with the precision of a man selecting a blade. 'I would observe that intelligence pertaining to British military movements is a matter for this department's consideration, not for independent transmission to other offices. If the Secretary of State is receiving field dispatches through separate channels, the department's capacity to form a coherent assessment is destroyed — not compromised, Mr. President. Destroyed. And the consequences of that destruction will be borne not by the gentlemen at this table but by the fifteen hundred men at the Wood Yard who require a single, clear order and have not received one.'

'I have directed Mr. Monroe,' Madison said, 'to ride to Benedict.'

Jones's hands remained flat. Rush's pen was still.

Armstrong said nothing for a moment. He completed the stack of dispatches. He set his hands on either side of it.

'I beg the President's pardon.'

'To observe the British movements directly and report his assessment to me. He will depart within the hour.'

Armstrong turned his head and looked at Monroe, who met his eyes without expression and without apology.

'I was not consulted on this order.'

'No.'

A fly traversed the ceiling above Rush's head. Somewhere below the window, a horse was being walked on cobblestones, the sound irregular and unhurried.

Armstrong stood. The chair scraped against the floor. Jones looked up. Rush's pen moved — a single involuntary stroke — and then stopped.

'Mr. President. Mr. Monroe is not, to my knowledge, a military officer. A reconnaissance toward a hostile landing force is a function of this department, conducted through its officers, under its authority.' He placed both hands on the table and leaned forward slightly, his voice dropping rather than rising. 'I would respectfully inquire whether the President intends hereafter to prosecute the military operations of this republic through the Department of State. Because if that is the President's design, this department will of course arrange itself accordingly — and I will arrange myself accordingly, sir, which I trust the President understands.'

Madison looked at him. The small hands did not move.

'Mr. Monroe possesses my confidence,' Madison said, 'and will ride with my authority. I should be grateful, Mr. Secretary, if your department would furnish him with such intelligence as it currently holds regarding the roads between here and the Patuxent.'

Monroe said, quietly, 'I have the roads, Mr. President.'

Armstrong remained standing. He understood, with a clarity that arrived all at once and without comfort, that Monroe had been preparing for this errand before the meeting convened — that the room had been assembled not to deliberate but to notify, the decision already executed, the order already written, and his presence here a courtesy of the most precise and insulting kind. Monroe's separate dispatch, produced at the table's centre and not passed to him — that had been the declaration. Not an oversight. Not a procedural lapse. A declaration, made in front of Jones and Rush and their pens and their careful, recording silence.

He sat down. He pulled the dispatches toward him, squared the edges one final time, and placed them inside his coat.

'This department will furnish nothing further until I have reviewed what intelligence Mr. Monroe has already obtained through his separate channels. The President will have my written assessment by evening.'

Madison opened his mouth. Armstrong stood again, inclined his head, and walked to the door.

'Mr. Secretary —'

He did not turn. He closed the door behind him with a care that was louder than any sound he could have made.

## Scene 4

The War Department building exhaled its clerks near the fifth hour of the afternoon, and Armstrong heard them go — the scrape of chair legs, the muffled exchange of voices in the corridor below, the outer door's particular thud that marked the building's daily evacuation. He kept his eyes on the page. The notes from the cabinet meeting lay before him in his own hand, compressed and precise, a record no one else would have occasion to read.

He sharpened his quill with the penknife, testing the nib against his thumbnail, cutting it finer, then finer still, the small blade turning at an angle that served no practical purpose beyond the angle itself. The shavings curled onto the blotter, pale and translucent, smelling faintly of the goose they had come from.

The pitcher on the sideboard sat empty. He did not rise to address it.

Winder's dispatch arrived on the heels of a corporal who knocked twice and did not wait for an answer, setting the folded paper on the near edge of the desk with the expression of a man delivering something he was glad to be rid of. Armstrong broke the seal and spread it flat.

Winder wrote as he did everything: earnestly, at length, and slightly too late. The dispatch ran to four pages, the hand unsteady in the lower margins where the general had evidently continued writing on horseback or in failing light. He required reinforcements at the Wood Yard — two full regiments, or three companies at the absolute minimum. He required clarification of his authority over the District militia, which had received orders he could not account for through channels he did not control. He required, above all, a clear line of march in the event the British column moved northwest from Benedict rather than northeast toward the bay road.

*Northwest from Benedict.*

Armstrong pressed his thumb against the page until the nail whitened. He had traced that road on the map this morning with his own finger. He knew its length to the mile. And he knew — had known since Barney's dispatch, had known since his own finger followed the road west from Benedict and stopped — that the column was not feinting. He had known it in the office this morning, and he had told Mason otherwise, and he had told Madison otherwise, and the knowing sat in his chest now like a stone swallowed whole.

He read on.

In the final paragraph, Winder wrote that he required a reply before morning. Without reinforcements, without a designated line of march, the general would have no recourse but to fall back from the Wood Yard and consolidate what forces remained at Bladensburg — abandoning the last defensible ground between the British column and the capital. If that position fell without a fight, there was nothing between Ross and the President's House but six miles of open road and the broken company Armstrong had watched drilling in the dust that afternoon.

Armstrong set Winder's pages to his left. From the pile to his right he extracted the second document the corporal had brought — a single sheet, a clerk's copy, the handwriting not his own department's. Madison's hand working through Monroe's office, which was the arrangement the cabinet meeting had established without anyone being so indelicate as to name it aloud. The order directed the call-up of all available militia in the district. It bore no countersignature from the War Department. It had been transmitted, received, and presumably acted upon through channels that began and ended without Armstrong's knowledge or approval.

He read it with the attention he reserved for documents that might later require quotation.

His right knee, stiffened from an old injury sustained on ground considerably more consequential than Pennsylvania Avenue, locked as he rose to retrieve a fresh sheet of paper from the cabinet behind his desk. He gripped the desk edge, straightened the leg manually, and crossed to the cabinet. The floorboards gave their familiar complaint underfoot, dry and sharp in the emptied building.

He settled again. Dipped the newly cut quill.

The reply to Winder took eleven minutes. It acknowledged receipt of the general's dispatch. It expressed confidence in the general's assessment of the terrain and his knowledge of his own forces' capacity. It directed him to exercise his best judgment in the disposition of those forces, consistent with the protection of the district's principal approaches. It noted that Mr. Monroe would be arriving in the field within the next day or two and that the general should avail himself of the Secretary's counsel and coordinate his movements accordingly.

It did not specify which approaches. It named no reinforcements. It issued no order that could be measured against a subsequent outcome.

Armstrong blotted it. He set it to dry. He looked at it for a long moment — not with satisfaction, not with remorse, but with the level, appraising attention he might have given a fortification whose design he recognised as sound and whose purpose he found repugnant.

A masterpiece of its kind: a document that said everything and ordered nothing, that distributed responsibility like smoke and would leave no residue on the hand that wrote it. He had composed it in eleven minutes. He was not proud of it. He was not ashamed of it. He recognised it for what it was — the instrument of a man who had been stripped of his authority and was now arranging the evidence of his dispossession with the same care another man might spend on his defence.

He drew a second sheet. This one was not for Winder.

The letter to his New York correspondent wanted precision — not the overworked precision of an advocate who has lost the jury and knows it, but the cooler precision of one who has simply noted what occurred and trusts the record to speak. He wrote steadily. The quill's scratch was the only sound in the building. Outside, the light had shifted to the flat amber of late afternoon, and the avenue below was quieter now — the foot traffic of the morning's alarms having resolved itself into the ordinary commerce of a city that did not yet know what was coming.

When he reached the description of the President's demeanour at the moment he disclosed Monroe's mission, Armstrong paused, quill lifted, and considered the available vocabulary. *Anxious* was accurate but insufficient. *Agitated* carried the wrong implication — too physical, too undignified for a man who had kept his voice level throughout. *Uncertain* was closer, but uncertainty implied a question not yet resolved, and what Armstrong had witnessed was something more settled than that, more fixed.

*Tremulous.*

He wrote the word. Read the sentence back to himself, then read it again.

He did not examine whether the word was just. He sealed the letter.

The reply to Winder lay dry and waiting. Armstrong folded it, addressed it, and set it with the outgoing correspondence. The corporal, or whoever came next, would carry it before dark. Winder would have his answer by morning, as requested — everything except what he had asked for.

On the road south toward Benedict, Monroe rode with the undefined authority of a man the President had sent and the Secretary of War had not. Somewhere between the capital and the Patuxent, the two chains of command ran parallel and irreconcilable, and Winder — who would receive Armstrong's careful non-order before dawn and Monroe's improvised counsel sometime after — would be left to navigate the gap between them with fifteen hundred militia and no reserve and the sound of an advancing column growing louder in the dark.

Armstrong capped his inkwell. The building was quiet around him, the kind of quiet that accumulates in emptied rooms — not peaceful, but suspended, the silence of a held breath not yet released. Down the avenue, a lamp had appeared in an upper window, its small flame steady against the deepening dusk. He watched it for a moment. Then he gathered his papers, rose from the desk, and left the lamp to burn alone.
