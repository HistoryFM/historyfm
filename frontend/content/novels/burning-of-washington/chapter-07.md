# Chapter Seven: Bladensburg

## I.

The road to Bladensburg had become, by half past nine in the morning of the twenty-fourth, a thing of contrary motion. Ammunition wagons ground northward along the ruts, their iron-shod wheels raising a sound like millstones turning without grain — a dry, purposeless grinding that carried above the drum-taps of three militia companies attempting, with imperfect success, to maintain their order on the verge. Against this tide moved a scattering of civilians, some in carts, some on foot, their possessions bundled with the haste of people who had not expected to need them. Madison rode into the middle of it and knew, before he could have named the moment of knowing, that the road itself was already a verdict.

Armstrong kept pace a half-length to his right, erect on the bay mare, his posture carrying its accustomed suggestion that the proceedings around him were of academic interest. Rush rode behind them both. Monroe had gone ahead — when, Madison could not say with certainty; the morning had disaggregated itself into fragments of report and counter-report, and the precise sequence of departures had not been recovered.

'General Monroe has seen fit to ride forward,' Armstrong said. Not a question.

'He has.'

The wagon ahead lurched across a deep rut and its left rear wheel dropped, then recovered, the whole vehicle yawing hard enough that its driver stood up on the box with a curse that carried back to them clearly through the still, humid air. Madison's horse stumbled on the same rut a moment later — a sudden, wrenching drop of the foreleg — and he bit his tongue against his back teeth, tasted copper, and brought the animal upright without visible disturbance. He removed his spectacles. Polished each lens with the corner of his handkerchief, the cloth coming away grey with road dust. Replaced them. Then he turned to face Armstrong directly — a thing he had not done since they left the city — and held the Secretary's gaze until the bay mare's bridle-rings chimed with Armstrong's involuntary shift of weight.

'Mr. Secretary,' he said, 'I should be grateful to know whether you have issued any orders to General Winder regarding the disposition of his lines.'

Armstrong's reply came without pause. 'I have not, Mr. President. It was my understanding that you preferred to direct the defense personally, and I have not wished to introduce conflicting authority into the general's command.' A brief interval. 'I thought it the more respectful course.'

The grinding of the wagon wheels filled the space between sentences. Somewhere behind them, a drum company lost its beat, recovered it, lost it again.

'Then let me be plain with you.' Madison kept his voice level, without elevation. 'The British column is reported within four miles of the ford. If Winder's lines are not correctly disposed before they arrive, there will be no second opportunity to correct them. I am asking you, as the officer of this government charged with the conduct of war, to tell me what you know of the ground and what you intend to do about it.'

Armstrong's eyes moved to the road ahead. A pause long enough to be deliberate.

'The disposition of troops in the field falls properly within the authority of the commanding general, sir. I have inspected the ground. I have formed my views. But to offer them now, without General Winder's invitation, would be to presume upon a command that is not mine to exercise.' He paused. 'I have formed no orders because I was not asked to form them. I should not wish the record to suggest otherwise.'

Rush, behind them, made no sound.

Madison did not answer at once. The copper taste had not left his mouth. He understood what Armstrong had just accomplished: every word of the exchange had been calibrated for the inquiry that would follow the battle — not for the battle itself. The failure, whatever form it took, had been placed on record as Madison's own. Around them the road continued its grinding commerce of men and wagons moving in contrary directions, and somewhere ahead, invisible in the white haze, three lines of militia waited on ground that a man with Armstrong's experience had assessed and chosen not to correct.

'You will ride to the field with me, Mr. Secretary, and you will stand where General Winder can call upon you.' Madison put his heels to the horse and rode forward. 'If he does not call upon you, I will direct him to do so. You will not absent yourself from the conduct of this battle on grounds of constitutional nicety while the city burns behind us.'

Armstrong, after a moment's stillness that Rush would remember and later record, followed.

## II.

The ridge above the Eastern Branch bridge offered no mercy — only elevation, which served to press the August heat downward more efficiently, as a hand flattening dough. The air tasted of dry clay and something faintly organic, the river smell carried up on the slight movement of air from the east, cut through with the acrid ghost of slow-match already burning somewhere below. Madison's coat had soaked through at the back and shoulders. The horse beneath him radiated warmth through the saddle leather, and when he shifted his weight the animal's sides were slick against his boot.

Below, Colonel Stansbury's Baltimore militia occupied the first defensive line along the riverbank — or occupied it in the sense that men stood there, which was not quite the same thing. They were strung at intervals that suggested arrangement without constituting it, some in the shade of the willows at the water's edge, others standing in the open with their faces turned skyward as though the heat were a thing they could negotiate with by direct address. Madison counted the gaps he could see from the heights and stopped counting.

Winder was perhaps forty yards to his left, mounted, surrounded by couriers he sent and recalled with the regularity of a man who could not settle on a purpose. Madison rode toward him at a walk.

'Captain Ridgely — forward to the willow stand, take the ground at the ford.' Winder pointed. The courier turned. 'No — wait.' Winder turned his horse a quarter-circle. 'Send Warfield's company to the willow stand. Ridgely is to hold where he is.' A second courier departed. Winder looked at the map his aide held open across the pommel, then back at the ground, and the two did not correspond in any way he could immediately resolve. 'Recall Warfield. I need Warfield on the left —' He caught himself. 'No. Keep Warfield. Send Sterrett.'

Sterrett's position was already occupied. The courier who knew this said nothing and rode.

Madison drew up beside Winder before he could dispatch a third rider. 'General.' He kept his voice beneath the hearing of the aides. 'The third line. Where is Beall's regiment?'

Winder looked at him with the expression of a man interrupted mid-calculation. 'On the hill, sir. Lowndes Hill, second position.'

'I have ridden the ground. Beall is not on Lowndes Hill.' The words came without inflection, a fact requiring only acknowledgment. 'He has been moved further back — a quarter-mile at the least. If the front line gives way, the centre cannot be reinforced in time to matter, and the rear cannot reach the centre at all. You will recall him now, General, and bring him forward to the hill. I require it done before the enemy reaches the ford.'

The colour that came into Winder's face was not the brick-red of heat but something darker. He turned to his aide. 'Recall Beall. Recall him now.' The aide spurred away. Winder looked back at Madison with an expression that contained, in roughly equal measure, gratitude and mortification.

'Monroe.' The name was quiet, almost gentle. 'I believe General Monroe adjusted the disposition when he rode forward this morning.'

Winder said nothing. There was nothing to say about that.

Madison held his reins in both hands and looked out over the field. The stillness cost him — the effort of standing motionless when every instinct pressed him to ride down the slope and physically place the men where they needed to stand. Below, a company in blue coats shuffled forward twenty yards, halted, shuffled back, and stood where they had begun. The heat rose from the road in visible currents. Somewhere along the bank a man sat down abruptly, hat in his lap, face the colour of raw brick, and no one came to him. The slow-match smell had strengthened; it caught in the back of the throat and stayed there, a thin chemical bitterness that no amount of swallowing would clear.

He rode to the low spur of ground he had been calculating since they crested the ridge — sixty yards north of Winder's position, where the slope fell away and all three lines lay visible in sequence. The front along the river. The centre on Lowndes Hill, five hundred yards back. The rear — Beall's regiment, still too far, its recall already riding out through the heat at the pace of a single horse. The weight of what he surveyed settled on him not as calculation but as something older and heavier: the knowledge that he had seen the disposition and spoken and been too late, and that the men standing in those gaps along the riverbank did not know yet what their position meant.

A horsefly bit the back of his neck and he slapped at it, the small violence startling his horse sideways. Rush came alongside. 'Sir —'

'I see it.'

Beall's recall would take twenty minutes. The enemy column, if the last report held, had perhaps thirty. The margin between those numbers was not arithmetic. It was the lives of whatever men stood in the centre when the front broke through it.

He held the reins and did not look away. Somewhere below, an officer was shouting — not in command but in the particular register of a man repeating an order that has not been heard.

## III.

They came through the ford at a walk.

That was the first wrong thing — the steadiness of it. Madison had expected the rush of men under fire, the disordered scramble across shallow water that a defending force might punish with musketry from the bank. What he saw instead was four hundred British infantrymen advancing through the Eastern Branch as though crossing a field on exercise, their Brown Bess muskets held at the trail, their officers riding at the same unhurried pace. The river reached their thighs. It did not slow them.

Then the rockets came.

The sound preceded the fire by a fraction of a second — a shriek that began at a pitch no human throat could produce and rose from there, tearing the air in a long descending arc. The first rocket struck short of the position and skipped, trailing its burning tail across the grass. Another arced wide and dropped into the trees. The third landed among the fence rails where Stansbury's men crouched. The detonation reached Madison as a concussion against the chest before it reached him as sound — a deep, flat blow that seemed to press the air from the lungs — and then the smoke bloomed white and acrid across the bank.

The fence rails were empty before it cleared.

Madison's horse shied hard at the first shriek, nearly unseating him, and he gripped the pommel and held the animal down by main force, his thighs burning with the effort, the reins cutting into his palms. The horse trembled beneath him with its ears laid flat, every muscle gathered for flight. He held it.

'Mr. President.' Rush, close and urgent. 'Sir, we must withdraw.'

'Not yet.'

He watched. The front line — if it could still be called a line — was in motion, and the word was insufficient for what he saw: a hundred men, then two hundred, shedding their formations the way a wave sheds its shape when it strikes rock, each man resolving suddenly into a separate creature with a separate destination, and every destination pointed away from the bridge. Some dropped their muskets. Most kept them — that detail lodged in him and would not leave: they kept their muskets and ran, which meant the weapons were available to whoever could stop them long enough to use them, which meant there was still something to be done if Beall's regiment had reached the hill in time, if the centre held long enough —

Another rocket screamed overhead. The concussion of the burst struck him in the chest like a flat hand, and for several seconds the world contracted to a high-pitched whine and the acrid sting of Congreve propellant in his throat. He could see Rush's mouth moving. He watched the centre — already leaning backward before it had been touched — as the men from the front poured through its ranks and past and away down the Georgetown road without stopping.

The British infantry had reached the near bank. Their volleys came in the flat, staggered sequence of men who had drilled the motion until it required no thought: front rank fires, kneels, reloads; rear rank fires over the front rank's head, kneels, reloads. The rhythm was almost mechanical, almost indifferent, as though the men doing it had no particular feeling about what they were producing.

He released one hand from the pommel and pointed toward the centre. 'That line must hold,' he said to Rush, though Rush had not asked a question. 'If it does not, the road to Washington is open and there is nothing between the column and the city. Ride to Winder. Tell him the centre is his last position and he must anchor it before the men coming through it carry it away.'

Rush turned his horse and rode.

The centre was still standing. Wavering, its left flank already beginning to compress as the fleeing men from the front pressed through it, but standing. He had seen enough broken things to know the difference between bending and breaking, and this had not yet broken. But the men pouring through it were not stopping, and every one who passed carried with him the particular contagion of a man who has already decided to run.

He held his position on the heights and watched.

Behind him, somewhere down the Georgetown road, Barney's sailors were still marching. He could hear the creak and rumble of the gun-carriages on the hard road, and the sound was the only orderly thing left in the world.

---

## IV.

The guns were the first thing that made sense all day.

Five fieldpieces — twelve-pounders and a pair of eighteens — served by men who moved without the frenzy of the militia, without the frozen stillness of those who had never heard a shot fired and could not make their bodies remember what to do. Barney's flotilla men loaded and ran out and fired with the rhythm of something built rather than assembled, and the first full discharge tore a gap in the red-coated files advancing up the road that was visible even from where Madison sat his horse, forty yards back and off to the right. The smoke rolled over him thick enough to burn his throat before he could draw the next breath, and he pressed his sleeve against his eyes and held it there.

The British fell back.

He had not permitted himself to believe it until he saw it — the files checking, recoiling, the officers gesticulating, and then the column pulling away from the road into the bordering fields, out of the lane of fire. Barney's sailors let out a sound that was not quite a cheer, more the compressed exhalation of men who have been holding something in their chests for a very long time.

Madison removed his hat and held it against his chest.

The smell of Congreve propellant had settled into the air like weather — something chemical and wrong beneath the clean sulphur smell of black powder, and beneath both of those, carrying on the slight movement of air from the south, the iron-thick smell of blood from the men being dragged back past the guns toward whatever passed for a surgeon's station in this field. He wiped his eyes. He watched.

The guns fired again. The sailors cheered again. For twenty minutes — perhaps twenty-five, time having become a thing measured in discharges rather than by the clock — the sailors held the road, and the enemy did not come on.

He turned to Rush. 'Ride to General Winder. Tell him Barney's position is holding the road and that the militia on the left flank must be reinforced before they attempt the fields. Tell him I require an answer within ten minutes.' Rush stared at him for a moment — the plain surprise of a man who had not expected a command. Then he wheeled away.

Madison looked back at the field. He understood with a precision that astonished him that he was watching what the defense could have been, should have been, would have been, if anyone had troubled to build it from more than militia and hope. He understood it the way a man understands a proof he has worked out too late to use — completely, and with a grief that had no object he could name.

Then the left flank opened.

Not with a crash or a volley or any sound at all — simply an absence where men had been. The District militia dissolved from their position on Barney's left in a matter of moments, streaming back past the guns without stopping, without looking at the sailors or at anything except the road behind them. A man threw down his musket without breaking stride. Then another. The right flank followed within the minute, and the sound of it — boots on dry road, the clatter of abandoned cartridge-boxes, the wordless urgency of men who have ceased to be soldiers — was somehow louder than the guns had been.

The enemy had regrouped in the fields on both sides of the road. They came forward now not in column but spread wide, moving to enclose Barney's position from two directions at once. The sailors could see it. They could not do anything about it. They served the guns.

Rush returned at a canter. His face said what Winder's answer had been before he spoke a word of it.

'General Winder has no reserves to commit, sir. The militia cannot be recalled.'

Madison sat his horse and did not move. He had no reserves. He had no orders that would be executed in time to matter. The sailors serving the twelve-pounders forty yards away would be surrounded and taken or killed within the quarter-hour, and there was not one thing he could do to prevent it. If he rode now, he might reach the city before the column. If he stayed another ten minutes, he might not.

He stayed. He owed Barney's men that much — that there would be one man present who would remember what they had done, and say so, when the reckoning came.

A twelve-pounder discharged. Through the clearing smoke he saw Barney himself go down — not the clean fall of a man stepping back but the sudden, sideways collapse of a body that has stopped receiving instructions, the commodore's horse shying away and the figure on the ground not moving, and the sailors around him not stopping, not looking, continuing to serve the gun because there was nothing else left to do.

Madison touched two fingers to the bridge of his nose and closed his eyes. When he opened them the field was unchanged. He put on his hat. He turned toward the Georgetown road.

## V.

The road was not a road so much as a dissolution of the republic's pretensions — men, horses, abandoned fieldpieces, cartridge-boxes split open and bleeding their paper cartridges into the dust, an army rendered into its constituent parts and scattered across six miles of Maryland turnpike. Madison rode through it with Rush half a length behind him, and the vibration of the horse beneath him had become indistinguishable from the trembling of his own hands on the reins, the leather worn slick from hours of gripping, his palms raw at the base of each finger.

He saw Armstrong before Armstrong saw him.

The Secretary of War sat his bay mare at the road's edge, apart from the stream of retreating men, watching the rout with the composed attention of a theatregoer who has found the production precisely as poor as he anticipated. His hat was straight. His coat was unsoiled. He had not been near the guns.

Madison's horse picked its way through the wreckage. The grit of the road had worked itself into the folds of his cravat, into the corners of his eyes, into the dry cracked skin of his lips; he tasted it when he swallowed, which was a thing he had been doing often because his throat had closed with the dust and the smoke and the long speechless hours of watching. The dueling pistols sat in their holsters against his thighs, unfired, heavier than they had been that morning.

He drew up alongside Armstrong.

'What orders have you given,' Madison said, 'for the defense of the city?'

Armstrong turned to regard him. 'None, Mr. President.' The composure did not alter; if anything it deepened, the voice dropping a register, the posture settling into something that was almost repose. 'My counsel was not solicited this morning, nor at any point during the disposition of the forces. I understood the matter to be in your hands and General Winder's.'

'You were at my side for three hours, Mr. Secretary. You inspected the ground. You formed your views, as you told me on the road this morning, and you formed no orders.' Madison's voice did not rise. It did not need to. 'I am not asking what you were invited to do. I am asking what you did.'

'The disposition of troops in the field —'

'Is the present state of that field.' Madison gestured toward the retreating men, toward the smoke now rising in two distinct columns behind them — one from the direction of the battlefield, one from further west, darker, the black-brown of something large and deliberate burning. 'You will ride to the city. You will find whatever officers remain with their commands and place them across the roads leading to the Capitol and the President's House. You will do this in your capacity as Secretary of War, and you will do it before the enemy column reaches the Navy Yard bridge. That is an order, Mr. Secretary.'

Armstrong looked at him for a long moment. 'The city cannot be held with what remains in the field.'

'That is not the question I put to you.'

The retreating men flowed past them on both sides. Somewhere behind them a horse screamed once and was silent. The smell of powder smoke drifted west on the faint stir of air, and beneath it something else — the first intimation of char, of wood already burning, of a city beginning to understand what was coming.

'The republic will require an accounting of this day,' Madison said. 'Of every day preceding it. I intend to be present for that accounting, Mr. Secretary, and I intend that you shall be as well.'

Armstrong reached up and touched the brim of his hat — not a salute, something between deference and dismissal, the gesture of a man concluding a transaction he considers already settled in his favour. 'Then the republic shall have mine. And others besides.'

He turned the bay mare north and rode, and the stream of retreating men parted for him without ceremony, and Madison sat motionless as the dust closed over Armstrong's figure and the smoke continued to rise — thicker now, darker, a second column joining the first from somewhere to the southeast.

Someone had fired the Yard to keep it from the enemy. Which meant the British were already close enough to the city that the order had been given. Which meant the distance between this crossroads and the President's House was measured now not in miles but in minutes.

Rush said: 'Sir. We must ride.'

Madison put his heels to the horse and rode west. The road ahead was empty of everything except the question of whether he would arrive before the column did — and behind that question, pressing against it from the other side like a hand against a door, the knowledge that Dolley was still in the house at the end of Pennsylvania Avenue. She had been there since morning. She had been there through everything he had watched and been unable to prevent, and the smoke rising above the tree-line was visible from the upper windows, and she would have seen it, and she would know what it meant, and she would not have left.

He rode. Behind him, the second column of smoke climbed and spread, joining the first until the two were indistinguishable, a single dark stain widening against the white sky — and somewhere in the city toward which he rode, in the house he had not yet lost, a woman who knew exactly what was coming was still deciding what to save.
