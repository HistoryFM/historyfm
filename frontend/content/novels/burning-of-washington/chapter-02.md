# Chapter 2: The Strategist's Certainty

The inkstand was not where he wished it. Armstrong moved it two inches to the left, reconsidered, moved it back one. He tilted the chair so that the window light fell across the desk's surface rather than into his eyes, and the desk — his own desk, brought down from the private office at his instruction three weeks prior, its mahogany surface wide enough to spread a full chart of the Chesapeake — sat between him and the door like a rampart. He settled into the chair. He straightened his coat. He placed his hands flat on the wood, lifted them, placed them again.

Then he told his clerk to admit the delegation from Georgetown.

Three of them. The first through the door wore a merchant's coat of good broadcloth pressed for the occasion, his neck thick above the collar, his hands the hands of a man who had once loaded his own wagons. Behind him came two aldermen of lesser consequence — the sort who attend important meetings in order to report having attended them. Armstrong did not rise.

The merchant introduced himself. Armstrong did not record the name.

"Mr. Secretary," he began, "we have come on behalf of two hundred and fourteen citizens of Georgetown and the District, who have affixed their names to this document in the earnest hope—"

He produced the petition. Several pages, folded and tied with a ribbon, laid on the mahogany with the solemnity of evidence at trial. Armstrong looked at it without touching it.

"—that the War Department will give immediate attention to the construction of defensive earthworks along the heights above Georgetown and the roads approaching the capital from the east. We are given to understand that His Excellency the President himself shares the concern—"

A horsefly entered through the open window and began a lazy orbit around the room. Armstrong swatted at it once, without ceremony, and missed.

"—that the present disposition of our forces in this district leaves the seat of government exposed to any naval force that might ascend the Patuxent or the Potomac with hostile intent."

Armstrong let the sentence complete itself and settle. The room smelled of hot paper and the dung baking in the unpaved street below. He let the silence extend a moment longer than courtesy required.

"I am sensible," he said, "of the civic spirit your petition represents. Two hundred and fourteen signatures is not a figure one dismisses." He drew Captain Gordon's report from the folder at his left hand and aligned it with the edge of the blotter, adjusting it a quarter-inch until it satisfied him. "I wonder, gentlemen, whether you have had occasion to review the most recent intelligence from our naval officers on the Patuxent."

He did not wait for an answer, which was its own answer.

"Captain Gordon, writing on the twenty-second of this month, characterizes the British movements in the bay as" — he read from the first paragraph — "'desultory in nature, consistent with the pattern of coastal raiding this squadron has conducted throughout the preceding season, and showing no indication of the concentrated logistical preparation that a major landing operation would require.' These are the words of a professional naval officer who has observed the enemy at close range." He set the report down. The second paragraph, with its brief accounting of vessels riding too deep in the water for their apparent tonnage, remained face-up and entirely unmentioned. "I have also here" — he gestured toward the folder — "tallies from the quartermaster's office estimating the cost of the earthworks you propose. Shall I share those figures?"

He did not wait for that answer either.

"The works you describe — a continuous line from the heights above Georgetown to the Eastern Branch bridge — would require eleven thousand man-days of labour, at a cost to the treasury of not less than forty thousand dollars, drawn from funds currently allocated to the Niagara frontier, where the enemy has demonstrated, at Lundy's Lane and at Chippawa, both the will and the capacity to press his advantage." He folded his hands. "I am to understand that you would have me strip those works of their resources to fortify a city that any British officer of professional standing would recognize as a military liability rather than an objective."

The merchant's jaw tightened. "Mr. Secretary, the President—"

"The President has my full and considered assessment of the Chesapeake theatre, as he has had it since this department's last formal communication on the subject. I would not presume to characterize his views." Armstrong drew the petition toward him, placed it in the folder without examining it, closed the cover. "Measures proportionate to the actual threat will be taken as the intelligence warrants. The republic does not lack for defenders, gentlemen. It sometimes lacks for patience."

One of the lesser aldermen began to speak. Armstrong looked at him, and the man stopped.

"I am grateful for your attendance," Armstrong said. He did not stand.

The merchant held his ground a moment longer than the others. He reached into his coat and produced a second document — not the petition, something smaller, a single folded sheet — and set it beside the folder. "The President received a copy of this communication this morning. I thought it possible the department had not."

Armstrong regarded the sheet without touching it.

"It is from Commodore Barney. He writes from the Patuxent. He estimates the British force now in the bay at four thousand men embarked, at a minimum. He requests, with some urgency, that earthworks be commenced at Bladensburg."

The room held its heat. The horsefly had gone; the street below offered nothing but the slow baking of dust.

Armstrong picked up the sheet, read it, set it down. "Commodore Barney commands a flotilla of gunboats and is not, in the present arrangement of our forces, positioned to observe the full complement of British shipping in the Chesapeake." He opened the folder, placed Barney's communication inside it beneath Gordon's report, closed it again. "His concern does him credit. His figures do not bind this department."

"There are other avenues," the merchant said, "through which these concerns may be expressed."

"There are," Armstrong agreed.

"And if the earthworks are not commenced, and the British do come up that road—"

"Then the officer responsible for this department," Armstrong said, "will answer for his judgment. As he has always done. Good afternoon, gentlemen."

They went. The door closed. The room returned to its previous composition: the chart of the Chesapeake on the table, the folder with its two reports, the smell of warm paper — and Armstrong alone with his mahogany fortification in the heat of late June, the folder now containing intelligence he had not communicated to the President.

---

The clerks had taken their hats and their midday hunger with them, and the office was his.

Armstrong pulled Gordon's report from the stack and laid it flat on the map table. The window admitted no movement of air; the room held the morning's accumulated warmth with the patient malice of old brick. A half-eaten biscuit sat on its saucer at the desk's edge, the exposed interior gone chalky and dry. He touched it absently, felt the stale give under his thumb, withdrew his hand.

He read the first paragraph again — not because the words had changed but because this was the paragraph he had committed to, and he did not commit without verification.

*The movements of His Majesty's naval vessels in the Patuxent and its approaches present, as of the date of this report, a character consistent with the raiding operations of previous seasons. No concentration of force has been observed that would indicate a design upon any fixed object of consequence. The several landings at Benedict and Port Tobacco have been conducted by parties of marines and seamen, and the damage to property, while considerable, has been indiscriminate rather than purposive.*

Consistent with raiding. Indiscriminate rather than purposive. Gordon's language — a competent officer's fondness for qualification — and Armstrong had borrowed it that morning with the ease of long practice.

He turned the page.

The second paragraph was shorter. Gordon had written it in a different hand — more compressed, the letters slightly smaller, as though the writer himself had contracted in the act of composition.

*I am further bound to report that on the morning of the 19th instant, a body of transports was observed standing into the bay from the southward, their number estimated by the pilot Carver, whose judgment in such matters I have found reliable, at no fewer than twenty sail of varying burden. The presence of these vessels, taken in conjunction with the landing parties previously described, suggests a capacity of embarkation exceeding what the present raiding operations would require. I submit this observation for such consideration as the Department may judge appropriate.*

Twenty sail of varying burden. Armstrong set the page down with the deliberate care of a man handling something that might, if dropped, make a sound he did not wish to hear. He reached for the quill. The cramp in his first finger had returned — it always returned in this weather — and he shifted his grip before drawing the nib across the margin in a slow, considered stroke: *Consistent with previous intelligence. Raiding complement. No indication of strategic intent.* The ink dried almost before it was written. He did not read the words back.

Armstrong sat with it. The heat pressed against the back of his neck like a compress. He became aware of the taste of the morning's coffee, still faintly sour at the root of his tongue, and of the tepid water in its glass — untouched, gathering the room's warmth — and of the dust settled on the near corner of the map where the Patuxent met the bay in a fine blue line that someone, years ago, had drawn with a steady hand.

Twenty sail was not a raiding complement. Twenty sail was a different thing entirely, and Gordon, who was careful with numbers, had written *no fewer than* with the deliberation of one who wished to be understood precisely.

He set down the report. He flexed the fingers of his right hand, spread them against the table's surface, feeling the grain of the wood and the faint grit of map-dust against his palm. Then he picked up the quill.

A cramp seized his writing hand as he formed the first letter — a sudden seizing of the muscle between thumb and forefinger that forced him to set the quill down and close his fist, open it, close it again. He stared at the half-formed word *probable* on the page's margin and waited. The cramp released. His pulse beat once, twice, in the webbing of his hand.

He completed the annotation in a deliberate, unhurried hand: *probable victualling vessels — no action required.*

The ink was wet. He regarded it.

The quill had written what the quill was told to write, and the hand that held it had known, before the first stroke, that victualling vessels did not carry Congreve rocket frames. Twenty sail provisioning a raiding party would be a redundancy that no flag officer of competence would order, and Cockburn — whatever else might be said of the man — was not deficient in competence.

Armstrong had served at Kosciuszko's side. He had written the orders for the reduction of Fort George. He understood the grammar of an expeditionary force as well as any man alive in the republic, and the grammar of twenty sail standing into the Chesapeake was not the grammar of resupply.

He knew this. The knowledge did not sit beside him; it sat inside him, in the same cavity as the stale coffee and the heat and the ache where the cramp had been. And yet the annotation lay on the page in his own hand, and the ink was drying, and he did not cross it out.

If those twenty sail carried what he suspected — if Cockburn was assembling a force sufficient to march inland — then the road from Benedict ran through Bladensburg and into the capital, and the capital had nothing between it and that road but Barney's gunboats and whatever militia could be scraped together in the time remaining. The earthworks the Georgetown men had asked for would take weeks. There were no weeks. There might not be days.

And if the capital fell — if the seat of government burned while this department's attention was fixed on the northern frontier — then the officer who had fixed it there would answer for the burning. Unless that officer had also created a command to defend the capital, and appointed a commander, and issued orders that were, in every clause, defensible. Unless the failure could be made to attach to the commander rather than to the department. Unless the commander were the sort of man to whom failure naturally attached.

He pulled Barney's communication from the folder and laid it beside Gordon's on the map table. Two reports, from two officers, neither of whom had reason to fabricate his figures, and both pointing toward the same road.

Armstrong turned Gordon's report face-down. Then face-up. He squared its edges against the table's corner and set the water glass upon it, the glass leaving a faint wet ring on Gordon's careful script.

Through the open window came the distant sound of a cartman working his horse up the unpaved avenue, the animal's hooves pulling free of the mud with a rhythmic, effortful sucking. Then nothing. Then the cartman's voice, indistinct, and the horse again.

He drew both reports toward him, positioned them beneath the stack, placed the aldermen's petition on top. Madison had received Barney's communication that morning. The question was not whether the President had read it. The question was whether, having read it, he would summon Armstrong before the afternoon was out — and what Armstrong would say when he did.

He had perhaps three hours. Three hours, and Armstrong knew how to build a great deal of architecture on paper.

---

Minor arrived at half past seven, which was itself a communication. A social call would have waited until the following morning; an official inquiry would have come by letter. That hour, with the June heat still pressed against the city like a hand that would not lift, was the hour of the informal — which is to say, the hour when men said what they had been sent to say without any of them being required to acknowledge the sending.

Armstrong received him in the parlour. Thomas had laid the cold supper on the sideboard — ham, bread, a half-wheel of cheese — and Armstrong had eaten perhaps four bites of the bread before the ham's salt smell, insufficiently masked by the evening air, reminded him that he was not hungry so much as merely past the point where he had last been fed. He pushed the plate aside as Minor was shown in.

'Colonel.' He rose from his chair. 'You find me at an ungentlemanly hour.'

'I had business on this side of the city.' Minor had the militia officer's bearing: erect enough to suggest training, relaxed enough to suggest he had never quite forgotten it was voluntary. 'I hope I do not intrude.'

'Not in the least. Thomas — wine for the Colonel.'

The Madeira was from a case brought down from New York the previous autumn. Armstrong poured with his own hand — a gesture of intimacy that cost him nothing and committed Minor to reciprocal candour, or at least its appearance. Minor accepted the glass. His eyes moved once to the papers on the side table, where draft orders lay beneath a folio, and then away.

Armstrong noted the glance and said nothing about it.

They spoke of the weather. They spoke of the Federalist newspapers. Minor offered the opinion that the *National Intelligencer* had been more measured than the opposition press. Armstrong offered the opinion that measurement, in journalism as in gunnery, was chiefly a function of whom one wished to hit.

A silence. The candles on the mantelpiece threw the room into planes of amber and shadow. The street outside had gone quiet; the evening carried the smell of cut grass from the vacant lot behind the house, and beneath it the river — a green, vegetable thickness that never entirely left the air in this city.

Minor set down his glass.

'I wonder whether you have given consideration to General Porter for the new district command. Moses Porter. The engineer.'

Armstrong's left knee required him to stand. He rose and moved to the window without haste, his hands clasped behind his back.

'Porter,' he said.

'The Secretary of State mentioned him. Informally. The man knows the Chesapeake country — he surveyed the battery positions at Hampton Roads. His knowledge of fieldworks is, by all accounts, considerable.'

'It is.' Armstrong turned from the window. 'And if fieldworks were the whole of the matter, I should endorse him without hesitation. But the command you describe is not an engineering problem. It is a political one.'

Minor waited.

Armstrong crossed to the sideboard and refilled Minor's glass with the unhurried attention of a host thoroughly at ease. His own glass he left where it stood. 'Governor Winder is Federalist. His cooperation — or the want of it — will determine whether the Maryland militia musters in any useful number when the call is issued. A district commander who lacks connexion to that community, whatever his professional excellences, will find himself conducting operations through the medium of a governor with every incentive to be uncooperative.' He returned to his chair. 'Porter would gratify the regular army. He would do nothing at all for the Maryland militia, which is what will actually stand between any British raiding party and this city, should such a party materialize.'

Minor turned his glass between his fingers. 'And you have a name that would serve both purposes.'

Armstrong said nothing.

'The Secretary of State has spoken with the President on the matter. His Excellency is inclined toward Porter.'

The candle on the side table guttered in a draught and steadied.

'Then I shall communicate my assessment directly,' Armstrong said. 'Tomorrow morning, before the hour of business. I should not wish His Excellency to act on incomplete counsel.' He paused. 'And Mr. Monroe would do well to reflect that a commander appointed on the recommendation of the State Department will find his position — administratively speaking — untenable. The orders will issue from this office or they will not issue at all. That is not a matter of preference. It is a matter of law.'

Minor set down his glass. 'I shall convey your views.'

'Pray convey also my warmest regards to the Secretary of State. He takes a commendable interest in military affairs. I have always found it—' Armstrong let the word hang, watching Minor's face. 'Admirable.'

After Minor had gone, Armstrong stood at the sideboard with the cold ham and the untouched remainder of his wine. Monroe had been to the President's House. If Monroe had written a formal recommendation — if Madison had received it before retiring — then the morning was not a margin. It was a closing door.

He left the wine. He went to his papers.

---

The candle on the left guttered and died while Armstrong was mid-sentence — the word *connexions* left incomplete on the page, the room suddenly contracted to a smaller compass. He lifted the remaining candle and repositioned it to the right of the inkstand, tilting the flame until the wick caught and steadied, then read back what he had written.

The street outside had been silent for an hour. Somewhere toward the river a dog had barked twice and then stopped. The clock on the mantelpiece showed the quarter past eleven. The room smelled of tallow and cooling ink and the faint sourness of the cheese Thomas had not cleared from the sideboard.

He dipped the quill and completed *connexions* and then continued.

*— in Maryland, together with his demonstrated familiarity with the terrain of the District and the adjacent counties, render Brigadier General Winder peculiarly suited to the exigencies of the command herein proposed. His appointment would, moreover, serve to conciliate those interests in the legislature of Maryland whose cooperation the successful prosecution of the district's defence must require —*

He paused. Read the sentence again. One reading would take it for an endorsement; a colder reading would find in it nothing but an account of political convenience dressed in the language of military necessity. Madison would read it quickly. Monroe, if Monroe saw it, would not — but Monroe had already been to the President's House, and the name Moses Porter might already be lodged in the President's ear like a seed that needed only morning to take root.

He set down the quill and took up the draft orders for the district.

Every blank page demanded the same thing: specificity. Lines on a map. Numbers of men. Positions named and fortified. The page was indifferent to his purposes; it required to be filled, and what filled it would either constitute a defence or expose the absence of one. Armstrong had spent the better part of an hour learning, again, what he already knew — that the most dangerous document a man could produce was one that appeared thorough.

He had met the difficulty with the only instrument adequate to it: syntax.

*Such regular forces as may be spared from other commands shall be assigned to the district at the discretion of the Secretary of War, in proportion to the intelligence received of enemy movements and the requirements of the northern campaign.*

Which was to say: none, until he chose to send them. He would not choose. And the sentence would bear any reading he required of it — in a cabinet meeting, before a committee of inquiry, in the columns of the *National Intelligencer* if it came to that. The clause was a locked door with the key in his pocket.

*Militia of the several states within the district's boundaries shall be called into service at the commander's discretion, in such numbers as he shall deem necessary for the protection of the district.*

Winder would deem. Winder, who had spent eleven months as a prisoner of the Crown after Stoney Creek — who had advanced without reconnaissance into a position any subaltern would have recognized as untenable — would assess the necessity and call up the militia and find, when the moment arrived, that the moment had already passed. Armstrong had not mentioned Stoney Creek in his endorsement of the man. He saw no occasion to.

He read through the orders once, from the boundary description — *Maryland, the District of Columbia, and that portion of Virginia lying between the Rappahannock and the Potomac* — to the final clause regarding the commander's authority to requisition supplies. The boundary description was accurate. The authority to requisition was genuine, in the sense that Winder could draw freely from a quartermaster's establishment that Armstrong had already stripped for the northern campaign. The orders were, in every clause, defensible. They would not defend Washington. But they would defend John Armstrong.

The remaining candle threw his shadow large against the far wall. The dog toward the river did not bark again. The tallow smell thickened as the wick burned low, and the wax pooled on the brass holder in a slow accretion, white over white.

Armstrong reached for the sand-caster, dusted the orders, shook the page once, set it to dry. He folded it along the crease he had already made, brought the sealing wax to the candle flame, let three drops fall. His personal seal — not the department's, not yet; that formality belonged to morning — pressed into the cooling wax with a faint resistance and held.

He did the same with the endorsement. Winder's name was on it. His own name was beneath.

He set both documents side by side at the corner of the desk and aligned their edges.

If Monroe's recommendation had already reached the President — if the appointment were settled before Armstrong crossed that threshold in the morning — then the documents on this table were a structure without a foundation, and the new command would belong to Monroe as surely as if the State Department had issued the warrants itself. And Porter, competent Porter, would build his earthworks and drill his militia and perhaps hold the road at Bladensburg. And Armstrong's northern campaign would be gutted for a secondary theatre, and the war would be Monroe's war, and the next election would be Monroe's election, and Armstrong would be the officer who had been overruled by a Secretary of State whose department had no authority over a single regiment.

He could not wait for morning.

Armstrong rose, took the endorsement, and moved toward the door. The candle threw his shadow across the wall, across the sealed orders still waiting at the table's corner, across the folder with its two buried reports that pointed, if a man were willing to follow them, toward the road the British would take.

He did not look back at them.

But in the passage — the night air no cooler than the room he had left, the house silent around him, the city beyond the windows dark and indifferent — he stopped.

Not from doubt. Doubt was what lesser minds called the space between a decision and its execution, and Armstrong had never suffered the affliction. What stopped him was something more precise: the involuntary reckoning of consequence, running its calculation without his permission.

Twenty sail. The road from Benedict through Bladensburg to the capital.

He had served this republic since the year its founding documents were wet ink. He had ridden with Gates a

He had ridden with Gates at Saratoga when the republic was a conjecture and the British were a certainty. He had written dispatches by candlelight in farmhouses that smelled of mildew and desperation,

and he had watched men die for an idea that had not yet proven itself worth dying for. He understood, with a precision that required no sentiment, what it had cost to build what now stood — the institutions, the offices, the physical architecture of a government that had survived, so far, every test the continent and its enemi

es had devised.

And he was walking down a dark passage with a document in his hand that would place a captured man in command of its defence.

He turned the fact in his mind as a man turns a coin — not to discover its value, which he already knew, but to feel the weight of it. Winder. The road from Benedict. Twenty sail riding at anchor while the Secretary of War carried an endorsement through a silent house and told himself the arithmetic was sound.

The arithmetic was sound.

The republic had survived worse appointments. It had survived incompetent generals and bankrupt treasuries and a Congress that could not agree on the colour of the sky. It would survive this. And if it did not — if the road at Bladensburg opened and the capital burned — then the question before the country would not be the competence of a district commander. It would be the competence of a president who had conducted three years of war by committee, who had permitted his cabinet to govern in his name while he governed in theory, who had chosen, at every critical juncture, the counsel that confirmed his anxieties rather than the counsel that might have resolved them.

Madison had made this situation. Armstrong had merely declined to unmake it.

He reached the front door. The endorsement was folded in his coat. The night beyond the threshold was close and without motion, the air carrying the river's low smell and beneath it something else — the faint char of cook-fires, or the imagination of char, the mind supplying what the intelligence already suspected. He stood in the open frame and did not permit himself the indulgence of the view, such as it was: a dark street, a darker sky, the distant smear of lantern-light along Pennsylvania Avenue where someone, somewhere, was still conducting the ordinary business of a city that did not yet know what it was.

He was still standing there when the gate at the end of the path struck its post with the force of a man who has ceased to care about gates.

The figure that came through it was not running so much as falling forward — a controlled collapse, each stride catching the body before it went down, the gait of a man who had been in the saddle past the point of the saddle's mercy and had finished the distance on foot. His uniform coat was black with sweat from collar to waist. He had lost his hat. He mounted the steps without slowing and put a folded paper directly into Armstrong's hand with no salute, no ceremony, none of the graduated grammar by which military men ordinarily approached cabinet officers.

'Mr. Secretary.' The breath came in broken intervals. 'Colonel Carbery sends this from the Upper Marlboro ferry. The fleet at Benedict — the ships are not the objective.'

Armstrong unfolded the paper. The handwriting was Carbery's, compressed and slanting, the letters of a man writing in haste by poor light.

'They have been on the road since before dark,' the rider said. 'Four thousand, perhaps more. Moving fast. No baggage train to speak of.'

Armstrong read the dispatch once. Then he read it again, not because the words had changed but because the endorsement in his coat pocket had just become a different document entirely — written for a different war, a different enemy, a different geometry of catastrophe than the one now moving up the road from Upper Marlboro in the August dark.

'Who else has seen this?' he said.

The rider opened his mouth. From somewhere down the avenue, a bell began to ring.
