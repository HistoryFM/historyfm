# Chapter Six: The Eve of Bladensburg

## Scene 1

The road to Bladensburg was not a road so much as a managed confusion — a rutted track pressed flat by the passage of ten thousand boots and a hundred wagon wheels, now occupied simultaneously by men going east and men going nowhere, their purposes as unclear as their formations. Armstrong rode through them at a walk, Lieutenant Jacobs half a length behind, and catalogued what he saw with the detachment of a surveyor measuring a property he does not intend to purchase.

A company of Virginia militia occupied the right margin of the turnpike, resting on their cartridge-boxes in the late afternoon heat. Their muskets were stacked in the approved manner, which was the only approved thing about them. Two slept with their mouths open. A third had removed his shoe and was examining his foot with the concentration of a physician confronting an unfamiliar complaint.

The sun had dropped to the treeline west of the Anacostia, throwing long shadows across the dust. Somewhere ahead, a wheel had sheared its axle — he could hear the grinding protest of iron on packed earth before he saw it, and then the shouting that followed, voices overlapping without resolution, each one louder than the last and none in command. A supply wagon sat at an angle across the left lane, its near wheel collapsed, its load of salt pork tilted precariously toward the ditch. Three men argued over it. No officer was visible.

Armstrong pulled his riding glove tight on the left hand, then the right, working each finger to the tip.

He did not stop.

Jacobs said nothing. This was Jacobs's most useful quality.

The argument about the salt pork fell behind them, replaced by the sound of a drum — someone attempting, without much conviction, to beat the assembly — and then by the voices of men who showed no inclination to answer it. Armstrong counted perhaps four hundred militia in the half-mile between the Anacostia crossing and the rise where the turnpike bent north. They were not marching. They were not encamped in any arrangement that would permit rapid movement. They were present in the way that weather is present — occupying space, generating noise, and subject to no one's direction.

He had seen this before. He had seen worse. At Saratoga, in the long retreat through New Jersey when the army had dissolved around Washington like sugar in rain — he had seen what militia became when the machinery of command broke down. What he saw now was that dissolution rendered in miniature, in the colours of a summer afternoon: green and brown and the grey of unpressed linen. By morning, Ross's column would be on this same ground. Four thousand veterans against this. Armstrong did not complete the thought. The arithmetic was too clean.

A stone caught his horse's near forefoot on the descent — a sharp check in the animal's stride, the hoof lifting and coming down wrong. Armstrong dismounted, ran his hand down the cannon bone, found the hoof and lifted it. A flat stone, wedged at the frog. He worked it free with his thumb, wiped the thumb on his breeches, and inspected the sole. No bruise. The horse set the foot down and bore weight without flinching. Armstrong remounted.

Jacobs had waited without comment.

They rode on.

It was at the bend, where a stand of tulip poplar threw the last of the afternoon into shade, that the captain appeared. He came from the right verge at a purposeful diagonal, placing himself in Armstrong's path with the deliberateness of a man who has rehearsed this moment. Virginia militia, a captain's insignia on his collar, his face darkened by sleeplessness and hours of anger. He held up one hand.

'Mr. Secretary.'

Armstrong drew his horse to a halt. He said nothing.

'Stull, sir. Captain, Fifth Virginia. I have two hundred and forty men on this turnpike and no orders where to form. I sent to General Winder's staff at four o'clock. No answer.' The captain's voice was level, which cost him something. 'I am asking you directly, sir. Where do you want us?'

The poplar leaves turned in a breath of wind. Somewhere east, a crow announced itself and fell silent.

Armstrong's gaze moved across Stull, settled briefly on the company ranged behind him in a rough crescent — close enough to hear — and then lifted to the road ahead, where the bend obscured whatever lay beyond.

'That matter falls within General Winder's competence. You will address your inquiry to him.'

'I have, sir. Six hours ago.' Stull's jaw tightened. 'The British are at Upper Marlboro. Six hours is time we do not possess.'

'Then address it again.'

Stull took one step forward — not threatening, but not retreating either. 'Sir. My men standing in a ditch while the enemy forms column. If you will not order us to a position, tell me plainly, and I will find one myself.'

The words landed in the space between them. The company behind Stull had gone very still.

Armstrong held the man's gaze — then reached into his coat and produced the folded order of march he had carried from the War Department, the one bearing Winder's name and his own countersignature. He held it up so Stull could see the seal. 'This order vests the field command in General Winder. Not in me. Not in you.' He returned it to his coat. 'Find him, Captain. That is my counsel.'

He touched his heel to the horse's flank and moved forward. Stull stepped aside — there was nothing else he could do — and Armstrong passed him at a walk, Jacobs following, the dust rising around the horse's hooves in small, purposeless clouds.

'God help us, Mr. Secretary.' Flat. Carrying. From behind. 'For you will not.'

Armstrong did not turn.

What struck him, in the silence that followed, was not the accusation — he had expected something of the kind — but the absence of heat in it. Stull had not shouted. He had spoken at the volume of a man reporting a fact. The flatness of it was worse than rage would have been, and Armstrong rode with it settling into him like a stone dropped into still water, the ripples spreading outward long after the surface had closed.

## Scene 2

The canvas fly above the campaign table had been stretched poorly, one corner lower than the others so that it pooled and sagged, and when the evening breeze moved through the elm boughs it bellied and snapped with the conviction of nothing. Winder did not appear to notice. He had been awake, by Armstrong's reckoning, for somewhere north of thirty hours, and the evidence had arranged itself across his face with the candour that exhaustion brings to men who have spent their last reserves on concealment.

Armstrong planted both hands on the table's edge and leaned over the map.

It was a good map, at least. Hand-drawn on linen, the Eastern Branch rendered with reasonable accuracy, the roads from Bladensburg marked in two distinct hands, the topographical lines scratched with an economy suggesting someone who had actually walked the ground. Stone weights held the corners. The smell of the encampment reached him even here: woodsmoke from three hundred fires, the sharp ammonia of horse lines, and beneath both, the fouler odour of latrines dug too close to the water. It was the smell of an army assembled in haste.

He could read the dispositions without being told. The forward position near the bridge — perhaps three hundred yards of front with the river on its left. The second line, five hundred yards behind, on the gentle slope the locals called Lowndes Hill. And the third — Stansbury's Baltimore men — nearly a mile to the rear, so far back they might as well have been in Georgetown.

Three lines. Each out of supporting distance of the other.

A fly landed on the map, at the bridge. Armstrong brushed it away.

'I had hoped you would have an opportunity to review the ground itself before the light failed,' Winder said. His voice was steady. That was the best that could be said for it. 'As you can see, I have disposed the forces in three successive lines, with the intention of—'

'I have seen the ground.' Armstrong straightened, pulling his hands from the table. 'I rode it this afternoon.'

The colonel to Armstrong's right — Minor, he thought, or possibly Hite — stepped back half a pace.

Winder's colour rose. 'Then you will have observed that the terrain between the second and third lines is broken, and that I have considered whether to bring Stansbury forward—'

'Considered.' Armstrong let the word sit.

'I have weighed the advantages, Mr. Secretary, and concluded—'

'What you have concluded, General, is that three bodies of militia, none within supporting distance of the others, will hold a bridge against four thousand regulars who took Badajoz by storm.' Armstrong took the nearest stone weight from the map's corner and set it down on the forward position with a crack that made Winder flinch. 'That is what you have concluded.'

The fly overhead snapped in the breeze. Winder's hands had gone to fists at his sides — not in threat, but in the involuntary compression of humiliation received before subordinates.

'Then correct me, sir.' Winder's voice had dropped, gone tight at the edges. 'I would respectfully submit that I have requisitioned regulars since June. I have requisitioned fieldpieces. Seven dispatches to your department, and I have received in return three companies of militia and a letter advising me to consult the district maps. So correct me, Mr. Secretary. Tell me where to place men I do not have.' A pause, and then something shifted in Winder's face — not anger now, but a kind of stripped candour, the expression of a man who has discarded his last scruple of pride. 'I am asking you — as I have asked you every week since my appointment — for an order. Any order at all.'

The fly had returned to its former position on the linen.

Armstrong looked at it. He looked at Winder. He looked at the map — at the small granite marker sitting where the forward line would stand in the morning — and felt the pull of the thing, the gravitational draw of a problem he could solve, lines he could redraw, positions he could correct with an hour's work and a competent aide. If he spoke now — if he said *bring Stansbury to within two hundred yards of the second line, site the twelve-pounders on the ridge where they can fire over the heads of the men at the crossing, refuse the left flank with Barney's sailors* — the dispositions would be his. The battle would be his. And when the men broke, as they would break, the defeat would carry his name rather than Winder's.

'I am satisfied,' he said, 'that General Winder has the matter in hand.'

Winder stared at him. The fists unclenched. Something behind the man's eyes — some last guttering expectation that the machinery of command still functioned — went out.

'Then God help the men at that crossing.' Barely above a murmur. 'For the War Department will not.'

Armstrong set the marker back at the map's corner and smoothed the linen flat with one palm. 'You have your command, General. I suggest you use what remains of the night.'

He turned and walked out from under the canvas fly, into the smell of woodsmoke and the sound of three hundred fires, and did not look back.

Behind him, he heard Winder say something to the colonel — a low exchange, too quiet to catch. He did not slow his pace. But he noticed, as he had not noticed on the road with Stull, that his hands were entirely steady, and this fact, which would once have satisfied him, produced instead a sensation he could not immediately name: not guilt, which was too simple, and not pride, which was too clean. Something closer to the recognition a physician feels when he has correctly diagnosed a disease he cannot treat.

## Scene 3

The farmhouse had been stripped of its domesticity by the army's passage — the family's pewter gone, the curtains taken down for some purpose no one had recorded, the hearth cold. Two tallow candles stood on the pine table, their flames leaning in unison when the door opened, then steadying. The room smelled of rendered fat and damp stone, the walls exhaling a cellar chill that the August night could not dispel.

Armstrong lowered himself into the nearer chair, set the National Intelligencer on the table before him, and spread it flat with both hands. Three days old. He had read it twice already on the road. He opened it now as one opens a breviary — not for instruction but for the discipline of the form.

Madison's servant arranged writing materials at the table's far end with the efficiency of long practice in improbable rooms. Madison himself stood for a moment near the door, his black coat gathering the candlelight, his face unreadable. Armstrong did not look up. He heard the scrape of the second chair, the soft settling of weight, the tap of a glass inkwell placed on pine.

Then the quill began.

It was the only sound for a time, beyond the distant noise of the encampment — a drum beaten irregularly, someone's voice lifted in what might have been a command or might have been a quarrel, the whole indistinguishable in the dark. Inside, the quill scratched. Madison was writing to his wife. Armstrong knew this without being told; there was a rhythm to it, something unhurried and exploratory in the pauses, as though he were composing not sentences but a room she might inhabit.

Armstrong turned a page. The paper crackled in the quiet like a small, dry cough.

The quill stopped.

'I understand you spoke with General Winder this evening.' Madison did not look up.

Armstrong set the Intelligencer down, folding it along the crease. 'I did.'

'And your assessment of the dispositions?'

The candle nearest Madison threw a thin shadow across the pine between them — a dark line bisecting the table as cleanly as a surveyor's mark. Armstrong held the silence for a beat. 'General Winder is doing his utmost with what the department has provided him.'

Madison looked up then. His eyes, in the candlelight, were very still. 'That is not what I asked.'

'The lines are as well-placed as the ground permits.'

'John.' The given name, rarely used and never without purpose. 'I have ridden that ground. I have seen where Stansbury is posted. I am asking you whether we can hold the bridge.'

Outside, a horse screamed — a sudden, ugly sound, some animal gone lame or panicked in the dark — and then men's voices, sharp and overlapping, bringing it under control. The sound died. The silence that replaced it was worse than what had preceded it.

If he spoke the truth now, Madison would act. Would send to Monroe, would pull Stansbury forward, would spend what remained of the night in frantic reorganisation that would produce worse disorder than the original fault. Or he would relieve Winder, and the command would fall to someone with even less time to learn the ground. Either course would make the morning's disaster more complete — and the question of who had seen it coming would be answered before the first shot was fired. Armstrong's name would be written into the record not as the man who had warned, but as the man who had intervened too late and made the ruin worse.

He uncrossed his legs beneath the table. He crossed them again.

'The bridge will be contested. Whether it is held depends on the men who stand at it.'

Madison studied him. The quill lay across the inkwell, untouched. 'You are telling me nothing.'

'I am telling you what I know, Mr. President.'

A long moment. Then Madison said, with a precision that had nothing of anger in it and was therefore more damaging than anger: 'It is to be observed that a man of your experience, Mr. Secretary, having ridden the ground and spoken with the commander, might reasonably be expected to offer more than that.' He stopped. Pressed two fingers to the bridge of his nose and closed his eyes for a moment precisely too long. When he opened them he looked not at Armstrong but at the page before him — as though the letter were the thing that required his attention and the question had already been answered to his satisfaction, which it had not. 'Very well.'

He dipped the quill and returned to his letter. But his hand paused above the page — held there, the nib suspended, a drop of ink swelling at its tip — and Armstrong understood that the pause was not composition but the visible form of a decision being made: to press the question or to let it die. The drop fell. It struck the page. Madison began to write.

Armstrong lifted the Intelligencer. He read, or appeared to read, a column on the prospects for peace at Ghent. His left knee, stiff from eight hours in the saddle, sent a dull ache up his thigh when he shifted. He extended the leg beneath the table and waited for the joint to ease.

The Intelligencer reported that Mr. Gallatin had been received at the court of the Tsar with every mark of distinction. Armstrong read this. He turned to the shipping intelligence. A brig out of Norfolk. Fourteen days to Bermuda. He read the tonnage, the cargo manifest, the master's name. He committed none of it to memory.

Madison's quill moved in its careful way across the paper. The sound of it filled the stripped room — scratch, pause, scratch — steady as a pulse, intimate as breathing. And Armstrong sat within it and said nothing, and the last hour in which the catastrophe might have been averted ran out between them like sand through a glass neither would turn.

## Scene 4

He could not sleep. He had not expected to.

Madison lay on the cot behind him, breathing with the measured regularity of exhaustion's surrender — had lain down in his coat, one arm folded beneath his head, and been gone within minutes. The small sound of it filled the room in a way the man himself, in waking, never managed.

Armstrong sat at the table. The candle had burned to a nub, its flame listing in some draught too faint to feel. Before him lay the two sentences he had written. He did not need to read them again. He had written *The Secretary of War, having attended the field positions on the evening of 23 August, observed that the general disposition of forces, as arranged under the direction of Brigadier General Winder—* and then the sentence had stopped, because it knew what it required next and he would not give it.

He set the quill aside. Picked it up. Set it down again.

Outside, the fires burned along the turnpike, their light orange and diffuse through the shuttered window. A voice carried — some officer's distant correction — and then the dark resumed its commerce of insects and wind. He pressed one hand flat against the table edge. His stomach had been hollow since noon, and the hunger announced itself now with the blunt insistence of the body refusing further deferral. He waited for it to pass. It did not pass. He pressed harder against the table, and after a time the hollow ache settled into something he could carry.

Five hours. Perhaps six. He had measured the distance twice on the ride from Upper Marlboro and arrived at the same figure both times: Ross's column would reach the ford by midmorning. This was not speculation. This was the mathematics of marching men — three miles to the hour on a good road, two and a half on a poor one, and the road from Marlboro was poor. By ten o'clock, the advance guard would be visible from the far bank. By half past, the column would be forming for assault. And the men standing at that crossing — Stull's company among them, if Stull had found anyone to tell him where to stand — would receive the first volley with the river at their backs and no second line close enough to matter.

He had spent the summer constructing the case that Washington was indefensible. Had said it in July, in August, to Madison and to Monroe and in the privacy of his own reasoning every morning he declined to send Winder what Winder had requested. The case was sound. He believed it still.

But Fabius had not abandoned Rome.

The thought arrived unbidden and would not leave. He turned it over, examined it, tried to set it aside. Fabius had traded ground for time, had preserved his army by refusing battle, and history had vindicated him. But Fabius had not abandoned *Rome*. He had abandoned fields, villages, provincial towns — ground that could be recovered. What was coming tomorrow was not the loss of a position. It was the loss of a symbol. The Capitol, the Navy Yard, the President's House. Ross would march his veterans through the streets and put the public buildings to the torch, and every newspaper in the country would demand — in print, in Congress, from every pulpit — who had permitted it.

And Winder would not be sufficient. The disaster would be too large for one man's shoulders, and the inquiry that followed — not *if* but *when*, not a possibility but a certainty already gathering force — would ask what the Secretary of War had known, and when, and what he had done with what he knew. It would ask about Captain Stull. It would ask about the map, and the stone weight he had set down and lifted again, and the words *I am satisfied that General Winder has the matter in hand.* It would ask these questions with the precision of men who had already determined the answer.

He took up the quill.

The two sentences waited. He set the nib to the page and wrote: *—the full responsibility for the disposition and conduct of thos

e forces, the Secretary having confined his attendance to observation only, in accordance with his understanding that the field command was properly vested in—*

He stopped. He read what he had written. He pushed back from the table — the chair legs scraping the stone floor with a sound that made Madison stir on the cot, murmur something unintelligible, and settle again.

Armstrong stood. He carried the page to the hearth, where the coals gave off their last thin glow. He held it over the embers. The sentence was complete. It was also, in the sense that would matter to a congressional committee, a lie — not in any single word, but in the architecture of its omissions. That he had seen the lines and offered nothing. That Winder had asked him for counsel and received a formula. That a militia captain on the turnpike had asked him where to place his men and Armstrong had told him to inquire elsewhere.

He let the page go. The paper caught at once, curling inward from the edges, the ink blackening before the words themselves were gone, and then there was nothing but a brief bright ember that faded as he watched.

He returned to the chair. He pulled the candle closer and took up a fresh sheet. The quill needed trimming; the nib had splayed from the last hour's work, and he drew his penknife and pared it back to a clean point, the small curl of quill-fibre falling to the table. He set the knife down. He dipped. He wrote the date at the head of the page, and beneath it the salutation, and beneath that nothing, because the sentence that needed to follow had not yet resolved itself into a form that would survive examination.

The candle guttered. He cupped his hand around it and the flame recovered.

Outside, along the road, a horse came fast — not the measured canter of a courier on a known errand but the hard, broken gallop of a man riding beyond the animal's capacity, and it did not slow as it approached but rather grew louder, closer, until the hoofbeats ceased abruptly in the yard and there was the sound of a man dropping heavily from the saddle, and then boots on the step, and then a fist against the door — not a knock but a blow, repeated, the wood shaking in its frame.

Madison did not wake.

Armstrong set down the quill.
