# Chapter 5: The Flotilla's End

## Scene 1

The candles had burned to within two inches of their holders, and the papers on Madison's desk had ceased to be documents and become instead a topography of failure.

He removed his spectacles and pressed two fingers against the bridge of his nose, then put them back on.

Monroe's dispatch lay before him, pencilled in the Secretary of State's rapid, tilted hand — letters compressed by urgency, some words smeared where the paper had caught his sleeve, the ink still faintly raised where the nib had pressed hard on the final lines. He had read it twice. He was reading it again, not because the meaning had changed but because he found himself unwilling to accept that it had not.

*The enemy encampment at Upper Marlboro presents every indication of a column prepared to advance rather than to consolidate. I estimate the force at no fewer than four thousand regulars, with artillery and cavalry support. The bridge at Bladensburg is open. There is no American force of consequence between General Ross and the capital. I write this within sight of their fires.*

He set the dispatch flat and reached for Armstrong's memorandum.

Four pages, the Secretary's hand neat and precise, the arguments arranged with the careful symmetry of a man who has decided his conclusion in advance and built the architecture to support it. He had read this document in July, and again in the first week of August, and had found it — he confessed it now, in the close heat of the study — adequate. The assessments had seemed reasonable. The language had seemed measured. He had read it as a man who wished to be persuaded reads anything: with the charity of one who has already decided.

*It is my considered judgment, based upon the intelligence presently available, that the British objective in the Chesapeake theatre is most probably Annapolis, with a secondary possibility of Baltimore. Washington, as a purely military target, offers the enemy little of strategic value, and I do not regard the capital as facing any immediate danger from the present force, which has conducted itself throughout the season in a manner consistent with coastal raiding rather than sustained inland advance.*

He set the memorandum aside.

A cramp seized his writing hand. He set down the quill he had not known he was still holding and worked the fingers slowly, joint by joint, against the swollen stiffness that the August damp had settled into them over the course of the evening. He waited until the cramp released.

The window was shut against the river air, and the study held the day's heat in its plaster and its shelves of bound volumes, releasing it now against his face and neck in slow, close waves. Somewhere below, a horse shifted in the yard — the courier's animal, still saddled, its rider asleep on a bench in the entrance hall by his secretary's report. The horse's hooves rang twice on the cobbles and then were still, and in the silence after them Madison heard the Potomac — the broad, indifferent sound of the river moving south toward the bay where the British fleet rode at anchor, unhurried, awaiting nothing.

The rider had given him what Monroe had not committed to paper.

*He said to tell you, sir, that the militia at Bladensburg is in a worse condition than what is written.*

Madison rose from the desk. He crossed to the window, unlatched it, and pushed it open. The river smell came in at once — mud and reeds and the faint corruption of low water — and with it the sound of the horse below, shifting again, and beyond that, nothing. No drums. No distant report of fieldpieces. Only the Potomac, indifferent.

He stood at the window for a moment. Then he turned back to the desk, took up Armstrong's memorandum, and placed it face-down beneath the inkstand. Monroe's dispatch he left face-up, where the morning's light would find it first.

He called for his secretary.

"Have General Armstrong at the President's House at first light," he said. "Tell him I require his attendance."

He did not say *invite.* He did not say *request.*

The secretary withdrew. Madison remained at the open window, his hands at his sides, the river air moving against his face, and did not look at the documents on the desk behind him.

## Scene 2

The oval drawing room received the morning light at a slant, pale and provisional. Armstrong stood near the door, hat in hand, shoulders squared — the bearing of a man who has worn a uniform long enough that the posture requires no thought, and who deploys it now as a kind of argument.

Madison had not invited him to sit.

He stood himself, having risen from behind the desk before Armstrong was fully through the door, and remained standing, though Armstrong was taller by half a head and carried himself with a soldier's habitual ease. From somewhere deeper in the house came the sound of trunks being dragged across floorboards — a sound that had been present since before dawn and that neither man acknowledged. The room smelled of candle-wax and the faint mineral residue of a fire burned out in the night, and beneath both of these, faintly, the cedar and beeswax of packing already under way in the rooms above.

"I have before me," Madison said, "a communication from Secretary Monroe, written in his own hand from a position within sight of the British encampment at Upper Marlboro." He lifted the dispatch and read without inflection. *Four thousand regulars at minimum, Peninsula men by their equipment and their drill, no American force of consequence between their present position and the Washington road.* He set the paper down. "I have also before me your memorandum of the nineteenth, which characterises the enemy's probable objective as Annapolis and concludes that the capital faces no immediate danger."

Armstrong's expression did not alter. He said nothing.

"I would observe," Madison said, "that these two documents cannot both be accurate."

"Mr. Monroe," Armstrong said, "rode without orders and without military escort into territory adjacent to an enemy column." A pause — not of hesitation but of selection, the pause of a man choosing which instrument to reach for. "His observations are those of a civilian on horseback. The Secretary of State's enthusiasm for the field is not, in itself, intelligence."

"Then you maintain the capital faces no immediate danger."

"I maintain that the intelligence is as yet insufficient to determine the enemy's true objective." He reached into his coat and produced a document — folded, clean-edged — and set it on the corner of Madison's desk with the flat finality of a man playing the last card in his hand. "The general order establishing the Tenth Military District. Signed by the President. The defense of the District falls within Winder's purview."

Madison looked at the document. He did not touch it.

What Armstrong had just done was not a courtesy. It was a foreclosure. The document on the desk — Madison's own signature at its foot — was the instrument by which Armstrong proposed to make the capital's loss the President's alone to bear. Every order not issued, every militia company not summoned, every dispatch not answered: all of it would resolve, in the record, into the space between that signature and whatever came next. Armstrong understood this. The slight forward tilt of his chin, the stillness of his hands, the precise angle at which he had set the paper down — these were not the gestures of a man offering assistance. They were the gestures of a man completing a transfer.

"Mr. Secretary." The words came out level and separate. "I will ask you plainly. Do you consider four thousand British regulars marching upon the capital of the United States to be a matter requiring the personal attention of the Secretary of War?"

"I consider it a matter requiring the attention of the officer to whom the President has entrusted the district's defense."

"You have issued no orders."

"I was not asked."

"I am asking now."

Armstrong's voice dropped — not in volume but in temperature. "Then I would respectfully suggest, Mr. President, that the question of what orders are to be issued is one for General Winder, who commands in the field, and for the President, who appointed him." He bowed — not deeply, but correctly. "If the President wishes to direct me to issue specific orders in my own name as Secretary of War, I await that direction in writing."

He retrieved his hat. He did not wait to be dismissed.

Madison watched him go. The door closed. From the passage came the sound of Armstrong's boot-heels on the marble floor, unhurried, diminishing — and then a second door, and then nothing. The trunks resumed their dragging somewhere above.

He picked up the document Armstrong had placed on the desk — the general order, his own signature at the foot of it — and read it through once. Then he set it beside Monroe's dispatch and went to find his wife.

---

He found her in the passage outside the blue sitting room, directing French John in the stacking of a second trunk. The smell reached him before he saw her — beeswax from the freshly wrapped silver, and beneath it the sharper scent of cedar shavings packed around the cabinet papers.

"Mrs. Madison."

She turned without surprise.

"Mr. Madison." Her eyes moved to French John. "That one may go down."

The steward lifted the trunk and departed toward the service stair. His footsteps diminished and were replaced by the sounds of the house — another trunk moving on the floor above, a window being latched against the morning wind, the low voice of a servant relaying instruction to another servant further away.

Madison looked at the second trunk. Its lid was closed and latched. Beside it, stacked with the purposeful order of a woman who had spent her morning in action rather than deliberation, were three leather document cases he recognised as belonging to the cabinet room.

"The papers," he said.

"The dispatches of the last fortnight, the correspondence with the governors of Maryland and Virginia, and the commission letters." She smoothed the front of her dress — inventory, not nerves. "French John has spoken with the coachman about the Georgetown road. We can have two wagons ready before dawn."

He had come to tell her. He understood now that he had not needed to.

"The portrait," he said.

"Still in its frame. I have given instructions that it is not to be cut until we are certain the frame cannot be removed entire." A servant dropped a silver candlestick somewhere in the adjacent room; the clatter rang through the corridor and both of them stilled, and then she continued without looking toward the sound. "French John believes the frame will yield to the right tools. He has sent for a carpenter."

Madison reached out and adjusted the lid of the second trunk. It was already perfectly closed.

"I shall ride toward the army's position tomorrow morning," he said. "To confer with General Winder."

"Yes," she said.

The smell of cedar and beeswax, and beneath both of them, from the yard below, the smell of horses — the dragoons Tench Tilghman had promised before he had gone off to find Winder and had not returned.

"If I have not sent word by the evening of the twenty-fourth," he said, "you are not to wait for it. Take what has been prepared and go. The papers, the portrait if it can be moved, the silver already packed." He held her gaze. "You have my full authority to act on your own judgment. I wish that understood."

She looked at him for a moment — the precise, unhurried attention she gave to things she intended to remember.

"It is understood," she said.

French John's footsteps returned on the stair. She turned back to the work.

## Scene 4

The road to Old Fields had been a road in the morning. By early afternoon it had become something else — a channel through which exhaustion moved in both directions, militia companies pressing east with the uncertain step of men told to march but not why, and others, singly or in pairs, drifting west toward Washington with no apparent orders and no apparent shame.

Madison's party threaded between them. The dragoons rode with their carbines across their saddles. The heat was a physical weight — it rose from the clay in visible waves and pressed down from above with a force that had nothing of air in it, and the horses moved through it with their heads low, blowing. Madison had shed his coat an hour past and rode in his shirtsleeves. The borrowed pistols were holstered at his belt — Jefferson's pair, the walnut grips worn smooth where Jefferson's own thumb had rested. He had left the colonel's pistols on the table in the President's House and had not examined why.

The courier came at a canter, his horse lathered to the shoulder, and pulled up hard enough that the animal's hindquarters dropped. He fumbled at the leather satchel across his chest with fingers that shook.

"Dispatch from General Winder, sir. For Colonel Stansbury's regiment."

Madison held out his hand.

The courier hesitated. The dispatch was not addressed to the President.

Madison kept his hand extended and said nothing.

The man surrendered it.

The order directed Colonel Stansbury to march his regiment immediately to the assembly point at Old Fields and await further instruction there.

Madison reached into his coat and produced the dispatch already folded across his saddle — Winder's own hand, dated that morning, three hours prior: *Stansbury to march on Bladensburg with all expedition and to hold the bridge road.*

He held both documents out toward Coles without a word.

His secretary read them. A stillness came over the young man's face — not composure, but the arrested expression of someone who has encountered something for which he has no ready category.

"Sir—"

"Paper," Madison said. "And your hand — mine will not serve in this saddle."

Coles had the writing board out before the sentence was finished. Around them, the road continued its disordered traffic — a sergeant shouting at stragglers, the creak of a wagon axle, somewhere behind them the flat report of a musket discharged by accident and a burst of profane recrimination from the men nearby. Madison did not look up.

He dictated without pause, his voice level and carrying, each sentence arriving in the order it was needed.

*The President of the United States directs General Winder to concentrate all available forces at Bladensburg without further division of that force, and to hold the bridge road and its approaches until the President shall arrive to confer with the General in person. No further orders dispersing the concentration are to be issued pending that conference.*

He signed it. Coles blotted it, folded it, held it out.

Madison took it and gave it directly to the courier — not through Coles, not through the nearest dragoon. He held the man's eye.

"Ride."

The courier looked at the signature. Then he rode.

## Scene 5

The farmhouse smelled of other men's occupation — sour straw, grease from a skillet abandoned on the hearth, the faint sweetness of hay drifting through the open doorway from the barn. Madison sat on a three-legged stool in the yard, the only furniture not already claimed by exhausted dragoons and their horses. A fire burned at the edge of the road, small and purposeful, and it was toward this that Coles held the dispatch, his face pale above the paper, the light unsteady on his features.

"From the beginning, Mr. Coles."

Coles read. His voice held for the first paragraph — Winder on the position of the Fifth Maryland, the state of the roads north of the capital, the tone of a man still in command of his material. Then the dispatch shifted. Not gradually. Winder wrote that the Fifth Maryland ought to concentrate at the muster ground; then, within four sentences, that the position there was untenable and the regiment should march toward Bladensburg; then, with a confidence that arrived from nowhere, that the muster ground was in fact the stronger position and that Bladensburg required fewer men than previously reckoned. No reason was given. Coles's voice faltered on *reckoned*, recovered, continued.

There was a paragraph about the artillery. It contradicted the paragraph before it.

Coles stopped.

"Continue," Madison said.

"There is only the closing salutation, sir."

The campfires in the middle distance — thirty of them, scattered without apparent system across a field whose boundaries the dark had swallowed — threw their light against nothing useful. A horse stamped. Two men argued in low voices about something that was not the British. The fire beside Madison hissed where a knot of green wood met the flame, and the smell of it — sharp, resinous, wrong for a summer fire — drifted across the yard and settled in his coat.

An audible clench from his stomach broke the quiet. He had not eaten since early morning. Coles, without being asked, produced a piece of hard bread from his coat and held it out. Madison took it. He held it.

He drew Barney's dispatch from inside his coat.

He had been carrying it since the Patuxent road, unread, because he had known — in the way one knows a thing before the words arrive to confirm it — what it would contain. He unfolded it and held it toward the fire, and read it in the unsteady light, and it was worse than he had anticipated.

Barney's hand was nothing like Winder's. Each letter was formed with the economy of someone who has spent forty years at sea writing orders in rough weather, who has learned that imprecision costs lives. The commodore did not begin with salutation or circumstance. He began with fact.

*The flotilla is destroyed. I have burned her myself, every vessel, at the mouth of the Patuxent, this morning at ten o'clock, that she should not fall to the enemy. The powder magazine I fired last. You will understand that I would not have done this had any other course remained to me.*

Madison read the next paragraph. His thumb pressed flat against the paper to hold it steady in the night air.

*I am marching my sailors and marines to Bladensburg. We number some four hundred men. I have five naval guns which we are hauling by rope, there being insufficient horses. We will arrive when we arrive. I am sixty-five years of age and have not marched infantry in thirty years, but I find I have not forgotten how. If there is a position to be held at Bladensburg, we will hold it. If General Winder has already abandoned the position, I should be grateful to receive that intelligence before we arrive, as it would save considerable effort.*

Madison lowered the dispatch.

The fire hissed again. Somewhere in the barn, a horse moved in its stall, the halter chain ringing once against the post and then going quiet.

He looked at what he held: Barney's dispatch in one hand, the bread in the other. The bread he set on the ground beside the stool. He read Barney's closing lines once more and then folded the paper with care and returned it to his coat.

Four hundred sailors. Five naval guns hauled by rope through the August dark. A sixty-five-year-old commodore who had burned his own ships and was now marching his men toward a position he could not be certain still existed, to serve under a commander whose orders contradicted themselves within the span of a paragraph.

This was what remained. Not the regular army — there was no regular army, not here, not in any number that signified. Not the militia, which was dissolving into the dark roads around him like sugar in warm water. What remained was Barney, and four hundred men who had served on ships, and five guns they were dragging by hand through a Maryland night because there were not horses enough to do it properly.

Madison stood. The stool scraped back on the packed earth. Coles looked up.

"Mr. Coles." His voice was quiet — not the quietness of a man suppressing agitation, but of one who has arrived at a decision and no longer requires volume to carry it. "You will note in the official record that the President will proceed to the army's position at first light to confer with General Winder on the disposition of forces. A conference. That word."

Coles held his pen. "Yes, sir."

A conference. The word that would appear in the record, the word that kept the form of the thing intact — the President as counsellor, not commander, the constitutional order preserved in its outward aspect even as he prepared to do what no president had done and what the Constitution neither sanctioned nor forbade, because the framers had not imagined this particular failure. They had imagined tyranny. They had imagined faction. They had not imagined Winder.

He turned toward the barn. "Have my horse ready before first light. And see that the dragoons are informed — we ride at four o'clock, or earlier if a dispatch comes."

He walked to the edge of the firelight and stood where the dark began, looking east. Somewhere out there, four thousand British regulars — Peninsula veterans, men who had broken Napoleon's marshals in the Spanish mud — were at rest. Somewhere on those same roads, Barney's sailors were moving through the dark with ropes over their shoulders, hauling iron toward a crossing they had never seen, to fight on land for a city that was not theirs to defend by any logic except the one that had been left to them by the failure of every other arrangement.

From the road behind him came the sound of a rider — not the easy canter of a messenger completing his errand, but the hard, driven rhythm of a horse pushed past its comfort, the hooves striking unevenly on the packed clay. Madison turned. The rider came out of the dark at a pace that scattered the dragoons to either side of the road, pulled up short of the fire, and was off the animal before it had fully stopped.

"Mr. President." The man was barely upright, one hand on the horse's neck for support, the other pressing a folded paper toward Madison. His coat was dark with sweat from collar to waist. "From General Winder, sir. He says — he says the position at Bladensburg cannot be held without you come yourself. He says the militia will not stand without the President on the field."

Madison took the dispatch. He did not open it.

"How long ago did you leave him?"

"Two hours, sir. Perhaps less. He was — the general was moving when I left him. I do not know where he is now."

Two hours. The bridge at Bladensburg lay perhaps eight miles east. If Winder was already moving — retreating, the word the rider would not use — then the road behind him would be open by morning, and what stood between Ross and the capital was four hundred sailors with five guns and a sixty-five-year-old commodore who had not yet arrived.

Madison folded the new dispatch without reading it and put it inside his coat beside Barney's.

"Mr. Coles," he said. "Four o'clock becomes now."
