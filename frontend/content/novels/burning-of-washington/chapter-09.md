# Chapter 9: The Smoke on the Horizon

The road north of Bladensburg smelled of sweat and scorched powder and something fouler beneath both — the exhalation of men who had run until their bodies began their own accounting. Armstrong rode through it with his collar turned up against a warmth that required no such defence, his military cloak gathering the August heat against his back, his aide Cutler keeping a half-length behind on a grey that had been pushed hard since morning.

The Maryland militia were everywhere, moving as water moves when it finds a slope. Cartridge-boxes lay in the road's ruts, some split, some simply abandoned mid-stride by men who had decided the weight was no longer worth carrying. A broken wheel tilted against a fence post. A dead horse, already swelling in the afternoon heat, its legs arranged in the attitude of a creature still in motion, occupied the right verge where the road narrowed between two stands of oak. The smell of it reached Armstrong at twenty paces and stayed with him long after he had passed — sweet and thick, the smell of consequence.

He had counted, without intending to, some two hundred men in the first quarter-mile. Not one of them looked up. This was, he had decided within the first quarter-hour of riding, the most salient fact of the afternoon: not the burning he could no longer see but whose smell the wind still carried south to north, not the absence of any organised rear-guard, not the discarded equipment that told its own sufficient story — but the studied vacancy of two hundred faces, each one turned away as he passed through them.

The column blocking the road was Maryland militia, a regiment or the remnant of one, moving in a mass rather than a formation. Armstrong drew rein at its edge and waited. The column continued moving.

'Colonel.' His voice carried without effort, the practiced projection of a man who had addressed formations in wind. The officer at the column's head — sunburned, hatless, his coat dark at the back — turned in his saddle. Their eyes met.

The colonel turned forward again. He did not slow. He did not raise a hand in acknowledgment or salute or the smallest gesture that might, in any court of military record, be interpreted as recognition of the order or the officer who gave it. The column flowed past Armstrong's horse, pressing it toward the verge, and the colonel rode at its head as though Armstrong were a feature of the road — a fallen branch, a standing water — that required only to be avoided.

Armstrong pressed his horse forward, cutting across the column's line of march, forcing the men nearest him to break step and part around him. The colonel's grey shied at the sudden movement. The colonel turned.

'You will halt this column,' Armstrong said. He kept his voice level and did not raise it. 'You will halt it, form your men on the right of the road, and tell me the present disposition of General Winder's command.'

The colonel looked at him. He was perhaps forty, with the reddened eyes of a man who had been in smoke. 'Sir,' he said, 'I have orders to fall back to the Frederick road. I intend to execute those orders.'

'Your orders are superseded. You answer to this office.'

'I answer,' the colonel said carefully, 'to the officer who signed my orders. With respect, I do not have written authority from your office, and the road is open.'

He pressed his horse forward. The column resumed its pace. Armstrong sat at the verge, the men filing past on both sides, the colonel's back receding northward with the unhurried certainty of someone who has calculated the cost of defiance and found it acceptable.

Cutler brought his grey alongside. He said nothing.

Armstrong adjusted his cloak at the collar, his fingers finding and releasing the wool.

'You observe,' he said, his voice addressed to the middle distance rather than to Cutler, 'that the militia will not stand in the field, will not hold a road, and will not acknowledge the officer charged with their direction. I have held this view since the third year of the war. The powder spent on their instruction represents a charge against the public treasury that no accounting will ever satisfactorily resolve.'

Cutler said: 'Yes, sir.'

The last of the column passed. The road north was open. Armstrong did not take it.

---

The tavern on the Frederick road had no name that Armstrong could discover, which suited it. A low room, a fire burning against the damp despite the August heat, a dozen men distributed between three tables with the particular dispersion of those who did not know one another well enough to consolidate. Tobacco smoke lay in a stratum just above head height. Somewhere behind the partition, a pot was striking iron with the rhythm of someone who had stopped caring about the rhythm.

Armstrong had taken the corner table nearest the wall. Cutler sat two tables distant, close enough to be useful, far enough to be deniable. The mutton before Armstrong was adequate. He had been eating since the first plate arrived without tasting any of it.

He was aware of the major before the major stood.

The man had been drinking since midafternoon — plain from the quality of his stillness, the kind that precedes motion the way a held breath precedes a shout. District of Columbia brigade, from the cut of what remained of his uniform. A heavyset figure, perhaps thirty-five, with the face of someone who had filed a grievance and been composing its presentation ever since.

Armstrong cut his mutton with precise, unhurried strokes.

The chair scraped. The room did not go quiet — it went careful, which was a different thing entirely. Armstrong heard the shift in the background noise the way one hears a clock stop: the absence announcing itself.

'Mr. Secretary.'

The major had rehearsed the title to make it an accusation. He was standing now, one hand braced on his own table, the other extended across the common room — not toward Armstrong's chest but toward his face, as though identifying a specimen for the benefit of the room.

'Mr. Secretary, I should like to know — I think every man here should like to know — why no earthworks were raised at Bladensburg. Why no fortification was ordered at the ford. Why the men of this district were sent to hold a position that any officer of experience could see was indefensible, without so much as a breastwork to cover their front.'

The fire popped. A bead of sweat ran from Armstrong's hairline to his eye and he pressed the corner of his napkin to his face, one motion, then set the napkin back across his knee.

He set down his fork. He looked at the major across the low-ceilinged room, across the intervening table with its abandoned cups, and held the man's gaze for three full seconds — not with anger, not with explanation, but with the flat, patient regard of a man watching a dog that has gotten into the larder and does not yet know it is about to be removed.

Then he picked up his fork and turned back to his plate.

The silence that followed had a weight to it. He heard the major's breathing — audible, ragged, the sound of someone who expected a response and received instead the ceiling pressing down. Two seconds. Four. The fire continued its indifferent business. Somewhere in the room a man coughed once into his fist.

The major did not sit down.

'I commanded a company at the ford,' he said. His voice had dropped, which made it carry further. 'I had forty-one men. We held our position for eleven minutes before the rockets drove us from the line. Eleven minutes, because there was no earthwork, no abatis, nothing but open ground and the word of our officers that the matter was in hand.' A pause. 'I left seven men on that ground. I should like to know what they died for.'

The room was very still. The pot behind the partition had stopped its striking.

Armstrong set down his fork a second time. He rose — not quickly, but with the economy of a man who has made a decision and intends its execution to be observed. He reached into his coat and produced a folded sheet, which he set on the table before him without opening it.

'Major.' His voice filled the room without effort. 'The fortification of Bladensburg ford was proposed to General Winder on the fourteenth of this month. General Winder declined it, on the grounds that earthworks would impede his capacity to manoeuvre. That decision was his to make. He was the officer charged by the President with the district's defence.' He left the folded sheet on the table — it was, in fact, a letter from his wife, but the major could not know that — and picked up his fork again. 'Your seven men died because their commanding general made a tactical error. I am sorry for them. I cannot answer for another man's errors.'

The silence shifted. The major's face had changed — not softened, but recalibrated, the grievance meeting an obstacle it had not anticipated.

He sat down.

Armstrong finished his mutton. He did not look up again. At the table by the door, Cutler had gone very still, his own fork suspended, watching for signals that Armstrong did not send. The folded letter remained on the table. Armstrong did not retrieve it until he called for the reckoning.

---

The candle had burned past its midpoint. Armstrong sat at the narrow table with paper before him and the quill in his right hand, and the room had the quality of rooms in which men compose their wills — still, close, the darkness beyond the single flame absolute.

He had meant to begin with the military situation. A clear accounting of positions: Winder's dispositions, the failure of the District militia to hold the first line, the absence of any reserve on the Georgetown road. Factual. Unassailable. The sort of memorandum a secretary of war prepared in the hours after a reversal, before the accounts of lesser men could calcify into received history.

He dipped the quill and wrote: *The dispositions at Bladensburg were established under the authority of Brigadier General Winder, acting upon his own judgment as commander of the Tenth Military District, and the Secretary of War was not—*

He stopped.

His own voice came back to him, with the clarity of things one wishes to unhear. The cabinet room. The maps. Monroe across the table with that expression he wore when he believed himself to be winning. *Baltimore is the object.* He had said it with the confidence of a man reporting established fact, not offering an assessment — had said it more than once, had said it in writing, had said it to Madison himself in terms that admitted no qualification. *The enemy will not expend four thousand veterans of the Peninsula upon a symbolic target when Baltimore offers a harbour, a fleet, and a commercial city worth the taking.*

The quill had not moved.

Below the window the night was quiet now, the militia company long since assembled and gone, their drums faded toward the south. Toward Washington. Toward Monroe's authority, expressed in signed orders, moving through the republic as though the office he held were a figure in a painting — present, observed, without function.

He wrote: *—was not in a position to override the tactical decisions of the officer charged by the President himself with the district's defence.*

He read it back. The sentence was sound. It would stand scrutiny. It was also, in the precise sense that would matter to a man examining the record two years hence, insufficient — because a careful reader would ask the question that followed, and every road that question opened led back to July. Back to the dispatches he had summarised and the intelligence he had weighed and the memoranda in which he had, with the full authority of his office, declared the capital not a target.

He drew a line through what he had written — not a single stroke but a series of close parallel lines, hatching the sentence until it was unreadable — and set the nib to a fresh position on the page.

*The inadequacy of the defences at Washington must be understood in light of Congress's persistent refusal to appropriate funds for the coastal fortifications recommended by this department in the spring session. Had the works requested been authorised and constructed, the question of militia dispositions at any particular ford would have been rendered secondary to—*

A cramp seized his writing hand without warning and he dropped the quill, his fingers splaying outward, the tendons along his palm suddenly rigid. He shook the hand once, twice. The quill had rolled to the table's edge and fallen to the floor. He pushed back the chair and crouched in the near-dark, feeling along the boards until his fingers found the shaft — cool, slightly gritty with the grime of the floorboards.

When he straightened and sat again, the candle steadied. He looked at what he had written. The sentence was longer than the one before it, more elaborate in its construction, and it suffered from the same deficiency: a careful reader would pursue the inquiry, and the inquiry led back to July. Every road led back to July.

He set the quill down deliberately, as one sets down a tool whose purpose has become unclear. He pressed his palms flat on the table and looked at the hatched lines, the crossed sentences, the small ruin of the page. Outside, the August dark pressed against the window — warm, carrying the smell of horses and the faint sweetness of something burning still, miles away, the capital making its slow return to the elements — and he understood, with the cold precision that had always been his particular gift, that he was not writing a memorandum. He was writing the first draft of a history, and the history had a problem he could not yet solve.

He picked up the quill. He would solve it in the next draft.

---

The church bell had been tolling since Armstrong first heard it, half a mile back, and it had not stopped. Not the measured cadence of the hours, not the call to worship — something else, a single note struck and restruck with the insistence of alarm, or grief, or both. He could not see the church yet through the tree-line, but the sound followed him down the road with the persistence of an unanswered charge.

Cutler rode a length behind. Neither of them had spoken since the crossroads.

The column appeared around a bend — sixty men, perhaps seventy, moving south in fair order, their muskets at the shoulder, their step regular. At their head a captain rode a grey horse, young, not yet thirty, his uniform dusty but intact, carrying himself with the precise bearing of a man who has clear orders and intends to execute them without incident.

Armstrong brought his horse across the road.

The captain drew up. His men halted behind him with a competence that Armstrong noted and did not acknowledge.

'You march south,' Armstrong said.

'Yes, sir.'

'On whose orders?'

The captain reached into his coat and produced a folded sheet. He extended it without ceremony, as a man produces a document he has already been asked for twice today and expects to be asked for again before nightfall. Armstrong took it.

He read it slowly. The bell continued its single, repeated note somewhere beyond the trees.

The orders directed one company of Maryland militia, under this captain's command, to proceed south toward Georgetown and place themselves under the authority of the Secretary of State for the purposes of maintaining order and facilitating the restoration of civil administration in the Tenth Military District. The orders were signed *James Monroe*. Below Monroe's signature, in a different hand and a different ink, was the countersignature of the President of the United States.

Armstrong read the document a second time. The leather of the reins creaked in his grip.

'By what authority,' he said, 'does the Secretary of State issue military commands?'

The captain pointed, without speaking, to the countersignature at the bottom of the page.

Armstrong looked at it. The President's hand was unmistakable — the compressed, authoritative script he had seen on a hundred documents, on orders and appointments and the letters that arrived at the War Department bearing the weight of executive sanction. There was nothing irregular about the signature itself. What was irregular was everything above it.

He refolded the paper along its original creases. He held it a moment, its edges sharp between his fingers, the paper still warm from the captain's coat. Then he opened it again.

'This order,' Armstrong said, 'directs you to the authority of the Secretary of State. It does not specify that you are removed from the authority of the War Department. You will therefore march south, as directed, and upon reaching Georgetown you will report your arrival to my office before presenting yourself to Mr. Monroe. You will inform my clerk that I have instructed you to do so. Do you understand me?'

The captain's face was careful. 'Sir, the orders direct me to—'

'I have read the orders, Captain. They do not preclude what I have described. You will comply.'

A long moment. The bell tolled. The captain's horse shifted its weight, and the men behind him stood in the road with the patient immobility of those accustomed to waiting for their betters to finish.

Then, from the rear of the column, a voice — not loud, but pitched with the clarity of a man who intends to be heard by everyone except the one he addresses: 'There rides the man who lost us our capital.'

The words moved through the column the way a stone moves through still water: a ripple, a settling, a stillness that was not the same stillness as before. The captain's jaw tightened. He did not turn. The men nearest Armstrong — four of them, five — looked at him directly for the first time, and what was in their faces was not hatred, which would have been manageable, but the flat, exhausted assessment of men deciding whether something is worth the trouble of contempt.

Armstrong did not move. He held the document. The bell tolled once, and again.

'Yes, sir,' the captain said at last, his voice entirely without inflection. He took the document back, pocketed it, and put his horse forward.

The column resumed its pace, moving past on both sides, boots finding the same rhythm they had kept before the interruption. Armstrong sat at the verge and did not watch them go. He was looking at the tree-line where the road curved south, and his face was composed, and his hands on the reins were steady, and the voice from the rear of the column — he could not have said which man, could not have identified the speaker if his tenure had depended on it, which perhaps it did — continued its work in the interior of his skull with the same insistence as the bell: one note, and then another, and then another.

Cutler brought his grey alongside.

'Do you think he will do it?' Cutler said. It was the first time he had offered an opinion in two days.

'No,' Armstrong said. He touched his heels to his horse and rode on. The bell was still tolling somewhere behind the trees, and the road south was Monroe's road now, and the words from the column's rear had not yet found the place in him where they would do their work — but they were looking for it, with the patience of things that have nowhere else to be.

---

The dispatch rider arrived at half past seven, mud-spattered to the knee and smelling of horse and hard miles, his satchel bearing the seal of the War Department's own clerical office — the seal of a man Armstrong had himself appointed and could, in other circumstances, have dismissed before breakfast.

He took the letter standing. The rider, a lean young man with a Virginia clerk's careful face, held himself in the doorway with the stillness of someone who has been told the reply will be ready before the hour is out and intends to hold the bearer to that promise.

Armstrong broke the seal. Four pages, close-written in Jacobs's meticulous hand.

*Washington, 26 August. The public meetings convened yesterday in Georgetown and this morning in Washington have produced formal resolutions calling for the removal of the Secretary of War from office. The Georgetown resolutions bear forty-one signatures. The Washington resolutions bear sixty-three. Among the signatories in both instances are named militia officers who have separately declared, in writing, that they will not serve under the Secretary's command. Major Fenton of the Twelfth Maryland appears on both lists.*

He read to the end without expression. Then he folded the letter along its original creases and set it on the desk beside the candle — which had burned to a finger's width, the tallow pooling in a small white lake around the base.

'Wait below,' he said to the rider. 'I will have a reply within the hour.'

The rider withdrew. Armstrong listened to his boots on the stairs, then to silence, then to the drums.

They had been beating for twenty minutes, the militia company in the street assembling by torchlight in the humid dark, their cadence steady and unremarkable — the sound of men preparing to march south under orders that made no reference to his office. He had watched them from the window earlier: two dozen men in varying states of uniform, a sergeant calling the roll with a lantern held at shoulder height, the whole enterprise conducted with the brisk self-sufficiency of men who have been told, in so many words, that the man in the room above them is not part of the arrangement.

He sat down. He drew a fresh sheet toward him — the innkeeper had produced better ink at last, a darker bottle with a proper iron-gall body — and set the nib to the page.

*His Excellency James Madison, President of the United States —*

He paused. The drums continued their steady work below. Through the floorboards came the faint vibration of boots on packed earth, the low exchange of the sergeant's commands, the creak and jingle of men taking up their equipment. The republic was reassembling itself in the street below, and it was doing so without him.

He began.

*The public meetings to which Your Excellency's attention may have been directed by interested parties represent not, as their organisers would have it, the spontaneous expression of a citizenry's just grievance, but rather the coordinated action of a faction long hostile to the vigorous prosecution of this war and to the office charged with that prosecution.*

His back seized — a hard contraction across the muscles above his left hip, the consequence of two days in the saddle and a chair built without reference to the human spine. He gripped the edge of the desk and breathed through his teeth until it passed, then lowered himself again and picked up the quill.

He wrote: *I have been made the scapegoat for the failures of others.*

Then he stopped. He read it back. It was true, and it was the one sentence in the letter that, if Madison read it, would ensure the letter was never answered. A man who wrote those words to the President of the United States had already lost the argument; the sentence announced its own defeat in the act of making its claim. He drew a line through it.

He dipped the quill and began again: *The question before Your Excellency is not whether errors were committed — errors attend every campaign, and the record of this war is not singular in that regard — but whether the head of the War Department is to be removed from office on the basis of resolutions signed by officers who refused their duty at Bladensburg ford and now seek, in the court of public opinion, the vindication they could not achieve in the field.*

Better. He read it twice. The sentence required an answer, and requiring an answer was the only purchase remaining to him.

The candle guttered in a draft from somewhere — a gap in the window frame, a crack in the plaster, the building itself exhaling the day's heat. In the street, someone shouted an order, and the drumming shifted tempo — the signal for column formation, for the march. The republic was reconstituting its defences in the dark, and the man whose office bore the word *War* upon its title was sitting in a rented room above a dry-goods store, his letter half-written, his rider waiting below.

He had perhaps forty minutes before the road south became uncertain.

He wrote faster. The quill scratched across the page, the iron-gall ink laying down its dark, authoritative line, and he wrote with the full force of his conviction — which was genuine, which had always been genuine, which was the thing that made him formidable and the thing that had brought him here. He wrote as a man writes who understands that the page before him is the only ground on which he can still manoeuvre — that the road to Washington belongs to Monroe now, that the militia companies march under Monroe's hand, that the public meetings have already inscribed his removal in sixty-three signatures and wait only for Madison's pen to make it official. He wrote as though three pages of iron-gall argument, sealed with the authority of an office they intended to strip from him, could arrive in Madison's hands before Monroe's account had time to harden into fact.

The drums faded. The street went quiet. Through the floorboards came the last tremor of departing feet, then nothing — only the rider's horse shifting against its post in the dark below, iron on stone, patient and impatient at once.

The voice from the column's rear was still in his skull — *there rides the man who lost us our capital* — and he wrote against it, sentence by sentence, each one a stone laid against the coming flood. He did not examine the voice. He did not permit himself to examine it. He had perhaps thirty minutes, and the seal of an office that was still, for thirty minutes more, his own.

Outside, a second rider turned into the inn-yard — he heard the hooves, the abrupt halt, a voice calling up to the window with an urgency that the hour itself confirmed. Not Jacobs's man. Someone else. Someone who had ridden hard from a direction he had not anticipated, bearing news he had not sent for.

He did not look up. He finished the sentence. But his hand, moving across the page, had slowed by a fraction — the fraction of a man who has heard, beneath the argument he is making, the sound of the argument already ending.
