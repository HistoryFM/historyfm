# Chapter Three: The Appointment

## Scene I

The dispatches lay across the writing desk in the order Madison had imposed upon them—not by date, not by theatre, but by a private taxonomy of alarm, the most troubling uppermost, the merely troubling beneath. He had been working since two o'clock. The afternoon light through the south window had gone from white to amber without his noticing, and the room, which faced the grounds and caught whatever breeze the grounds admitted, had ceased to catch any. The air pressed against his face, unmoving, warm as breath.

He moved his spectacles to the left of the nearest dispatch, nudged them a fraction further, until they lay parallel to the paper's edge. He did not put them on.

The knock came at half past four.

Armstrong entered without waiting—a habit Madison had long ceased to mark as discourtesy, having understood that Armstrong's relationship to a closed door was the same as his relationship to a subordinate clause: he acknowledged its existence and passed through it without slowing. He wore his coat despite the heat, which Madison had also come to read as a form of argument.

'Mr. Secretary.' Madison did not rise. 'Be seated, if you will.'

Armstrong settled into the chair across the desk with the unhurried economy of a large man who has learned to conserve himself. His gaze swept the papers briefly—the dispatch from Barney, which Madison had positioned so its first lines were legible at that distance—and returned to Madison's face without comment.

'The district requires a commander,' Madison said. 'I should like to know, before another week passes, who that commander is to be.'

'I have given the question considerable attention.' Armstrong's voice was even, the voice of a man reporting on a matter already resolved. 'The district, as currently constituted, encompasses Maryland, the District, and portions of Virginia. The cooperation of Maryland's executive is not incidental to its functioning—it is prerequisite to it. Without the militia call-ups the governor can authorize, the district command is a title without an army beneath it.'

Madison waited.

Armstrong reached into his inner coat pocket and produced a letter, folded once, its seal already broken. He set it on the edge of the desk—not within Madison's reach, but visible. 'Governor Winder has communicated his willingness to cooperate in the matter of militia call-ups, contingent upon the district commander being a man in whom his government reposes confidence. His precise language.' He tapped the letter once with one finger. 'The governor names his nephew.'

The room's heat had settled into Madison's collar and the back of his hands. He did not look at the letter.

'Brigadier General William Winder,' Armstrong continued. 'A competent officer. Not without experience. He served creditably in the northern theatre—'

A sharp twinge drove up through Madison's left foot and he shifted his weight in the chair. He pressed his heel to the floor and found his breath.

'—and his connexion to the governor's office provides the single element the district cannot otherwise obtain, which is the assured cooperation of Maryland's Federalist administration in a time when that cooperation cannot be compelled.'

Madison took up his spectacles from the desk. He set them down again, and then he looked directly at Armstrong for the first time since the man had entered the room.

'You present this,' he said, 'as a political argument.'

'It is a political argument. It is also the operative argument, in the present circumstance.'

'Then I will require a second argument.' Madison's voice did not rise. 'Before I place any man in command of the district that lies between this city and whatever the British intend for it, I will require your written assessment of his military qualifications—separate from the political case, separate from the question of his uncle's cooperation, committed to paper and to your professional judgment. If General Winder is the right man for this command, that case can be made on its merits and I will read it with the attention it deserves.' He paused. 'If it cannot be made on its merits, then the governor's letter changes nothing.'

'Mr. President.' Armstrong's tone did not shift, but he leaned forward a fraction—a small, deliberate motion. 'The British squadron has been in the bay since the fourth of July. Every day this district passes without a lawful commander is a day its militia cannot be called. If the enemy lands a body of men at Benedict or the Patuxent mouth tomorrow morning, there is no officer empowered to order a single company into the field. Not one.' He let the silence hold for a beat. 'I would not wish the President to find, when the question becomes urgent, that the machinery of defense was left unengaged because the Secretary's memorandum was not yet to his satisfaction.'

The words were courteous. Their meaning was not.

Madison reached across the desk and drew the governor's letter toward him. The broken seal caught the lamplight. He read the first line, set it down, and placed his spectacles upon it.

'I am aware of the calendar, Mr. Secretary. I am also aware that an officer appointed in haste and found wanting in the field will cost this republic more than a week's delay. If the enemy lands at Benedict tomorrow, the want of earthworks at Bladensburg will matter a good deal more than the want of a signature on this paper.' He held Armstrong's gaze. 'I will have your written assessment before I sign any appointment. That is my condition.'

Armstrong's expression did not shift. He retrieved the letter from beneath the spectacles and returned it to his coat pocket. 'As you wish, Mr. President. I will have the assessment to you before the week is out.'

He rose.

From the street below came the sound of iron wheels on unpaved ground—a wagon, or a gun carriage, Madison could not tell which—and then silence again.

He adjusted the spectacles one final time. Parallel. Precise.

Armstrong's footsteps crossed the floor, and the door closed behind him.

Madison sat for a moment without moving. Then he drew Barney's dispatch from beneath the others—the one he had positioned for Armstrong to see—and read it again from the beginning, as though the words might have changed since morning. They had not. He folded it and returned it to the stack, the most troubling uppermost, and reached for his pen.

## Scene II

The East Room was bright enough to hurt. Forty candles in the twin chandeliers, their light thrown back from the mirrors in doubled, wavering columns, and beneath them some sixty persons in their summer finery, moving and conversing with the particular industry of people who understood that to be seen here, on a Wednesday evening in July, was itself a form of political statement.

Madison had stationed himself near the north window with a glass of Madeira he did not intend to drink, and he had been watching Dolley for the better part of twenty minutes.

She was near the east wall, beside the refreshment table, speaking with a young naval officer whose name he did not immediately place—a lieutenant, by his uniform, slight and sun-darkened, with the weathered economy of someone who had spent considerable time at sea and somewhat less in drawing rooms. Her turban caught the candlelight and held it. Her fan was closed in her right hand, perfectly still, and she was nodding with the quality of attention Madison had observed in her for twenty years and never quite ceased to find remarkable—the ability to make a man feel that the room had contracted to encompass only himself and her, that what he was saying was the most important intelligence she had received in a fortnight.

The windows stood open to the July evening. Through them came the sound of a carriage on the gravel below, the distant bark of a dog somewhere toward the Navy Yard, and beneath both the thick marshy exhalation of the Tiber canal—a smell that arrived in waves, heavier when the air moved, which it did now and then without cooling anything.

He set his glass on the window ledge, excused himself from the congressman at his elbow with a word and a half-turn, and moved along the wall toward the east. Not directly—the room required indirection; a president crossing a room with purpose drew every eye—but by the oblique route, pausing once to acknowledge a Virginia planter whose name he supplied from memory, once more to accept a word from the French chargé, until he had closed the distance to perhaps twelve feet and could hear, beneath the general conversation, the lieutenant's voice.

The man was speaking of the transports—not *toward* the bay mouth, Madison caught, but *standing upriver*—and then Dolley asked a question in the warm, mildly curious voice she employed when she was not mildly curious at all, and the lieutenant answered with the candor of an officer who had been waiting weeks for someone to ask him the right question.

Madison went still. He kept his face composed toward the middle distance, as though studying the far mirror, but every faculty of attention was directed at the east wall.

The stench from the canal thickened abruptly through the nearest window. He did not turn away. He heard: *not garrison troops, ma'am, not the usual colonial complement, but veterans of the Peninsula*—and then a count of sail that he fixed in his mind with the same cold precision he had once applied to the enumeration of votes in the Virginia ratifying convention—*twenty transports confirmed, perhaps more standing off*—and then the number itself, stated with the flat certainty of an officer who had looked through a glass and written down what he counted: *four thousand, at the lowest reckoning.*

Four thousand. Peninsula men. The words settled into him like cold water finding its level.

Armstrong's assessments for six weeks had been qualified, conditional, hedged with procedural uncertainty—*movements consistent with resupply operations, no design upon any fixed objective thus far confirmed*—while this lieutenant, speaking to the President's wife over the sound of the Tiber canal, was describing a landing force sufficient to burn the city to its foundations.

'Mr. President.'

Senator Hanson of Maryland had come up on his left during the moment his attention had been wholly elsewhere—a Federalist, a man whose studied affability Madison had long understood as the outer finish on a very hard object. He stood now at a comfortable distance, his expression composed into something that might have been sociable concern.

'I find myself remarking,' Hanson said, 'that the capital presents a remarkably open aspect for a city said to be under some degree of threat. No earthworks, no redoubts visible from the road. I drove up from Georgetown this afternoon and noted nothing that would inconvenience even a modest incursion.' He paused. 'The citizens, I am told, have some anxiety on the point. As, I confess, do certain of my colleagues in the Senate, who may feel compelled to raise the matter formally if the administration's preparations remain—' he selected the word with care '—invisible.'

Every conversation within eight feet had slowed. Not stopped—these were practiced people—but slowed, their participants attending to two things simultaneously in the manner long cultivated at gatherings where the President was present.

Madison turned to face Hanson fully. A formal Senate inquiry, opened before the session was a week old, would compel public testimony from the Secretary of War—testimony that officer could shape to his own purposes—and would hand Hanson's faction the instrument they required to make the capital's vulnerability the President's personal failure. If Madison answered vaguely, Hanson would have his pretext. If he answered with specifics, he would be committing to preparations that did not yet exist.

He chose the narrower ground—and as he chose it, he felt the trap's teeth.

'The organization of the district's defenses is a matter I have under active consideration. I expect to name a commander before the week is out—I would not wish to characterize the arrangements prematurely, before they are complete, nor would I wish the Senator to carry an incomplete account back to colleagues who deserve better than conjecture.'

Hanson received this. His expression did not change, but something behind it performed a brief, quiet calculation. 'That is welcome news, Mr. President. I mention the matter only because the session resumes in a fortnight, and the mood of the chamber—on both sides, I should say—is not, at present, a patient one.'

A fortnight. The same word the lieutenant had given the room, by indirection, not three minutes past. Madison had just committed himself to a timeline he could not meet without signing Winder's commission—the commission he had, four hours earlier, refused to sign without Armstrong's memorandum. Armstrong's memorandum, which was not due before the week was out. Hanson had walked him onto ground from which there was only one exit, and the faint, courteous inclination of the Senator's head said so without troubling to say it aloud.

'You are very good to apprise me of the chamber's temper, Senator.' Madison inclined his head. 'You will excuse me. I believe Mrs. Madison requires a word.'

He crossed the room directly now—the social geometry no longer mattered. The lieutenant was still speaking, and Dolley had not looked up, had not looked up because she knew he was coming and did not wish the lieutenant to become conscious of being observed. Madison took his position slightly behind her shoulder, close enough to hear, and the lieutenant—unaware that the second set of ears now attending him belonged to the President of the United States—continued to describe, with the earnest precision of an officer grateful to be believed, the composition and probable object of the force assembling in the Chesapeake.

Madison listened. He did not speak. Dolley's questions did the work, and the lieutenant's answers came freely, and each one was a stone laid on the pile that Armstrong had left unmortared.

When the lieutenant paused to accept a glass from a passing servant, Madison leaned slightly toward Dolley's ear. 'After the guests have thinned,' he said, very quietly, 'I should like a word with your lieutenant.'

She did not turn. Her fan remained closed. 'He has a duplicate of Barney's letter,' she said, at the same volume. 'He has been carrying it for a fortnight, not knowing to whom to give it.'

Madison straightened. He took up a fresh glass from the nearest tray—Madeira again, warm, the same temperature as the room—and held it without drinking, and waited for the evening to end.

## Scene III

The tallow candle on the dressing table had burned past its midpoint. Its flame moved with each pass through the room—Dolley crossing from the window to the mirror, Madison on the edge of the bed working at his shoes—so that the shadows on the wallpaper rose and fell in a slow, unsteady rhythm.

She drew the pin from her turban and set it on the table. The turban followed, placed with care, and then the earrings, each one set down with a small click against the wood. Beneath them, faintly, the distant scrape of servants moving furniture in the East Room below.

'I want to know how long,' Madison said.

She did not turn from the mirror. 'How long.'

'You have been at this some weeks, I think. Perhaps longer.'

She reached for her dressing gown, drew it around her shoulders, and then turned to face him. The candlelight caught one side of her face and left the other in shadow.

'The lieutenant first spoke to me at the Cabell dinner,' she said. 'The twenty-second of June. Before that, Madame Sérurier had mentioned—in what she took to be passing conversation—that her cousin in Hamilton had written of unusual troop movements. Men held past their expected discharge. Stores purchased beyond what any garrison requires.'

'And Carver.'

She paused. 'You heard more of that conversation than I knew.'

'I heard enough.' He worked his left shoe loose and set it on the floor. The right came free with a twinge of white, specific pain that made him draw a short breath through his nose. Dolley paused. Three seconds, perhaps four. The candle flame wavered in a draught from the window, and the smell of warm tallow thickened in the close air before settling again.

'Carver's factor in Bermuda was turned away from three of his usual suppliers. The army had purchased out the stores.' She folded her hands over the front of her dressing gown. 'That is not a raiding complement, James. That is an army going somewhere it means to stay.'

He set the shoe beside the first.

'Armstrong tells me Baltimore.'

'Armstrong told Barney nothing at all.' Her voice did not rise, but something in it sharpened—the quality of a woman who has been patient long enough. 'Three weeks past, Barney wrote to the department—his own hand, his own count—and described what he had observed in the bay. Twenty sail of transports. Peninsula veterans. He asked for earthworks at the Bladensburg crossing and reinforcement of the flotilla.' She paused. 'The department returned no reply.'

Madison looked up. 'Say that again.'

'No reply, James. Three weeks.' She took a step toward him. 'If the British land at Benedict and march before Winder has mustered so much as a single company of militia, they will reach this house in two days. Not the capital in the abstract. This house. These rooms. And when the Senate convenes in the ashes, your Secretary of War will produce his memorandum showing he advised you the appointment was political, and you will bear the whole of it alone.'

The candle guttered in a draught from the window. From somewhere below on the street a horse stamped once on the cobbles and was still.

Madison looked at his hands. 'I gave Hanson my word tonight. A commander named within the week. Which means I must sign Winder's commission before Armstrong delivers the memorandum I required of him—which means his condition is met and mine is not.' He was quiet for a moment. 'That is the consequence Armstrong has arranged, whether by design or by the ordinary negligence of a man who believes the threat is elsewhere.'

'Then you must not allow him to arrange it.'

'He never mentioned Barney's letter,' Madison said.

'No. He did not.'

The servants below had finished their work. The house had gone quiet. Madison looked at his shoes on the floor beside the bed—two small dark shapes in the candlelight, placed with the habitual precision of a man who, having lost purchase on larger things, imposes order on what remains within reach.

'I will approve the commission tonight. Winder, whatever his limitations, is a commander in law, and without one the district cannot call its militia. That much cannot wait another sunrise.' He looked up at her. 'But I will require of Armstrong, in writing and specifically, that he address the Bladensburg question. The earthworks. The reinforcements Barney requested. I will put the questions as my own—framed from the dispatches I have seen, not from yours.'

She held his gaze. 'And my sources.'

'Remain yours.'

A beat of silence. Then: 'Do you trust him to answer honestly, even now?'

Madison considered the question without the comfort of a quick answer. The candle flame made its small adjustments to the air. He could hear Dolley's breathing, and the faint creak of the house settling against the summer heat, and nothing else.

'I trust him to answer in writing,' he said at last. 'That is sufficient for my present purpose.'

She crossed to the dressing table and pinched out the candle with two fingers. The room went dark except for the faint grey rectangle of the window, and the smell of extinguished tallow hung in the air—sharp, animal, briefly sharp—before the night absorbed it.

## Scene IV

The whale-oil lamp had burned to a small, steady flame. Its amber circle fell across the desk and stopped; beyond that margin the study was dark—bookshelves mere shapes, the window a grey oblong against a moonless sky. The commission lay before him in a clerk's neat hand, every loop correct, every flourish disciplined, the name *William Henry Winder* rendered with a confidence the document's author was not required to feel.

Madison sat in his dressing gown and did not touch it.

The mantel clock ticked. He had not attended to it for years—it was part of the room's furniture, as unobtrusive as the wainscoting—but in the silence after the house had emptied of servants and guests and the last footfall had died on the stair, the mechanism asserted itself. Each beat distinct. Each one a second the Bladensburg road lay open and unguarded.

He took up Armstrong's memorandum—four pages in the Secretary's own hand, submitted that afternoon with the patience of a man who had already won—and turned to the passage he had read four times since the reception. *General Winder's political utility in the present circumstance recommends itself with a force that his military record, while not without competence, does not independently supply.* Armstrong had buried the caveat not because he feared Madison would miss it but because he knew Madison would find it. Four pages of elegant construction, and what they said, in language only slightly less direct than candour, was: *I have told you he is insufficient.* What they did not say—anywhere in those four pages—was what ought to be done about that insufficiency. If the district fell, the memorandum would be produced. It would show that the Secretary had discharged his obligation. The commission would be the President's.

He set it aside and spread Barney's letter flat on the desk—the duplicate the lieutenant had carried for a fortnight, waiting for someone to receive it. Barney's hand was blunt, practical, a sailor's hand unaccustomed to petition: *I have requested of the Department earthworks at Bladensburg and reinforcement of the flotilla. I have received no answer. I write to you, sir, because I do not know to whom else I may write.*

He read the last sentence twice.

He placed the two documents side by side and looked at them. The Secretary's four pages of measured evasion. The sailor's single page of plain alarm. The distance between them was the distance between the republic as it was governed and the republic as it was threatened, and that distance was not abstract—it was measured in days, and the days were running.

There was a question that would not leave him—one he had put to the Constitutional Convention in 1787 with a different purpose entirely. He had argued, in Philadelphia, that a republic could not survive executive inertia; that the great danger of confederate government was precisely the danger of a sovereign who watched while the machinery of crisis ground on without him, who deferred to subordinates and called it principle. He had written it. He had argued it before men who were still alive to remember. He could feel the weight of that argument now, pressing against the inside of his chest like a stone placed there very carefully and left.

He picked up the quill and trimmed it with the penknife. The scrape of the blade was small and distinct in the quiet room. The lamp flame shivered at the motion and steadied.

On the final page of Armstrong's memorandum, in a parenthetical note occupying less than three lines, the Secretary had referred to the estate at *Montpellier*—the French rendering, the superfluous *l*, a clerk's error or the Secretary's own carelessness. *Montpellier.* As though the red clay of Orange County, the mulberry trees his father had planted, the fields he had ridden since before he could name what he was riding through—as though all of it were merely a French word with a surplus consonant, a place that could be misspelled without consequence.

Madison dipped the quill. He held it above the signature line, the ink trembling at the tip in the lamplight—

He signed. One quick stroke. The pen set down immediately.

He blotted it. He folded the document and wrote on the verso, in his own hand: *For dispatch at first light.* He set it to the left of the lamp.

Then he drew a fresh sheet of plain folio—not the official stationery, not the cream vellum bearing the executive seal—and began to write without pausing for revision, because revision was a form of hope and what he was doing now required no hope, only accuracy. He wrote that the commission had been made against the President's better judgment. He wrote that the grounds advanced by the War Department were of a political character that the President did not regard as sufficient warrant for the selection of a military officer in a district under imminent threat. He wrote that the President, at the time of this decision, possessed intelligence—including a communication from Commodore Barney, which the War Department had received and to which it had returned no reply—sufficient to occasion the gravest concern regarding the composition and probable object of the British force in the Chesapeake.

He did not sign it. He did not address it. He folded it once and placed it in the lower drawer, beneath the other correspondence, where it would keep.

Then he took up the official stationery—the cream vellum—and wrote in a hand steadier than the hour warranted a direct order to the Secretary of War: that Commodore Barney's request for earthworks at the Bladensburg crossing was to be answered within forty-eight hours, that the answer was to specify what force would be allocated and by what date, and that the President expected the reply on his desk before the close of business on Friday. He signed it. He sealed it. He set it beside the commission, where the same courier would carry both.

The commission and the order lay side by side in the lamplight—one to install an officer Madison did not trust, the other to compel a cabinet minister he could not yet afford to dismiss. And in the lower drawer, the unsigned memorandum lay waiting: the President's private record that he had seen the danger, named it, and acted within the machinery available to him.

He recapped the inkwell and replaced the quill in its stand. The lamp oil was nearly spent; he could smell the change in the flame, a faint acrid note beneath the animal warmth, the smell of a thing almost consumed. He did not reach for a fresh supply.

What he had done tonight was not enough. He had done it anyway. That was the whole of it—the whole of what remained when principle met the calendar and the calendar won.

But the calendar, he now understood, was not Armstrong's instrument alone. Hanson had moved it this evening with a senator's easy precision, and Dolley had been working against it for weeks through means that no dispatch from the War Department had matched. The commission would go out at first light. Armstrong would receive his orders. And somewhere in the Chesapeake, in the darkness beyond the reach of any lamp Madison could light, four thousand Peninsula veterans were already at anchor, waiting for a tide that owed nothing to the republic's deliberations.

The lamp guttered once, flared briefly, and went out.
