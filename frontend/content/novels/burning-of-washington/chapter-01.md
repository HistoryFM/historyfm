# Chapter One: The Arithmetic of Freed Regiments

The dispatches had arrived in a leather satchel, the brass clasp gone green at the hinge, and Madison had carried it upstairs himself rather than wait for Jennings. He set it on the desk beside the letter to Jefferson — begun on Tuesday, its argument suspended at the second sentence — and left it unopened for perhaps a quarter of an hour while he stood at the window watching the afternoon light flatten across the marshland below. The Potomac was invisible from here. Only the insects were audible, their chorus rising and falling with the heat, which had not broken in three days and showed no disposition to do so.

He opened the case.

Four documents. He read them in the order they had been placed, which was not, he suspected, the order of their significance.

The first was a departmental précis from the War Office, prepared in Armstrong's staff's hand — clerkly penmanship whose very neatness Madison had come to read as a form of concealment. The document acknowledged that certain formations of the British Peninsular army had been released from European service following Bonaparte's capitulation in April and noted, in language whose studied imprecision he recognised as deliberate, that *some portion* of these forces might be directed toward North American operations. Which formations. What portion. The document did not say.

The second dispatch had arrived not through the War Office's channels but through his wife's hands — a report from Captain Gordon, of the Chesapeake intelligence network. Gordon wrote without ornament: specific, unadorned, the prose of a man who had counted ships and set down what he counted. The First Division of Foot. The Fourth. The Twenty-first Regiment, which had served at Vittoria and at the crossing of the Bidassoa. Numbers beside each name. Transports. Bermuda.

Madison placed the two documents side by side.

The précis did not mention the First Division. It did not mention the Fourth. It did not mention the Twenty-first.

He read the précis again, with the particular attention he reserved for documents he suspected of artful incompleteness. A staff officer preparing a summary might reasonably confine himself to confirmed intelligence, might regard Gordon's account as insufficiently verified for formal inclusion. All of this was possible. A man of Armstrong's experience would say precisely that, and say it with the quiet authority of a soldier addressing a civilian, and the civilian would have no clean answer.

He set both sheets down.

The room was close and damp, the air carrying the faint mildew of old plaster and the sharper bite of ink drying on the blotter. He rose, crossed to the window, and stood there with his palms flat against the sill — feeling the grit of the wood, the faint give of the frame in the heat — before returning to the desk.

The third dispatch was from a Baltimore merchant whose name appeared on no official roster but whose information had proven more reliable than half the reports arriving through properly constituted channels. The man wrote in a compressed, practical hand about unusual activity in the lower Chesapeake — British vessels taking soundings, landing parties observed along the western shore, a pattern of movement inconsistent with the coastal raids that had characterised Cockburn's operations through the previous season.

The fourth dispatch he read twice.

He closed his eyes.

What the accounting produced, when he had worked through it with the cold deliberation he brought to the most unwelcome calculations, was this: the forces Armstrong's précis had characterised as a *potential* augmentation to British strength in North America were not potential. They were assembled. They were named. They were in Bermuda, which was not New Orleans or Mobile or any of the other targets Armstrong's strategic reasoning had been directing the cabinet's attention toward for the better part of two months.

He reached for the bell-pull.

He did not ring it.

The question — and he knew it for a question he had asked before, in other forms, in the Virginia ratifying convention and in the long debates over the structure of the executive that had preceded the Constitution's adoption — was whether a president who summoned his Secretary of War for a private accounting on the basis of intelligence the Secretary had not himself provided was exercising legitimate executive oversight or something that, pursued to its logical end, became something else entirely. The power to demand accounting was also the power to intimidate. The power to intimidate was the power to control. And the power to control, concentrated in the executive, was the thing the Constitution had been designed to prevent.

He withdrew his hand.

He sat down, and the chair's familiar creak subsided into silence, and he remained there long enough for the light to shift perceptibly on the wall before him, the afternoon dying by degrees.

The cabinet meeting was scheduled for the following morning. Armstrong would be there. Monroe would be there. He could raise the discrepancy then, in the presence of the full cabinet, framed as a general inquiry about the completeness of the intelligence picture — not a confrontation, not a private accounting, but a question publicly posed, to which Armstrong would be required to give a public answer.

He took up Gordon's report and placed it beneath the War Office précis, so that the regimental designations were concealed but still accessible. He would paraphrase. He would observe what Armstrong did with the paraphrase. And if Armstrong's answer was what he expected — smooth, geometric, arranged to point south toward coasts that were not Washington — then he would know something he did not yet know with certainty.

Outside, the insects continued their chorus, and the heat pressed against the panes like a hand that would not be refused, and the letter to Jefferson waited on the desk, its argument unfinished, its leisure not yet come.

---

Armstrong had taken the chair by the window.

It was the one with the carved back and the wider seat, positioned where the morning sun came through the shutters in pale bars and fell across the table's dark wood. By unspoken arrangement governing every cabinet meeting Madison could recall, that chair was his — not by proclamation, not by any rule a man could point to, but by the simple fact of long occupation. Armstrong sat in it with his papers spread before him and did not look up when Madison entered.

Madison took the chair to his left, which was Monroe's, and Monroe, already standing near the map board, moved without comment to the far end of the table. The clerk by the door dipped his pen. The scratch of it filled the room.

'Mr. Secretary,' Madison said.

'Mr. President.' Armstrong set down the paper he had been reading — laid it flat with the care of a man concluding one matter before consenting to address another. 'I have prepared some observations on the intelligence that has been circulating.'

'I should be glad to hear them.'

Armstrong rose and moved to the map board with the ease of long habit. The map he unrolled was large, hand-drawn on good linen, the coastline of North America rendered in careful ink from the Saint Lawrence south to the Gulf. Someone in the department — some anonymous draughtsman who had worked by candlelight — had marked the principal British naval stations with small crosses: Halifax, Bermuda, Nassau.

'The assembly at Bermuda,' Armstrong said, 'is a matter of record.' He touched the cross at that island with one finger. 'What the fragmentary reports from junior officers of no strategic consequence have failed to observe is what the assembly *means*.' He traced a line south and west. 'Bermuda is equidistant from three theatres. The Chesapeake. The Carolina coast. And the Gulf approaches to New Orleans and Mobile.' He paused. 'A commander positioned at Bermuda has not committed himself. The question is not where the British *are* — it is where their logistical calculus *compels* them to go. And that calculus,' he said, 'points south.'

The clerk's pen moved steadily. Monroe shifted in his chair at the far end of the table.

'The regiments named in the intelligence,' Madison said. 'The Eighty-Fifth Foot. The Fourth. The Twenty-First.' He kept his voice level. 'Has the department independent confirmation of their presence in those waters?'

A brief silence. Armstrong's hand rested on the map.

'We have confirmation of British reinforcement of the North American command.' The smoothness of it was itself a form of precision. 'The specific enumeration of formations, as reported through the channel you describe, is the product of observation at a distance by men untrained in military identification. The Twenty-First Fusiliers last drew rations in Bordeaux. Their presence in the western Atlantic would require a transatlantic crossing of some weeks, and no reliable report places them west of the Azores.'

'And yet the report places them there.' Madison put both hands flat on the table. 'Specifically. By designation.'

'The report places *someone* there. The designation is an inference by a naval officer who has not served in a theatre where he might reliably distinguish one regiment's facings from another's.'

The pen scratched on.

'It is to be observed,' — and here Madison set down his pen, the small sound of it carrying in the room's silence — 'that the question of which regiment occupies a given anchorage is somewhat less consequential than the question of how many regiments occupy it, and what equipment accompanies them. I will require a written assessment of the present state of Chesapeake defenses — the forces currently constituted, the dispositions, the artillery in position at the principal approaches. Within the week.'

Armstrong turned from the map. His voice, when he spoke, had dropped half a register — not in anger, but in the manner of a man choosing, with great deliberateness, to remain composed.

'The Chesapeake defenses are a matter for the commander of the district, Mr. President, not for a general assessment of the kind you describe. General Winder is in possession of the relevant facts and has the authority to —'

'The assessment will be prepared by your department and delivered to me.' Madison did not raise his voice. 'Within the week.'

A moment in which nothing moved except the slow bars of light across the table.

'As you wish,' Armstrong said.

He returned to the window chair, gathered his papers, and sat. The map remained on the board, its geometry unchanged, the line from Bermuda pointing south and west toward a coast that was not Washington.

Monroe caught Madison's eye as the meeting moved to other business. He said nothing. Neither did Madison. But the glance held for a beat longer than courtesy required, and in it was the thing neither man would say aloud: that Armstrong had answered a question Madison had not quite asked, and had not answered the one he had.

---

The corridor outside the cabinet room smelled of stale tobacco and map-paper dust, and Madison carried that smell with him up the stairs and along the passage until Dolley's sitting room replaced it with something altogether different — rosewater, and the sharper note of fresh ink, and beneath both the faint brine of letters that had travelled some distance before arriving in this bright, well-furnished space.

Dolley sat at her writing desk near the window, a sheaf of correspondence in her lap, and she looked up when he entered with an attention that was not surprise. The French wallpaper behind her caught the late-morning light in shades of pale green and ivory, and the room felt, after the shuttered cabinet below, almost immoderately alive — warm in a way that had nothing to do with the season.

He stood in the doorway a moment longer than he needed to. Twenty-five years of marriage, and he still felt, entering this room, something that was not quite relief and not quite gratitude but partook of both — the particular ease of a man who has spent the morning in armour and may now, briefly, set it down.

He would not permit himself that ease today.

'The meeting concluded early,' he said.

'I know.' She set the correspondence on the desk. 'Sukey told me General Armstrong's carriage was still in the yard when you came upstairs.'

He moved to the window and stood there looking out at the grounds and the distant grey-green line of the river.

'The letter you forwarded,' he said. 'From Captain Gordon's network. I should like to know how it came to you.'

'It came through a friend. A lady in Norfolk whose husband is frequently in the bay.'

'And the letter itself — was it addressed to you?'

'It was addressed to no one in particular.'

He turned. She met his gaze without difficulty, and there was in her expression neither guilt nor apology — only the precise, warm attention she brought to every difficult conversation, which was itself, he had learned over many years, a form of armour not so different from his own.

'There is a second letter,' he said.

She opened the desk drawer without hesitation and produced a folded sheet, the paper slightly stiff. When she spread it open, the smell of the sea entered the room — salt and something tarry beneath, the ghost of a long journey, stronger than the rosewater, stronger than the ink. She read without preamble.

The passage was brief. Scaling ladders. Congreve rocket frames. The enumeration of transport vessels in the harbour at Bermuda sufficient, the writer estimated, for five thousand men with their equipment and ordnance.

He said nothing until she had finished.

'The source,' he said.

'A person whose husband has reason to know what ships carry.'

'Dolley—'

'James.' Her voice did not rise, did not sharpen. 'The source is safe. I will not say more than that. If I name names in this room, I cannot answer for what becomes of them.'

A servant entered carrying a tea tray. Dolley received her with the full warmth of her attention — directing the placement of the cups, asking after the girl's mother, her voice easy and unhurried — and Madison waited at the window with his back to the room, listening to the small domestic sounds of cups and saucers, the ordinary music of the house going on around the thing they were discussing, which was not ordinary at all. The girl withdrew. The door closed.

He turned from the window.

'Scaling ladders,' he said, 'are not the equipment of a coastal raid.'

'No.' She folded the letter and held it out. 'They are not.'

He took it. The paper was cool against his fingers, faintly rough where the salt air had stiffened the grain, and he held it a moment without looking at it before placing it in his coat.

'If you receive further communications of this nature,' he said, 'I would ask that you forward them to me through a private channel. Not the official post. Not through Armstrong's office.'

She looked at him steadily.

'I have been doing so,' she said.

'I know.' He buttoned his coat. 'I am asking you to continue.'

She picked up her correspondence and returned her attention to it. He stood in the bright room for another moment, watching her — the small precise movements of her hands among the letters, the way she held her pen, the absolute composure of a woman who understood exactly what she had done and had no intention of undoing it — before he went back into the passage and the smell of rosewater gave way to dust.

---

The candles had burned a third of their length by the time Madison drew fresh paper toward him. The fire — unnecessary, absurd in this heat, but Sukey had laid it and he had not the will to order it undone — threw copper light across the ceiling in patterns that shifted when the window admitted the faintest movement of air from the direction of the river. Somewhere below, a door closed. Footsteps crossed the hall and receded. Then the house was quiet, and the only sounds were the fire's low breath and the dry scratch of a moth beating against the window glass, drawn by the candle it could not reach.

He dipped the pen and wrote: *My dear Monroe —*

His hand stopped.

Not from any physical cause. What arrested him was a voice — his own voice, as he had used it in the Virginia ratifying convention of 1788, before men who needed persuading that the executive power in the new instrument would not consume the republic it was designed to sustain. *The accumulation of intelligence through channels answerable only to the person of the executive, and not to the cabinet he is constitutionally obliged to consult —* He had said something very near to that. He had meant it. He had meant it when he spoke it and he had meant it for twenty-six years since, through every administration including his own.

The fire shifted. A log settled with a soft report.

He looked at what he had written: three words and a dash.

The republic's intelligence of its own peril was passing through a single man who had decided, on grounds he had not disclosed to the president he served, that the peril lay elsewhere. That was the sum of it. The dispatches were clear; the assembly in those waters was clear; the omissions in Armstrong's summary were too precise, too convenient, too perfectly aligned with the conclusion Armstrong had already reached and was determined not to revise. And the second letter — with its scaling apparatus and its Congreve frames and its five thousand men — sat now in Madison's coat pocket alongside the letter he had not yet finished writing, and those two documents together constituted a picture that Armstrong's map, for all its elegant logistical lines running south toward Mobile and New Orleans, could not accommodate.

He was sixty-three years old, with a bilious flutter behind his ribs that was not quite pain, writing by candlelight in the capital of a republic at war. He did not have the luxury of his own principles.

He wrote the letter.

It took him twenty minutes. He wrote plainly, without the subordinate architecture he deployed in cabinet — short declarative sentences, the kind he used with Dolley and almost no one else. He asked Monroe to make independent inquiries regarding the present disposition of defensive works along the Chesapeake approaches. He specified no channel. He named no source. He gave Monroe nothing that could be traced back to Dolley's correspondents or to Gordon's report, and he asked for Monroe's observations to be conveyed by private means rather than through the department's ordinary routing.

He read it through once.

Then he reached for the sealing wax.

The stick had softened in the heat before he held it to the flame, and the wax ran readily onto the fold — dark red in the candlelight, nearly black. He pressed his personal seal into it, the small one, the private one, and held it there while the wax firmed. Not the president's seal. Not an official directive. A private man writing to another private man, in a republic whose official channels had become, without his consent and without his knowledge until this evening, unreliable.

He set the letter on the desk.

The moth was still at the window glass, its wings a dry whisper against the pane, its shadow enormous and erratic on the wall above the desk. Madison took up Armstrong's précis one final time and went through it from the first line to the last — not searching now, not comparing, simply receiving it as the document Armstrong had chosen to place in his hands. He set it down. He took up Gordon's report and received it in the same way.

He placed Gordon's report on top of the précis and weighted both with the inkwell.

Outside, through the open window, the lights of Georgetown burned low across the marshland. Beyond them, invisible in the dark, the road ran north and east toward the bay, toward the wide Chesapeake approaches, toward the deep-water anchorages where, if the letters in his coat were to be credited, five thousand veterans of the Peninsula were at this hour waiting for the tide and the order to sail.

A courier arrived at the outer gate — hooves on the drive, sharp and sudden in the still night, then the voice of the night-watch, then a second voice, lower, speaking with some urgency.

Madison did not move from his chair.

He had been expecting this, or something like it, since he had opened the satchel that afternoon. He had known it was coming the way a man knows a storm is coming when the air takes on a particular stillness and the light goes wrong — not from any single sign but from the accumulation of signs, the pattern they made together, the thing they added up to.

The moth had gone quiet.

The knock came at the study door less than two minutes later.
